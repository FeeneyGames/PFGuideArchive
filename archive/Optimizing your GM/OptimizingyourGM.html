Abraham Spalding’s Guide to GM Optimization.

Yeah it’s me again.  This time I’m going over how to optimize your GM.

What is this?

This guide is for players.  Yes it’s about GMs but this is for players.  The idea is to give players ways to help get the most out of their GM.  GM’s are difficult with many demands but helping them grow into well rounded great GMs is a very rewarding experience that will provide years of fun adventures for people around the world.  Optimizing your GM helps them run the game smoother, allowing for more actual play and less metagame worries.

How do I use this?
The guide has several sections, some may be more useful for you than others.  If you are having a problem with a GM check out the “Deconfliction Techniques”, if you want to help prepare the GM or take some of the burdens off his back consider “The GM’s Battle Buddy”, if you are a new GM and need help consider, “Techniques GMs use to give you a better game”.  About to get into a new game or sit down for the first time with a GM?  Look into “Questions to ask before you start.”  Finally there are definitely some extra odds and ends laying around towards that might help a GM or player come up with something to simply help a good table get a little more out of their game.

What if I hate my GM?

Then stop playing with him.  If you are at the point of hating someone over a game then it’s just not meant to be.  Don’t waste your, or the GM’s, time.  Some people just shouldn’t game together, and that isn’t a bad thing.  Everyone has a different style, and it’s better to realize that and walk away then to have bad blood at a table.

Why do you keep talking about relationships and talking?

Because gaming, at the end of the day, is completely about relationships.  The trust between a GM and the players is paramount to having an excellent game.  Trust cannot be established if people are not honest with each other.  Also there are only three ways to solve a problem between people:  1 kill one of the people (not a good choice and I do not advocate this) 2 do not associate with the person(people) you have a problem with (not really a solution) 3  talk it out.  Truthfully two is a non-option in the same way that ignoring the problem is a non-option… the problem doesn’t go away you are simply ignoring it.  That pretty much just leaves killing or talking, and as I do not advocate killing people I would full heartedly suggest talking.

Deconfliction

Given enough time any two people will have an argument or disagree over something.  This is normal, and a natural part of human relationships.  Generally these will blow over quickly, however sometimes such disagreements last and the only real way to solve them is to dig through them like a rust monster going through armor.  Typically conflicts in role playing games between players and GMs break down into the following types:

Rules Conflicts:

You think one thing, the GM thinks another.  Pretty standard fare really.

Optional Conflicts:

You want one thing included and the GM doesn’t want it in ‘his’ game.

Perception discrepancy:

You think one thing happened, and the GM thought something different happened.

Expectations Conflict:

You were expecting something to happen and the GM is expecting or providing something else.

Solving Rules Conflict:

First off rules conflicts are fast.  You want to state your position, quickly and then be ready to let play continue.  Perhaps the GM simply misremembered how something worked and you saying something reminds him of the right way.  One thing I try not to do is assume I have all the knowledge the GM does on the situation -- after all there could be a spell or effect in play that changes the situation from what I think it is.  Again you don’t want to have a full on argument in the middle of play as that almost never ends well.  After the game is usually the best time to address rules issues, or if you are using something you think might cause a rule issue before game is a good time to address it.  One thing I try not to do is bring how something is ruled on the forums to the game or going to the forums to “prove someone wrong” -- this will not help the game.  The forums are not at the table and do not exist to give you weight with your fellow players.  Rules forums can be helpful in clarifying how something might work or how it should work but that usually isn’t useful at the table -- it’s something to cover before or after the game.  Again if you have a rules issue with your GM, be quick, be concise then let play continue.  Even if the GM is wrong realize sometimes that happens, just as sometimes referees get the call wrong in sports, keep your head in the game and move on if possible.  The biggest thing is to have consistency for a session.  As long as you can expect something to go the same way each time it comes up, at least you can plan for it accordingly.

Getting past Optional conflicts:

Either the GM is going to allow the material -- or he is not, but there are methods you can use to help make it easier for him to say yes.  One method for solving this I have found useful as a GM is to spell out what books I’m most willing to allow and then let the group vote on which they want to use.  Any book they use plus the bestiaries are available for my use as a GM.  If they don’t use an optional book (say ultimate combat) then I will not use that book.  

Another method that has worked for me is I (as GM) limit the group to seven races, eleven classes and ten prestige classes total.  The players can pick what seven races they want available what eleven base classes they want available and what ten prestige classes they want available.  That’s all there is in the world.  This helps because it gives me the exact same number of options I would have had to deal with in the core book, but it also allows the players to build more exactly what they want.  As a GM this can also help define what the campaign setting is like.  For example if the only spell casters are wizards, witches, magus, alchemist, investigators, and oracles it tells you a lot about the magic of the world -- it’s mostly accomplished by studying and those that have magic without studying are weird (don’t follow the normal rules for arcane magic due to them being divine) and cursed (well because they are oracles).

One other method that has worked for me is to “build the class” that I want to play across twenty levels.  By this I mean if I’m going to take levels in different classes or archetypes I place them in order with what abilities I would get at each level in the same format that the core rulebook uses.  Below that I list out the abilities I would get following the ‘class’ I have built so that the GM literally has the twenty level progression for my character in front of him with everything about it right there.  Some GMs like this, others don’t.

Seeing eye to eye:

People see things differently, and when that happens in a game of imagination between you and the GM the difference it can make is huge!  The important thing to remember is if you are seeing things differently neither of you are necessarily wrong, but you have to find common ground.  Use neutral language and tell the GM what you thought was going on, and what you think he is saying he thinks is going on, ask him to do the same for you.  Once you both know what the other thought, and what the other thought you thought you can start coming to a consensus on what is actually happening.

Expectations Conflicts:

The GM wants to run an epic battle of good versus evil -- you want something more sandboxy where you build a kingdom.  He wants people that save the world, you want people that live in a down to earth setting.  You both want and expect different things from the campaign and this could tear the world apart.  Instead of fighting over which should happen try to find a way to combine the two.  Perhaps you are a hero trying to keep a good kingdom from being overwhelmed by the evil empire and you have to rebuild its fortunes (and lands).  He gets epic battles, you get to set about figuring out how to get it done.  He wants you saving the world, you want down to earth heroes -- may I suggest the hobbit or lord of the rings as reference material to do both?  You get regular “hobbits” he gets to see the world saved.

GM Behaviors you might not like

Every person is different, every gaming group is different, every session is different.  Sometimes what the GM does is going to irk you.  It’s important to realize why and exactly what  he did that upset you though.  This isn’t always easy;  I know I have left tables when I was younger feeling unsatisfied about the way things went but unable to realize what exactly had upset me.  The following is a list of various behaviors to help you identify exactly what it is that bothered you so you can talk to the GM about the specific behavior that’s the problem as that is more useful than, “I don’t know I just don’t like him GMing for me.”  

WITH THAT SAID:  you might think “Hey my GM does that, but I don’t see it as a problem.”  and that’s GREAT!  If you don’t mind, and the other players don’t mind, then it’s not a problem -- DO NOT try to ‘fix’ it if it is not broken.  If your GM runs massive battles all the time and then it’s a huge monty haul and your table is loving it more power to you.

1) The Counter GM 

You get to the game things are going alright and suddenly your major thing no longer works. If you are a fire mage all the enemies are fire-proof, if you are a paladin all the enemies are neutral. If you are ranged there is always fog, wind walls, deeper darkness or some other means that prevents you from attacking at range. Regardless of what you want to do it no longer works. High attack bonus? Everything's AC jumps, high AC? Everything's attack bonus jumps, lots of damage? More HP, SoS/SoD? Nothing fails its saves. So you develop new tactics just to have them closed down too, eventually nothing works because everything is immune to everything.

What to do: Talk to your GM about the problem. It could be he has a weird idea of just how effective things are or he doesn't realize much he's overcompensating for your abilities. Assure your GM you aren't looking to have an easy time, but you would like to be able to do the things you are good at sometimes.

2) The Director 

Hey everybody get on the train! You have a part to play and by the gods you are going to play it. This play has a plot and if you side track it everything is going to blow up or just plain not work.

What to do: Talk to the GM about the problem. It could be he's uncomfortable with the system and doesn't understand how to work around various abilities. Maybe he's really only worried about a specific power and he's overcompensating in order to prevent that one type of power from entering the game. Perhaps too much time on the forums has him intimidated about "players taking over my table". Perhaps give him some plot hooks and ideas before the game and early in the planning for future games so he can more directly work in what you would like to see your character develop towards too. Giving him information can help the GM better develop the story to take the actual characters into account instead of having to guess at what everyone is trying to do.

3) The Actor 

Oh no! The world needs saved! Fortunately the GM has an NPC for that. Maybe not just one, perhaps he has several. At the end of the day you feel more like you are there to simply witness the NPC's doing everything while you walk around.

What to do: Talk to the GM about the problem. He might miss playing and need someone else to take over so he can get a game or two in from the other side of the table. Maybe he's afraid your PCs can't handle what he is throwing at you so he overcompensates in the other direction. You need to let him know that while it's his world you do want to play in it too. Interact with the NPCs so he gets some acting time in too. If you usually simply say, "We go buy stuff at the store" instead start showing interest in the store and the NPC running it. This allows the GM to get his acting on without having to run all over the PCs to do it.

4) The Tormentor 

This GM is a lot like the counter but there is a key exception. All your stuff works, it just takes forever for any fight to end. Literally every fight seems like a three hour slog where whatever you are doing works, but seems to have little to no effect in just ending the fight. Every fight ends with a near TPK, and he swears he's taking it easy on you!

What to do: First consider your party make up. If you are a bunch of intelligence 10 wizards trying to go with a two handed weapon melee build then that might be your problem. However if you have good party balance and your characters are generally well built (your fighter has armor and power attack, your wizard has a good casting stat and so on) then perhaps it really is the GM. He might think that if you don't just barely get by you won't feel like you got your money's worth so to speak. If that's the case assure him that you like a good epic battle every now and then, but not every battle needs to be epic (nor should every battle be so).

5) The Unprepared 

You know the GM is generally good. But he just isn't prepared. He has to look up monsters all the time, and he doesn't know what's behind door A and just a minute while he looks in section 47 to see what's in the room.

What to do: Talk to the GM about it. Could be the poor guy just has too much going on. Maybe he's getting burned out and needs a break. If those aren't the case perhaps you can help him more directly. Perhaps once a month someone else should run a one off to give him more time to prepare. Perhaps you can offer to come over and make up some index cards for the monsters he's going to need for the next week. Have him walk through each area before the game with someone so he has a feel for what's on the map before the game starts, to help with preparation. In general this one tends to come down to time and lack of organizational skills.

6) The "Oldschool" 

This GM either played 1st edition or just feels this way but everything needs to be "challenging." Skill DC's are always about 10 higher than the highest skill. AC is about 10 higher than highest attack bonus. Saves are at least about 10 higher than spell DC's etc...

What to do: Talk to the GM about it. Let him know that some things are supposed to be easy as you level up. Also that if you scale challenges to be hard for the best than it's impossible for the middling people. You players should be rewarded if they work to do something well.

7) The Gold Depriver 

For this GM it doesn't make sense that Goblins have 100gp on them, or that Monsters have gold, or Bandits to have 3,000gp at their camp, or for the locals to pay thousands of GP for the quest they want you to do. (Especially if your GM wants you to do it for the greater good and not for personal wealth) Whatever the reason, you're tens of thousands of gold behind WBL and still going up against CR+3 encounters

What to do: Talk to the GM about it. The GM should understand that CR expects you to have certain gear at certain levels, and if you don't have the wealth than some classes will under-perform. Or to lower the CR since the party isn't at full APL strength.

8) The Warzone Lover

Every fight this GM throws at you is just way too huge. Sure, the fight itself may not be that hard, but he wants to make sure each and every fight feels "epic." He rolls individually for the initiative of each of his 50 monsters on the field, even using them before you have access to such board-clearing spells as Fireball. He's meticulous to the core, making intricate markings on the map, and this would be well and good if only he didn't make encounters that took hours before a single turn was over. You start walking away from the table for a break the moment this GM mentions NPC allies are helping you in a coming fight... This GM is similar to the Tormentor, but rather than the fights taking too long because of your party composition or because the monsters are that tough, the fights take forever because there's too much to keep track of.

What to do: As always, talk to your GM about it. He needs to understand that you're not having fun when it takes that long for your turn to come. Massive battles have a time and a place; "all the time" is not it.

9) The Unhappy

The Unhappy GM is someone who just doesn't like what they're running. It may not have an impact on you at first, but his mood quickly starts to bring the mood of the players down. Maybe he wishes he was running a different game; maybe he wishes you'd designed your party a bit more traditionally; or maybe it's something else. Whatever the case, he's your GM, and when he's not in a good mood, nobody will be in a good mood.

What to do: Find out what's bugging your GM. It could be a lot of things, but if he's not having fun running the game then you won't have fun playing in it. There may be a compromise you need to make as players so the GM has a bit more fun, or maybe your GM just needs a break from GMing. Either way, if your GM is in a good mood and is engaged by the game, you're more likely to be engaged by the game.

10) The Monty Haul GM

Everything's easy. You have loot coming out your ears, no fight is ever dangerous - maybe the enemies are lightweights, maybe the rolls are fudged - and short of another PC enacting the Head of Vecna scam nothing bad can ever happen to your PC.

What to do:  Care!  This one can backfire so fast it’s not even funny!  But you do need to let the GM know if this is a problem for you.  The GM might be concerned that if he ups the difficulty people will get upset.

11) The Overly Graphic

Every scene just gets too graphic.  He spells out everything in all it’s gory, slick, wet, sloppy, pulsing, vile detail.  From scenes that should have faded to black 10 minutes ago, to the literal feces on the walls in the evil temple to just how the orc is dismembering and eating the puppies this GM just goes TMI on things.

What to do:  This one is hard -- you simply have to own it and let him know you are NOT comfortable with this level of detail and it has to stop.  This is one of the few ones you simply don’t sugar coat, if someone is making you uncomfortable like this they need to know.  If they are unwilling to stop I would suggest not returning to this table at all.

12) The Wide Open World

Some people like playing (or running) a sandbox game.  They simply give you the area around you and let you drive everything.  If you are in the mood and creative this can be great!  You get to play the character you want, you get to give him the hooks, watch them get set and decide which order you take things.  Sometimes however GMs leave thing a little too open.  With no direction and no idea of what is around you it’s difficult to impossible to keep driving things forward.

What to do:  Ask the GM for some direction.  Give him some background story for him to work some hooks in to help everyone get moving.  It could be he is just waiting for some inspiration so offer some ideas of what sort of game you are looking for.

13)  The Aggressor

He’s waiting out there waiting for you.  He cannot be stopped, he will find every orphan you take in, every NPC you marry, every relative of your character regardless of how well you thought they were protected and kill them.

What to do:  Talk to him about.  Remind him that you are simply trying to play your character up and it would be nice to not have to always play an emotionally stunted, orphaned, lone wolf.

14) The Antagonist

Somewhere, somehow, this GM got something fundamentally wrong ingrained into his head. This person believes that his role in the game is to kill your characters. He states it openly, and every encounter is CR+3 or higher, or even comes with additional house rules like max monster hit points, or even 1.5 times max hp's and other buff spells already in effect. Sometimes he pulls a "D*ck Move" and will randomly ambush a player when they're in town and away from the group with encounters specifically made to hit a character's weak point. He is a fan of save or suck/die spells and likes throwing them at you with stupidly high DC's. He will spend countless amounts of time looking up the worst monsters, traps, and places to throw his victims into. Playing his game stresses both your nerves and your reserve of blank character sheets.

What to do Most of the time it seems that the answer is 'talk to the DM', and this might work with the Antagonist, but the core issue is the way they view being a GM. If possible, try to get them to enjoy playing in a game where the GM does things differently, or put him in the seat of his victims and run a game for him that goes the same way and be absolutely brutal about it, though this avenue has a chance to backfire.

Alternatively, perhaps Pathfinder isn't the way to go with them. Offer him the chance to run a game using a system more built for his style of play. Like Call of Cthulhu or Dark Heresy.

Questions To ask before you start

These questions are designed to help you and the GM match your expectations (and perceptions) before you actually begin the game.  Just as importantly to asking these questions though is letting the GM know what you were thinking too.  Everyone has a say at a good table.  While you and the GM might not fully agree on a point if you are both aware of it you can work at minimizing the negative impact of the disagreement, and it lets the GM know that there could be an issue before hand, which could change how he approaches the subject.

What type of campaign/setting are you looking to run?

Specifically is this a horror game, is it an epic adventure, is the the journey of heroes, or are we just everyday grungy adventurers no different than anyone else?  What level of magic should we expect?  Are there literal magic stores on every corner or are magical items really rare?  These help you create a character that is ‘in sync’ with the setting and campaign type.

What are somethings you don’t like to see in a character?

Yes it is your character.  But if the GM has a hate on (like a hard on but powered by hate) for wizards you might not want to play one.  If he hates it when a character has a 20 coming out of the gate that’s good to know too.  Conversely maybe he hates characters with a really low stat -- Raistlin is not welcome at his table!  You are going to want to know if the GM doesn’t like guns or if he isn’t a fan of paladins that aren’t lawful stupid since this will influence how he views your character.

Do you have any house rules?

I forget to ask this sometimes and it can really bite you in the butt, especially if it’s a long time group and you are new to it.  People forget to tell you “oh yeah I control familiars and animal companions in this game” or “summoning spells directly affect your alignment” or “Paladins are not allowed to use ranged weapons” (yes I have seen that one).  Not knowing how a GM has changed the rules is like playing russian roulette with a pistol -- you are going to get shot.

How do you handle disagreements at the table?

Some GMs are perfectly willing to discuss how something should have played out by the rules -- after the game.  Others want you to bring it up right in the middle of the action at the table.  Yet others never want to hear a cross word about how they run the rules.  At the end of the day you need to know so you don’t step on toes at the wrong time.

How do you handle character backgrounds?

Many GMs like character backgrounds and will use hooks that are presented… but you might not be prepared for how they use those hooks.  Talking this point out with a GM can help you from being unpleasantly surprised when he kills all your loved ones, or when he hates the fact that all the players only play orphans who never develop attachments.

What do you consider to be a weak/normal/epic combat?

Some GMs want each and every combat to be an edge of your seat cliff hanger.  Others expect top end optimization… some are just as content if your wizard is weapon focused in rock throwing.  Knowing what the GM expects from different fights helps everyone match expectations.

What are the pre-game rituals for your group?
I’ve had groups where we literally get together for breakfast on Saturday game all day then sleep and continue on Sunday.  One group I was with did exercises and combat drills before game and sometimes cooked together.  Another group was in a gaming shop and people had different chores on game day to help keep the place tidy in exchange for the ability to game there.  Finally I was part of a group that had no eating at the table -- the table was game only and everything else was not to happen there.  Knowing these helps you fit into the flow and not be disruptive out of game, which helps prevent disruptions in game.

How long do you game at a time?

You don’t want to sit down for what you think is a four hour session only to find out this is the weekend long group!

What are your dice rolling conventions?

Keep it as it lands?  Only rolls on the table count?  What if it’s a leaner?  Do you get stabby if other people touch your dice (yeah that happens).  Again got to know the culture you are walking into.

How comfortable are you with the rules?

You have to be careful asking this one, a lot of people don’t like you calling their understanding and “ability” to play the game into question.  But you need to understand just how well they understand the rule set and how closely they follow changes to the game.  If they are brand new to the system that’s going to affect the level of system mastery you should display and what materials you should consider using.  It’s not bad to be new or to not fully know the rules, but if you know that’s the case you can give them more leeway if they aren’t quite up to ‘your speed’ so to speak.

Things you can do to make your GM’s Life easier

So the GM rules the world and so on, ultimate power blah blah blah.  Except he’s only one guy and there is a whole lot of world to rule.  If you make his life less tedious he can come up with better and more ways to give you a good game.  Some things you can do:

  1. Monster cards:  Get some index cards and put the combat stats for various monsters on them.  This way the GM has the stock monsters ready at his fingertips without having to open the book and wade through extra information.  If you have a weekly game with 4~5 players plus the GM and every player does one card a day during the week except game night and the night after each weekend your table has 20~25 new easy to reference monsters a week.  You could keep them in a binder or an index card box with dividers for easy access.  The GM can pull out the cards he thinks he’ll need before the game and have them paper clipped/ stowed in the area that they’ll come about.
  2. Offer to track combat for him:  The GM has to run every monster, track everything they do and everything the players do too.  If one of the players tracks initiative counts and when spells/effects start and stop then you take a major load off of the GM’s lap.  Generally players have less overall to manage so the task shouldn’t be a huge deal for the player involved, but again it frees up the GM to run everything else smoother.  This really helps too if someone is tracking buffs and debuffs for the group too.  In networking this would be called load sharing.
  3. Have easy reference for your abilities:  I like to print off a “mock class” for my characters based on when they are going to take what levels in what classes and the abilities they’ll gain from this.  If each player tracks and maintains a quick reference for their abilities plus any FAQs or what not the table uses then the GM doesn’t have to have these things memorized.  You can go “I’m going to use this ability, I have the write up here if you want to see it, and it does this.”  Granted you probably know what it does already but by having the specific rules ready for him for hot loading the GM can focus more on general rules knowing that when he needs to refresh on something it will be available.
  4. Verify, don’t accuse:  You might think the GM is misusing a rule or forgetting that a creature has been hit with an ability that normally works one way, but that doesn’t mean it is actually the case.  Perhaps the creature is ignoring the difficult terrain you created because he has a ring of freedom of movement.  If you think the GM is doing something that doesn’t jive with what you think should be happening don’t accuse the GM of cheating.  Instead go with something like, “Um, I thought that was difficult terrain there, is the (creature) seem to be getting through it easier?”  Give the GM a way out so he doesn’t feel attacked.  This helps communication stay clear.
  5. Spell cards/buff cards:  Some companies offer spell or buff cards for easy reference to material provided.  These can be great because they are small, quick to use, with just the immediate information that matters on them.  There are several people that have free versions for you to make on your own too.  Many GMs like these better than say having your computer there because they are a direct tool that doesn’t have the distraction factor that a computer at the table can provide, and they are faster to reference than books.
  6. Quick dungeon set up:  My wife made plexiglass tiles with grid lines on the bottom.  This way we can quickly draw a map for use.  She used ¼ inch thick plexiglass and had them cut to 12 inch by 12 inch blocks.  This means we can also set them over terrain maps from books and not have to write on something else.  We can also put several together for big maps or just have a few out for smaller areas.  Even if you don’t use tactical maps having a quick means (say a dry erase board) that someone can draw out (even the GM) what the GM is describing can help everyone else quickly see what he is trying to describe without as much perception discrepancy.

Basic Etiquette

Alright I can’t believe this really needs covered but people highly suggest it does so here we go.  A role playing game is supposed to be about immersion.  However just because your character is a loud mouth rude slob that hasn’t bathed in weeks doesn’t mean you should be too.  Good game manners help keep the game focused where it should be, in the game.  A player that is otherwise great but can’t get along with his table is just going to ruin it for everyone.  Here are just some basic rules that should serve you well no matter your situation:

  1. Bath:  For the love of all that is holy, unholy, sane, insane, and in between bath.  I know we all work hard, I get done with a day in the field and I just don’t want to do much of anything.  However a shower will help revitalize you, freshen you up and a change of clothes will change how you look at the day.  Now some people have an odor to them.  They are clean, and doing everything they should they just… smell.  Hey it happens, and you should be aware of it (both as the person the the odor and the person around him) and still be polite.  Assumptions are generally a bad way to go which brings us to a very important part.
  2. When in doubt ASK:  No one (not even me) knows everything.  If you don’t know you should ask, “Hey man is it alright if I bring some pizza over, or should I eat before the game?”  If the host (not always the GM) has young children it’s likely that how they get fed and when is kind of a big deal.  As such the host might not want you to bring in pizza right after he had the kids eat as the children might bug you for a slice or cause other issues.  Ask if there is a dress code.  Ask if you should take your shoes off before you come in.  Here’s the big deal:  IF you ask then when you don’t and you screw up people will realize it wasn’t on purpose.  If you generally ask and try to follow the answer people will appreciate your good manners and give you the benefit of the doubt more often.
  3. Politeness:  Look I get it, you don’t have kids, mine are bugging you, and it drives you nuts.  As a parent though let me let you in on a secret -- the ruder you are to them, and the more you try to drive them off the more the kids are going to mess with you.  It’s easier to simply be polite, smile once or twice or ask them to move on.   On the subject of spouses, look the host has to live with their spouse -- they don’t have to live with you.  Don’t make the host chose between the two of you because the spouse will win.  If the spouse makes the host chose well, you really don’t want to get in on their domestic squabbles anyways.
  4. Gamer Essentials:  When you are new to the hobby people expect you won’t have everything you need.  Sometimes the best of us forgets stuff too (ah man I forgot my dice/character sheet/ books/ et al).  This happens, but it shouldn’t be an every time thing.  You should have what you need with you when you come to game, without having to recreate your character from scratch each time.
  5. Leave IRL in IRL:  Look some of us are passionate about our religion, politics, sports teams, or jobs.  That is good!  Everyone should have something that they care deeply about.  However leave those things out of the game.  Sometimes something will happen that drags part of it in, (perhaps you don’t care for homosexuality and some PC or NPC is homosexual) -- don’t let the game escape into real life and don’t let real life ruin a game.  If the subject is one you simply can’t deal with I would suggest either learning to deal with it, or moving on.  The game is not here for you to “prove” to someone their philosophy/religion/politics are wrong.
  6. Randomness:  Gamers are quirky.  Double check with each before you do something with their stuff.  Yeah most the group might not mind if you hand them their dice or something, but personally I hate other people rolling my dice unless I give the go ahead.  Heck I hate being touched in general.  You want to look over my books, character sheet, tiles, minis what-have-you we are good.  Stay off my dice and don’t touch me.  Hey, we all have our quirks.  If someone is about to hit one of yours say something politely first.  They probably don’t know.

Random Stuff:

A player’s Responsibility:

Talk to your GM: Just as the GM is there to help the players have a good time the players are there to help the GM have a good time.  You should provide feedback after every session even if it’s just, “I loved the way you ran that monster” or “That trap was devious” or “Thanks for a great time.”  GMs need the reminder that they are appreciated, and to know if something isn’t up to par.  Also letting them know the parts you really liked can help the GM put more of that into the game.

Try not to be a problem player:  Gee great advice right!?!?  But seriously, if you notice you are standing head and shoulders over the other characters scale it back some so they have some time to shine.  If you find everyone is constantly having to bail you out consider getting some help stepping your character up so you aren’t a drain on resources.
