INTRODUCTION

        

This guide was inspired by an offhand comment made to a friend, and many Pathfinder sessions stymied by the adventurer’s true greatest foe: the humble door. Adventurers may or may not face a decent amount of kobolds, ogres, demons, and monsters of all descriptions over the course of their adventuring career, but one obstacle is certain: doors.

The goal of this guide is to examine various techniques in which adventurers can overcome doors, gates, walls, and portals of all descriptors. The guide will cover not only ways to unlock or batter down these obstacles, but also methodologies for hunting down elusive “secret” doors and dispelling magical wards and entryways. As a bonus option, at the end of the guide we’ll go over some ways to lock, bar, and create barriers for your foes (Coming Soon).

Formatting for this guide is loosely based on N. Jolly’s Guide to the Pathfinder Alchemist, which is a great read and can be checked out here.

Paths

There are a few basic “paths” to Pathfinder doorbreaking that this guide will go over, from both a philosophical and mechanical standpoint. The best doorbreakers will of course incorporate options from all three paths, but each has definitive benefits and drawbacks to its pursuit.

The Thief in the Night (TN)

Pros: The Thief in the Night focuses on stealth and subtlety in bypassing doors, wards, and portals. A properly-built Thief in the Night should be difficult if not impossible to detect in their breaking and entering activities-- in fact, a good Thief in the Night’s entry should never be noticed at all. The Thief in the Night focuses on lock-picking and stealth, and prioritizes unobtrusiveness over efficiency.

Cons: The Thief in the Night is the least-likely option to “play nice” with a traditional party of pathfinding rapscallions, who tend to make their presence known. There are a number of obstacles that are difficult for a Thief in the Night to overcome: anything that requires a test of strength or magical potency will generally need to be bypassed with creative thought and guile. When the “jig is up” for a Thief in the Night, it will generally be made difficult or impossible to revisit newly-fortified areas.

Archetypal Classes: Rogue/Ninja, Monk, Shadowdancer

The Gatecrasher (GC)

Pros: The Gatecrasher is the polar opposite of the previous option, tackling every barred door and possessed portal the same ways: head-first. A well-built Gatecrasher should let nothing stand in their path. When presented with a hallway forking to the left and the right, a Gatecrasher goes straight. The Gatecrasher focuses on brute strength and sundering, with the mightiest Gatecrashers able to smash through even adamantine barriers and walls of force.

Cons: The Gatecrasher is likely to run afoul of all that he comes across, although he is admittedly unlikely to care. While the Gatecrasher goes well with a more “traditional” adventuring party, he is likely to give Dungeon Masters headaches with his very direct route of dungeoneering. Depending on his class makeup, a Gatecrasher might be utterly unable to deal with certain challenges whatsoever (generally anything requiring stealth or magical potency).

Archetypal Classes: Barbarian/Bloodrager, Fighter, Gunslinger

The Arcane Trickster (AT)

Pros: Christened after the prestige class of the same name, the Arcane Trickster solves their problems with both guile and force, infused with eldritch energies. A good Arcane Trickster should be able to bypass any situation, albeit with extensive preparation and forethought. The Arcane Trickster focuses on spells and abilities that can force through ancient portcullises, augment their natural capabilities for stealth and trapbreaking, and dispel magical barriers.

Cons: The Arcane Trickster has the most limited resources at her disposal, able to accomplish mighty feats of doorbreaking or subterfuge but unable to sustain these efforts for long. The Arcane Trickster is likely to cause some friction with the traditional “party rogue” with her “superior” abilities, even though every spell expended cuts into the Trickster’s true potential as a spellcaster. The Arcane Trickster can deal with any situation, but does so only with excellent planning or loss of versatility.

Archetypal Classes: Wizard/Arcane Trickster, Alchemist, Bard

ABILITY SCORES & RACES

Ability Scores

Strength (TN/GC/AT) Both the Thief in the Night and the Arcane Trickster can safely ignore this statistic. The Gatecrasher, on the other hand, values STR above all else, as it drives every one of his abilities.

Dexterity (TN/GC/AT) The Gatecrasher can see some benefit from DEX (as much as any martial character), but it is ultimately unhelpful for his approach to life. The Thief in the Night on the other hand should pump DEX as high as possible, as it governs their stealthiness, lock-picking and trap-disarming capabilities, and likely many of their other feats and abilities. The Arcane Trickster can make good use of DEX for similar pursuits, especially when enhanced with spells and magical abilities.

Constitution (TN/GC/AT) Every character likes CON to not die, but the Gatecrasher needs this a little more than his compatriots. Of the three, the Gatecrasher is the most likely to bring the roof crashing down on his head; if the Thief in the Night or the Arcane Trickster is taking damage, something has already gone seriously wrong with their plans.

Intelligence (TN/GC/AT) The Arcane Trickster is likely to have levels in wizard, in which case INT will drive the majority of their abilities. The Thief in the Night tends to be skill-focused and, from at least a roleplaying perspective, must be capable of formulating complex plans and some quick thinking when things go south. The Gatecrasher’s best use for literature is breaking through walls with an adamantine spellbook.

Wisdom (TN/GC/AT) Arguably, if the Thief in the Night had WIS, he wouldn’t be sneaking around alone in hostile environments. WIS governs a very important skill, however (Perception) which raises its importance even with rogue class features providing boosts. The Arcane Trickster can often bypass the need for Perception with magic, but won’t be happy being driven insane when trying to dispel a ward. The Gatecrasher’s best use for WIS is a helpful cleric on-hand.

Charisma (TN or TN/GC or GC/AT or AT) CHA is an interesting skill, varying wildly in helpfulness between different types of doorbreakers. A Thief in the Night with access to certain traits can remove all dependence on CHA for social skills and Use Magic Device, making it essentially useless. The Arcane Trickster will care very heavily about CHA if it governs their spellcasting; otherwise, not at all. While the Gatecrasher would normally ignore CHA, it does govern the one social skill the Gatecrasher is likely to have (Intimidate).

Races

Some races are universally good for doorbreakers (generally, any race with a floating ability score bonus is going to be “good enough”), but there are a few special abilities keyed to various races that are worth mentioning.

Core

Dwarf

TN: Dwarves don’t generally go in for being sneaky, although their racial ability score bonuses do include WIS and CON which are both helpful to a Thief in the Night, as well as a penalty to a score they usually don’t care about (CHA). Stonecunning can also be useful to a Thief.

GC: Their ability bonuses are only okay for a Gatecrasher, and no bonus to STR hurts a lot, but they do have a couple of interesting racial options.

AT: While the “stock” Arcane Trickster isn’t well-served by being a dwarf, the cleric makes a surprisingly capable magical infiltrator due to a few spells that will be discussed later in this guide. Since Dwarves make good clerics, that makes them half-decent “Arcane” Tricksters.

Elf

TN: With their bonus to DEX and INT, Elves make pretty good Thieves in the Night. Access to a particular 1st-level spell that will be discussed later on gives them a huge boost (even if this does cut in slightly to the Arcane Trickster’s territory, 1st-level wands should be within any canny Thief’s grasp).

GC: A penalty to CON and no bonus to STR, with no relevant racial abilities? Sign me up!

AT: Just like dwarven clerics, Elves make good wizards, and wizards make good Arcane Tricksters. Arcane Tricksters are also just as happy to make use of Blend.

Gnome

TN: Gnomes make decent thieves due to their Small size, but the lack of a DEX bonus hurts them. They have a smattering of useful spell-like abilities and alternate racial traits, and the CHA boost is useful if unable to substitute INT for those skills.

GC: On the one hand, Gnome Barbarians are only slightly less hilarious than Halfling Barbarians. On the other hand, please don’t make a Gatecrasher with a STR penalty.

AT: This rating is assuming the Trickster is a CHA-based spellcaster, in which case the ability bonuses are good and the Small size continues to be helpful for Stealth.

Halfling

TN: Between the DEX bonus, the Small size, and the racial Perception boost Halflings make great Thieves. Just make sure to trade out for Fleet of Foot. Swift as Shadows is a half-decent Stealth booster, although it trades an Acrobatics bonus which can be useful.

GC: Strictly deadlier than Gnome Barbarians, still not a good idea.

AT: As with Gnomes, Halflings get some nice boosts for CHA-based Arcane Tricksters, with their Small size and CHA bonus, in addition to the racial options discussed above.

Half-Elf

TN: Everything that applies to Elves applies here, especially when Half-Elves have access to Blend. The free Skill Focus can be invaluable for any Thief pursuing Hellcat Stealth, a very powerful feat that will be discussed later.

GC: Half-Elves can pick up a bonus to STR and a free exotic weapon proficiency, which makes them decent Gatecrashers.

AT: See the notes on the Thief above. Additionally, Half-Elves have access to Paragon Surge which is an incredibly powerful spell for any character.

Half-Orc

TN: The floating ability bonus makes half-orcs decent Thieves, and they have some powerful racial abilities that are good for most characters such as Sacred Tattoo. Darkvision is a necessity for a Thief in the Night (don’t ever be the rogue carrying a torch!), and getting it for free is a nice perk.

GC: Half-Orcs are very good at smashing, and that’s what the Gatecrasher wants to do. In fact, Half-Orcs have option to a racial ability called Gatecrasher, giving a great racial bonus to everything they want to do in life (namely, STR checks and Sunder maneuvers).

AT: As with the thief, the floating ability bonus is great, and spellcasters certainly can’t say no to a stackable bonus to their saves.

Human

TN: Humans excel at anything they do. Of particular interest to a Thief in the Night is the Focused Study alternate trait, which trades the bonus feat for three different Skill Focuses over the course of the character’s career. Lack of Darkvision (an option many of the other core races have or can get) can be a bit of a drawback for a Thief, however. Get Darkvision as soon as possible, if not sooner.

GC: Gatecrashers tend to be Fighters and Barbarians, and martial classes can always use more feats. Hearts of the Fields* can be a useful option for Barbarians seeking some early rage-cycling.

AT: You know who enjoys bonus feats even more than martial classes? Spellcasting classes. More metamagic, more Improved Initiative, more Skill Focus if going that route.

Featured Races

Note: Only Pathfinder Society-legal non-boon racial options, Aasimar, and Tieflings are listed at the moment.

Aasimar

TN: Aasimar are mostly upsides, with the ability to get just about any combination of ability score increases and powerful spell-like abilities. The Garuda-Blooded Aasimar in particular is great for a Thief in the Night, with bonuses to DEX and WIS and the ability to See Invisibility 1/day. If only the default Aasimar is allowed in your game, however, they become much less effective Thieves; although every type of Aasimar does at least have Darkvision.

GC: The Angel-Blooded Aasimar is great at smashing and Intimidating, two things the Gatecrasher likes. The Alter Self ability gives an extra STR boost 1/day, as well as some utility with movement modes and natural attacks. Just note that Aasimar are not people, and cannot benefit from Enlarge Person without the Scion of Humanity trait.

AT: Peri-Blooded are usually best, although the default Aasimar is good for a cleric Arcane Trickster.

Kitsune

TN:  These cunning shapechangers make for fairly decent Thieves of a charismatic persuasion, with an ability penalty that doesn’t matter (STR) and bonuses to DEX and CHA. While they lack Darkvision, they have some interesting options for boosting Stealth via shapeshifting, and even open up some combat options like Pounce with certain feats.

GC: STR penalty? Agh! The bite attack is nice, but overall Kitsune are best avoided.

AT: Kitsune make for decent CHA-based Arcane Tricksters, but where they really shine is an Enchanter. Kitsune can get into just about anywhere staffed by fallible guardians using charms and suggestions; unfortunately there are areas where their talents are less applicable, such as areas warded with undead and constructs.

Nagaji

TN/TN: Nagaji have little to recommend them as Thieves beyond a boost to their CHA score, which as stated elsewhere is best dumped using traits to replace social skills and UMD with INT. The sole exception is if the Thief is question is an alchemist that would like to specialize in poisons, in which case the boost to poison save DC is fairly unique.

GC: Nagaji have STR, which is needed for smashing, and CHA, which helps with Intimidation. Seems solid as a Gatecrasher, if unexciting.

AT: Nagaji have a very powerful “spellcaster” Druid archetype, the Naga Aspirant which recommends itself by granting access to spells such as invisibility and greater invisibility, while simultaneously not depowering the wildshape ability (instead simply adding another option, and then some nifty tricks at level 9 with Augmented Form).

Tiefling

TN: Stock Tieflings are basically elves, but with a penalty to a statistic that doesn’t matter and a spell-like ability that’s actually pretty awesome. Tieflings also gain access through a racial feat to some nice tricks involving Deeper Darkness that will be discussed later in the guide. Bonus points for Darkvision.

GC: Demon/Oni/Qlippoth-Spawn are all good. If #16 on the Variant Tiefling chart can be accessed, the Tiefling’s power as a Gatecrasher shoots up tremendously. Just note that Tieflings are not people, and sadly have (almost) no way of benefiting from Enlarge Person.

AT: Tieflings are good spellcasters. Bonus points for the ability to use their Prehensile Tail to retrieve wands, rods, and other useful implements from about their person.

Tengu

TN: There are some good ability score bonuses and racial bonuses to skill checks on offer here, although most of the “unique” Tengu abilities don’t do overmuch for a Thief, and lack of darkvision hurts here.

GC: Bird people fly over walls, they don’t break through them.

AT: Decent statistics for a cleric, but the CON penalty hurts and the Tengu abilities don’t do much here.

Wayang

TN: It’s difficult not to recommend a class with these statistic bonuses, and Small-sized with no STR penalty to boot. The WIS penalty hurts a little but is easily accommodated for (especially with options like the Empiricist archetype keying Perception off of INT). Note that the 20 foot move speed is a bit of a bummer when stealthing, although Darkvision and Dissolution’s Child keep this race firmly blue.

GC: Small, moving on-- although there is no STR penalty to be found with this race, making them ever so slightly better than other Small races. The WIS penalty hurts a Druid Gatecrasher option more than a bit.

AT: The size bonus is all upside here, and the statistics are perfect for a Wizard, making this race an automatic winner. Additionally the alternate favored class bonuses for Bard and Oracle are interesting options for a Trickster.

TRAITS

Traits are amazing. Many of them are much better than the half-a-feat they’re meant to be-- some can even give bonuses that can’t be found anywhere else. At the very worst, there’s lots of flavorful traits that can give useful class skills to doorbreakers that otherwise lack them. Note that this guide will only go over unique and interesting traits for a doorbreaker.

For a guide to traits in general, check out: Tips and Traits: A guide to Pathfinder Traits

Combat Traits

Muscle of the Society (TN/GC/AT) Only the Gatecrasher need apply, this trait is great for those crashing both doors and actual gates.

Vandal (TN/GC/AT) A buff for both sundering and forcing open doors? What more do you need?

Faith Traits

Magic Traits

Pragmatic Activator (TN/GC/AT) Use Magic Device is one of the best skills in the game, and a Thief in the Night benefits hugely from good access; the Arcane Trickster, as well, to a lesser degree.

Social Traits

Student of Philosophy (TN/GC/AT) If you want to be social, this is the way to do it. Remove CHA nearly entirely from the picture, all at the cost of being a little pedantic.

Race Traits

Region Traits

Chainbreaker (TN/GC/AT) While very flavorful, the bonuses this trait provides are probably too limited to be worth it. A good Doorbreaker should be equally good at removing any obstacle in his way, not just certain types of locks and chains.

Destructive Blows (TN/GC/AT) While this does lock you into worshipping a specific god, it IS the god of breaking things, which is the Gatecrasher’s mission in life. Essentially a free Sunder feat, with a bonus to STR checks to boot, you can’t go wrong with Rovagug’s blessing.

Egorian School Apprentice (TN/GC/AT) Very cool trait, with an exceptional trait bonus to the associated Knowledge skill and Perception check, but probably not worth it unless doing a lot of adventuring in areas with Egorian architecture.

Oregent Vandal (TN/GC/AT) A little situational, but much better than Chainbreaker for your Andoran freedom fighter. Most traits give +1 damage, this gives +2-- but note that it will not combine with attacks that are ignoring hardness for any reason.

Religion Traits

Opener of Doors (TN/GC/AT) Great flavor, but even the most dedicated finder of secret doors might enjoy a less narrow trait. That being said, Alseta is the goddess of “doorways,” so a Thief of the Night might want her on their side.

Equipment Traits

Heirloom Weapon (TN/GC/AT) Heirloom Weapon is a strong trait, granting virtual proficiency in the indicated weapon. Unfortunately the proficiency only applies to that specific weapon; even if a Gatecrasher chooses a Greatclub, the Maul of the Titans will not benefit from this trait. Otherwise, a +2 trait bonus to Sunder is a good deal.

CLASS ABILITIES AND SKILLS

Good use of class abilities and skills are two of the most important ways to enhance your adventurer’s effectiveness, whatever their pathway through life. This guide will of course be specifically focusing on those abilities and skills that help spot, unlock, break through or bypass doors and portals.

Class Abilities

Core Classes

Barbarian (TN/GC/AT) Arcane Trickster need not apply, although a dip in Urban Barbarian can be useful as a “quick fix” for a stealthy Thief with a low point buy due to their ability to boost DEX with Rage. For the Gatecrasher, there are a few Rage Powers that greatly add to the concept.

Rage Powers

Armor Ripper* This is a very narrow boost (only for Wereboar Skinshifters), but it does give a bonus to one of the most important Combat Maneuvers, Sunder.

Internal Fortitude For Barbarian purists, this is the only way to get early access to rage-cycling, when combined with this item. Rage-cycling will be important to make use of…

Strength Surge Still good when only 1/rage, much better with access to rage-cycling. This gives a scaling bonus to the two favorite things a Gatecrasher likes to do in life.

Smasher The serious Gatecrasher comes prepared to break anything, and this lets him do just that. This can be somewhat replicated with an adamantine weapon, but at the end of the day even adamantine can’t overcome everything.

Spell Sunder While this does have a hefty tree of prerequisites, this removes one of the greatest obstacles for a Gatecrasher: spells. Now you can put that massive Sunder bonus to good use.

Bard (TN/GC/AT) Bards have a nice mix of skills, spellcasting, and class abilities that make them great as a Trickster. Thieves might want to tend more toward Perform: Dance than Perform: Sing for Bardic Performances; it is worth noting that bardic spells always have verbal components, a notable annoyance for Thieves. While certain Bard archetypes can get a decent boost to hit and damage for themselves, it’s probably not worth dipping for a Gatecrasher.

Masterpieces

The Depths of the Mountain Mentioned here for its Gatecrashing applications, this performance duplicates Earthquake at the same level primary spellcasters would receive it; the sheer number of Bardic Performance rounds available at this level, however, mean that a persistent Bard could destroy quite a few mountains before running out of performance.

The Symphony of the Elysian Heart Mass Freedom of Movement in exchange for a 3rd level spell known is an amazing deal, especially enjoyed by Thieves and Tricksters who likely lack a prayer of ever breaking out of a grapple.

Cleric (TN/GC/AT) Both Thieves in the Night and Gatecrashers can benefit from some amount of cleric dip, with a few of the domains discussed below. Cleric spells that benefit the Arcane Trickster concept will be discussed in more detail later in the guide, but here are a few samples.

Domains

Darkness

Trickery

Druid (TN/GC/AT) There’s not much that druids can’t do. While they might have a few less tricky magical options at their disposal than a cleric, the versatility that Wildshape adds more than makes up for this loss. There’s a little too much to cover just in this guide, but as a general rule the Domain will be more useful than an Animal Companion for any type of doorbreaker druid. Note that too many druid levels will rapidly reclassify a Thief in the Night as an Arcane Trickster, so the Thief rating is assuming a dip of some number of levels combined with Shaping Focus. Note that druids are PURPLE when it comes to breaking down doors, as trednis describes here.

Wildshape

Elemental Body I/II/III/IV Elementals have some great statistics boosts to offer, but the real gem of the bunch is the Earth Elemental form. Earth Glide allows for easy bypassing of a huge number of barriers to the average adventurer’s progress, provided wooden fortresses are avoided.

Beast Shape I/II/III Combat capabilities aside, the only ability on this list that aids in our doorbreaking endeavors is burrow, which poorly-defined by the rules and sure to cause headaches for players and DMs alike. Of course any form that gives a STR bonus will help out a Gatecrasher. Note that a Thief could benefit from the ability to assume a Tiny form, but the lack of opposable thumbs could be a bit of an obstacle in terms of navigating obstacles such as doors and stowing purloined treasure.

Plant Shape I/II/III Unfortunately, not a single helpful ability from this list. If the Gatecrasher could pick up the Treant’s Double Damage Against Objects ability, then we’d be talking, but without that it’s a no-grow.

Fighter (TN/GC/AT) The Thief in the Night can benefit from a couple levels of Lore Warden, adding martial weapon proficiencies, several bonus feats, and Knowledge skill training. If it isn’t already gained from multiclassing, the Lore Warden can add Use Magic Device as a class skill if the Pragmatic Activator trait is taken. For a Gatecrasher, Fighter gives enough feats to satisfy any number of Sunder-related endeavors. There are some interesting tricks an Arcane Archer can do that add to the Arcane Trickster concept, in which case a level of Fighter will probably be necessary.

Monk (TN/GC/AT) Monk offers a little something to everyone. Monk thieves benefit from Evasion, and with the Qigong archetype monks can grab useful ki powers such as Gaseous Form. Several monk archetypes offer bonuses to Sunder, and the Brother of the Seal can gain a scaling ability to ignore Hardness. WIS-based Tricksters can consider monk dips for defensive purposes.

Favored Class Bonus

Dwarf An interesting FCB, this reduces the hardness of anything hit with an unarmed attack by 1 each time you take it. Combined with some of the other feats and traits in this guide, you could make a very potent Sundering monk. Serves as a nice progression into Brother of the Seal.

Paladin (TN/GC/AT) A paladin Thief is a bit of an oxymoron (although it is certainly possible to build a stealthy paladin). As Gatecrashers, paladins lead something to be desired, as most doors do not head south of the Neutral part of the alignment axis (Mimics excepted), and paladins struggle with feats compared to many martial classes. The exception to this rule is the Stonelord, which is practically a complete rebuild of the paladin class for dwarves. As with monk, a paladin dip might be considered for a CHA-based Trickster for defensive purposes.

Archetypes

Stonelord Not only is the Stonelord tough-as-nails, with scaling damage reduction and Defensive Stance, this dwarf-only archetype gets two amazing features for a Doorbreaker. Stonestrike gives a scaling method of bypassing hardness and increasing damage, and access to an Earth Elemental pet gives this paladin a huge amount of versatility when it comes to bypassing obstacles.

Ranger (TN/GC/AT) Rangers can easily slip into the role of a Thief, albeit with most effect in their Favored Terrain(s). They are much more geared toward traditional combat, however, with no real effective way to gain Favored Enemy: Door. This regrettable lack of true enmity toward inanimate objects limits the Ranger’s capacity as a Gatecrasher-- at least until certain archetypes come to bear. Of note for martially-blended Tricksters, a level of Ranger over Fighter offers access to a second spell list with no UMD required.

Archetypes

Skirmisher The loss of spellcasting is very, very painful. That being said, there’s a lot of options on display here, including Chameleon Step for fast stealth and Skill Sage for increasing odds on skill checks.

Shapeshifter While the uses per day are limited, these abilities offer some nice boons for Thieves and Gatecrashers in a pinch.

Wild Stalker This is a fairly transformative archetype; rage and the various rage powers available make Ranger a solid choice for a nature-themed Gatecrasher (although the timing on when you receive rage powers is a little feast-or-famine).

Rogue (TN/GC/AT) The classic rogue brings a lot to the table for an aspiring Thief, including bonuses to locating and disarming traps, picking locks, and avoiding backlash from exploding portals. While many of the Rogue Talents are underwhelming, there are a few diamonds in the rough that will be discussed below; as well as a couple of archetypes that greatly enhance the rogue’s doorbreaking capabilities. Gatecrasher and precision damage don’t really mix. The Arcane Trickster can benefit from rogue levels, but they tend to be overshadowed if certain options are legal.

Talents

Trap Spotter (Basic) There’s nothing worse than wandering into a trap that could have been avoided, and this talent takes that option (mostly) off the table for a Perceptive rogue.

Hide in Plain Sight (Advanced) While this is an ability that every Thief in the Night dreams of, limiting it to a single terrain per selection of this talent hurts a lot in an average terrain-hopping campaign. If the Thief can expect to stay more-or-less in one type of locale, however, this obviously improves greatly.

Archetypes

Burglar Uncanny Dodge is nice in combat considering that most of the Rogue’s “armor” is lost when flat-footed. However, the Burglar’s lifeline against traps is invaluable for the Thief in the Night (and the Distraction ability is decent as well).

Trapsmith Same 4th-level ability, another half-decent 8th level ability that plays well with the Thief’s gameplan of leaving behind no trace of their activities.

Sorcerer (TN/GC/AT) There’s nothing much that would inspire a Thief to dip into sorcerer; if the goal is to avoid needing to make UMD checks, wizard is the way to go, with a lot of goodies to be gained up-front from arcane schools. While the “Fireball Sorcerer” can dish out a lot of damage, objects take half damage from energy, applied before Hardness; not the best proposition for Gatecrashing. An Arcane Trickster going Sorcerer is going to have difficulty making Knowledge and Spellcraft checks compared to a wizard, and will have to compromise their effectiveness a lot more in order to focus on sneaky spells.

Archetypes

Wildblooded [Sage] Keying off of INT is a major boost for the Sorcerer as a Trickster. With the use of Paragon Surge or the Human favored class bonus for additional spells, a Sage Sorcerer can make a formidable Trickster, always having the right spell for the job.

Wizard (TN/GC/AT) The best class to dip as a Thief, and the best class as a base for the Arcane Trickster, almost invariably. Some of the Arcane Schools add a lot to the doorbreaker concept, and the excellent spell list and INT focus can’t be overlooked. Note that Gatecrasher is certainly a “possible” role for a wizard-- likely a Transmutation specialist, potentially an Eldritch Knight, or even the absurd Siege Mage.

Archetypes

Siege Mage* Loss of Cantrips is brutal. Losing Scribe Scroll and Arcane Bond (more-or-less) isn’t much fun, either. The nail in the coffin is forsaking an extra school of magic compared to a specialist wizard. In return, the Siege Mage gets to rock the world with bonded Siege Engines launching pure magical destruction into his enemies. Sacrificing high level spell slots for bonus to-hit and damage probably isn’t worth it, but it sure is cool. Best when paired with Shrink Object to carry around convenient ballistae, and an Unseen Crew to man the cannons.

Cavalier (TN/GC/AT) Can one Challenge a door? Not sure. Bringing a horse dungeon-diving is probably a bad idea. A Charging Cavalier build might be able to do some keep-breaking, depending on their choice in mount.

Gunslinger (TN/GC/AT) The Gunslinger gets some cute tricks for a Thief, such as being able to shoot the locks off a door, and synergizes well with DEX-based damage for ranged sneak attacks when a fight breaks out. The real star of the show in terms of doorbreaking, however, is as a Gatecrasher. A Gunslinger is capable of unleashing an astronomical amount of damage in a single full attack; combined with Adamantine Bullets and Abundant Ammunition, a Gunslinger can dispel a Wall of Stone in a single turn, from 20-40 feet away to boot. That being said, a Gunslinger generally won’t be quite as hardy as a “normal” Gatecrasher, and is in a lot of trouble if they lose access to their signature weapon.

Inquisitor (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Magus (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Oracle (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Witch (TN/GC/AT) At first glance the Witch is just a slightly inferior wizard for all three roles, with its reduced spell list and more precarious casting access, but there are a few interesting tricks Witches can make use of to set them apart. Their Hexes do not scale well when multiclassing, but a pure Witch Trickster with the Shadow patron can be respectably sneaky. Notably, the Scar hex allows them to serve as an amazing “Mission Control,” aiding their infiltration partners from afar with Fortune. Just avoid getting too close to the action, as maniacal cackling tends to be a dead giveaway to castle security.

Archetypes

Beast-bonded Note up-front that the rating for this archetype is predicated on playing at level 10 and onward. The loss of a couple of hexes is more than made up for what is essentially an at-will unlimited Magic Jar effect, sending out the witch’s soul or her familiar’s soul at her option. The possibilities for chicanery in all three roles are nigh-endless with Magic Jar, and the best part is, Twin Soul removes the pesky range limitation as death immediately snaps the witch or her familiar back to the survivor’s body.

Advanced Class Guide Classes

Arcanist (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Bloodrager (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Brawler (TN/GC/AT) Brawler offers a little of something to every role. The Gatecrasher is going to see the most benefit from the Steel-Breaker or stock Brawler, with in-class scaling bonuses to Sunder and some free versatility (which Gatecrashers often lack) in the form of “Martial Flexibility.” For the Thief, a dip into Brawler provides Brawler’s Cunning, which allows dim-witted Thieves to bypass INT requirements for feats.

Archetypes

Snakebite Striker This archetype offers a very rare opportunity: the ability to pick up 1d6 Sneak Attack, without losing a point of BAB to boot. Excellent for a Thief looking to add some weight to their attacks, or an Arcane Trickster looking to qualify that much sooner for its namesake prestige class.

Steel-Breaker I’m dubious of Exploit Weakness’ effectiveness, but the tradeoff isn’t painful. The extra +1 to Sunder over the stock Brawler could be worth it for a Gatecrasher.

Hunter (TN/GC/AT) The Hunter doesn’t offer much to the Gatecrasher or Arcane Trickster roles that the Druid cannot do better. With its loss of wildshape, one might think the Hunter would also make an exceptionally poor Thief in the Night; however, the Teamwork feat sharing of the class makes Stealth Synergy an option, leading to astronomical Stealth results with a minimum of effort. The ability to cherry-pick Druid and Ranger spells gives access to most of the spells that complement stealthing, as well. Some traditional rogue-like abilities such as Evasion can also be gained using Animal Focus, the better for surviving obstinate traps.

Investigator [Alchemist/Rogue] (TN/GC/AT) There isn’t really a conceivable reason that a Gatecrasher or Trickster should ever take levels in this class. The loss of Sneak Attack, Bombs, and generally poor offensive abilities make the Investigator pale in comparison to the Alchemist and Rogue in terms of combat. However, the Investigator has some key advantages compared to a rogue, as a Thief in the Night: full access to extracts, and (to a lesser extent) optional access to both Rogue Talents and Alchemist Discoveries. Note that this class is vastly improved by selecting the archetype below.

Archetypes

Empiricist This archetype trades the worst class features the Investigator has (poison use, really?) for the ability to be nearly SAD for skill checks. INT to Disable Device, Perception, Sense Motive, and Use Magic device is too good to pass up. Also nice flavorful bonuses against illusions, which doesn’t hurt.

Extracts

Alchemical Allocation As with the Alchemist, this is the unique offering here. Consult N. Jolly’s Guide to learn more. An important note is that while the Investigator loses out on many Alchemist Discoveries, they do retain access to the potion-based discoveries.

Talents

Amazing Inspiration Going from a d6 to a d8 is only about one point on average; at 20th level the difference becomes significant, but most games don’t make it that far (nor, to be honest, do most Thieves).

Expanded Inspiration / Underworld Inspiration Grouped together because they both add new pools of skills that can be boosted without spending Inspiration points. Adding 3.5 on average to skill rolls for “free” is great for a Thief; without these Talents, the Inspiration will will rapidly run dry, as the Investigator has no way to refill their pool.

Eidetic Recollection Taking 10 on Knowledge checks is very strong; taking 20 is even better. And Inspiration dice can still be added. Another Talent that comes painfully late, though, not legal until 11th level. I find myself wishing there were options for other skills (Perception, Disable Device, etc.).

Tenacious Inspiration Taking the better of two Inspiration dice ups the potential for bonuses significantly, but sadly is not legal until late, late into the Thief’s career.

Shaman (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Skald (TN/GC/AT) The Skald has the same spell list and access to Masterpieces that the Bard does, so anything that applies there applies here. A lot of the versatility of the bard has been lost in the transition; but in return, the Skald gains access to Rage and Rage Powers that make it an exceptional Gatecrasher. Anything mentioned under Barbarian is still good here, although remember that anything that requires the expenditure of rage rounds cannot be doled out to the party.

Archetypes

Belkzen War Drummer This is a phenomenal archetype for Gatecrashers, trading away the pesky vestiges of the Bard class (various bonuses to skills) in exchange for a number of boosts to wielding Greatclubs. Why is this awesome? Because the premiere Gatecrasher weapon, the Maul of the Titans, counts as a Greatclub while wielded. The archetype even gives the option to craft one at half-price. Note that while the Siege Drums ability is amazingly flavorful, the War Drummer will often be better served just full attacking obstacles with the Maul; the ability to destroy obstacles at range is a nice boon, though.

Slayer (TN/GC/AT) The Slayer makes an excellent Thief, gaining access to most of the rogue talents that matter, with increased combat efficacy (and a Favored Target ability that even adds to certain skill checks made against their target). As with the Ranger, the Slayer’s ability cannot be used against inanimate objects, making them a less-than-optimal Gatecrasher.

Archetypes

Cleaner The Cleaner is difficult to rate, as its signature ability is entirely unique. It replaces a Slayer talent and takes a long time to use, but there’s no way to more efficiently cover one’s tracks (lacking high level magic) than “Without a Trace.” The “Mislead” ability comes a little late, but does last indefinitely, a sure boon to an enterprising Thief.

Stygian Slayer While this archetype loses some armor proficiencies, they’re nothing a Thief would’ve wanted anyway. In exchange for this and a couple of Slayer Talents, Invisibility and Gaseous Form as spell-like abilities and the ability to use scrolls of a menagerie of useful spells without UMD are pretty amazing perks.

Occult Adventures Playtest Classes

Kineticist (TN/GC/AT) The Kineticist offers some of the same capabilities that a druid in elemental form brings to the table, such as Earth Glide and Move Earth, but without all that pesky spellcasting to enhance it. Of note is the fact that since their powers are unlimited in use, technically a patient Kineticist can break down any barrier in their way eventually.

Medium (TN/GC/AT) In its current state, the Medium appears to offer a lot of options while in reality doing very little to excel at anything. Of interest to a Gatecrasher would be a dip for the Bear spirit, to gain access to the Enlarge Person spell, always-on claws, a +2 bonus to break objects, and the ability to spend a full round action 1/day to gain various minor benefits for a minute. The loss of BAB is offset by a +1 to attack and damage while the Bear is seanced. Alternatively the Beating could be seanced to gain Improved Unarmed Strike for feat prerequisites, and further boosts to damage and to-hit under certain circumstances.

Mesmerist (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Occultist (TN/GC/AT) Due to the way psychic magic works, psychic spells lack verbal components. As detailed below, the Illusion implement focus allows for a uniquely powerful stealth build for a canny Occultist. While the Occultist’s spellcasting is nothing to write home about, another power of said implement allows for some impressive “spellcasting” at levels 9 and up.

Implements

Illusion Investing a single mental focus in an Illusion implement for “Distortion” gives the Occultist a continual 5% concealment chance until the Occultist makes an attack. Due to the wording of the stealth skill, any concealment allows enterprising Thieves to make stealth checks. As long as the Occultist never attacks, with some investment in stealth and some of the magicks discussed in this guide he should be nigh-undetectable very, very early on. At level 9 and onward, the “Shadow Beast” ability allows the Occultist to do a passable Summoner impression, spending mental focus to mimic Summon Monsters with full spellcaster progression (albeit with an associated will save to avoid halving their effectiveness). As summoning does not break stealth, the Illusion Occultist should continue going unnoticed.

Psychic (TN/GC/AT) Coming Soon

Spiritualist (TN/GC/AT) Look no farther than the Phantom for one of the best rogue-likes introduced to Pathfinder. From level one, the ability to have an incorporeal Phantom (that can still interact with the environment using a derivative of Mage Hand) trivializes the age-old adventuring past-time of breaking and entering, with no breaking required. Even when manifesting the Phantom physically in Ectoplasmic form, it retains the ability to walk through inanimate obstacles; if the door can be unlocked at all, the Phantom can step through the wall and unlock it. The Spiritualist himself can provide invisibility, speed boosts, and everything else the Phantom needs to shine-- and the Phantom itself is not bad at all at UMD. The main drawback here is that the Phantom cannot exceed 100 feet in range from the Spiritualist, which will hopefully be addressable in the final product with some sort of feat or magic item. Additionally, unlike an Eidolon it is difficult to create a combat-effective Phantom at present time.

Emotional Foci

Fear Notable here for its 7th-level “Increase Fear” power, combined with Dazzling Display. An incorporeal Phantom can clear out a room in a hurry by 5-foot stepping out of a wall, taking its full round action to render all foes Shaken, then increasing this state to Frightened automatically as a swift action.

Prestige Classes

Shieldmarshal (TN/GC/AT) Some people might think that the reason to take levels of this prestige class is “Danger Sense”, which allows the Shieldmarshal to act in the surprise round. Those people are wrong. The real gem here is “Labyrinthine Cunning” at level three, giving Shieldmarshals the peerless ability to open or close doors as a swift action, an unlimited amount of times per day. Eat your heart out, spellcasters Quickening Open/Close.

Skills

Appraise (TN/GC/AT) It’s generally useful to know the value of the things one is planning to steal; not to mention the fact that many doors are worth as much as the treasure they guard, if a canny Thief can pry them off their hinges. The Trickster loses very little by throwing a point toward Appraise; the Gatecrasher’s job is to make things worthless, so knowing the original value seems a moot point.

Craft (TN/GC/AT) If you are an Alchemist, and/or if Master Craftsman is allowed, the Craft skills become valuable by dint of allowing for magic item creation. Most Gatecrashers will still give it a pass.

Disable Device (TN/GC/AT) This skill is one of the Big Three that enables the Thief in the Night, and should always be maxed, period. The Gatecrasher has no use for it, while the Arcane Trickster can often avoid having to make use of it.

Fly (TN/GC/AT) A good way to avoid obstacles is to fly over them. Fancy aerial maneuvering probably won’t be necessary when undetected, but it’s never good to be shot out of the air when the bows start firing.

Heal (TN/GC/AT) This is the Pathfinder Doorbreaker’s Guide, not the Pathfinder Doctor’s Guide.

Intimidate (TN/GC/AT) Thieves and Tricksters don’t need to be scary. Gatecrashers, on the other hand, seem a little silly when they’re unable to strike fear into the hearts of their enemies. Intimidating Prowess is what the Gatecrasher wants, or the equivalent Rage Power.

Knowledges (TN/GC/AT) There’s a lot of obstacles that are difficult to overcome without the proper know-how, even with raw skill. Even the Gatecrasher can benefit from Kn. Engineering, in order to efficiently identify which support structure will bring down a building the fastest. Note that some Arcane Tricksters (most classes not based on INT) might have some difficulty finding the skill points for proper knowledge investiture.

Perception (TN/GC/AT) The “best skill in the game,” this is particularly true for Thieves that need to perceive traps and find hidden doors. The Gatecrasher needs it a little less than usual, as they tend to open their own passageways and bring their enemies to them.

Professions (TN/GC/AT) Profession checks are mostly for making money, and adventurers will make far more money bypassing doors than they ever will at selling them. Depending on the GM, it’s worth noting that relevant professions such as Profession: Locksmith might give circumstance bonuses to the Thief’s activities.

Sleight of Hand (TN/GC/AT) The Arcane Trickster probably has better methods of hiding things, but considering the high DEX that Thieves already possess, might as well make available the option to snatch the keys from right underneath a guard’s nose.

Social Skills [Diplomacy, Bluff, Sense Motive] (TN/GC/AT) A good Bluff or an appropriately-amicable discussion could bypass guards even more efficiently than a good Stealth check. Gatecrashers shouldn’t bother, although Sense Motive can be useful for a mercenary-minded Gatecrasher.

Spellcraft (TN/GC/AT) A necessity for spellcasters (especially wizards), every good Thief should have a way to Detect Magic and should be prepared to roll Spellcraft. Identifying hazardous magical traps and runes beforehand is a much better idea than attempting a blind Disable Device check.

Stealth (TN/GC/AT) The last of the Big Three skills, this must be maxed for a Thief in the Night, and added to with a variety of feats and class features. The Gatecrasher scorns stealth, but the Arcane Trickster might find use for it; however, access to spells like Invisibility lessen its importance somewhat.

Survival (TN/GC/AT) Helpful for navigating mazes, or tracking down people who owe a Thief a debt. Not something that most classes will be great at, but WIS-based characters should make some effort to be good at this.

Use Magic Device (TN/GC/AT) Magic is amazing, and a Thief with absolutely no access to magic is going to struggle at higher levels. Gatecrashers will likely have neither the skill ranks nor the ability scores to succeed in UMD.

FEATS

Feats are needed for every type of adventurer, and the doorbreaker is no different. This guide will focus specifically on feats that directly enhance the Thief, the Gatecrasher, and the Trickster’s goals, although there are a number of other feats that can be helpful for the concepts.

Combat

Breaker of Barriers (TN/GC/AT) An untyped +2 bonus to break down doors, two combat maneuver bonuses, and bypassing 5 points of hardness makes this very solid for a dedicated Gatecrasher.

Equipment Trick [Anvil] (TN/GC/AT) The concept is a little silly, but the “Smash Through” benefit lets us break the action economy in a huge way. While normal people are taking multiple turns to get through doors, the (slightly more circumspect version of) Gatecrasher can barge right through.

Smash (TN/GC/AT) Amazingly named, amazing benefits for a doorbreaker. It might be a little “cute”, but +5 is a huge bonus, and it’s untyped! Bypassing hardness is nice as well.

Stunning Irruption* (TN/GC/AT) Possibly the best feat of all time. In actuality it’s a little difficult to work around the requirements to get this to activate (as any allies within 20 feet need to worry about making the save), and the DC will not be that high, and also targets the enemy’s best save on average... but on the other hand, this is a feat where you burst through the wall like the Kool-Aid Man and stun your foes and is therefore amazing.

General

Dampen Presence (TN/GC/AT) The prerequisites overlap with Hellcat Stealth, making this an easy add for a Thief looking to get as untraceable as possible. Best combined with Negate Aroma and some means of flight to ignore all the senses imaginable. This is nice for the Arcane Trickster as well, but she likely won’t have room for the feat with her various metamagic purchases.

Deific Obedience (TN/GC/AT) Specifically, Deific Obedience for Rovagug. The three ranks in Knowledge: Religion might be a painful requirement for some Gatecrashers, but the whopping +4 bonus to attack and damage rolls vs. objects outstrips any singular Sunder feat, and stacks to boot.

Hellcat Stealth (TN/GC/AT) This is basically Hide in Plain Sight. If the Thief wants to consistently get in and out without being observed by anyone, Hide in Plain Sight is the way to do it.

Skill Focuses [Disable Device, Perception, Stealth, Use Magic Device] (TN/GC/AT) While it can be difficult to fit in the Skill Focus feats, they give large bonuses to skills that the Thief in the Night needs and can’t work around with magic. Skill Focus (Stealth) is basically a necessity due to allowing access to Hellcat Stealth.

SPELLS

Battering Blast (Sorcerer/Wizard 3) This has a nice damage type and useful battlefield control capabilities, but the real kicker is the ability to make multiple Strength checks to open a door (if the sheer damage isn’t sufficient, as the spell itself points out).

Blend (Alchemist 1, Druid 1, Magus 1, Ranger 1, Sorcerer/Wizard 1, Witch 1) Elf-Only This spell is amazing for a Thief in the Night, and only slightly less so for an Arcane Trickster-- although only Elves may make use of it. A sizeable, stackable bonus to Stealth, long duration, and a virtual Hide in Plain Sight effect make for a staggeringly good first level spell. It’s featured on a large number of spell lists, and easily emulated with UMD (albeit at a much shorter potential duration).

Clairaudience/Clairvoyance (Bard 3, Sorcerer/Wizard 3, Witch 3) The casting time knocks this down a peg, but it’s still a pretty great information-to-spell slot tradeoff. Remember that there are specific rules for how far you can see-- even a Trickster with Darkvision can’t see farther than the 10-foot radius in pitch black locations.

Detect Magic (Bard 0, Cleric 0, Druid 0, Inquisitor 0, Magus 0, Sorcerer/Wizard 0, Summoner 0, Witch 0) Any spellcaster who doesn’t prepare Detect Magic should be ashamed. Even Thieves in the Night should look into obtaining this simple but powerful spell. In addition to identifying the magical signatures of traps and wards, note that thinner doors can actually be penetrated by Detect Magic to get a limited sense of what lies beyond. Please note that a particularly cruel DM or a clever spellcasting NPCs could anticipate “Detect Magic spamming” and set traps or alarms to trigger with Detect Magic as a conditional.

Dimension Door (Bard 4, Magus 4, Sorcerer/Wizard 4, Summoner 3, Witch 4; Domain: Travel 4; Bloodline: Arcane 4) When you need to be somewhere else very quickly for a bargain price in magical power, open a Dimension Door. Bonus points for transporting your entire party past the network of precarious walkways overlooking pools of lava filled with elementally-attuned dire sharks. This (and similar spells) have a nice interaction with a certain item.

Dispel Magic ((Anti)paladin 3, Bard 3, Cleric 3, Druid 4, Inquisitor 3, Magus 3, Sorcerer/Wizard 3, Summoner 3, Witch 3; Domain: Magic 3; Bloodline: Arcane 3) The classic go-to spell for disarming magical wards, if the trapfinding ability isn’t cutting it. Note that without a little bit of specialization, it can be difficult to overcome the presumably more powerful spellcaster who placed this ward.

Heart of the Metal (Magus 3, Sorcerer/Wizard 3, Witch 3) Best used to bypass adamantine, the awesome thing about this spell is that unlike something like a weapon blanch, this spell allows your entire parties’ weapons to overcome hardness as well! It may get a little expensive without a little blood spilled.

Invisibility (Alchemist 2, Antipaladin 2, Bard 2, Inquisitor 2, Magus 2, Sorcerer/Wizard 2, Summoner 2; Domain: Trickery 2; Bloodline: Arcane 2) So many guardians are utterly unable to overcome this spell, and look how many spellcasters have access to it! Note that there are some amusing utility uses of Invisibility, such as rendering a door invisible to peek inside. Just remember that whatever’s inside can see out, as well.

Gaseous Form (Alchemist 3, Bard 3, Magus 3, Sorcerer/Wizard 3; Domain: Air 3) With some exceptions, Gaseous Form renders the caster completely harmless to their enemies, but also allows access to any area that could feasibly have cracks or openings. A decent duration means a number of doors could be slipped through before Gaseous Form expires. One thing to note is that while it’s a little confusing if a creature in Gaseous Form can be grappled, Sean K. Reynolds weighs in here to the contrary.

Open/Close (Bard 0, Magus 0, Sorcerer/Wizard 0, Summoner 0) An invaluable aid to a cautious adventurer. Defeating readied actions has never been so easy, nor has embarrassing foes attempting to escape from safely out of harm’s way. Never let the Fighter break down a perfectly good unlocked door again! To really destroy the action economy at higher levels, considering Quickening this for ultimate open/closing efficiency. Or handing your Improved Familiar a wand.

Passwall (Sorcerer/Wizard 5) No matter what the material and, in most cases, thickness of the wall in question, Passwall will render it accessible for the better part of a day. What more does a Trickster need? The party can even come along, unlike with spells such as Gaseous Form.

Shrink Item (Sorcerer/Wizard 3) This is a spell that rewards creativity. At the most basic, it should be able to make most non-magical doors shrink down to a more convenient size, very quietly. Great for concealing objects about one’s person for a Trickster inclined to smuggle, as well.

Vanish (Bard 1, Magus 1, Sorcerer/Wizard 1) Did something go wrong? Vanish, and make it someone else’s problem. Note that while wands of Vanish last only a single round, they can be useful for positioning purposes and avoiding attacks of opportunity from angry, long-armed monstrosities.

WEAPONS, ARMOR, AND SPECIAL MATERIALS

There aren’t too many items that make up this category, but the ones that do exist are integral to certain doorbreaker builds.

Named Weapons

Maul of the Titans While a named item and therefore impossible to upgrade in most games, a +3 Greatclub is respectable enough for a normal Barbarian or Fighter. What makes this weapon purple for a Gatecrasher is its special property of dealing triple damage to inanimate objects. In addition to its direct combat capabilities (causing massive damage with the Greater Sunder feat), the Maul single-handedly allows the Gatecrasher to smash through any obstacle in his way. As featured in the sample build section below, the Maul of the Titans allows for enough damage to destroy even a Wall of Force in a single round. It’s possible to do without the Maul, but the Maul is still an incredibly powerful choice for a Gatecrasher.

Mattock of the Titans Potentially confusable with the Maul above, worse in every conceivable way. It’s very difficult for a Gatecrasher to even be large enough to wield this; if you are a Druid or other spellcaster capable of becoming a Huge-sized humanoid Elemental, you get the ability to smash through 10-foot cubes of stone in about ten times the time of just applying the Maul of the Titans to it. It can be wielded as a warhammer, but as it is treated as Gargantuan a size penalty will be applied to attacks.

Armor Properties

Shadow The price is right for the +5 bonus to Stealth this armor property provides. Although there are many other Wondrous Items that provide the same bonus, this is essentially slotless if added to existing armor. One thing to note is that if you’re making use of a unique armor, it cannot have additional properties applied.

Special Materials

Adamantine Ignoring hardness is great for anyone who wants to smash through all obstacles, like the Gatecrasher. It is important to note that adamantine does not reduce the effective hardness of objects by 20, but rather ignores any hardness of less than 20; that means that any hardness of 20 or above is still fully effective against an adamantine weapon.

MUNDANE AND MAGICAL ITEMS

Equipment, the panacea that turns even a commoner into a mighty adventurer if they’re wealthy enough. Doorbreakers can benefit from a huge host of both mundane and magical items; as usual, this guide will focus specifically on those items that provide bonuses central to the concepts. Since any character can benefit from items, these items will be rated for their general usefulness-to-cost ratio, with special notes made for different types of doorbreakers.

Mundane Equipment

Key, Skeleton A +10 to opening locks will likely be useless to a dedicated doorbreaker, but at 85 GP for a reusable key to any door, this is great for a dabbler.

Key, Wax Ideally a Thief in the Night should be so good they don’t need a key, but in the case of a particularly difficult heist making a copy of the real thing could be just what’s needed.

Ram, Portable Now, a good Gatecrasher shouldn’t need a second person’s help, but at minimum a +2 stackable bonus on breaking down a door can’t be passed up.

Magical Items

Belt

Belt of the Weasel This is an interesting item, one of the few ways to get the Compression monster ability on a PC. It does take up the Belt slot which is unfortunate, but it adds to DEX, which is the statistic that a tiny Thief who would like to squeeze under doors will be using.

Chest

Spectral Shroud A bit pricey, but the abilities of this item are amazing if a Thief can afford it. In additional to continual See Invisibility to spot tricky guardians or secret doors, the Shroud provides a nice “exit strategy” with its once-a-day incorporeality.

Eyes

Treasure Hunter’s Goggles There’s a lot of good effects stapled together for this price. The “Eyes” slot is fairly precious to a doorbreaker, but nothing’s stopping an enterprising Thief from switching out his goggles depending on the situation.

Feet

Boots of the Soft Step An incredibly cheap way to bypass Tremorsense. Flying is “better”, but these boots are a lot easier to attain.

Hands

Gloves of Reconnaissance These are how cheap? 2000 gold? Buy five. But seriously, this obviates so many ambushes and traps it’s just silly. Just make sure you have sufficient vision to experience the Other Side.

Ring

Ring of Emancipation While the skill bonuses are largely inconsequential, competence bonuses to attack rolls are pretty rare. If the Gatecrasher isn’t carrying around a friendly bard, a +2 to his primary Combat Maneuver for this price is a great deal.

Ring of the Ram This is a limited use item, but it has a lot of uses for its price and could be pretty handy for when a door absolutely needs to be forced open (a +9 STR bonus is more than respectable for this task).

Steelhand Circle Purchasing part of a feat for 12K isn’t the best deal, but this could be attractive for a Doorbreaker both strapped for feats and unable or unwilling to take Barbarian levels. 

Slotless

Blood Reservoir of Physical Prowess This handy item is insanely useful for its price; curing ability damage is powerful enough, but that temporary stackable bonus to Strength (for a Gatecrasher) or Dexterity (for a Thief) makes this a tough tool to pass up.

Chime of Opening Unless you’re specifically breaking into a mage’s vault (in which case the ability to bypass Arcane Locks automatically is very nice), there’s a few too many drawbacks for this item despite its relatively cheap price. The caster level check is not a guarantee against most well-made locks, barred doors, etc., and the Chime will only function 10 times before crumbling.

Eldritch Egress Passwall for 10,000 GP is still a pretty good deal. Unlike the doorknocker below, this is the full spell, allowing access through the thickest of adamantine doors.

Insistent Doorknocker This is an indispensable 5th-level spell, 1/day, for 5000 GP with no slot requirement. What’s not to like? Note that very secure vaults might have walls too thick for this item, in which case see above.

Rod of Lordly Might There’s a lot going on here, but let’s focus on what we care about: opening doors. Forcing open doors with a +12 STR modifier is good, but not 70K good.

Wayfinder of Revelation 8000 GP for a short duration, 1/day ability to get a +10 to find secret doors is not worth any doorbreaker’s gold.

Wayfinder of Infinite Doorways* While the price is quite steep, in any adventure with enough downtime to recharge this item, it can give some serious benefits to a high-level Thief in the Night. The ability to Detect Secret Doors at will, Knock a number of times, and even teleport away from danger is amazing for a Thief.

SAMPLE CHARACTERS

Please note that the sample characters below are designed as fun but extreme examples of the three doorbreaker paths. They may incorporate non-standard character building options, or make odd choices in pursuit of their path. Each character is built to level 10. Compatibility adjustments are offered for each character.

The Thief in the Night

Auriel Darter, “The Thief in the Night”

Human Trapsmith Rogue 4 / Lore Warden 2 / Shadow Dancer 3 / Pathfinder Delver 1

NG Medium Humanoid (Human)

Ability Scores: STR 8 / DEX 24 / CON 14 / INT 16 / WIS 12 / CHA 7

Design Notes: Auriel is a master thief and shadowdancer that has been horribly blighted by contact with cruel denizens of the Shadow Plane. It is rumored that “The Thief in the Night” managed to spirit a piece of the Shadow Plane itself back to the material plane, manifesting as Shadowshard. Despite her blackened appearance, Auriel is a good person at heart, and has never been known to steal from the innocent and the helpless. She is likely to go out of her way to help young women and children, or anyone suffering from an evident deformity.

Auriel is loathe to fight, preferring to avoid contact with others all together. When possible, she murmurs platitudes as to why conflict should be avoided-- while Shadowshard lurks beneath the ground, ready to spring out and flank any opponent unwilling to arrive at a diplomatic solution and too stubborn to believe her falsehoods. Auriel is a master at finding the weak spots in her opponents' defenses, but prefers to pass off her critical hits to Shadowshard using Butterfly’s Sting, weakening her opponents until they are unable to fight. Whenever possible, Auriel enhances Shadowshard's combat abilities with her Allying Gauntlet, Desecrate wand, and wand of Haste.

Design Notes: Unglukk is twice-cursed, once by the god of battle and once by the goddess of beauty, finding some solace only in the revels of Rovagug. Unglukk is more of a force of nature than a normal fighter, an inexorable juggernaut whose slow and steady steps always carry him forward through any obstacle. A mercenary by trade, he is often hired in place of personal armies to destroy fortifications and any hapless defenders that stand in his way. Retaining Uglukk’s services can be dangerous, as he takes no heed of the value of anything he destroys, and many a client has been left fuming in the wreckage of their enemy’s treasury.

If engaged in actual combat, Unglukk’s strategy is simple: if there is something between him and his foe, he breaks it. If his foe brandishes swords or shields, he breaks them, and shatters the limbs that hold them. While not particularly intelligent, Unglukk possesses some measure of cunning and has equipped himself to handle difficult situations such as flying foes or strange magic, and doesn’t hesitate to use anything at his disposal to accomplish his tasks.

Design Notes: This enigmatic assassin’s name is said to have never been uttered or written down; even Legend Lore and other powerful divinations have failed to reveal it. He is usually referred to as “the Lurker.” The Lurker sacrifices some spellcasting progression in exchange for the unique abilities of the Arcanist and Arcane Trickster classes. He employs a range of divination and abjuration magic to hide his presence, as well as various spells designed to aid entry into his targets’ domiciles.

The Lurker’s preferred combat strategy is to sneak through areas using Blend and Invisibility, and destroy frailer foes in a single blow with a Death Attack guided by True Strike. He wields a Dazing Icicle Dagger whenever possible. Against hardier foes, the Lurker uses Potent Magic and Persistent Spell to incapacitate with Glitterdust and Hideous Laughter. He finishes combat by dropping enemies into an Acid Pit to leave behind no traces. If his efforts fail, or he is forced to face a challenge he judges to be too great, the Lurker Dimensional Slides away and activates his Blend to stealth away from the area.

Compatibility: For Pathfinder Society Organized Play or games in which characters of evil alignment are not legal, alter the alignment to LN.
