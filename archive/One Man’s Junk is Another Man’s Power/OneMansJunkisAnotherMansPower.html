CockroachTeaParty’s guide to the Pathfinder Occultist

“How did I do that?  Well, this was my grandfather’s sword, you see…”

~Milton Croft, adventurer-occultist

Introduction

        Of the new classes introduced in Occult Adventures, the (titular?) Occultist is one of the initially more perplexing.  What does it do?  What is its intended role?  The occultist draws psychic power from physical objects of historical or emotional significance, but what does that mean, exactly?  They also gain the ability to lay down magic circles and bargain with outsiders for information; they’re all over the place!

        Well fret no more, dear reader, for I have examined the Occultist thoroughly.  It’s best to think of an occultist existing somewhere between the Incarnate from 3.5’s Magic of Incarnum and the Artificer from Eberron, or perhaps a sort of bard/wizard fusion.  Like an artificer, an occultist is very dependent on his collection of items, called implements, and proves impressively adept with the Use Magic Device skill.  Similar to Magic of Incarnum, the occultist invests points from a personal reservoir of power (mental focus rather than essentia), gaining scaling benefits depending on the sort of item in question.  Unlike essentia, which can shift around from round to round, the occultist locks in these points, but can spend them to unleash useful magical effects.  And like a specialist wizard, the occultist’s powers are intimately tied to the eight schools of magic.

        The Occultist is a versatile tier 3 class.  For a 1-6 caster class, they have surprising adventuring stamina, for their mental focus ability translates into all-day buffs and bonus spells throughout the day.  They’re an interesting choice for someone interested in psychic magic and the new occult classes, but prefers their spells closer to the classic arcane fare.  They can heal, fight in melee or at range, beguile with enchantments and illusions, or even animate the dead to serve them.  In the end, the occultist can do just about anything you want them to.

Occultist Roles

“I don’t understand it.  It took me three years of study before I could make a corpse twitch.  That bastard stares at a skull for a few moments, and suddenly the dead dance at his very whim.”

~Arkoth the Black, jealous necromancer

        The occultist is extremely diverse, capable of performing a variety of tasks within an adventuring party.  Like any jack-of-all-trades class, the occultist will never be as strong in a specific role as a more dedicated character.  Being decent at melee or ranged combat is an attractive option; you’ll always have something to do in a fight if your spells and focus powers aren’t suitable for the situation.  That said, the occultist excels at certain tasks easier than others.

Melee Support:  Although the occultist can potentially tank in the absence of a more robust melee class, they best serve as support in melee, strengthening the front line with their generous weapon and armor proficiencies.  Buffing themselves and their allies, the melee occultist can meet a variety of challenges in the field, favoring the transmutation and abjuration implement schools.

Ranged Support:  Similar to the melee occultist, an occultist that takes up the bow has similar offensive output from the safety of range.  While more feat-starved and ammunition dependent, archery is a strong combat style in Pathfinder.

Scouting / Stealth:  Starting at 5th level, occultists gain access to the Mind Eye focus power from the divination implement school.  Combined with the Third Eye resonant power, they become incredible scouts, able to safely and discreetly map out entire dungeons or strongholds.  The illusion implement school provides access to spells and powers that also increase an occultist’s stealth.

Healing:  Occultists can fill in for a secondary healer in a pinch through the conjuration implement school.  Their access to new spells is extremely rigid, however, so they make poor primary healers.

Blasting:  The evocation implement school offers a variety of direct-damage spells and focus powers, but an occultist will trail far behind a sorcerer or even a wizard when it comes to blasting.  It’s an inefficient use of your spell slots and mental focus, but they can at least make passable blasters if you put some effort into it.

Utility:  While they lack the day-to-day flexibility of prepared casters, an occultist can usually produce a solution to any given problem through their spells or focus powers.  Teleportation, flight, cunning illusions; the occultist has access to a wide breadth of magical options, even if they’re lacking depth.

Minion Master:  Although occultists make poor summoners, they make surprisingly competent necromancers.  Certainly comparable to a wizard in their capacity to animate and control minions, a necromancy-focused occultist can produce disposable allies at the drop of a hat.  

Information Gathering:  The divination implement school comes highly recommended, so most occultists will end up with a decent array of information gathering powers.  While they’re best at scouting, they can also use their Object Reading class feature to discover clues through items, and their various planar informants can also help them unravel mysteries.

Party Face:  While occultists have most of the important social skills, CHA is not a high-priority ability for them.  The enchantment and illusion implement schools can help out in social situations, so most occultists can participate in social intrigue, but you likely won’t be as adept as a bard or mesmerist.  The enchantment school is one of the weaker options as well, so you should probably not plan on being the party face unless nobody else in your group is up to the task.

Occultist Builds & Ability Scores

“You could easily best me in single combat, barbarian, but the armor of the arena champion might help even the odds.”

~Phillis the Inquisitive, occultist of note

        The occultist is a very flexible class that can be built in a variety of ways.  Below I outline three broad builds, but there’s going to be overlap with any role.  The occultist is very flexible and diverse, being a strong Tier 3 class.  

Melee Occultist:  With martial weapon proficiency, medium armor proficiency (and you can pick up heavy armor with a feat), and a slew of useful powers, an occultist can make for an interesting melee combatant.  The magus will out-damage you, the warpriest will out-buff you, but you can pull some tricks out of your hat that neither class can.  A melee occultist best serves as melee support, shielding squishier characters, being a rogue’s flank-buddy, and pulling their weight in a fight when swords are drawn.

        A simple two-handed weapon build is light on feat requirements while providing solid damage, but you can sword-and-board for extra defense and rely on your Legacy Weapon focus power for damage.  The only melee build I would advise against is a two-weapon fighting build; you don’t have a lot of feats to spare, and it makes you slightly more MAD.

        The transmutation and abjuration implement schools are your bread and butter here.  Supplement them with divination, illusion, and conjuration for extra utility and defenses.  Necromancy makes another interesting choice, providing you with disposable flank-buddies of your own.

Ranged Occultist:  Similar in principle to the melee occultist, the ranged occultist relies on a nice longbow for ranged damage support.  The advantage over melee in this case is that it’s safer for you, keeping you out of harm’s way.  With feats like Rapid Shot and spells such as haste or the Quickness focus power, you’ll manage to keep pace with full BAB classes.  The only downside is all of your feats become pretty much spoken for.

        You’ll want the same implement schools as a melee occultist, firing bane arrows as often as possible.  Hiding behind a wall of undead flesh is a helpful strategy for an archer as well.

Caster Occultist:  You can put a nominal investment in your physical ability scores and instead focus on INT above all else, relying on your spells and focus powers to solve problems.  This is the best build to use the more esoteric implement schools such as enchantment or evocation; diversity is the way to go.  A caster occultist winds up somewhere between a bard and a wizard-lite, reminiscent of the artificer class from Eberron; it’s a unique option for someone that loves to play mages, but doesn’t want the overwhelming raw power of a full 1-9 caster.

        You can build such a character several ways.  You can serve as a primary or secondary healer, a minion-focused necromancer, a diviner, and you can mix-and-match these roles as you gain access to new schools.  You’re tougher than a wizard, so keep a backup weapon and a nice suit of armor at hand.  UMD is also your friend here, affording you diversity and adventuring endurance.

        Those truly intent on becoming dedicated casters are well-advised to consider the Silksworn archetype, which increases your access to implement schools and increases your daily spell allotment at the cost of your better equipment proficiencies.  

With the above in mind, an occultist’s ability score priorities are as follows:

  • STR -  Melee occultists will want STR as a primary score, unless you’re going for a finesse build.  Archers will want decent strength for a nice composite bow.  Caster occultists with no intention of joining melee can potentially dump STR, but you’ll want a little bit to help carry your armor and gear around at low levels.
  • DEX -  Ranged or finesse melee occultists will want DEX as a primary score.  Occultists have weak Reflex saves, so any bit of DEX helps.  An occultist aiming for heavy armor can get by with a 12 or lower here.
  • CON - Everybody needs hp!  This is more important to melee occultists; archers and certain casters will be fine with a slightly lower score.  Put a 12-14 here and call it a day.
  • INT - Your spells, focus powers, and mental focus pool are all governed by your INT.  You want it as high as possible, especially for caster-focused occultists.  Archers and melee occultists want at least a 16 here at the start of their career if they can manage it, while casters want at least an 18.
  • WIS -  With a strong Will save and Perception as a class skill, you can get by with a modest WIS score.  A 10-12 will be fine, but more is nice if you can afford it.
  • CHA - While you can be passable as party face, there’s not a whole lot else dependent on CHA for you, with the important exception of UMD.  If you start at high levels, you can rely on your Magic Item Skill class feature and dump this; at low levels, try not to go below a 10.  If you’re using traits, the Pragmatic Activator trait can allow you to safely dump this regardless.

Suggested Point Buy Arrays

The scores below assume a generalist occultist leaning towards caster with a melee subfocus, before racial modifiers.  Swap STR with DEX for a ranged build.  A ranged or melee occultist can rely on the Physical Enhancement resonant power from the transmutation implement school to help keep their STR or DEX high.

15 point buy:  STR 14, DEX 10, CON 12, INT 16, WIS 10, CHA 8

20 point buy:  STR 14, DEX 12, CON 13, INT 16, WIS 10, CHA 10

25 point buy:  STR 14, DEX 12, CON 14, INT 17, WIS 10, CHA 10

Races Sensitive to Psychic Impressions: Occultist Racial Selection

“This bow has defended the border of these woods for 500 years.  I barely have to pull the string; it thirsts for orcish blood entirely on its own.”

~Thuviel Seven-Stars, Elven Occultist

        In general, you’re looking for races with INT bonuses, or boosts to physical ability scores relevant for your chosen build.

Dwarf:  Dwarves make for a functional defensive choice.  The CHA penalty hurts your UMD a bit, but Magic Item Talent helps compensate.  The favored class bonus is very niche, so you’re better off sticking to bonus hp.  There’s good flavor here too, for dwarves value physical objects and the psychic imprints of their ancestors.  Overall it’s the least impressive of the core races, but certainly usable.  

Elf:  As a long-lived race, elves appreciate the history behind fabled objects of power, and they produce fantastic occultists.  Their ability score bonuses make them strong as casters and archers, although they might want to shy away from melee due to their CON penalty.  Their racial traits benefit your magic well; consider swapping out elven weapon proficiency for something else, such as Arcane Focus.  Their racial favored class option for occultists is extremely strong, and you should take it every level; you’ll wind up with 50% more points of mental focus than other races.

Gnome:  Insatiable tinkers and collectors, gnomes make for surprisingly strong occultists, leaning towards the caster-focused variety.  Their talent with illusion magic makes the illusion implement school a no-brainer, and their CHA bonus helps out with UMD.  Their racial favored class bonus is interesting; I only recommend taking it 5 times, for a +10% boost to the Distortion resonant power.  By level 10, you’ll have at-will invisibility instead of waiting until level 18.  You can take it more to reduce the amount of mental focus you need to invest to gain the invisibility power, but you’ll probably want a decent amount invested there anyway.

Half-Elf:  Combining the ambition of a human with the magical puissance of an elf, half-elves make flexible occultists perfect for any build.  Their main strength is access to their Elven parent’s racial favored class bonus, gaining sweet bonus mental focus.  Skill Focus (UMD) allows them to dump CHA with impunity, or you can swap out Adaptability for something fun like Ancestral Arms.

Halfling:  Plucky and curious, halflings are not to be underestimated when it comes to occult mysteries.  With the same racial favored class bonus as an elf, halflings enjoy a 50% boost to their mental focus capacity, which more than makes up for their lack of an INT bonus.  While an elf or half-elf edges them out slightly, halflings make for great stealthy occultists, and their CHA boost helps with UMD.

Half-Orc:  Ostracized half-orcs often seek unorthodox paths to power, and make for intimidating occultists.  Nice and flexible, they’ll prove themselves worthy of any occultist build.  Taking the human favored class bonus is a strong choice, roughly worth 3 bonus feats by level 18, but a melee occultist might be better off springing for bonus hp.  If you’re hell-bent on playing a blaster occultist, the half-orc racial favored class bonus makes them the best choice.

Human:  Ambitious humans seek relics of significance around the globe, making humans more likely to take up the path of the occultist than any other race.  Flexible to the extreme, humans have no weaknesses.  Their favored class bonus equates to 3 bonus feats, and more focus powers are always useful.  So strong are the elf and halfling racial favored class bonuses that humans have to settle for a green ranking, which is a rare occurrence

Other Races:  In general, you’re looking for races with an INT bonus, and avoiding races with an INT penalty.  Ratfolk, Sylph, and Tiefling are a few decent options.  

Occultist Class Features

“Careful with that!”

~Sonya the Veiled, said to her companions during the exploration of haunted Greymoor Manor

        The following is a brief overview of the occultist’s class features and what they mean for you, the player.

Chassis:  ¾ BAB, d8 HD, Good Fort. & Will saves, simple & martial weapons, light & medium armor, shields.

You have the same chassis as the cleric, with the addition of martial weapons.  Right out of the gate, this makes occultists very well-rounded with multiple options at their fingertips.

Skills: 4/level

With an INT focus, the occultist will never want for skill points.

  • Perception, Use Magic Device:  The God Skill, and the awesomeness that is UMD.  You’ll want to max these.
  • Spellcraft, various Knowledges, Linguistics:  You have the INT to make a fine party sage.  You don’t get all knowledge skills, but you get most of the important ones.
  • Appraise, Craft, Profession, Disable Device, Sleight of Hand, Fly:  These skills are flavorful, but not necessarily the most useful in your hands.  Fly is worth putting ranks in eventually, especially if you wear heavier armor.
  • Diplomacy, Disguise, Sense Motive:  You get some social skills; enchantment or illusion-focused occultists might be interested here, but CHA isn’t high on your priority list.

Psychic Spellcasting: 1-6 level spells

Your magical potential is no slouch.  Like all 1-6 casters, your daily spell slots will be very dear.  The occultist spell list is most reminiscent of the sorcerer/wizard spell list, which is a bit unusual for the psychic casters.  Oddly, you’ll wind up with more spells known than a bard, but you gain access to new spells in an odd fashion.  Your spells known and general spellcasting endurance are deceptive at first glance, since you have to take your focus powers into account.

Focus Powers

You learn focus powers at the same rate you gain feats.  Tied to your implement schools, these abilities run the gamut from unimpressive to game-changingly useful.  Many of them replicate spells, expanding your magical repertoire.  A few of them replicate effects higher than 6th level spells!  I break down the various focus powers further down, by school.

Implements

        Implements are the most important thing for an occultist.  Everything is tied to them.  Your spells known, your focus powers, even your access to your spells, all require implements.  There is one category of implements for each of the eight schools of magic, and you only gain access to 7 implements over the course of your career.  You’ll never have access to the full suit of focus powers.

        Much like a wizard’s bonded object, you have great difficulty casting your spells without your implements; even if you succeed, they are cast at reduced power.  You will want to protect your implements carefully.

        You access new implements at a painfully slow pace.  Thus, your implement selection, and the order in which you pick them, is of paramount importance.  I break down the implement schools below; each is tailored towards a certain sort of character, from warrior to spellslinger to creepy necromancer.  The first three implements you pick at 1st and 2nd level will likely form the ‘core’ of your capabilities, with the later additions expanding your options.

        You also can select an implement school multiple times, which allows you to access more spells known from that school.  While there is power in diversity, doubling-up on an implement school is not necessarily a bad thing: there will be fewer implements competing for your precious mental focus.

Mental Focus

        Reminiscent of essentia from Magic of Incarnum, mental focus is the precious currency upon which your focus powers run.  Much like essentia, you will never have enough of it; how you choose to invest it in your implements is an important process every day.

        Once invested, resonant powers function at full power until the implement is completely empty of focus, so don’t be afraid to spend your points!  There is no ‘cap’ for investing focus in any given implement, but the resonant powers do possess a cap, providing a decent point to stop investing if you’re eyeballing things.  

        Remember that a single point of mental focus can often replicate a spell, from enlarge person to fly to invisibility.  As a general rule of thumb, if a focus power’s effect is less powerful than a spell, it’s probably inferior to your other options.  Also, most powers only cost 1 point of mental focus to use; a few cost more, but they are rarely worth it.  With this in mind, the occultist’s daily spell slots are deceptive; an occultist can effectively ‘cast’ a number of extra spells each day equal to their level+INT modifier (before feats).

        Lastly, it is rarely ever worth it to not invest mental focus in an implement.  ‘Generic focus’ is twice as expensive to use, which is not really worth the flexibility advantage.

Magic Item Skill:  UMD is an amazing skill that you should definitely use.  This ability takes the sting off of an average CHA score.

Object Reading:  You become the party magic-item-identifier from level 2 onwards.  Fun, flavorful, and extremely useful.

Shift Focus:  After 4th level, you can move focus around between your implements.  It’s inefficient and wastes focus, but it’s no worse than using ‘generic’ focus, and actually saves you points if you use a power costing more than 1 point of focus.  Warning: it can lessen the potency of resonant powers, so beware.  Ideally, you want to avoid using this if you can, but it’s helpful when fate throws you a curveball.  It takes 1 minute to do, so it’s best used out of combat.

Aura Sight:  An at-will 3rd level spell.  Cool!  You can now hang out with paladins and inquisitors.

Magic Circles:  This is an odd ability, mostly used to set up your Outside Contact class feature, which comes online at the same time.  The circle seems to be stationary, so it doesn’t have a lot of in-combat utility unless you’re making a stand on a very specific spot.

Outside Contact:  You slowly gather a network of extraplanar informants.  This is a very open-ended ability, and the value of the information you receive will be largely up to the GM.  It costs a bit of money, and 2 points of mental focus, so it’s best used during downtime.  Consider this a fun, flavorful tool to help with information gathering (and eventually other minor tasks).

Binding Circles:  This is basically a bonus focus power that allows you to create a magical trap.  If you can lure a target within the circle, there’s a good chance they’ll be stuck for at least an hour.  Attacking the victim breaks the trap, but it can buy you time, allow you to parley with a hostile foe, or fulfill a variety of other useful functions.  The only downside is it costs 2 points of mental focus, and requires at least 1 minute of prep time to set up.  Still, it’s a useful tool if you think creatively.

Fast Circles:  This late-career acquisition makes your various circles into a slightly-more-feasible in-combat option, but you’re probably still better off with your regular focus powers and spells.

Implement Mastery:  Your capstone is pretty tame, but still nice.  Pick your favorite implement and enjoy some extra power.  

Implement Schools

“My late husband gave me this ring.  Funny, how I can’t draw anything from it but illusions.  What does that say about love?”

~Abigail Willowswitch, halfling occultist

        Below I will break down the eight implement schools, rating them on the desirability of their focus powers, the spell lists they provide, the convenience of the physical implements themselves, how ‘greedy’ they are, and whether they are worth learning more than once.  What do I mean by ‘greed?’  Well, some implements will demand a great deal of mental focus investment, while others will function better in the background.  In general, if you’re taking an implement school primarily for its spell list, you can leave it with only a token investment of mental focus.  I will also include suggestions as to when you should take an implement school, as some start slow but become extremely powerful later in your career.

Abjuration Implement School

Spell List: Good

  • Defenses are important.  The abjuration school provides you with several staple spells that all adventurers should have access to.  Only the 6th level list is somewhat lacking in essentials, but that’s not until the end of your career.

Greed: Moderate

  • You probably don’t have to invest more than the cap from your Warding Talisman resonant power.  Approximately 2 points + 2 per four levels over the course of your career.  If you land a really nice cloak of resistance, you can afford to invest less here, but the resonant power frees up your cloak slot for something more fun.

Implements: Easy

  • Since armor works as an implement, you’ll always have it ‘available,’ and it’s difficult to steal / tough to sunder.

More than once? - No

  • Each spell level has only one real standout, making this a single-time pick.

When to Take:  1st or 2nd level, no later than 6th.

Resonant Power: Warding Talisman

  • This power frees up your shoulder slot, scaling at approximately the same rate as a cloak of resistance of your appropriate level would.  It’s a nice thing not to have to worry about, but it does require a steady investment of mental focus, and you should never willingly completely drain your implement.

Base Focus Power: Mind Barrier

  • While this power is useful in a pinch, it’s expensive for what it does, especially if you use it as an immediate action.  

Focus Powers:

  • Aegis:  Slow scaling, a standard action activation, and a 1 minute duration make this power only mildly useful.
  • Energy Shield:  Swift action activation, and great scaling.  The immediate action usage is okay for emergencies, but it’s expensive.
  • Globe of Negation:  It’s expensive and stationary, but surprisingly potent.  Best for an archer or a caster using ranged spells, it provides a solid defense against magic.
  • Loci Sentry:  Very similar to the alarm spell, it’s more useful when you’ve got some levels under your belt.  
  • Planar Ward:  Outsiders are common enough that this is a solid choice, but its value depends on the campaign.  If you’re playing something outsider heavy, it’s extremely useful, but it is a bit on the expensive side.  Also handy against enemy summoners.
  • Unraveling:  This saves you a spell known on dispel magic, but the fact you have to touch what you’re trying to dispel makes this risky.  Probably better on a melee occultist that would like to take a different spell instead of dispel magic.

Thoughts:  The abjuration school is recommended for all types of occultists.  Melee, ranged, or caster, this school provides you with the defenses expected of any adventurer.  You can wait to take it until 6th level, just before dispel magic comes online, but you’ll be able to benefit from the resonant power right out of the gate.

The primary reason to take it is spell access.  The focus powers are generally useful, but err on the side of expensive.  Top off Warding Talisman and spend focus from there as situations call for it.

Conjuration Implement School

Spell List: Great

  • The conjuration list provides two useful things: utility, largely in the form of transportation, and healing.

Greed: Low

  • The resonant power is low-priority, and there are only a few focus powers worth investing for; the primary draw here is spell access.  That said, if you find yourself in the role of primary healer, you might need to sink more points here.

Implements: Fragile

  • The implements for this school are primarily small, fragile objects.  Easy to steal or break if you’re not careful.

More than once? - Possibly

  • There are enough strong spells on the list that doubling up could be useful, especially if you find yourself playing a healer.  If you’re a healer, consider doubling up by 2nd level.  Otherwise, you can wait until 10th, 14th, or 18th level.  

When to take:  1st or 2nd (if you’re a healer), otherwise 10th level is a fine time to jump in.

Resonant Power: Caster Focus

  • This will probably only benefit your glitterdust spells.  Even if you hand it off to an ally that favors summon spells, the benefit is too small for the investment required.  This is largely an afterthought.

Base Focus Power: Servitor

  • Let’s get something straight: an occultist is not a summoner.  This power is not the best combat option.  That said, it scales reasonably well, and the cost in mental focus is a steal.  At 16th level, producing a lillend azata for some healing or bardic music isn’t too shabby for a single point of focus.  Use this for utility and access to SLA’s.  It doesn’t get really useful until later in your career, thus why waiting until 10th level to pick up this implement school is a decent option.

Focus Powers:

  • Conjure Implement:  Very niche.  I would only consider it in a game where you expect your equipment to be routinely threatened, and if that’s the case, you probably shouldn’t be playing an occultist.
  • Flesh Mend:  You can spare the occasional spell for healing, or better yet, grab a wand.  While functional, it’s just not worth one of your focus powers known.
  • Mind Steed:  Both mount and phantom steed are available from this school, and are actually both some of your better choices.  Use those instead.
  • Psychic Fog:  This is basically two bonus spells known.  The cost is reasonable, and it has great utility and battlefield control potential.  Most attractive to a caster-focused occultist.
  • Purge Corruption:  Poison and disease are a real pain.  If you’ve got a divine caster in your party doing their job, you can skip this.  If you’re saddled with the healer role, this is an okay choice for a focus power.
  • Side Step: This is useful for everybody.  Use it to get out of a bad situation, ignore some difficult terrain, or bypass obstacles.  

Thoughts:  The conjuration school opens up the healer role for occultists, and they’re passable.  While no cleric or oracle, you can keep pace with UMD and some wise spell selection.  That said, the main strength of this school is the spell access; there are no focus powers particularly earth-shattering in their potency, especially compared to other schools.  Unless you plan on healing, I recommend this school as a mid-to-late career selection to round out your spell list.  If you’re in a group with a caster that handles teleportation, you can skip this school relatively painlessly.

Divination Implement School

Spell List: Good

  • There’s some strong options here, but it’s not the reason you came to this party.

Greed: Thirsty

  • This school has a lot of great focus powers, and a resonant power that demands quite a bit of your investment.  It’s worth it though!

Implements: Average

  • If you get the option, choose goggles or a headband; an article of clothing is less likely to be misplaced.

More than once? - No

When to take: 6th level or earlier.

Resonant Power: Third Eye

  • This power starts off nice, providing a bonus to Perception checks.  It’s also nifty for races without low-light vision or darkvision.  Where it becomes sick-nasty-awesome is at 7th level, when you can just have see invisibility up all day.  It only continues to get better from there.  The only downside is it takes a substantial investment of mental power, but there are plenty of useful focus powers to be used below.

Base Focus Power: Sudden Insight

  • A swift action insight bonus for any attack, skill, or ability check.  It only gets better with age, and has utility in and out of combat, but it’s a bit expensive for a one-time bonus to a roll.

Focus Powers:

  • Danger Sight: Basically expands your Sudden Insight power to apply to saves and AC.  For an immediate action power it has a very reasonable mental focus cost.
  • Future Gaze:  Essentially a bonus spell known, but with an improved casting time.  Handy for the indecisive!
  • Mind Eye: This is an amazing power.  The ultimate scouting and spying tool, you can map out entire dungeon layers quickly, discreetly, and safely with this.  Prying eyes is a 5th level spell for you, but you get access to this gem at level 5!  Be the envy of a diviner wizard and TAKE THIS.
  • Object Seer: While this can provide more detailed information, you can probably get by with your Object Reading class feature.
  • Powerful Connection: If scrying becomes your job, this makes you better at it.  That said, if you already possess items or a piece of your target’s body, the odds are pretty stacked against them.  You can skip this.
  • Watchful Eye: This is an odd little power.  If it had a better duration it could be more useful, but as it stands it serves as a sort of early-warning alarm ability.  Hopefully you won’t need it after a thorough scouting with Mind Eye.

Thoughts:  This is a pretty straightforward implement school.  You’re here for Third Eye and Mind Eye; everything else is gravy.  It’s recommended for all builds; everyone can benefit from enhanced senses and amazing scouting ability.  

Enchantment Implement School

Spell List: Good

  • The main reason to pick up this school.

Greed: Low

  • The resonant and focus powers here are not that great, so you can leave this implement empty and be fine.

Implements: Average

  • Again, spring for wearable items here.

More than once? - No

When to take: A caster-focused occultist might consider this at 1st or 2nd level.  Everyone else can wait until they have more essential schools under their belt (10th level or later).  This is also a pretty easy school to skip entirely.

Resonant Power: Glorious Presence

  • A modestly scaling bonus to social skills.  If you want to dabble in the party face role, this is nice; everyone else doesn’t have to worry about this.

Base Focus Power:  Cloud Mind

  • The crappy duration makes this little better than a slightly-more-relevant daze spell.  Not worth the point expenditure.

Focus Powers:

Binding Pattern:  Basically an extra spell known, for what amounts to an early access hold monster.

Forced Alliance:  A short duration, fragile, less-thorough charm effect.  Use your spells!

Inspired Assault:  This is serviceable, but scales poorly, and has a short duration.  If you’ve got a bard in your team, forget it.

Mental Discord:  Unless you fight a lot of psychic casters, your Binding Pattern will be just as disruptive.

Mental Slumber:  Inferior to Binding Pattern in every way.

Obey:  Basically a bonus spell known in the form of command.  It’s obsolete by the time Binding Pattern comes online.

Thoughts:  This is one of the less painful schools to skip entirely.  It’s only of interest to caster-focused occultists, who will benefit from the spell list and the Binding Pattern focus power.  Consider it in a less combat-focused game, otherwise you’ve got better options.

Evocation Implement School

Spell List: Decent

  • This is the main reason to pick up this school.  Most of the really good spells are late access.  It’s also the only real option if you want to do direct damage (but a sorcerer you ain’t!).

Greed:  Thirsty

  • If you want to shoot your little lasers, you’ll need to invest points in here for adventuring stamina alone.  These are points better spent elsewhere in most cases.

Implements: Meh

  • If you’re relying on UMD for offense, this isn’t too bad; most other occultists will likely be saving wands and staves for pre-combat or utility.  Otherwise go for a pair of gloves.

More than once? - Probably Not

  • There’s at least one solid spell per spell level worth taking.  If you like the idea of blasting as an occultist, you might consider taking it twice to diversify your damage spells a bit, but this is pretty sub-optimal.

When to take:  10th or 18th, to pick up etheric shards or contingency respectively.  If you like this school’s powers and want to blast your little heart out, obviously take it earlier.

Resonant Power: Intense Focus

  • This is a very modest scaling bonus to damage with evocation spells and focus powers.  This is largely an afterthought for most characters, although a blaster will be thankful for any edge they can get.

Base Focus Power: Energy Ray

  • The range could be better, but it’s pretty cheap if you’re interested in blasting.  Consider it a fallback power if you don’t feel like using a more powerful spell.  By the time you leave the mid levels, it’s becoming pretty obsolete.

Focus Powers:

  • Energy Blast:  This power is a bit expensive and does not age well.  You’re better off relying on spells for AoE damage, but the energy type flexibility is a nice perk.
  • Energy Ward:  If this was a swift action to activate, it might be attractive to melee occultists, but the duration and scaling on this are abysmal.  Skip it.
  • Light Matrix:  This is an interesting power for the duration alone.  On the one hand, you can just use the light spell (or rely on darkvision from your divination implement).  The 5th level ability to blind a target is interesting, perhaps handy if you have a rogue in your team.
  • Radiance:  Are you a crit-fishing melee build that took the evocation school?  Then MAYBE this power would be of interest to you, but the standard action activation and meh duration make this a really hard sell.
  • Shape Mastery:  This gets expensive quickly.  In my experience, situations like these are rare enough that you probably don’t need this.
  • Wall of Power:  If by ‘power’ they mean pathetic damage and duration, they are correct.

Thoughts:  Perhaps the best school to skip, you won’t miss much if you pass on evocation.  Contingency won’t be available until late in your career, so many games probably won’t see it ever used.  Etheric shards is a damn-fine spell, but losing access to one good spell is worth the choice of a better implement school.  Occultists make for inferior blasters compared to a full caster, but this option is here if you want to play a gimped pseudo-wizard.

Illusion Implement School

Spell List: Good

  • Some of your best defensive spells are here.  The strength of illusions is strong until the later levels when true seeing rears its ugly head.

Greed:  Moderate

  • There are a lot of good focus powers here that are worthy of mental focus.  The resonant power is potentially greedy, but it’s more of a nice side-benefit of investing than something worth building around.

Implements: Good

  • A hat is okay, but you really want to spring for a ring if you can.  Rings make for small targets, and there’s basically no reason to ever take one off.

More than once? - No

  • Illusions are strong, but they have a bit of a shelf life.  In a campaign featuring lots of enemy casters or outsiders, you don’t want to dedicate additional resources to a school of magic negated by a single divination.  That said, there’s only a few must-have spells on your list, so picking the school only once will serve you just fine.

When to take: No later than 10th (when Shadow Beast comes online).  I suggest taking it at level 2 or 6, to grab the Unseen focus power and mirror image respectively.  If you wait until level 14, the power of illusions is beginning to wane.

Resonant Power: Distortion

  • This is a fragile power that scales very slowly.  Just remember that you have it, so that it’s on when a fight breaks out; it’s decent insurance against ambushes and sneak attacks.  By 7th level, it’s like an at-will blur spell.  At 18th level, it’s an at-will, all-day invisibility, which is pretty neat, but it does require a 10 point investment.

Base Focus Power: Minor Figment

  • Never use this power to replicate ghost sound, since you learn it automatically by taking this school.  That said, this is essentially a bonus spell known in the form of an early-access minor image.  By 7th level the duration is great; this is a solid power for the creative out there.

Focus Powers:

  • Cloak Image: Roughly the equivalent of two bonus spells known (disguise self and disguise other), but with crappier durations.  This is a serviceable power, but hats of disguise are cheap!
  • Color Beam:  This isn’t worth the cost.
  • Masquerade:  This is an interesting power, basically a more powerful, but more limited, disguise self.  You can probably still get by with a hat of disguise.
  • Mirage:  Somewhere between major image and mirage arcana, you can probably get by with Minor Figment, but it’s another tool for a dedicated illusionist.
  • Shadow Beast:  Remember when I said occultists aren’t summoners?  Well, this is how you fake it.  Even if an enemy disbelieves, a shadow summon is still an obstacle, still a distraction.  This power lets you fake up to a 9th level spell, making it one of your most powerful options; a heal spell from a shadow trumpet archon works almost as well as the real thing!  At 1 point of focus, this is a steal.
  • Unseen:  Saves you a spell known on invisibility, and is slightly early access for you.

Thoughts:  This is an attractive implement school for all occultists, particularly from the low to upper mid levels.  You’re here primarily for three things: mirror image, and the Shadow Beast and Unseen focus powers.  Mirror image is arguably your best defensive spell for a large portion of your career.  Creative or more caster-focused occultists will enjoy many of the focus powers and spells here.

Necromancy Implement School

Spell List: Good

  • There’s basically two flavors here: solid debuffs, and undead creation.

Greed:  Low

  • The main draw here is the spell selection, and the focus powers that might be of interest have pretty good duration, so you won’t have to spam them necessarily.  

Implements:  Fragile

  • None of your implements are wearable, and must be held in hand.  This risks them being stolen, misplaced, or destroyed.

More than once? - Possibly

  • If you’re here for debuffs and focus powers, you’ll be fine taking this once.  If you plan on doing more hardcore minionmancy, you might want to supplement your animation spells with undead-healing capabilities.  With this in mind, there are several good spell choices at the upper levels.

When to take:  6th is a good time to take it for those interested in making minions.  It’s a decent 1st or 2nd level choice for a more caster-focused occultist.  If you’re squeamish, or need to make room for other schools, you can skip this school without much difficulty.

Resonant Power: Necromantic Focus

  • The save penalty to undead is a minor footnote.  On the surface, a necromancer would rejoice at the chance to expand their HD cap for controlling minions, but there’s a potential problem here: when your mental focus resets after resting, all of these extra HD of undead would become uncontrolled.  If your GM is kind, perhaps you won’t have to worry about this, but it does ‘lock in’ your mental focus if you want to maintain control of these extra mooks.  As-written, it’s probably best used to create a few more undead before sending them on a suicide mission, but this quickly becomes costly.

Base Focus Power:  Mind Fear

  • Basically a bonus spell known in the form of cause fear that maintains relevance in the later levels.  Necromancers are pros at fear-stacking, and this is a useful tool to that end.

Focus Powers:

  • Flesh Rot:  Skip this.  You have better offensive options, and it doesn’t even heal undead.
  • Necromantic Servant:  This is a really cool ability.  It allows you to make minions right out of the gate, and they scale in relevance pretty well.  You’re basically ‘summoning’ these undead, so more uptight party members won’t have as much to complain about (no Evil descriptor).  Whether you want to make minions without as much bookkeeping or moral implications, or you want to supplement your actual minions with additional support, this is a nifty ability.
  • Pain Wave:  Roughly the equivalent of a bonus spell known, in this case an AoE debuff with respectable range.
  • Psychic Curse:  Of the three effects this provides you, only the memory loss ability strikes me as particularly useful, and even then, it’s pretty niche.
  • Soulbound Puppet:  While not as powerful as Necromantic Servant, this ability is much more subtle, and still very useful.  You can have a collection of bones or dolls to produce whatever critter you need, and a humanoid doll could potentially manipulate items or even UMD wands!  This is as useful as a disposable temporary familiar would be, which is quite a bit if you’re creative.
  • Spirit Shroud:  This is a nifty little ability, mostly of note for melee occultists.  I wish it had better duration, but it’s a better use of your focus than the abjuration base power certainly.

Thoughts:  While this school is certainly skippable for many builds, this is a really fun and flavorful implement school.  It’s most attractive to a more caster-focused occultist; indeed, it’s a school to make a build around.  This is the way to be a true minion-mancer as an occultist; you’ll never be as strong at it as a cleric (unless you grab a wand of desecrate, which you probably should), and it’s expensive, but you can certainly hang out in the professional necromancer club.  Even if you don’t pick up animate dead, the spell list is no slouch, with heavy hitters like possession in the mix.  Become a zombie-chucking, doll-animating weirdo with this school, and have fun with it.

Transmutation Implement School

Spell List: Great

  • Interestingly, the focus powers replicate many of the best spells on this list, which helps ease the painful decision making.

Greed:  Thirsty

  • The base and resonant powers are in high demand for a warrior, and the focus powers will be demanding a lot of attention, but they’re almost entirely worth it!

Implements: Easy

  • A warrior’s going to have their weapon on hand at all times.  A more caster focused occultist can settle for a vest or a pair of boots.

More than once? - Possibly

  • If you plan on taking a lot of the focus powers, you can probably get by with taking it once.  If you want to diversify your powers a bit with other schools, you might pick up this school twice to grab some more of the better spells on the list.

When to take:  A melee or archer occultist should take this at level 1.  Better to take it earlier than later regardless of your role, since it’s got great powers and spells.

Resonant Power: Physical Enhancement

  • This frees up your wealth slightly.  Warriors want to use it on their main attacking ability, but casters can benefit from the defensive boost of a DEX or CON increase.  It’s expensive right out of the gate, but you’ll never regret having points invested in this school.

Base Focus Power: Legacy Weapon

  • While this power starts off okay, it gets really good once you can grab a +1 weapon.  Once you have a magical weapon, you can take a standard action at the start of any combat to slap bane on your weapon, tailored to whatever you’re fighting.  Reminiscent of early artificer stratagems of old, having a bane weapon on your side helps keep your attack and damage competitive with full BAB classes.  This power is less useful to more caster-focused occultists, but there’s still a lot this school has to offer.

Focus Powers:

  • Mind Over Gravity:  Basically a personal-only improved fly spell.  Thumbs up!
  • Philosopher’s Touch:  The ‘special metal’ forms of damage reduction aren’t terribly difficult to overcome; you probably don’t need to waste a focus power on them.
  • Quickness:  Single-target improved haste.  Sexy.
  • Size Alteration:  Basically two bonus spells known, and they work on non-humanoids!  The only downside is their duration, but this is a versatile option.  Probably best for a melee build.
  • Sudden Speed:  Want a quickened expeditious retreat that stacks with haste?  Sure!
  • Telekinetic Mastery:  Yet another ‘bonus spell known’ power, this time with the versatile telekinesis.  Probably most attractive to a caster-focused occultist.

Thoughts:  This is THE implement school for combat occultists, melee or ranged.  Regardless of build, all occultists should pick up this school, possibly picking it twice.  It has the largest spell list, and is brimming with utility and useful buffs.  The focus powers mostly duplicate spell effects, often with minor improvements, proving the power of a single point of mental focus.  The value of this school begins to decline as your wealth increases, but it will never be entirely irrelevant.  Remember: spells known can be swapped out, but focus powers are permanent.  

Occultist Spell Selection

“You’re certain that wasn’t metamagic?”

~Mayna, sorceress new to psychic magic

        Because your spells known are tied so intimately to your implement school selection, I’ve broken down the spell list by school.  Remember: if you’ve only picked an implement school once, you’ll only know one spell of each spell level for that school.  Because of this, I’ve only highlighted the best options for each spell level.  These are the spells with the greatest bang for your buck; typically your best bet will be to pick a blue spell, perhaps swapping it out for a green if it becomes obsolete or redundant.

        I’ve also pointed out a rare few trap spells.  In general, though, you want to compare your spell selection with your focus powers.  Many of your focus powers will do what a spell does, often at a better ‘price.’  Your focus powers often have a short duration, making them better for in-combat use, while spells with better durations will be better for utility or pre-combat buffing.

Abjuration School

0-Level / Knacks

  • resistance - No choice here.  Too bad the resonant power makes this mostly useless out of the gate.  You can still use it on your friends, but it will be a dead knack as soon as cloaks of resistance start showing up.

1st Level

  • alarm - Long duration utility is the name of the game here.  More subtle than the Loci Sentry focus power.
  • shield - If you plan on using a two-handed weapon, this is probably your best choice.  A solid defensive buff.

2nd Level

  • bullet shield - Great duration and defense against ranged attacks.  Best on an archer or blaster that hangs back from combat.
  • node of blasting - Use it on your implements to discourage theft.  Also great for setting traps if you’re a creative thinker.
  • resist energy - While Energy Shield is a great focus power, resist energy has amazing duration and an extremely long shelf life of usefulness.  

3rd Level

  • dispel magic - Always useful, with utility in and out of combat.  
  • magic circle against chaos/evil/good/law & thaumaturgic circle - Your magic circle class feature provides many of the utility benefits of these spells.  The defensive benefits are mostly covered by your resonant power.  Binding outsiders for combat is ill-advised, as you’ll always be behind full casters and will never gain access to greater planar binding.  

4th Level

  • dimensional anchor - This can ruin a high-level caster or outsider’s day.
  • freedom of movement - Incredible defense with great duration.  Probably your best choice if you’re more caster-focused, or in a campaign featuring aquatic environments.
  • stoneskin - A bit pricey, but this spell is fantastic for a melee-focused occultist.

5th Level

  • greater dispel magic - The improved version of an already great spell.  You can swap out dispel magic for a different 3rd level or lower spell at this point.
  • communal stoneskin - If you skipped stoneskin last level, you can pick it up here; it’s a better deal for the price.

6th Level

  • antimagic field - Arguably the most powerful abjuration available to you, but occultists are heavily reliant on magical effects.  This is a good choice for archers in certain situations, or anyone who just wants to have the ability to shut off magic in emergencies.
  • repulsion - This is most useful to archers or caster-focused occultists who want to keep the enemy at a distance.

Conjuration School

0-Level / Knacks

  • create water - Worthy of consideration in a desert or survival-themed game.
  • stabilize - Always useful; ignore the Heal skill and save your pals.

1st Level

  • cure light wounds - This is probably your best choice for this level.
  • mount - Once you have access to regular healing wands, you might consider swapping out CLW for a utility spell such as this.
  • unseen servant - See above.

2nd Level

  • glitterdust - No other real competition at this level.  A must-have.

3rd Level

  • cure serious wounds - Likely your best bet for most games.
  • phantom steed - This is a powerful transportation spell, and might make for an interesting mounted combat build.

4th Level

  • dimension door - The only real choice for this level.

5th Level

  • teleport - Amazing transportation and convenience.  If you’re happy with dimension door for teleporting needs, picked up phantom steed for long-distance travel, or have an ally that can cast this, you could skip this.
  • wall of stone - A fine battlefield control spell with some utility mixed in; best choice if you don’t spring for teleport. 

6th Level

  • getaway - Great adventuring insurance; the closest you’ll get to astral projection.
  • heal - One of the best healing spells in the game.  Doubles as an undead nuke.

Divination School

0-Level / Knacks

  • detect magic - There’s a good chance other casters in your party have this, so you may be able to skip this, but it’s still one of the best knacks.
  • detect psychic significance - If someone already has detect magic, this is a decent substitute.  It helps you locate items for you to use your object reading ability on.
  • guidance - If your party has detection cantrips covered, this is a fine knack for general utility.

1st Level

  • heightened awareness - Awesome duration, great utility, excellent for scouting, and better than anticipate peril for most of your career if you’re looking for an initiative boost.
  • detect secret doors - Always handy to have up your sleeve, consider this spell if you double up on the divination school.

2nd Level

  • blood biography
  • create treasure map
  • locate object
  • residual tracking - Any of the above spells make for interesting divinations that provide information unavailable from your object reading ability.
  • see invisibility - The best spell for you this level, but if you take it, be sure to retrain it post 7th level, when your Third Eye gains see invisibility.

3rd Level

  • arcane sight - Probably your best bet for this level.
  • locate weakness - A combat option for crit fishers.
  • pierce disguise - A poor man’s true seeing for a while.
  • retrocognition - A fun source of information.

4th Level

  • detect scrying - A great ‘fire and forget’ spell for the paranoid.
  • locate creature - A useful trick.
  • scrying - This might be someone else’s job, but it’s still a fantastic spell.

5th Level

  • battlemind link - A great option for combat-focused occultists.
  • true seeing - The one thing your Third Eye can’t replicate.  You’ll be glad to have this.

6th Level

  • find the path - A ‘get out of maze free’ card.
  • prediction of failure - A nasty debuff spell.
  • greater scrying - Consider the above spell if somebody else already has this; if you do take it, remember to swap out scrying if you took it earlier.

Enchantment School

0-Level / Knacks

  • daze - No choice here.

1st Level

  • charm person - Never goes out of style.
  • sleep - The low-level win button.  Be sure to swap it out at 5th level.

2nd Level

  • aversion - Surprisingly potent and useful in a variety of situations.
  • investigative mind - Good for mystery solving.
  • tactical acumen - An interesting choice for combat focused occultists.

3rd Level

  • suggestion - Really the only relevant option that isn’t redundant with your focus powers.

4th Level

  • charm monster
  • confusion - One of the more terrifying offensive spells on your entire class list.

5th Level

  • dominate person - Hard to pass this bad boy up.
  • mind fog - A good tool to soften up targets for your other powers, or allies that rely on mind-affecting magic.
  • mass suggestion

6th Level

  • mass charm monster - Best option for a ‘caster’ occultist.
  • cloak of dreams - A powerful tool for a melee occultist.

Evocation School

0-Level / Knacks

  • dancing lights
  • light - Either of these spells are probably your best bet for general utility.  The offensive knacks aren’t really worth it, unless you’re really not keen on using weapons and want an at-will attack at low levels.

1st Level

  • burning hands - Your best and only AoE option at this level.
  • floating disk - Good utility to swap out for when burning hands or shocking grasp become outdated.

2nd Level

  • darkness - An interesting defensive choice for an archer.
  • flaming sphere - Probably your best option for DPS at this level.
  • frost fall - Mild battlefield control.
  • gust of wind - Largely utility, this is handy to have when you need it.

3rd Level

  • daylight - Valuable in an Underdark-style game.
  • deeper darkness - Decent for battlefield control or stealth.
  • fireball - Pretty much the standard by which direct damage spells are judged.
  • talismanic implement - This is an occultist exclusive, but it’s not that great.  Your selection of spells is extremely limited, so you’ll likely only have a small handful of personal-range spells of 3rd level or lower.  You give up a 3rd level evocation spell known for the privilege, and it eats up your precious mental focus to do so.
  • wind wall - The best defense against archers in the game.

4th Level

  • etheric shards - This spell is amazing.  Preposterous duration, absolutely brutal battlefield control.
  • fire shield - A nice option for melee occultists, combining offense with defense.

5th Level

  • call lightning storm - A decent choice if you’re looking to expand your energy types.
  • cone of cold - See above.
  • wall of force - A nice wall option if you’re looking for something more solid than etheric shards.

6th Level

  • chain lightning - The apex of your direct damage spells.
  • cold ice strike - The swift action casting time makes this a fun little blast.
  • contingency - Arguably the best evocation spell; probably worth a spell known if you’ve got some good spells to couple with it (and you should).

Illusion School

0-Level / Knacks

  • ghost sound - No choice here; good thing it rocks!

1st Level

  • disguise self - Better duration than Cloak Image.  Retrain it if you grab a hat of disguise.
  • vanish - Nice to have up your sleeve if you’re looking to save mental focus.

2nd Level

  • mirror image - There’s no other choice this level.  One of the best defensive spells in the game.

3rd Level

  • displacement - Even more defensive goodness.
  • invisibility sphere - Best in its class for team stealth.
  • major image - Surpasses the power of your Minor Figment, it’s a great spell for the creative.

4th Level

  • greater invisibility - Nothing else comes close for this level.  Great for you or your friends.

5th Level

  • mislead - Fun, but enemies are becoming less susceptible to these sorts of things by the time this comes online.
  • scripted hallucination (UI) - An interesting higher-level option from Ultimate Intrigue.

6th Level

  • permanent image - It’s one or the other.  Both are pretty decent spells.
  • project image

Necromancy School

  • touch of fatigue - This is the choice if you want something that’s even slightly useful in a fight.

1st Level

  • cause fear - Rendered somewhat redundant by your Mind Fear focus power, and eventually rendered obsolete.
  • inflict light wounds - Nice if you want to heal any undead you create.  It’s either this, or spells that mess around with corpses (which have potential).

2nd Level

  • false life - A fine choice to swap the above spell out for.

4th Level

5th Level

6th Level

  • harm - Nothing beats it for healing your minions, or just delivering an unpleasant slap.
  • greater possession - As long as you’ve got an escape ready, this requires less logistics than regular possession.

Feat Selection

“My clan’s ancestral axe does half the work.  Muscles do the rest.”

~Brunor Flintbrow, dwarven occultist

        An occultist’s thirst for feats is largely dependent on the build in question.  An archer occultist will likely have most of their feats spoken for, while a melee or caster occultist will have more flexibility.  Aside from a few classic staples, an occultist isn’t terribly reliant on feats.

General Feats

Additional Traits (APG):  Some traits are really amazing.  Handy for expanding your class skills, shoring up saves, or boosting initiative as well.

Combat Casting:  This is of most value to a melee occultist that might find themselves casting in close quarters.

Charnel Soldiers (C&C):  This is an odd little feat for a necromancer occultist who doesn’t know what to do with his feats.  You’re usually better off with one or two powerful undead, but if you’re into an army of archers, say, you might be able to do something interesting with this feat.

Destructive Dispel (UC):  Since dispel magic is a likely choice if you pick up the abjuration implement school, this is a fun little pick if you’re going to be casting it often in combat.

Dimensional Agility (UC):  A melee occultist could pick this up around level 11.  It’s the gateway to an extensive chain of feats, but this one, and Dimensional Assault are probably all you’ll really need.  

Efficient Focus Shift (OA):  If you find yourself shuffling your mental focus around, this feat will save you 2 points of mental focus a day.  It’s not quite as good as Extra Mental Focus, but you can’t take that feat more than once...

Expanded Arcana (APG):  This feat is in need of some clarification; so far, I haven’t found a ruling yet.  Since your spells known are tied so intimately to individual implements, I’m not sure how this feat would work for an occultist exactly.  Probably the easiest way is to tie the spells you gain from this feat to one or more specific implements and proceed as normal.  In this case, this is a really handy feat for expanding your spells known, especially if you don’t feel like doubling up on a school.

Extend Resonant Power (OA):  When it comes to your best resonant powers, you typically want them running at peak performance.  This feat dilutes your resonant powers, allowing you to share half the effect with a single ally from a single school you can’t change, and the effect goes away if they move farther than 30 ft. away.  This is just too restrictive to be worth it.

Extra Focus Power (OA):  You learn new focus powers at the same rate you learn feats, but many focus powers are better than a feat for you.  More focus powers means more options for your mental focus expenditure.  This is best taken when multiple fantastic powers become available, reducing the wait time for your best toys.  Note that you can only take it once per implement school you know.

Extra Mental Focus (OA):  This is pretty much required.  Take it at level 1 and never look back.  It’s a shame you can’t take it more than once…

Hidden Presence, Manipulative Presence, Intrusive Presence (OA):  These feats are all useful if you plan on using the possession line of spells and generally being a creep.  If it weren’t for the intensive feat investment, I’d rank them higher; you either commit to this or you don’t.  That said, Intrusive Presence can have nightmarish repercussions.  Definitely a mid to late game consideration.

Implement Focus (OA):  You have to pick an implement school with this feat, likely the one you use most often.  If that’s the case, why not invest your focus there normally and enjoy the resonant power?  Your generic focus will still be wasteful if you use it on other schools, so this feat doesn’t really afford you any more flexibility than you had in the first place.

Iron Will:  Even though Will is your strong save, and you’ll have lots of spells and class features to improve it even further, nothing messes with your casting ability like a failed Will save.  Every little bit helps.  You’ll be happy to have the improved version when you need it as well; consider taking it in a very caster-heavy or occult-y game.

Lightning Reflexes:  Although Reflex is likely to be your worst save, I wouldn’t bother investing too much into it unless you’re in a dragon-heavy campaign or something.

Mage’s Tattoo (ISWG):  This is an easy +1 to CL if you’re already taking Spell Focus.

Spell Focus, Greater Spell Focus:  As a 1-6 caster, your save DCs will start to trail behind a full caster, so this helps close the gap.  If you’re a melee or ranged occultist mostly focusing on buffs, you can skip these easily.  They’re of most use for occultists interested in enchantment, illusion, or offensive necromancy.

Spell Penetration, Greater Spell Penetration:  Another choice for caster-focused occultists, these are preferable to Piercing Spell, as metamagic is a little harsher for an occultist with limited spell slots.

Strong Implement Link (OA):  This is more valuable if you take an archetype that reduces you to a single implement (such as tome eater or battle host).  You should have enough implements on your person that dropping or losing one won’t put you completely out of commission.

Toughness:  For a melee occultist, this is basically essential; you’ll need the extra hp to keep up with your full BAB compatriots.  Archer or caster occultists can benefit from this feat as well, but it’s not as high a priority.

Undead Master:  If you’re interested in necromancy, this feat allows you to animate bigger / more things at once.  It won’t allow you to control any more than you normally can, but it’s helpful if you need a small army quickly or find yourself with a particularly large and impressive corpse.

Combat Feats

        As a general rule, you should probably avoid combat maneuver feats; you lack the BAB to stay competitive, even with buffs and spells.

Armor Proficiency (Heavy):  Non-archer occultists might consider picking this up when they can start to afford full plate.  

Cleave:  This is a fine feat at lower levels, and says relevant for you a bit longer before iterative attacks come online.

Combat Expertise:  While you’ll certainly have the INT to take this, most of the feats this is a prereq for aren’t necessarily your best bet.

Critical Focus:  The critical feats are a late-career consideration, best if you’re using a weapon with a generous crit range.  Of the critical feats this unlocks for you, Staggering Critical is probably your best bet.

Improved Critical:  This saves you a bit of money on a keen weapon.  

Dazzling Display:  If you can pick up Intimidate through a trait, intimidation tactics are a useful tool, especially against other psychic characters.  It combines well with the necromancy implement school.

Deadly Aim:  An archer will want to pick this up eventually, once the essentials are obtained.  Bane arrows will take the sting off the attack penalty.

Furious Focus (APG):  This is a nice companion to Power Attack; your lower BAB means avoiding penalties is extra useful for you.

Improved Initiative:  Is there a guide out there where this isn’t blue?  Going first means getting your spells off before something bad happens.  Always worth picking up if you can.

Lunge:  Another solid melee feat that affords you some extra casting security.

Point-Blank Shot (Rapid Shot, Precise Shot, Manyshot):  These are the foundational feats for an archer build, and there are many more solid archery-related feats beyond these.  

Power Attack:  The king of melee feats is a solid addition to any STR-based melee occultist.

Quick Draw:  This feat is usually reserved for thrown weapon or two-weapon fighting builds, but if your GM is a stickler about juggling your various implements when it comes time to use your focus powers or cast your spells, you might need to grab this.  The language in the implements section seems to imply that ‘presenting an implement’ isn’t any more complicated than a cleric using a divine focus, but you never know…

Weapon Finesse:  This is a good backup feat for an archer if you can fit it in, and mandatory if you’re going for a DEX-based melee build.

Weapon Focus:  It’s a prereq for several decent feats.  Considering your lower BAB every little attack bonus helps.

Item Creation Feats

If you’re in a campaign that has plenty of downtime, you might consider item crafting feats; you’re basically trading feats for more magic gear, which can be worth it.  While the occultist has a lingering scent of the 3.5 Artificer about it, you’ll have a harder time meeting prereqs than wizards or arcanists; it’s not an insurmountable obstacle however.  

Craft Rod:  This is likely a late-game pick.  Metamagic rods can be extremely expensive, but they’re absolutely better for an occultist than knowing the feats themselves.  You won’t have to worry about greater rods, since lesser and normal rods cover all of your spells except your most powerful 6th level ones.

Craft Wand:  This is nice if you’re especially paranoid about fear effects.  A wand of Remove Fear is nice and cheap, and can help get you back in the fight.  There’s all sorts of spells that are handy to have in wand-form.

Craft Wondrous Item:  Probably the best item creation feat for the sheer volume of wondrous items out there; the possibilities are almost endless.  

Metamagic Feats

You’ve got two things working against you when it comes to metamagic feats: you’re not a full caster, and thus have fewer spell slots to work with, and you’re a spontaneous caster on top of that, so using metamagic in combat is cumbersome.  Thus, you’re best off relying on metamagic rods.  That said, there are a few that are worth consideration.

Extend Spell:  Possibly the most popular metamagic feat, this is an old standby that’s great for buffing before a dungeon delve.  A lesser rod of this is nice and cheap, so you can probably skip it.

Intuitive Spell (OA):  You can negate the downside of thought components with a move action; using this feat will take a full round action.  Grab a rod if you can spare some extra cash, otherwise don’t worry about it.

Logical Spell (OA):  The big weakness of psychic magic is emotion components.  There are ways to defend against negative emotion effects, but inevitably something’s going to get through.  This is handy in rod form, but you won’t regret simply knowing this feat either.  

Quicken Spell:  Quicken metamagic rods are insanely expensive, so knowing this feat is easier on the pocketbook.  However, even at the apex of your power you’ll only be able to quicken 2nd level or lower spells.

Trait Selection

If your game features the optional trait rules (and most seem to these days), you basically have two options: pick something flavorful or appropriate for your backstory, or min-max them like the conniving Munchkin you are.  We’ll be looking at ‘optimal’ traits, many of which are basically half-feats.  There are so many of these damn things, from so many different sources, that I’m basically going to throw up my hands and say ‘look them up on the SRD.’  

Reactionary:  This applies to any similar initiative-boosting trait.  Going first is very important, so any trait that boosts this for you is gold.

Magical Lineage:  One of the only examples of metamagic reducers in Pathfinder, a wisely-picked spell coupled with this feat is potent indeed.

Disillusioned (QaC):  +2 to saves against any effect that could shut you off from emotion component spells is certainly handy.

Bully:  Traits that give you new class skills are great.  This one gives you Intimidate, if you’re interested in Dazzling Display builds or you want to round out your social skills.

Deft Dodger:  Any of the save-boosting traits are great.  Ref. in particular is a weakness deserving of mention, but really any of these are a solid choice.

Focused Mind:  Most folks might not have the room to fit in a feat like Combat Casting.  Traits like this are a nice compromise.

Fate’s Favored:  Awesome for half-orcs with the sacred tattoo racial trait, this is a handy trait for anybody really.  Pick up a four leaf clover or a luckstone!

Pragmatic Activator:  Who needs CHA?  Make UMD based off of INT, and win.

Archetypes

“Me fear no words!  ME EAT WORDS!”

~Inkbreath, goblin tome eater

There are four archetypes for the occultist included in Occult Adventures.  Some are just more focused versions of a regular occultist, while others are more radically different.  Let’s take a look!

Ancestral Aspirant (UI)

        A more courtly occultist, the ancestral aspirant is practically as powerful as the standard occultist.  If you want a spy network of regular people rather than random outsiders, or if you like the enchantment implement school, this archetype will serve you well.

Class Skills:  Gain Bluff and Knowledge (nobility), lose Profession and Knowledge (planes).  A wash.

Family Jewels:  One of your starting implements must be enchantment.  Enchantment isn’t the best implement school, but if you’re in for an intrigue-heavy, social campaign you could certainly do worse.

Emotional Reading:  Losing object reading kind of sucks, but ultimately it’s for minor information gathering and flavor, which is what this replacement is for.

Courtly Contacts:  You still keep all your weird magic circle nonsense, but you can’t use it to ply outsiders for information.  Instead, you have a social network that serves largely the same purpose.

        So yeah.  Ancestral aspirants don’t really gain or lose anything.  If you want to play an occultist more comfortable in a courtly environment, you can certainly do worse than this archetype.

Battle Host

        This is the focused warrior archetype for the occultist.  It saves you a few feats (heavy armor proficiency!) and has some built-in conveniences, as well as granting you some bonus feats.  These come at the cost of versatility, school access, and spells known.

Panoply Bond:  The good news: you only have to worry about one implement, which is a MW weapon, shield, or suit of armor you get for free at level 1 (hello, MW full plate!).  It’s almost indestructible, so you’ll never have to worry about losing an implement, especially if it’s your suit of armor.  The bad news: this implement is your implement for life.  You can’t change it for anything else, even if you find a better weapon or suit of armor.   You also lose out on 2 implement schools, so you’ll know 14 (!) fewer spells than a regular occultist.  Since you’re probably going for a melee or archery build here, you’ll mostly be interested in transmutation and abjuration, but you’ll have significantly fewer options than a regular occultist, both in spell selection and implement flexibility.

Battle Skill & Battle Reading:  This is a straight downgrade.  Battle Skill hurts the most; you’ll ultimately have less of a focus on UMD.

Bonus Feats:  Four bonus combat feats is a nice addition for a warrior occultist.

Spiritual Warrior:  Essentially an early-access bonus spell, this is an upgrade for a combat-focused occultist certainly.

Heroic Splendor:  The flavorful Outside Contact is replaced by an awesome combat buff.  Definitely an upgrade.

        If being a generalist melee or archer occultist seems like too much work, or if you don’t care about the more flavorful information-gathering class features, Battle Host is a pretty solid choice for a warrior.  You’ll be doing less implement-juggling and a lot more shooting/head smashing.  I’d say this archetype is stronger in the lower to mid levels, before awesome magic items start getting dumped in your lap.  At higher levels, you may start to resent your locked-in implement.

Necroccultist

        This archetype is for the dedicated necromancer.  You give up some versatility in exchange for MOAR NECROMANCY.

Necromantic Bond:  The bad news: you lose 2 implement schools, your 1st level implement school is spoken for, and your implement mastery is ‘locked in.’  The good news: you learn a bonus necromancy spell every level, so you actually come out slightly ahead in spells known, and your necromancy spells and powers will be harder to resist.  It’s roughly a wash, but you come out slightly ahead of the Battle Host in my opinion.

Deadspeaker:  Essentially a bonus spell known in the form of blood biography, the more limited uses per day makes this something of a downgrade, actually.

Ghostly Horde:  You lose Aura Sight for what amounts to a bonus focus power.  It’s okay, but probably not worth wasting mental focus to extend its duration.

Life Drain:  A pseudo-enervation focus power in exchange for Outside Contact is decent.

        The main draw of this archetype is the spell access to wizard necromancy spells.  Everything else is roughly a wash.  You’ll end up a more versatile necromancer if you stay a standard occultist, but this archetype is a decent option for someone who wants to be mega-focused on necromancy.

Reliquarian (OO)

        Very close to a standard occultist in terms of power, the reliquarian is simply a divine caster version of the class.  If you don’t want to mess with the newness of psychic casting, the reliquarian gives you a fine alternative.

Faithful:  You must worship a deity, which isn’t a big deal.  You also gain a weapon proficiency, which could be quite choice.

Domain, Diminished Focus Power, Sacred Implements:  You lose one of your starting implement schools, replacing it with a cleric domain.  The domain’s powers and spells require an implement to use, but it doesn’t require focus power investment, which frees up more points for your other implement schools.  Domains can give you access to some cool spells and abilities, many of them off the occultist’s normal spell list.  Overall this ability is roughly a wash in terms of power.

Divine Focus:  You use WIS instead of INT for determining your mental focus pool size.  Your casting is still INT based, though, so this just makes you slightly more MAD.  There isn’t a whole lot of use for WIS for occultists natively; ultimately, this means a reliquarian will have slightly less mental focus to throw around than standard occultists.  But at least you don’t have to worry about fear effects shutting down your spell casting.

Secret Broker (UI)

        Secret Brokers function very similarly to the standard occultist.  You lose magic circles and all related class features, gaining the ability to transfer information through objects, and eventually the power to erase facts from the minds of people.  This comes at the cost of flexibility; ultimately it’s slightly less powerful than the standard occultist, but still certainly functional.

Class Skills:  You add Bluff and all remaining Knowledge skills to your class list.  A straight upgrade.

Knowledge is Power:  You start with only 1 implement at 1st level, and it has to be divination.  Losing a whole implement is pretty rough.  Divination is a solid implement school, though, and your resonant power expands to include Sense Motive, Profession, and Knowledge checks.  This is a downgrade overall, but it’s bearable.

Broker Secrets:  You lose your UMD bonus, but gain the ability to transfer secrets through items.  In an intrigue-focused game, hopefully you have a bit higher CHA to keep your UMD competitive, and can use this ability to pass secret notes without teacher noticing.

Share Memory:  You lose Shift Focus and gain an at-will 2nd level spell.  Ideally you don’t want to be shifting focus points around in the first place.  Sure, you lose a bit of flexibility, but share memory is a cool spell to have at the drop of a hat, especially in an intrigue-heavy game.

Steal Secret:  Your share memory SLA can now be used against unwilling targets.  Considering magic circles is best used to gather information, this is likely an upgrade; who knows what that imp knows, but you can pick and choose memories from a target with an informed decision.

Erase Secret:  This is basically a bonus focus power, at the cost of an upgrade to a class feature you’ve already traded out.  Nice!

Purge Secret:  An upgrade to Erase Secret, this is extremely costly in terms of focus power; use sparingly.

        Overall this is a pretty solid archetype.  Losing an implement school is the worst aspect, and a tough burden to bear.  However, it’s one less thing demanding focus power investment, and you gain lots of nifty intrigue abilities.  

Sha’ir

        Flavorful, and tempting to people that like having little squads of minions, the sha’ir is ultimately more of a liability than a regular occultist.

Jin:  Instead of implements, a sha’ir has a small menagerie of jin, which are weak, almost-powerless small elementals.  The jin must remain within 30 ft. of the sha’ir to allow him access to his focus powers and spells.  Starting at 2nd level the jin scale in hp and base saving throws like a wizard’s familiar.  You lose access to your spells and powers should a jin be slain, and while you can resurrect them for free the following day, in the meantime you’ll be hosed.  You also lose access to 4 normal implement schools’ worth of spells known, base focus powers, and resonant powers, getting in exchange partial-access to three elementalist wizard schools.  Even if it weren’t for the jin’s frailty, this is a pretty hard sell.

Augment Jin:  Sending your jin into battle is a risky endeavor, since you lose much of your power should they die.  Augmenting them to become more powerful elementals is costly in terms of mental focus, takes a full round action, and has a very short duration.  In exchange you lose your UMD bonus and Object Reading powers.  Harsh.

Manifest Jin:  You lose Aura Sight in exchange for one of your jin being a normal small elemental with powers… yay?

Jin Spy:  Your jin serve as your Outside Contact servitors.  You lose access to their powers while they’re out running errands for you!

        The sha’ir is one bad fireball or dragon’s breath weapon away from being almost useless.  Your jin don’t even get improved evasion like a regular familiar; they’re just a big target for your enemies.  You don’t really gain much in exchange, losing out on versatility in exchange for weak pseudo-summoning.  Remember: everybody else does summoning better than you, and this archetype doesn’t help.

Silksworn (HotHC)

        Behold the power of bling.  If you want to be the castiest of casty occultists, you’ve come to the right place.

Skills:  Swap out a couple knowledges for another knowledge and Bluff.  Sure.

Spells:  You go from a psychic caster to an arcane caster.  Not as stealthy, but you’re safe from the irritation of emotional and thought components.  It’s actually an upgrade.

Implements:  Your implements change to fancy clothing.  This is actually pretty nice, as it means you’re much less likely to get disarmed.

Mental Focus:  Add your CHA to your pool, in addition to INT.  You’re probably not going to be fighting, so you can sink some points into CHA for a nice little boost.

Devoted Mystic:  Lose armor, shield, and martial weapon proficiency in exchange for 2 more implement schools right out the gate, and eventually a boost to your spells per day, +3 for each level by level 16.  By level 20, you’ll have 8 spells per level before bonus spells.  This is rock-solid.

Silksworn Eloquence:  Lose magic circles for a boost to social skill checks.

Silksworn Deception:  Lose binding circles for the chance to conceal your spellcasting from observers.  That was half the attraction of psychic magic to begin with, so by this point you’ve 100% pulled ahead on the casting front.

Silksworn Arcana:  It’s a long wait, but this is an easy +2 to your spells’ save DCs.

Implement Mastery:  Double the pleasure, double the fun.

        So yeah.  If you don’t feel like you have enough implement school choices, or if you just want to be the 1-6 caster with the best adventuring endurance ever, silksworn is where it’s at.  Obviously, if you want to be a melee or archery guy you might be better served with the stock occultist, but for caster-leaning types this is an easy win.  While you’ll never be quite as earth-shatteringly powerful as a proper 1-9 caster, by level 16 you’re damn close to a proper sorcerer with arguably even better endurance thanks to your mental focus pool and implement powers.  This archetype is good enough that there’s an argument to be made that it pushes the occultist to the border of tier 2.

Tome Eater

        While this archetype has fun and wacky flavor, it’s pretty so-so.  It’s a great archetype for goblins or people that love getting paper cuts on their tongue.

Bonded Tome:  Much like the battle host, a tome eater has all of their implements consolidated in a single item: in this case, a book.  While it means less items to juggle or worry about losing, you have all of your eggs in one basket, and the basket is much less durable than the battle host’s weapon or armor.  You also lose access to a single implement school, which sucks, but you do gain a nifty little pseudo-focus power that lets you bump up your CL or save DC’s for your spells a limited number of times per day.  I’d say this is a bit of a downgrade overall.

Devour Books and Scrolls:  In exchange for Outside Contact and all of your magic circle class features, you gain the ability to counterspell scrolls only, and once per day eat a book or scroll to regain mental focus.  Not sure how often counterspelling a scroll is going to come up  (...never?), which leaves you with your mental focus recovery mechanic.  If you got more uses per day I’d be tempted to rate it higher.  As it stands, you can just have a cheap scroll at hand for what amounts to 1 bonus point of mental focus a day.  If you happen upon a scroll of questionable usage you can choke it down for a more significant replenishment, but it quickly becomes expensive to rely on every day.  Considering most of the stuff you lose in exchange is largely flavor, this is roughly a wash.

Word Sense:  This is roughly a wash in exchange for Aura Sight.

The tome eater isn’t bad, but ultimately it’s a slightly-gimped regular occultist.  It’s an oddball option for a more caster-focused occultist, but you’ll be honestly better served skipping this archetype.  

Equipment Considerations

“You’d think that cluttered old attic was a dragon’s hoard, he was so excited…”

~Vixx the Knife, puzzled adventuring companion of an occultist

Mundane Gear:

Breastplate, Full Plate, Mithral Chain Shirt:  Archers want to grab the chain shirt to take advantage of their high DEX scores.  Everybody else wants the heaviest armor they can reasonably wear.

Composite Longbow:  Your best ranged weapon choice.

Greatsword:  Hard to beat for a good two-handed weapon.  Really, you can mix it up with whatever melee weapon floats your fancy.

Backup weapons:  It doesn’t hurt to have weapons of a different damage type than your main weapon, as well as a light weapon for grapples or if you get swallowed whole.

Automatic Writing Planchette (OA):  The DCs for the automatic writing occult skill unlock are pretty high; this thing’s an easy +2 for cheap.  

Occult Reference Material (OA):  If you’ve got the time, this is a modest investment for a nice bonus on topics that might be common in an occult campaign.  

Prismatic Crystal (OA):  These are nifty little trinkets; you might as well enjoy the benefits of being psychic!  

Talking Board (OA):  This cheap item gives you all sorts of useful goodies in a spooky occult game, especially if there’s more than one occult character in the party.

Magic Items

Headband of Vast Intelligence:  Make peace with your headband slot, because you’ll want the best one of these you can afford.

Belt of Giant’s Strength / Incredible Dexterity / Mighty Constitution:  The transmutation resonant power will likely cover your main offensive physical ability score, but investing in a belt can supplement your secondary scores nicely.

Hat of Disguise:  These things are just the best.  In an intrigue heavy or social game you can get up to all kinds of mischief.  

Handy Haversack:  Useful for lugging your stuff around, especially if you dumped STR.

Runestones of Power (ACG):  These are basically pearls of power for spontaneous casters.  They’re certainly handy, but they are quite expensive for what they do, and you shouldn’t be particularly strapped for spell slots.  Probably not worth purchasing, but you can put them on your wish list for inquiring GMs…

Ring of Psychic Mastery (OA):  With the above in mind, it’s actually cheaper to own runestones of power, levels 1 - 4, than it is to buy this ring, which does what those four runestones would do while eating up a ring slot.  IT’S A TRAP!

Ring of the Hidden Psyche (OA):  This isn’t something you buy; it’s a plot device.

Metamagic Rods:  You want these!  Ideally several different kinds.  Some are very reasonably priced, such as the lesser rod of extend.  Others are outrageously expensive, but still incredibly useful and powerful.  On your wishlist: Quicken, Persistent, Logical, and Piercing to name a few.

Pages of Spell Knowledge (UE) / Spell Lattices (ACG):  Much like the Extra Arcana feat, how these items interact with your spells known and implements is unclear.  If your GM allows you to simply tie them to an implement, these are a fantastic way to expand your spells known, particularly lower level spells.

Wands:  1st level wands are the best deal.  Wishlist: endure elements, remove fear, air bubble.  

Staves:  Staves are expensive, but they’re a nice tool to stretch out your adventuring endurance in higher level play.  Typically, you find one in a treasure pile that you can use.  Like wands, they’re good fodder for your impressive UMD skill.

Potions:  Potions are handy, especially if you’re under a fear or negative emotion effect.  Potions of remove fear should always be on hand, as well as healing potions.

Dreamcatcher (OA):  In an occult game featuring lots of dream-wackiness, this is a pretty cheap item that gives you a nice defensive edge during sleepytime.

False Coin (OA):  Nice and cheap, but surprisingly handy in an intrigue-laden game.

Four-Leaf Clover (OA):  Much cheaper than a luckstone, these are handy little trinkets useful for any character.  Even better if you’ve got the Fate’s Favored trait.

Lucky Horseshoe (OA):  Much like the four-leaf clover, this is like a discount luckstone.  It’s nice that it’s ‘always on,’ but you can probably get by with a four-leaf clover until later in your career.

Shrunken Head (OA):  This creepy doodad is extremely expensive, but if you’re into fear spells it’s a nice high-level toy with built-in defense.

Talismans (OA):  You don’t have a whole lot of high-priority things for your neck slot, so the various talismans are pretty nifty to have around.  Obviously the greater ones are better in the long run, but the lesser ones are handy in a pinch.  Danger sense, freedom, good fortune, and life’s breath are the real stand-outs.
