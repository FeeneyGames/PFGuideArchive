With Great Power Comes Great Complexity

CTP’s Guide to the Pathfinder Vigilante

Introduction

Ultimate Intrigue introduced a new base class in the form of the Vigilante, giving Pathfinder players the opportunity to play the fantasy equivalent of a masked superhero.  A vigilante is a character of dualities, possessing a social identity (Bruce Wayne, Clark Kent, Peter Parker) and a vigilante identity (Batman, Superman, Spiderman).  Designed for use in intrigue-laden games, the vigilante is best viewed as a skeletal framework upon which multiple different superhero archetypes can be built.

Not all adventures are suited to vigilantes, nor are all adventuring parties conducive to their inclusion.  The vigilante identity is best suited to traditional adventuring, with plentiful combat options, but the social identity is (shockingly!) best suited to social scenarios and role play.  Thus, as-written the vigilante is at risk of losing almost half of their (non-combat) class features in a more traditional dungeon delve or hero’s journey campaign.
        To further complicate matters, the group needs to be ready to handle the dual nature of the vigilante’s identities, juggling in and out of character knowledge with a delicacy that might be unsuited for some groups.  However, in the right scenario, the vigilante can be a fun class to play; indeed, the class is diverse enough that an entire party of vigilantes could operate within the confines of a city, forming the Pathfinder equivalent of the Avengers or the Justice League.

Color Rating Key

Blue: Excellent.  A great choice.  Much better than nearly any other option.

Green: Good.  A solid choice.  Perhaps not the best, but these will serve admirably.

Black: Average.  Functional.  A good baseline measurement.

Orange: Sub-par or situational.  Not actively harmful, but too narrow or niche.

Red: Poor.  A bad choice.  Broken, or else actively works against you.

Should I Play a Vigilante?

A Vigilante’s Role

        While all vigilantes should be capable in social situations, their combat role largely depends on their vigilante specialization or archetype.

Information Retrieval:  Thanks to their social persona, all vigilantes will have plentiful tools and perks to aid in gathering information and solving mysteries.  While vigilantes with magical archetypes will have an edge here (thanks to divination magic), the non-magical social perks alone offer plentiful options, particularly within a vigilante’s city or town of influence.

Party Face:  The vigilante was designed with social and intrigue-heavy games in mind.  Unsurprisingly, both a vigilante’s personas are skilled at social interaction.  With access to all social skills and tons of perks that enhance navigation of social situations (or scaring the crap out of your foes), even the least charismatic vigilante can participate, and even excel, in social situations.

Stealth:  Whether magical or mundane, a vigilante has several options for staying unnoticed in the shadows.  Even the more heavily armored avenger-type vigilantes will be better at sneaking than most of their melee class peers.

The various specializations and archetypes further determine the roles a vigilante will excel at.  The stock avenger and stalker, as well as archetypes such as the brute, mounted fury, or wild soul will have a melee combat focus, while others such as the gun master or warlock can be built for ranged battle.  The caster archetypes such as warlock, cabalist, zealot, and magical child can offer utility casting, buffing, debuffing, or even blasting.

Chassis & Class Features

D8 HD, ¾ BAB, good Ref. & Will saves:  A pretty standard chassis for ‘skilled’ classes, the vigilante can hold its own in a fight, but they’re not as inherently hardy as full BAB types.  Note that avenger vigilantes have full BAB while assuming their vigilante identity.  A good Will save is always appreciated, especially to resist charms and mind-control.  However, a low Fort. save will be missed, especially if sinister nobles start poisoning the wine.

6 skill points/level:  Only the rogue has more skill points, and the vigilante’s list of class skills is robust.  It covers all important social skills, as well as many rogue-like skills such as disable device, perception, and stealth.  A few skills are worth noting in particular:

  • Bluff, Disguise, Stealth:  All vigilantes require these skills.  Not only do they help with general adventuring, scouting, and navigating social situations, but they are vital to help protect secret identities.
  • Perception, Use Magic Device:  Two of the best skills in the game, these are of particular value to vigilantes that find themselves engaged in more traditional adventuring activities.
  • Diplomacy, Sense Motive, Intimidate:  In a social or intrigue heavy game, there’s no reason not to keep the rest of the social skills high.  Vigilantes get lots of ways to boost Intimidate checks, especially when in their scarier persona.

The rest of the vigilante skill list contains useful things such as Acrobatics or Escape Artist, depending on the needs of your character.  It’s worth noting, somewhat strangely, that Knowledge (nobility) is missing from the vigilante class skill list, which seems like something of an oversight.

Simple & Martial Weapon Proficiency, Light & Medium Armor, + Shields:  Vigilantes have solid proficiencies, and there are even options for heavy armor depending on specialization.  

Dual Identity:  This spells out the mechanics of having two identities, essentially.  It’s an interesting non-magical way to foil certain powerful divinations, certainly.  Note that should someone learn the truth behind a vigilante’s identities, they can bypass the social and magical ‘defenses’ this ability provides.  A secret is increasingly difficult to keep the more people know it; even letting your closest allies or party members know the truth is an exploitable weakness by your enemies.

        Each identity can also have a different alignment (each within one ‘step’ of each other).  Again, this is a double-edged sword: it can impact restrictions on multiclassing or feat selection, for instance, since you can’t select something unless both alignments qualify for it.  

        Note that changing identities is a time-consuming process.  Barring certain talents that improve upon it, changing identities must be accomplished before combat (or at the very least during a lull in the danger).

Seamless Guise:  A massive bonus to Disguise checks to deflect suspicion while in either of your guises.  Considering how potentially ruinous failing a Disguise check might be for a vigilante, this is a vital ability out of the gate.  Note that it stacks with Disguise Self (or a Hat of Disguise); investing skill ranks periodically into Disguise helps determine whether your disguise is merely unlikely to be foiled, or practically impossible.

Social Talents:  At every odd level a vigilante gains a social talent, abilities that benefit both of his identities but are primarily meant to enhance his social identity.  The various archetypes rarely alter these talents, so their selection will prove surprisingly consistent across vigilantes of all stripes.  They are deserving of their own section below; for the most part, they serve flavor or role playing purposes.

Vigilante Talents:  Received at every even level, vigilante talents provide the combat perks and mechanics of the vigilante identity.  While under vigilante guise, these abilities are of vital importance, but while under your social identity these abilities are a potential liability.  Should an observer see the social persona use a vigilante talent, they are allowed a check to pierce your disguise.  I will break down vigilante talents in their own section below.

Vigilante Specialization:  While the standard vigilante has a choice between avenger and stalker (fighter or rogue, essentially), many of the archetypes function as ‘super specializations.’  Your choice here provides a combat perk and determines access to certain Vigilante Talents.  This choice also influences your ability score placement, feats, and other concerns.  Indeed, the vigilante is practically several different classes with a few unifying mechanics; in some ways, your specialization is your most important choice.  I’ll be breaking down the differences in the various specializations and archetypes in their own segment below.

Unshakable:  Vigilantes become harder to Intimidate than any other class.  This helps you from cracking under pressure or getting demoralized in battle.

Startling Appearance, Frightening Appearance, Stunning Appearance:  Beginning with Startling Appearance at 5th level, the vigilante gets the ability to inflict short-lived debuffs on targets he gets the jump on.  This ability ties into many vigilante talents, especially for stalkers.  Frightening Appearance is perhaps the best upgrade, since it can affect multiple foes and sets them up for fear-stacking.  While this ability is nice, it relies on you consistently getting the drop on the enemy, which isn’t guaranteed.  While it’s not the most reliable ability, it’s certainly handy when you get the opportunity to use it.

Vengeance Strike:  The vigilante’s capstone is very circumstantial.  Similar to an assassin’s death strike, the vigilante can study a target, gaining multiple benefits for each round of study (up to 5 rounds).  It only works on targets unaware of the vigilante, so it’s of no use in the middle of a fight.  If you can pull it off, it’s certainly potent, but much like Startling Appearance and its improvements, it’s hardly the most reliable tool in your utility belt.  By level 20, targets that are vulnerable to this attack are likely doomed regardless, while hiding from real threats is going to be a much harder challenge.

Social Talents

        Vigilantes of all stripes accumulate social talents.  These run the gamut from consistently, mechanically beneficial to largely flavor or roleplay enhancement.  None of them are particularly bad per se, and there are three broad ‘paths’ you can take them: celebrity, artisan, or master of disguise.  You can certainly mix and match a bit, but many of the later talents in the celebrity or master of disguise line require investment in the earlier talents.

        These abilities are available regardless of your current guise, but some of them function differently depending on whether you’re in vigilante or social mode.  Importantly, using them never risks exposing your dual identity.

Any Guise:  The ‘capstone’ of the ‘master of disguise’ social path, by level 17 you’ll already be a formidable con artist.  This ability allows you to add your +20 circumstance bonus from Seamless Guise on to practically any disguise you take on, taking your mimicry to near perfection.  This is a very open-ended ability, and if you’ve made it this far you’ve certainly earned it.

Case the Joint:  This ability can only be ‘set up’ by the social identity, but afterwards the benefit can be used regardless of guise.  It requires a time investment that makes it somewhat situational, and the benefit only applies to skill checks.  There are various other limitations as well that make this talent flavorful but not particularly vital.  It works as filler in between more potent talents if you have nothing else to take.

Celebrity Discount:  The amount of money this saves you, especially if you pursue the ‘celebrity path,’ is a drop in the bucket.  At most, you’re saving 50, 200, or 800 gp depending on how much renown you have.  For most adventurers, particularly at high levels, cash flow isn’t much of a problem.

Celebrity Perks:  I like this ability more than Celebrity Discount, but it’s almost entirely flavor.  The fact that you can potentially permanently lose it is a mark against its value as well.  At a certain point, I could see a lot of these benefits a hand wave for any high level adventurer depending on the campaign (or GM).  If you really must have hard mechanics behind being a celebrity, this is a fun and flavorful choice.

Double Time:  The way I see it, if you’ve got the downtime available for crafting or making money with Profession checks, you probably don’t need to save 2-4 hours in a day.  But in a really immersive, time sensitive, intrigue-laden game, I could potentially see this being useful.  

Everyman:  The second main talent of the ‘master of disguise’ path, this allows you to take on specific roles, restricted to working-class identities.  It’s an odd upgrade, and the fact that it comes online at level 11 makes it a long wait for something of only situational usefulness.  The easily-abandoned, disposable identities provided by Many Guises are probably going to suffice for most tasks that require an anonymous or mundane disguise.  While it’s powerful in the hands of the creative, this is largely just a filler prerequisite for Any Guise.

Feign Innocence:  The bonus this provides stacks with the bonus from Everyman and its improvements, making for a formidable Bluff check indeed.  The only frustrating aspect is the Renown prerequisite.

Gossip Collector:  Improved by the ‘celebrity’ line, this talent saves you time on rumor gathering.  Certainly appropriate in a social-heavy game, but unless things are running on an extremely precise time schedule, saving 1-2 hours is unlikely to change much.

Great Renown:  See Renown.

Immediate Change:  It’s a long wait, but just about all vigilantes should select this.  This finally allows you to change identities in the middle of combat (assuming there’s something to hide behind, etc.), or just at the drop of a hat whenever you need to switch gears.  The Medium wishes it had this kind of instant flexibility.

In Vogue:  For ‘artisan path’ types, the Craft application I’m having trouble fathoming a real use for.  Ultimately this ability just generates slightly more money for you.  It likely accomplishes the same thing mechanically/economically as Celebrity Discount or Celebrity Perks: in other words, a drop in the bucket.

Incredible Renown:  See Renown.

Instant Recognition:  With heavy prereqs, this is a late-game ability for the ‘celebrity path,’ and it’s only really useful in a game that features consistent traveling.  You’ve got to struggle through 13 levels beforehand at a minimum, so unless the game dynamic shifts to a more map-hopping style by the time you get this, it feels more like desperate relief rather than a solid perk.  Still, 4 hours vs. 1 week is enough of a time-saver to really make a difference regardless of playstyle.

Loyal Aid:  The benefits of this ‘celebrity path’ talent are relatively mild, and only of use in a game that takes place in a single community.  If the ‘defensive’ benefits were always-on, it would be slightly better.  This ability suggests that your helpers know both of your identities, which makes this a potential liability.  You might be able to replicate some of the power of this talent through the Leadership feat, or just plain old RP.

Many Guises:  The foundation for masters of disguise, this ability gives you a disposable, neutral identity or identities.  It foils divinations (and is itself non-magical), and often allows you to hide in plain sight, blending into the crowd.  Who takes the time to notice a beggar, a farmhand, or a stableboy?  You can get a lot of reconnaissance done with this ability; it’s overall a great and versatile tool for general skullduggery.  

Mockingbird:  Useful for anyone, this ability folds three different spells into one non-magical package.  The only question is how caster level and save DCs work; I assume it’s CHA based?  

Quick Change:  While this isn’t as useful as Immediate Change, it’s a prerequisite for it, and it’s certainly still useful.  By the time you’re level 7, you’ll be grateful for the chance to cut the time it takes to change identities down to 1-2 rounds.  Doing this in combat or a dangerous situation is still a tall order, but it’s at least feasible with this ability.  If at all possible, take the extra time to put the polish on your disguise; you want to negate risk whenever possible.

Renown:  The foundational ability of the ‘celebrity path,’ the main benefit is the boost to Intimidate when in your vigilante identity; the mile radius effect makes it useful even in a large city from the get-go.  This talent, and its improved versions, are the prereqs for several other talents, so it’s going to be difficult to avoid.  If only it scaled automatically with level… taking up 3 of your 10 social talents is quite the ‘talent tax.’

Safe House:  It doesn’t say so explicitly, but Renown is a prerequisite for this talent.  I wouldn’t take it until 7th level; the vigilante already has ways to avoid divinations, so this talent is of most use for the vigilante’s friends and allies (or prisoners).  It’s got fun flavor too; who doesn’t want a bat cave?

Social Grace:  No muss, no fuss, and it automatically scales with level.  Brilliant!  Everyone should take this at some point.

Subjective Truth:  The value of this talent depends on how often spells such as zone of truth and discern lies show up in your game.  It’s minimum level 9 before you can get this, so it’s quite the wait as well.  You can probably skip this; if you’re in the hands of some inquisitor you’ve probably screwed up more than this talent can help you.

Triumphant Return:  One of the prereqs for Instant Recognition, it’s certainly better than nothing if you’re travelling a lot and want to pursue the ‘celebrity path’ talents.

CTP’s Thoughts:  In my opinion, going for the master of disguise route is likely your best bet.  A selection of social talents might go as follows:

1 - Social Grace

3 - Case the Joint

5 - Many Guises

7 - Quick Change

9 - Mockingbird

11 - Everyman

13 - Immediate Change

15 - Renown

17 - Any Guise

19 - Safe House

If you wanted to go a ‘traveling celebrity’ route, you might instead go for something like this:

1 - Renown

3 - Triumphant Return

5 - Feign Innocence

7 - Great Renown

9 - Subjective Truth

11 - Incredible Renown

13 - Instant Recognition

15 - Mockingbird

17 - Social Grace

19 - Safe House

However, losing out on Quick Change / Immediate Change is tough; you could swap out Safe House or Subjective Truth and re-shuffle things a bit for the sake of convenience.  

General Vigilante Talents

        Vigilante talents are how you customize your vigilante identity for battle.  Remember: using them under your social guise risks exposure of your dual nature.  Many of them simply give you a feat plus some bonus perk; if you were planning on taking the feat in question anyway, especially if it’s a cornerstone to your build, it’s probably worth picking up the vigilante talent version; most of them even let you pick a new feat if you already had the feat in question earlier.

        Note that most of the following talents are Extraordinary Abilities; they can’t be dispelled, function in an antimagic field, and are perfect for making ‘badass normals’ in a world of wizardry and winged owlbears.

The following are vigilante talents available to everyone, regardless of specialization or archetype.  The specialist talents are tied so closely to the specializations and archetypes themselves that I will be discussing each in their own section.

Another Day:  This ability is more useful for NPCs, and in a solo game is actually decent insurance.  Otherwise, auto-stabilizing is nice, but if you’re in a party of adventurers you can usually count on them for aid should you get knocked out.

Armor Skin:  For most vigilantes, light armor will probably serve.  But certain builds with less dependence on DEX might enjoy the extra mobility and stealth this talent affords you.

Chase Master:  If your game features the chase rules with any regularity, this talent could be pretty useful.  In such a case, take it early; it starts to pay dividends after level 10, but it’s more useful the earlier you grab it.  

Close the Gap:  This ability is nice, straightforward, and useful.  That said, if you’re planning on boosting your base speed and keep your Acrobatics skill topped off, you can probably just rely on your skills to avoid AoO’s rather than blowing a vigilante talent.  Likely more useful to more heavily armored avenger-types; compare to Sure-Footed.

Cunning Feint:  I wouldn’t take this before level 8, but it’s pretty solid afterwards, especially for stalkers.  

Environmental Weapon:  It’s a cute gimmick, and you could certainly build around it, but there’s just no substitution for a nice magic weapon (or even properly enhanced unarmed strikes).  It’s relatively open-ended and has the potential to reward creativity; gauge things by your GM’s playstyle and how often you expect to be caught without a weapon handy.

Expose Weakness:  If this gave you Improved Dirty Trick and was simply a rider benefit, I’d rate it higher.  Dirty Tricks are already somewhat hard to pull off effectively, and the benefit this talent gives you is already somewhat niche.

Favored Maneuver:  If you’re going down the combat maneuver route, this is a nice perk.

Inspired Vigilante:  Even with the restrictions, having an inspiration pool is a versatile and handy tool.  Vigilantes of all stripes can benefit here; skills, ability checks, attacks, saves… it only gets better with age.

Lethal Grace:  Weapon Finesse+.  Unless you’re going for full DEX to damage, this talent will save you a feat (or more) and provide a nice damage buff as well.

Living Shield:  If you’re going for a grapple build, this is a handy defensive ability; it’s a bit risky, but it’s got nice flavor.

Perfect Fall:  Sure, rings of feather falling are cheap, but this talent will save you a ring slot, and it’s really handy.  Stylish, too!

Perfect Vulnerability:  This ability is best for strikers or anyone that can attach a rider ability to the attack.  It’s especially nice for ranged stalkers.

Pull into the Shadows:  How likely are you to take Improved Drag?  This ability is a full-round action to use… and it only works against opponents unaware of you.  Can you use this in the surprise round?  All the limitations make this of dubious use.

Returning Weapon:  If you’re planning on doing a thrown weapon build, you’ve probably already taken it.  It’s certainly handy, but the wait for level 14 is a long one; you’ll need backup weapons / ammunition for most of your career.  Certainly saves you money, and it’s stylish too.

Rooftop Infiltrator:  A climb speed opens up amazing maneuverability, tactical options, and scouting potential.  

Shadow’s Sight:  Races with native darkvision can obviously skip this, but humans and their ilk will savor the stealth convenience of this talent.  You think darkness is your ally?

Shadow’s Speed:  Speed boosts are great for several reasons, both on a tactical level as well as improving skills and other talents.  The 10th level improvement is icing on the cake.

Shield of Blades:  Power Attack+.  This is for 2-hander vigilantes; dish out the pain and enjoy a nice boost to your defenses.

Shield of Fury:  If you instead want to sword-and-board it, this is Improved Shield Bash + Two Weapon Fighting (eventually) rolled into one.  Pretty sweet.

Silent Dispatch:  If your GM is a stickler for triggering general alarms, this talent can go a long way toward maintaining the element of surprise.  Sadly, the wording suggests that the vigilante must be working solo; your allies will still make lots of noise.  If you’re doing a stealth-heavy solo run, this could be a vital tool; otherwise, you’ll probably want to skip it or resort to magic (silence, etc.).

Strike the Unseen:  Three feats rolled into one talent (and decent feats, at that).  It’s more useful for stalkers.

Sure-Footed:  I prefer this to Close the Gap; it has in and out of combat utility, and the 8th level improvement can be a godsend.  

Surprise Strike:  While of more value to stalkers, this is still useful for anyone, especially if combined with Cunning Feint.  

Unexpected Strike:  Quickdraw is a useful feat, but it doesn’t have quite the shiny improvements of comparable talents; you might be better off spending an actual feat here.

Vital Punishment:  Early-access Vital Strike with some rider perks; it’s nice if you like the Vital Strike line, especially with a reach weapon or melee control build.

Avenger Specialization & Vigilante Talents

        The avenger vigilante is an in-your-face combat machine, sporting a full BAB.  You’re a little more fragile than most full BAB characters from a HD perspective, but you’ll have the weapons, armor, and combat perks to throw down with the best of them.  This is the archetype exemplified by heroes such as Captain America or Batman: solve problems by punching them.  

        While stalkers have more stealth and assassination perks, avengers are more accurate with all of their attacks, and they’re not worried about jumping through hoops to get precision damage.  Lots of stalker perks require surprise or a flat-footed target, but most of an avenger’s talents are always ‘on.’  You can often go less MAD with an avenger, relying less on DEX for defense and springing for medium or heavy armor.

        Avenger-only vigilante talents make your vigilante resemble something closer to a fighter, brawler, or barbarian.  They emphasize strength over skill, but most avengers will certainly wind up sneakier than their full BAB cousins.  There are fewer avenger-only talents than stalker-only talents, so avengers can often afford to take more of the general talents.

Combat Skill:  It’s hard to complain about a bonus combat feat.  Remember, many combat feats can be taken as their own vigilante talents, often with extra bonuses (Lethal Grace, Shield of Blades, etc.), but you have a few ‘virtual fighter levels’ when taking this talent, so you can get further along a feat tree as the need arises.  

Fist of the Avenger:  While the damage buff reduces the pain of not having a scaling damage dice increase like a monk or brawler, it’s generally more expensive to go the unarmed route; there’s simply more hoops to jump through than using a weapon.  Still, it’s iconic, and it never hurts to always be ‘armed.’

Heavy Training:  If you want to spring for heavy armor, you might as well take it through this perk.  If you take Armor Skin you’ll still be reasonably stealthy.

Mad Rush:  Basically pounce; ideally the target is dead and unable to take advantage of that -6 to your AC(!).  Hell yes!

Nothing Can Stop Me:  This ability is situational enough to be practically useless.  Still, it’s got fun flavor.

Signature Weapon:  Two solid feats rolled up into one talent.  Nice!

Sucker-Punch:  A rare avenger-only talent requiring surprise to use.  I’m not sure up to 4d6 extra nonlethal damage is really worth it, but if you like taking’em alive it certainly won’t hurt.

Unkillable:  Diehard++.  You can probably wait until the later levels to take it; the 12th level upgrade is the best perk of the bunch.  

Stalker Specialization & Vigilante Talents

        The vigilante has the chassis and skills of a sneaky, skill-monkey-ish class, so the stalker specialization is the baseline measurement of the class’s abilities as a whole (thus the black rating).  The stalker gets an ability called hidden strike.  It works like (unchained) sneak attack 90% of the time, only using d4’s instead of d6’s.  However, against targets completely unaware of the vigilante (or who believe they’re an ally), it does d8’s instead.  While they require more set-up for optimal combat performance, stalkers are no slouches; Stalker Sense alone is the envy of any avenger.

Stalker vigilantes are silent shadows in the night, relying on fear, surprise, and lethal precision.  They are exemplified by heroes such as Hawkeye, Daredevil, or Batman.

There are more stalker-only talents than avenger-only, but many of them can only be used with hidden strike, and are marked with an asterisk*.

Blind Spot:  So in order to prevent your foes from gaining a massive bonus to notice you anyway, you need to be negating the super-sense somehow, likely through magic?  Then what’s the point of this ability, really?  I don’t care how good your Stealth check is, a +20 (or more) on Perception checks to notice you might as well be auto-detect.

Evasive:  Evasion and eventually Improved Evasion for a single talent.  Looks like a solid deal to me; of course, in an intrigue-heavy game breath weapons and fireballs might be more rare, so be judicious in your selection here.

Foe Collision*:  Very circumstantial, for a small amount of nonlethal damage; it’s got fun flavor, but you’re probably best off skipping this.

Hide in Plain Sight:  Awesome for stealth, and extra great for snipers.  If you can get a reliable source of shadowy illumination from an ally or magic item, this is even better.

Leave an Opening*:  This is a nice ‘free attack’ ability, hopefully enough to silence a target quickly.  Best if you can use it with a flank, so the AoO also gets hidden strike damage.

Mighty Ambush*:  Great for taking prisoners or simply ganking unconscious foes, there’s little reason not to attempt this on every new opponent.

Rogue Talent:  You can only pick one, so stuff like Major Magic is out of the game.  Most rogue talents aren’t much better than vigilante talents; indeed, most are probably worse!  Expert Leaper, Stand Up, and Rogue Crawl are decent choices.

Sniper:  Also known as the 6th level vigilante talent for ranged stalkers.  Simply a must-have.

Stalker Sense:  Uncanny Dodge and it’s improved version are one thing, but the main draw here is the ability to always act on the surprise round.  There’s a reason diviner wizards are OP; take this.

Throat Jab*:  Not only is this great for stealth take-downs, but it’s also awesome for harassing enemy spellcasters.  

Twisting Fear:  This is an interesting option for Intimidate builds, essentially weaponizing demoralization.  It’s a nice little perk, but most such builds are there for the debuffing and control.

Up Close and Personal:  Melee stalkers will likely be using Acrobatics to get into flanks quite often; this talent gives you a free hidden strike en route!  Very handy.

Vigilante Archetypes

        Most of the vigilante archetypes function as ‘super specializations,’ often taking the place of the stalker or avenger specialization and auto-assigning many of your vigilante talents.  Some are practically different classes entirely, but I present them here to compare them with the avenger and stalker specializations.

Brute

        The Incredible Hulk might be one of the most powerful comic book characters ever, so you’d think an archetype that lets you simulate him would be great.  You would be very, very wrong.  It’s flavorful, but the mechanics do not offer enough reward to offset the needlessly harsh penalties it levies upon you.

Chaotic Vigilante:  Your Brute identity has to be Chaotic.  Sure.

Brutish Fortitude:  You lose your good Ref. and Will save, but gain a good Fort. save.  Okay…

Weapon and Armor Proficiency:  You lose medium armor and martial weapons, but gain Improved Unarmed Strike.  That’s… a downgrade.

Brute Form:  So instead of being a stalker or an avenger, you have this.  Full BAB while in brute form makes it comparable to the avenger…  And hey, large size!  That’s cool!  ...Kind of wish you got a STR or CON boost…

And then you start reading the lunacy that is this ‘class feature.’  Limited to 2 hours per day.  Fatigue and Exhaustion.  Unable to use CHA, DEX, or INT based skills (no Stealth!).  Damage your own equipment.  Involuntary transformation when under duress or attacked.  A risk of attacking your own allies or innocent bystanders.  What the hell?

        This isn’t worth being large size.  This wouldn’t be worth it if it gave you 9th level spells.  By this point you should be running away from this archetype screaming.  But if you must play Pathfinder Hulk, read on:

Brute Talents:

Awesome Blow:  Awesome Blow is actually a pretty useful feat.  You get some nice extra perks here.

Heavy Punches:  Monk damage scaling for your (large) fists.  You get Improved Unarmed Strike for free, so you can certainly get some nasty punching done.

Scale Surroundings:  Better than Rooftop Infiltrator.

Sizing Equipment:  You’ll need to take this eventually.

Total Destruction:  Throw Anything+.  Fun and flavorful, but you’ll often be better served with Awesome Blow.

Tear Them Apart:  Rend is about as useful as Vengeance Strike; at least you don’t need a long, drawn-out setup.  How the hell did you make it to level 20?

This archetype is terrible.  It’s okay as an NPC, or maybe for a goofy solo game.  Where’s the mutagen discoveries?  Where’s the alchemy overlap?  This should have been Mr. Hyde, dammit!  

Cabalist

        A spooky, blood-thirsty witch/vigilante hybrid, the cabalist is pretty cool.  You can replicate heroes like Blade or Constantine, getting all occult-up-ins.  You can’t tell me a dhampir cabalist wouldn’t be metal as hell.

        You can either focus more on the casting side of things, prioritizing INT and taking defensive or spell-enhancing vigilante talents, or you can do a pseudo-gish; the witch list has some solid buffs, and the Bond of Blood talent can turn you into a blood-drenched tank.  Shadow-stealth enhancements round out the package, making this a solid archetype for the more magically-inclined.

Class Skills, Weapon & Armor Proficiency:  You only get 4 skill points a level, but you’re an INT-based caster so it’s mostly a wash.  Losing Perception sucks, but you can get it back with a trait.  You also lose medium armor proficiency, but you can still cast in light armor.

Spellcasting:  INT-based prepared arcane spellcasting like a magus, but drawing from the witch spell list.  This nets you healing, tons of debuffs, and lots of other great spells.  You lose half your vigilante talents for the privilege; magic is a vital part of your arsenal.  Note that you can use your magic in either identity without risk of discovery; indeed, your healing spells could let you pass as a fake cleric or inquisitor.

Spill Blood:  Instead of hidden strike, you can inflict bleed damage.  DoT is not the best strategy in Pathfinder, but you can do spooky stuff to bleeding targets.  This replaces stalker or avenger specialization.

Cabalist Talents:

Bond of Blood:  If you plan on mixing it up in melee, this ability makes you surprisingly tanky, generating temp hp with consistency.  You also get a pretty ‘meh’ SLA at level 4.

Familiar:  Familiars are an extremely handy tool, both for normal adventuring and intrigue situations.  Your familiar also has two identities, so it can be disguised as another parrot or what-have-you.  Of course, taking the Improved Familiar feat might make this somewhat pointless, but several of the better ones can turn invisible anyway.  

Living Shadow:  The 1/day limitation is a pain, but shadow body is a higher level spell than you can natively cast.  It’s excellent for stealth and scouting, and after level 14 you can even make it a passable combat buff if you’ve got some decent offensive spells.  You’ll have to take it at 12th level if you’re interested, since you lose your 14th and 16th level vigilante talents.  You could wait until 18th level to take it, but few games get that far, let alone as high as 12th level in the first place.

Necromantic Focus:  It’s Spell Focus (Necromancy)+, throwing in some free spells known, most of them pretty solid.  

Shadow Jump:  If you don’t like the 1/day limitation on Living Shadow, you might instead wish to take this talent, which replicates the Shadowdancer’s ability of the same name.  Since you can divide it up into 10 ft. increments, you can potentially have multiple jumps per day.  Note that the earliest you can take this is 12th level, so you’ll have a minimum of 80 ft. to work with from the start.

Tattoo Chamber:  Extradimensional storage space is always a nice ace in the hole, and the fact that you can use wands while stored in your tattoo is delicious, savory gravy.  This gets better with age, eventually allowing up to seven stored items by level 18.  You can secretly transport weapons, evidence, doses of poison… go nuts.

Bloodbound Spell:  If you can manage to land bleed damage, this replacement for Startling Appearance gives you an edge when casting spells against a bleeding target.

Bloody Horror:  This is an interesting tool for fear stacking; you’ve got most of the best fear spells anyway, so if you can get some bleeding targets go nuts.

Shadowy Appearance:  I’ll take constant blur and a few free rounds of greater invisiblity over Stunning Appearance, thanks.

Losing 5 vigilante talents and either full BAB or pseudo-sneak attack sucks, but the power of 1-6 witch casting is actually quite potent.  Magical healing is great, but you can also grab awesome stuff like greater heroism, dominate person, confusion, scrying, and more.  The witch list has great spells for intrigue and social situations, as well as afflicting your enemies with curses, fear, and disease.  Perhaps the hardest thing for a cabalist is figuring out what vigilante talents to take, since the cabalist-only ones are pretty sweet (you can’t take them all!).  

Gunmaster

        This is the archetype that uses guns!  If you want guns, you can probably do that here!  You can use this archetype to recreate heroes like the Punisher, most things made by Rob Liefeld, or Batman.

Weapon and Armor Proficiency:  You lose martial weapons and medium armor, but gain firearm proficiency.  This is ultimately something of a downgrade, but for what you’re planning on doing here it’s basically a wash.

Gunmaster:  Instead of full BAB or pseudo-sneak-attack, you gain Gunsmithing, a starting firearm, and a scaling bonus to attacks and damage with guns.  It’s comparable to the full BAB of the avenger specialization, but losing access to some of the better avenger or stalker vigilante talents is the worst aspect of this change.  Can the gunmaster talents make up for it?

Gunmaster Talents:

Deadeye:  The limited times per day and range increment restrictions make this of questionable use.  It’s decent for snipers...

Death’s Shot:  Basically a capstone ability, so there’s not a whole lot to lose here.  Still, how often do you plan on critting with your gun?  Can you squeeze out 3 crits in a day?  Maybe?  Save or die!  Wee!

Gunmaster Initiative:  Initiative boosts are nice, but this is only a +2, and the other benefits are pretty ‘meh.’

Lightning Reload:  Reload speed is an issue for gun-jugglers.  While the limited uses per day kind of sucks, a free action reload can be pretty clutch.

Quick Clear: If you’re taking Amateur Gunslinger, just pick the Quick Clear gunslinger talent; as long as you have a point of grit, you don’t have to worry about the 1/day limitation from this vigilante talent.

Nimble:  Instead of resistance to Intimidate, you get a scaling dodge bonus to AC; that’s probably going to be more universally useful.

If you want to play a more social-oriented gun-haver type, I’d suggest a Mysterious Stranger gunslinger instead.  The unique gunmaster talents aren’t nearly as useful as the avenger or stalker vigilante talents, and like any gun-using class you’ll have to find a good source of bonus damage to help make your choice of weapon remain relevant.  Not to mention that loud gunshots are much less stealthy than quiet bows or crossbows (I know there’s no real mechanics that pertain to this, but still).  If you’re going for a sniper, you’re probably better off with a stalker vigilante.

The Amateur Gunslinger feat and the various Grit feats will be a no-brainer if you’re interested in this archetype, but I highly advise multiclassing or VMC to make this more palatable.

**Magical Child**

        Likely to be a popular option for the sheer crushing might of anime fandom alone, the Magical Child archetype turns the vigilante into something sparkly, twirling, sugoi, kawaii-to-the-point-of-diabetes madness.  It literally has a Transformation Sequence.  I know that nobody is reading the rest of this guide, because this is the only type of vigilante that will ever see play, ever.  *^_^*  :3  ;D

        Use this archetype to create heroes such as Sailor Moon, Matoi Ryuko, or my fan character, Princess Darkrain.  She’s totally awesome.  She’s half-demon, half-angel, and she has a demon wing and an angel wing, and she dual-wields katanas, but one is made of light, and the other is made of fire, but it’s cold fire, and nobody understands her but she’s totally cool, guys, for cereal.

        On a more serious note, you can use this archetype to play a spontaneous arcane caster vigilante of any age.  It’s a vigilante/unchained summoner hybrid, although you’re nowhere near as powerful as the later class.

Class Skills & Proficiencies:  Without INT-based casting, you can’t offset the hit you take to your skill points.  You also lose access to class skills like the Cabalist, but gain a few relevant Knowledges and Spellcraft.  You also lose access to medium armor, but can cast in light armor.

Spellcasting:  Like the cabalist, you lose half your vigilante talents but gain unchained summoner spontaneous casting.  The unchained summoner spell list has lots of solid spells that make up for your lost talents quite adequately.  You’ve got access to nice party buffs like haste, transportation spells, summoning, and other tricks that mundane vigilantes will never have access to.

Transformation Sequence:  Since it doesn’t bother to explain the mechanical significance of your transformation being ‘more noticeable,’ this is a straight upgrade, allowing you to change identities in half the time, and 3000% more cuteness.  If your GM assigns some really easy Perception check DC to notice it, then you’ll obviously have to be careful if you give a crap about your secret identity, but let’s be honest, you didn’t pick this archetype for subtlety.  

Animal Guide:  A super-familiar is a very powerful tool, certainly worth trading in pseudo-sneak-attack or full BAB for.  The stealth penalty to this ability is that it locks you out of all but the generalist vigilante talents.  Having a stealthy spy or flank buddy at your beck and call is really handy in intrigue or combat scenarios, however.

Staunch Ally:  Your familiar can use your startling presence and vengeance strike class features.  Hooray!

        This archetype has a lot going for it, but it’s something of a mixed bag.  The hit to skill points is nasty, especially in a more intrigue-focused game, and you’ll be heavily reliant on your spells, familiar, and feat selection to determine your effectiveness in a fight.  Most generalist vigilante talents don’t offer much for a dedicated caster, so your few talents are likely best spent on bonus feats, mobility enhancers, or Inspiration.

While your familiar is a useful tool, and potentially quite potent in a fight, it’s not nearly as rugged or expendable as a proper eidolon.  I’m not well versed on the various familiar archetypes, but they can help you out in combat or… magic item production, I think?  There are guides out there that detail more information concerning familiars, so check them out!  Just be aware that one of your main strengths is also something of a liability.

Jokes aside, I’m leaning towards a black rating, bordering on orange for this archetype as a whole; it’s a bit unfocused, and lacks the raw power of a true summoner or even a bard.  You only get general vigilante talents to choose from, while the cabalist and warlock get a few custom options.  Considering the cabalist and warlock can get a familiar on top of other toys, it feels like the magical child is a little shortchanged.  

Mounted Fury

You want to do a mounted build?  You’ve got everything you need with this archetype!  Rock!  Use the Mounted Fury to recreate heroes similar to The Lone Ranger… old-timey knight Spawn…  Aquaman?  Oh, and Batman.

Class Skills:  You lose UMD and Swim but gain Handle Animal.  You can get UMD back with a trait; it’s a wash.

Thorough Change:  No changing identities time reduction for you!  You’ve got a horse to disguise as well!  HIGH HO SILVER!!

Mount:  You get a noble steed animal companion with extra bonuses, similar to a druid or cavalier’s animal companion.  Note that you can double any teamwork feats you take in place of vigilante talents, and your mount shares said feats.  You also can take avenger vigilante talents, which is sweet!  Just don’t let your horsey die, because it sucks in the interim.  

Furious Charge & Mighty Charge:  Your 6th and 12th level vigilante talents are spoken for, but they’re pretty good!  You’ll be doing a lot of charging on your mount, and these make you better at it.

Vengeance Strike:  You get an extra limitation to an already limited capstone ability, but you’re probably sovereign-glued to your saddle by level 20 anyway.  

        This archetype is basically just a mount build template for interested avengers.  It’s totally solid, with the only caveat being that most intrigue campaigns probably don’t have room for horses.  If you’re looking to play a more theatrical, robin-hood style cavalier, this is certainly an option, although you’ll miss full BAB and d10 HD.

        A mount is something of a money sink, since you’ll need to protect it with barding and equipment, but if you can reliably pull off Spirited Charges you’ll be too busy murdering things to care.

Psychometrist

        Not quite a spellcasting archetype, the psychometrist gains spooky occult powers and access to mystic implements.  It’s a vigilante/occultist hybrid, giving it some unique options that can actually be pretty cool.  As the ‘pychic vigilante’ you can use this archetype to recreate heroes like Jean Grey, but it also seems to suggest you can flavor the implement powers as unique gizmos or gadgets that work only for the vigilante, allowing you to replicate something closer to Iron Man or Batman.

Class Skills:  You re-shuffle some Knowledges, but you keep your full 6 skill points a level.  Hooray!

Occult Awareness:  Wow, I never thought losing a social talent would be a downgrade, but occult skill unlocks are actually less useful than most of the better social talents.  Still, you’re trading something mostly meant for fluff and flavor for something that’s basically just fluff and flavor.

Implements and Focus Powers:  You replace 4 of your vigilante talents with occultist implement school focus powers.  You can’t pick Conjuration (which is fine, since most of its focus powers suck), and you can’t cast spells or use magic items without UMD, and you also don’t get resonant powers.  That said, there are lots of really great focus powers out there.  They are decidedly more supernatural than regular vigilante talents, and are limited in uses per day based on your mental focus pool (which is smaller than an actual occultist), but the best options are at least the equal of any vigilante talents you’re giving up.  Necromancy can net you disposable minions or spies, transmutation gives you access to flight, haste, and other goodies, illusion can provide invisibility, and divination can give you an awesome spying tool in the form of the Mind Eye power.  Check out my Occultist Guide for more ideas.

Object Reading:  Lose Unshakable for the ability to auto-ID magic items and read secret message imparted with psychic magic.  Sounds like a decent trade to me.

Psychometric Strike:  Technically an upgrade to your capstone, but unlikely to make a huge difference.

        The psychometrist is a good compromise for vigilantes interested in magical powers without sacrificing skills or too many vigilante talents.  Your access to implement powers is slow, and some of your decisions will be painful ones: do you take Mind Eye at level 6, or Quickness (or Unseen)?  Another nice aspect of psychometrist is that you retain your avenger or stalker specialization, so you can take focus powers that complement your fighting style.  

Warlock

        The wizard/vigilante hybrid archetype, the warlock can be used to replicate heroes such as Dr. Strange, Scarlet Witch, or any hero that fires energy blasts from their hands.  (Although a regular wizard could probably do it better)

        Unlike the cabalist which provides powers that synergize well with many traditional fighting styles, the warlock gets a gimmick-power that ultimately lacks the support it needs to be truly useful.  Access to one of the most powerful spell lists in the game is the main draw here.

Class Skills, Weapon and Armor Proficiency:  Just like the cabalist, you take a hit to your skills per level and class skill list, losing Perception (rats!).  Again, INT-based casting will help make up for that.  You also can cast in light armor, but lose medium armor proficiency.  No biggy.

Spellcasting:  1-6 INT-based prepared casting from arguably the best spell list in the game, at the cost of half your vigilante talents.  There are nearly endless opportunities here.  Stealth, information gathering, combat, trickery and guile… It’s a powerful trade off to be sure.  I could see some good synergy with necromancy’s fear spells and your startling presence class feature.

Mystic Bolts & Piercing Bolts:  Instead of a vigilante specialization, you receive an at-will blasty… thing.  There are lots of problems with mystic bolts, sadly.  You can never increase their range beyond 30 ft., so they’re not really an option for a true ranged build.  You could focus on melee touch attacks (post level 5), which is an interesting strategy, but you have to jump through a lot of hoops to keep your damage output even slightly competitive.  You’ll need to take the Arcane Striker talent for the Arcane Strike damage boost and the rider effects like Flaming or Holy, but at level 20 you’re looking at 3d6+10 damage per attack.  Hasted, and with the full Two-Weapon Fighting tree, you could theoretically get seven of these little touch attacks off in a round.  This looks okay on paper, but it’s mostly energy damage; the 2nd level spell resist energy shuts you down hard, and you’re at the apex of your power!  There’s a lot that needs clarification here.

        If Mystic Bolts receive any more support, like better scaling damage dice or a way to increase their range, they might be worth looking into.  As it stands, a kineticist (or 3.5 Warlock) you are not.  It’s not even worth using until level 3; before then you’re better off with a bow or crossbow (and with the right feats, a bow laughs at Mystic Bolts mercilessly).  You know things are bad when you’re probably better off using firearms instead of these things.

 Warlock Talents

Arcane Striker:  If you plan on using Mystic Bolts at all, this is a required talent.  If you plan on mixing things up with an actual weapon, this is a really nice package for you, essentially Arcane Strike+.  If you’re focusing more on being a caster and skill monkey, you can probably skip this though.

Elemental Armor:  The main benefit here is that it’s essentially an always-on buff while in your vigilante identity.  The problem is it’s not the greatest buff in the world, but if you’re planning on fighting in melee it’s not too bad.

Familiar:  A familiar is a fantastic boon for intrigue, stealth, and even combat as I’ve stated before.

Social Simulacrum:  This ability is cool on paper, but it’s not the most elegant thing in the world.  The first problem is that it takes 1 hour to create, but only lasts 4 hours itself.  If you plan on using it, you’ll have to get your timing down perfectly without raising several questions.  People are bound to get suspicious if they witness you melt into a pile of snow.  The second problem is that you have no direct control over your simulacrum, so it becomes quite the liability.  While you’re off running around, your simulacrum could be accidentally making enemies, getting you into debt, or causing general confusion.  It’s probably safer to skip this ability and take something reliable.

Tattoo Chamber:  Just as useful for you as the cabalist.

        While the Spill Blood ability of the cabalist is a nice-if-forgettable little perk, the Mystic Bolts of the warlock are something of an albatross hanging from the neck.  You either take the time and energy to make them somewhat useful and still wind up sucking compared to nearly everything else, or you just ignore them.  If you want to play a vigilante with arcane spells and don’t like the witch spell list or the wackiness of the magical child, the warlock can certainly fit the bill; it’s hard to argue with 1-6 sorcerer/wizard spell list casting.  Just know that a magus out-fights you natively, and a wizard out-casts you… leaving you with a strange psuedo-wizard/social rogue hybrid.  

Wildsoul

        If you want a dash of animal-themed abilities for your vigilante, look no farther than the wildsoul!  Essentially three ‘prepackaged’ vigilante talents to slap on your stalker or avenger, you have three choices: spider-themed, falcon-themed, or bear-themed.  Use this archetype to recreate heroes like Spiderman, Birdman, or… Bear… man?  Manbearpig?

Arachnid

Heightened Senses:  Stalker Sense is a really good vigilante talent, so hooray!

Shoot Webs:  This ability starts out okay.  Entangling foes is a decent debuff, but tanglefoot bags are already starting to lose their potency by level 6.  

Web Specialist:  At 12th level, you get a climb speed, which is nice, and the ability to produce 50 ft. lengths of ‘rope.’  You probably have magic items that do more for you than this ability at this point.  (A robe of infinite twine is only 1,000 gp.)  You’re starting to regret not having your regular talents to pick from…

Web Master:  Look, I get it.  Swinging around the city with your webs is cool.  It’s probably the reason you wanted to be Not-Spiderman in the first place.  But level 18?  Really?  You have to wait until level 18 to do this.  This is basically worse than a fly speed.  Why can’t you do this at… level 6?  

Falconine

Soft Landing:  The Perfect Fall talent is pretty solid, so no real loss here.

Eagle Eye:  Half your level competence bonus to Perception checks is pretty solid.

Take to the Air:  Hey, you’re a little late to the party, but an Ex fly speed is always helpful.  Especially nice for archers and snipers.

Deadly Dive:  This ability is pretty silly.  The fact that it provokes an AoO from your target is super lame.  Only really of note for a melee build, the fact that this doesn’t arrive until level 18 means it’s really unlikely to ever see the light of day in the first place.

Ursine

Deadly Claws:  If you want to do a natural attack focused build, this starts as a decent foundation…

Bear Jaws:  More natural attacks is good, but this replaces an entire vigilante talent… can’t Deadly Claws just scale with level?

Thick Hide:  A late-game, crappily scaling natural armor bonus… yay?

Bear Form:  At level 18, you get a more limited version of something the druid’s been doing better than you since the single-digit levels.  Unlikely to ever see play, but I’d still rather just have a regular vigilante talent.

        While this can make for fun flavor or an interesting gimmick, you’re usually better off with your regular old vigilante talent selection.  Of the three, Falconine is at least passable, but the wait for level 12 is a long one.  

Zealot

        Vigilantes receive a divine magic option with this archetype.  It’s almost like an inquisitor archetype, since it can replicate several inquisitor class features and uses the inquisitor spell list.  I can’t really think of a lot of divine-power superheroes…  This archetype could be interesting for a villain, or anyone practicing a religion that is forbidden or secret.

Alignment:  You have alignment restrictions, which is common for divine classes.  The social identity can stray a bit farther, but you can’t cast spells in your social identity if your alignment is more than one step from your god’s.  

Skills:  You don’t lose Perception as a class skill, which is nice, but without INT-based casting your reduced skills per level will definitely make you feel stretched thin.  Note that the inquisitor is sitting pretty at 6 skill points per level…

Weapon Proficiency:  You get your deity’s favored weapon (or Improved Unarmed Strike).  Your simple and martial weapon proficiency covers most of this already, but you might get a nice perk here.

Spellcasting:  You know the drill: half your vigilante talents for inquisitor spellcasting.  It’s a solid list, to be sure, but the potency of the inquisitor itself is reliant on the package of judgement and bane.  Can your vigilante talents help close the gap?

Aura:  While it only applies to your vigilante identity, it’s just another potential liability in an intrigue campaign.

Inquisition:  Even the best inquisitions aren’t the equal of stalker or avenger specializations.  Most inquisitions are worse than the powers provided by domains…  Still, there are a few diamonds in the rough.  Consult an inquisitor guide for thoughts and opinions; for my two coppers, most of them are pretty crappy.

Zealot Talents:

Channel Energy:  Even though you’re 4 levels behind the cleric, the extra healing this provides can be a nice fallback, especially considering your limited spell slots.

Discern Lies:  A ‘filler’ class feature for the inquisitor takes up an entire vigilante talent selection for you.  Get used to that.  While appropriate for an intrigue game, the target gets a save, so it’s hardly foolproof.

Empower Symbol:  While this has great duration (apparently you can just have this on all day?), this mostly deals with the undead, either debuffing or buffing them.  The value of this ability thus depends on how often you expect to face the undead.  Animate dead isn’t on the inquisitor spell list, so you probably won’t need to screw around with this.

Stalwart:  You’ve got a poor Fort. save, and thanks to your WIS-based casting you’ll have a better Will save than most vigilantes.  Most Will save things are all-or-nothing (with the exception of fear effects), so the benefit of this ability is likely to be dependent on good luck.  

Stern Gaze:  You can take a social talent that gives you bonuses to these skills.

Zealot Smite:  Finally, something good!  While you don’t get the AC boost, this is basically a paladin’s smite evil ability.  The only downside is it makes you that much more MAD, since it’s based off of CHA.

        Man…  I think I’d rather just play an inquisitor.  Inquisitors get most of the bells and whistles of this archetype, while keeping 6 skill points per level and rocking stuff like judgements, bane, and bonus teamwork feats.  Inquisitors are a strong choice in a social or intrigue game to boot…  

        It’s not that this archetype is unplayable: zealot smite and inquisitor casting are strong abilities.  But the cabalist can heal, and their unique vigilante talents give you a lot more to work with.  It’s also the most MAD of the caster archetypes, requiring investment in WIS and CHA.

Vigilante Feat Selection

        Below I’m going to go over worthwhile feats for various types of vigilantes, as well as take a closer look at several of the new feats introduced in Ultimate Intrigue.  Don’t forget that lots of foundational feats can be acquired through vigilante talents, many of them with extra perks tacked on.  Weapon Finesse, Vital Strike, Blind-Fight, Weapon Focus… the list goes on.  Consult the section on Vigilante talents for details.

        It’s worth noting that if you plan on using a certain feat regularly in your social identity, you actually don’t want to acquire it through a vigilante talent.  Using it in your social guise would give an observer a chance to see through your disguise.  So if your social identity is a talented swordsman, consider picking up the legitimate feats and pick other vigilante talents instead.

        You’ll notice that a lot of feats from Ultimate Intrigue introduce mechanics for things that might have previously been covered with simple RP.  Depending on the nature of the game, this still might be the case.  I recommend GMs and players look at these feats and discuss whether these should just be new uses for extant skills.  If there are any real diamonds in the rough though, I’ll point them out here.

General Feats

Agent of Fear (UI):  Crappy prereq aside, how often do you plan on repeatedly subjecting someone to Frightening or Stunning Appearance?  I imagine most of those victims will end up dead or in jail.  Very circumstantial, to the point of likely uselessness.

Blustering Bluff (UI):  All this feat really does is lower the DC for telling certain kinds of lies by 5, but the target eventually realizes they’ve been duped.  This is really only useful for your vigilante identity, since you could then switch back to social and feign ignorance when the target gets upset.  Very circumstantial.

Brilliant Planner (UI):  I really want to like this feat!  Basically you spend some gold, and then you can use that gold to pull an item or service out of your ass.  The biggest problem is that it takes 10 minutes to ‘enact’ the plan.  Since you’re carrying around extra weight already, I don’t understand why you couldn’t just produce an object on the spot.  Since this feat essentially abstracts the process of planning the perfect contingency, I guess the 10 minute buffer is there to represent a service arriving late or other factors that might stretch believability.

        Again, it’s a little fuzzy, but this feat could potentially greatly reward creative thought.  50 gp per character level is a lot of money in a community, particularly for mundane services.  You could arrange a stagecoach or small boat to arrive as a getaway vehicle.  You could have a troupe of mummers start a fake riot as a distraction.  You could arrange for a crew to arrive and cleanup a crime scene.  The possibilities are nearly limitless for the service aspect of this feat.  Could a ‘service’ represent a bribe?  Could you spend your fund to prevent the city watch from showing up at a suspiciously convenient time?  

        The item aspect is much more restrictive, since any item must weigh 10 pounds or less.  It reminds me of the Deep Pockets ability from the Pathfinder Chronicler prestige class; potentially very handy, although a Handy Haversack and some forethought can accomplish much the same thing, and without a 10 minute activation buffer.

Call Truce (UI):  This should just be an RP option.  You’re still a far cry from the Diplomancers of old; way too many things can shatter the fragile truce this feat allows you to mechanically produce, and there’s a clause that basically says ‘if the GM doesn’t want this feat to work, it won’t.’  Don’t forget the Persuasive prereq!  This isn’t worth a feat slot.

Careful Sneak, Exquisite Sneak (UI):  This is a more restrictive version of the Armor Skin vigilante talent.  If you want to save on a talent, this feat could serve in a pinch, but it’s really only of value if you’re springing for medium armor, since the penalty on the better light armors is negligible at worst.  Taking the second feat in this chain is pretty indulgent; you might just want to invest in a shadow enhancement on the armor itself.

Cat’s Fall (UI):  If you’re taking the Perfect Fall vigilante talent, obviously you can skip this.  A ring of feather falling is pretty cheap, too…

City Sprinter (UI):  A crappy prereq for fiddly bonuses.  The ability to move through crowds unimpeded is cool, but only the densest of urban campaigns will see crowds with any regularity.  

Confabulist (UI):  A pseudo-retry for a failed Bluff check could be potentially valuable for a vigilante, especially when covering your secret identity.  Minimum level 9 before you can take this, sadly, but at least there’s no bad feat prereqs.

Criminal Reputation (UI):  This feat is more restrictive than Persuasive.  It provides the same bonus as Persuasive, but doesn’t stack with Persuasive.  You can take Persuasive at level 1, and this at minimum level 5.  So… just take Persuasive?

Cutting Humiliation (UI):  While the duration is okay, this should really be covered by RP.  The fact that the target can remove the penalty on their own is a mark against it, and the chance of being humiliated yourself if you fail the check by 5 or more is another.  If you’re very Intimidate focused and want something slightly less disruptive in more refined social encounters, this is okay.

Deft Catcher (UI):  The DCs against spells or disarm/steal attempts will be ludicrously high; you’ll need that Skill Focus (Sleight of Hand).  If you’re worried about being disarmed, maybe invest in a locked gauntlet, or carry a backup weapon?

Drunkard’s Recovery (UI):  If nobody has a healing potion, and nobody knows the level 0 spell stabilize, and nobody has a Heal check worth a damn, then maybe this feat might save your life once during a campaign.  You could make sure your familiar knows where your hip flask is, I guess?  This isn’t worth a feat slot.

Expeditious Sleuth (UI):  Assuming you have the time to take 20, saving half that time probably isn’t going to make much of a difference, nor will a +2.  Inspiration is a decent choice for a vigilante talent, but this feat isn’t worth your time.

Feign Curse (UI):  Most combat-focused vigilantes will likely have better things to do, but this is a noteworthy feat for any character that wants an interesting combat option (or for certain non-combat encounters).  Yes, Deceitful is an annoying prereq, but this emulates one of the better witch hexes (misfortune), which is pretty cool.  Better against the gullible and non-casters, but the DC to foil it should remain pretty competetive.

Fool Magic (UI):  It’s unlikely your alternate identity will be a different ‘race,’ and you already get a different alignment if you wish, but this is an interesting feat for dedicated masters of disguise that happen to need some very specific magic items.  I just really doubt this feat will ever see the light of day.


Insightful Advice (UI):  This is more the bard’s schtick, but it’s nice for team players or heists.  It should work on your familiar if you have one from an archetype.  

Intoxicating Flattery (UI):  This is slightly better than Cutting Humiliation, as lowering Will saves can help set someone up for your allies’ spells and abilities as well.  

Manipulative Agility (UI):  This is for characters interested in feinting but that don’t want to invest in Cha.  

Nerve-Racking Negotiator (UI):  You already have an Intimidate repercussions safety net in the form of your dual identities.  Even if you’re worried about upsetting people, the Will DC is based on your ranks alone, and thus won’t be too difficult.

Persuasive Bribery (UI):  This feat is potentially of interest to casters using planar binding, but if you’re going to try bribing someone and they get ‘offended,’ it’s pretty easy to just increase the bribe substantially.  ‘Everybody has a price.’  You could likely accomplish much of this with the more interesting Brilliant Planner feat.

Quick Favor (UI):  Once per day limitation on a specific use of a skill, and all it does is save you some time.  Meh.

Sense Assumptions (UI):  This is comparable to Blustering Bluff, but a bit safer to use.  Still a bit niche, and probably should just be an expanded use of Sense Motive.

Sense Relationships (UI):  This should be another expanded use of Sense Motive.  Restrictions and a crappy prereq push this into the red.

Street Smarts (UI):  This is a prereq feat for several feats from UI.  It’s a +2/+2 feat like Alertness, so most folks should probably skip it.

Subtle Enchantments (UI):  If you’re a magic-using archetype, or simply an enchantment-focused caster in a social or intrigue heavy game, this feat is potentially useful.  Lots of prereqs though.

Superior Scryer (UI):  The benefits of this feat are mostly ‘fiddly.’  There are work-arounds for most of the issues this feat tries to help solve, and you don’t have to spend a feat to get them (namely higher level divinations and items).

Telepathy Tap (UI): This is a trick for higher level magical vigilantes or other characters with regular access to telepathic communication, such as psychics.  Pretty cool if you can pull it off, but its usefulness depends on the sort of creatures you encounter.  If you’re fighting a lot of demons for instance, this is great, but in most cities this will be limited to other magic users, and thus much more rare.

Threatening Negotiator (UI):  All of those prereqs, and for what?  Perhaps I’m biased, but NPCs you’re intimidating are often somewhat disposable in nature; who cares what they’re doing 2d6 hours from now, if they’re even alive at all?  

Walking Sleight (UI):  This feat removes a substantial penalty, but I’ve rarely seen Sleight of Hand used in any game, period.  

Willing Accomplice (UI):  This could probably just pass for a house rule.  Odd that it isn’t a teamwork feat.  Vigilantes don’t want to take this, but cohorts or other allies could help a vigilante friend out.

Toughness:  For vigilantes expecting to mix it up in melee, this helps offset your d8 HD.

Combat Feats

Bludgeoner (UC)/Enforcer (APG): The classic combo. Bludgeoner lets you deal nonlethal damage with bludgeoning weapon with no penalty, Enforcer lets you make free Intimidate checks when you deal nonlethal damage with a bludgeoning weapon.  Excellent for a demoralizing build.

Dazzling Display:  Another great Intimidate feat for mass demoralizing.  

Deadly Aim:  Any ranged vigilante will want to pick this up after acquiring the fundamentals.

Furious Focus (APG):  This is a nice companion to Power Attack.

Improved Initiative:  Getting the drop on the enemy is vital, especially for stalker vigilantes.

Lunge:  Another solid melee feat, great for lock-down builds.

Piranha Strike (PC: SLC):  Going pure Dex is a bit tough for vigilantes, but if you’re dumping Str entirely this is the next best thing to Power Attack.

Point-Blank Shot (Rapid Shot, Precise Shot, Manyshot):  These are the foundational feats for an archer build, and there are many more solid archery-related feats beyond these.  

Power Attack:  The king of melee feats is a solid addition to any STR-based build.  Grab it if you aren’t getting it through your vigilante talents.

Slashing Grace / Fencing Grace / Dervish Dance (ACG / ACO / ISWG):  It’s a little tough to meet the prereqs, but these feats allow you to get DEX to damage.  Unfortunately, you can’t do two-weapon fighting with these, or use a shield bigger than a buckler.

Weapon Finesse:  DEX-based melee is likely the preferred combat method for stalker vigilantes.

Weapon Focus:  It’s a prereq for Dazzling Display, and it’s not bad for your ¾ BAB chassis.

Should I Play a Vigilante?

        For all of its fun flavor, you can actually replicate a lot of what the vigilante class does with other classes, often times more successfully.  Below I’m going to ponder some of these class comparisons, look at the pros and cons, and offer some suggestions.

Stalker Vigilante vs. Social Rogue or Investigator

        The vigilante has better proficiencies, and in general vigilante talents are better than rogue talents.  A vigilante will be better at disguise with minimal investment compared to a rogue.

        However, the rogue’s sneak attack does more damage consistently than the stalker’s hidden strike.  Unchained rogue gets the awesome Debilitating Injury ability, which helps offset their ¾ BAB.  Rogues have more skills and a slightly better class skill list.

        This one’s something of a toss up.  A rogue that invests in Disguise can pretty much emulate a stalker vigilante with minimal difference, and is less likely to get odd looks from the GM.

        A vigilante can ape investigators to a certain extent (aside from alchemy), and they don’t have to wait quite as long to ‘come online’ in a fight.  If you picture your character with more of an Int focus, you might be better served with a more social investigator.

Avenger Vigilante vs. Cavalier, Paladin, Ranger, and Fighter

        Full BAB classes don’t fare too well in social situations, usually.  Cavaliers have a bit of built-in intrigue compatibility, with better skills, teamwork feats, and bard-esque abilities.  Paladins have Cha synergy.  However, both paladins and cavaliers have baggage in the form of their moral code and knightly order tenets.  

        Fighters have a tough time in social settings, barring certain archetypes.  If you want to play a non-magic melee character in a more social or intrigue game, the avenger vigilante is an attractive alternative to the fighter.  Their baggage is what you make of it, and while you have smaller HD you keep similar weapon proficiencies.  Vigilante talents can match fighter feat progression, and you get the social talents on top of them.  Also, your skills blow the fighter’s out of the water.

        Rangers are perhaps the closest comparison to the avenger vigilante.  There are certain archetypes (particularly in UI) that make the traditionally rustic ranger better suited to intrigue.  An urban ranger with favored enemy (human) will pretty much wipe the floor with an avenger vigilante in most cases.

Warlock, Cabalist, and Magical Child Vigilantes vs. Bard, Mesmerist, and Full Casters (Sorcerer, Wizard, etc.)

        While the magical vigilante archetypes have some fun gimmicks, most proper casters will blow a vigilante out of the water.  Bards are obscene in social situations, as are mesmerists.  Any enchanter or illusionist will likewise have better tricks up their sleeves than a magical vigilante, and the sorcerer’s Cha focus can’t be denied.  

        I will say that for a social game, I might choose a cabalist vigilante over a magus.  Magi are pretty much only interested in destruction, but a cabalist has a better balance of social tricks and fighting potency if you build them correctly.
