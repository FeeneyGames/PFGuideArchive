What is Demonic Obedience?

 

A Demonic Obedience is a ritual or activity that corresponds with a particular Demon Lord’s area(s) of influence.  By taking the feat and performing these rituals on a daily basis*, practitioners receive additional powers from their demonic patron.

These powers come in the form of three Boons: the first at 12 Hit Die, the second at 16 Hit Die, and the third at 20 Hit Die.

It’s here that the Demoniac comes in.  Assuming that you enter into it at the earliest opportunity, you will instead get these Boons at 10, 13, and 16 Hit Die, respectively, by taking levels in Demoniac.

 

*quick note: unless you are a Demoniac, you don’t face any consequences for not doing your Demonic Obedience every day – other than not gaining its benefits.

 

 

If you are familiar with the Deific Obedience feat (and its associated PrCs), this follows most of the same design philosophy – except with more variable quality.  If you are not familiar with it, I would heartily recommend taking a look at Walter’s Guide to Deific Obediences, which is really cool.

What is the Demoniac?

 

In-universe, the Demoniac is a particularly devoted servant of a Demon Lord: the most powerful type of Demon.

Considered to be on even footing with minor deities, these personifications of evil are able to grant spells to those who worship them – but that’s not all:  some of their followers go one step further, taking part in vile rituals and practices to grant them even more power, willingly damning their souls to bring themselves closer to the dread masters they serve.  Such practices are otherwise known as Demonic Obediences.

 

In-game, The Demoniac is a 10-level Prestige Class with 9/10 spellcasting progression, and some unique benefits revolving around the Demonic Obedience feat.  We’ll talk about it some more later.

Demon Lords

 

Just a little note before we begin: some of these Obediences – read: most of them – are not for the squeamish.  They, as said in the feat’s description, require you to “physically defile yourself, others, or holy objects, in order to prove your blasphemous devotion to a Demon Lord”.

While I have a pretty strong stomach for this sort of thing, other people do not – as much as possible, try to keep in mind the age range and maturity of the players at your table before picking up this feat/PrC.

 

If you would like to look at the specifics of each Demon Lord’s Boons and Obediences (as well as their Domains, Subdomains, favored weapon and more) I would recommend this page on the Pathfinder Community site.

 

Also, in addition to going over the technical aspects of each Obedience and the strengths and weaknesses of the given powers, I will also have a section detailing the Consequences of performing each Obedience.

 

Without further ado, let us begin our journey into the Abyss.

 

 

Abraxas - Master of the Final Incantation

Demon Lord of forbidden lore, magic, and snakes

Obedience: You have to hit yourself with a small whip or tree branch, chanting while you do so.  Nothing too extreme or onerous.  Gain a +4 bonus to saves against Charm effects and spells involving the written word.

Consequences: At worst, you’re looking at a couple of cuts and bruises, as well as some funny looks from your neighbors.  By comparison to the others, that’s really good.

 

1st Boon: Decent utility spells, but nothing too exciting.  I don’t think I’ve ever heard of anyone using Illusory Script, but it’s solid and thematic, so I’ll take it.

2nd Boon: Dang.  So, yes, this boon does have some limitations – it’s mind-affecting, it’s a little bit random, the DC is based off of CHA, and you have to be next to the target – but if it connects, it’s pretty terrifying.  This is the sort of thing I want to see from a 2nd tier Boon.

3rd Boon: …And this is what I want to see from a 3rd tier Boon.  At the time you get this, most enemies will be spellcasters (or be around them), so something to potentially strip an enemy of their defensive AND offensive buffs, AND deal a little bit of fire damage to them in the process? Yes, please.

 

Overall: Abraxas is a solid, thematic choice – especially if you’re a Charisma-based caster.  We’re off to a good start.

 

 

Aldinach – She of the Six Venoms

Demon Lord of sand, scorpions, and thirst

Obedience: Lie on some sand, pray, and eat at least one live scorpion.  This one is situational – either you have the necessary components (which means, most likely, that you are adventuring in a desert.), or you don’t.  Your reward, should you be able to claim it, is a +4 bonus to saves against poison caused by Vermin, and on effects that cause fatigue or exhaustion.

Consequences: Assuming that you can get the required stuff for it, your best case scenario is that you will have sand in your everything.  Worst case scenario (under a particularity unforgiving GM): you might have to make a Fortitude save every morning against the venom you are digesting – unless you can find a variety of scorpion that isn’t venomous, in which case, good job.  I still don’t envy you.

 

1st Boon: A well-rounded group of spells, you get a little bit of everything – buffs, debuffs, and damage.

2nd Boon: “Well, that was unexpected.”  That sentence accurately describes both my feelings towards this ability, and one of the many thoughts going through the heads of your enemies when they see the corpse of one of their fallen brethren turn into a Fiendish Giant Scorpion – that then attacks them.  It’s only once per day, but free pseudo-summoning is free pseudo-summoning.

3rd Boon: Not bad, but not great.  It’s only once per day, and requires a Fortitude save (with a Con-based DC), but being able to piggyback a Stun onto any effect that deals damage – or Stagger them if they make their save – is pretty good.  It fares particularly well against squishy casters & other enemies with low Strength, as a high roll on the STR damage effect could cripple or incapacitate them.

 

Overall: Decent, if situational - If you know that you will have ready access to enough sand to lie on, a fresh scorpion to munch on every day, and that you will be facing lots of vermin and long travel days though the desert, then this Scorpion Queen becomes almost Blue.

 

Andirifkhu – The Razor Princess

Demon Lord of illusions, traps, and knives

Obedience: You have to either A: torture a living creature of your size or smaller on a device that involves blades or spikes, or B: torture a bound creature of any size with a knife.  Oh, and the creature has to live through the torture, and then die at the end of the hour.  Ouch.  Similar to Aldinach’s Obedience above, completing this activity on a daily basis will require a bit of forethought – In my mind, your best case scenario is that your DM allows you to complete this obedience with a nearby alley rat, or even a suitably sturdy insect*.  For all that, you gain a +4 bonus on saves against illusions, and an equal bonus to AC against traps that deal slashing damage.

 

*Or, if you have the stomach for it, you could go to a nearby shop that sells animals (assuming you are in a town or settlement) and procure one for the next day.  Here’s list of prices; I won’t judge.

Consequences: Well, for one, you’ve just killed something.  While a couple of dead rats or spiders might not raise an alarm, anything bigger than that will probably cause some problems if seen.  In addition, you may have to wash off the blood from your morning activities before you go out for the day – though adventurers have an easier time explaining stuff like that than most folk.

 

1st Boon: These spells are pretty flavorful, and Snare seems like it could be funny, but they don’t really give you much “oomph”.  That being said, illusions and traps appeal most to those with creative minds, so the devotees of the Razor Princess might find some uses for them.

2nd Boon: I like this Boon.  Perhaps it’s the simplicity of it, or maybe just its wording.  When you hurt someone with Slashing damage, you can, “at your discretion”, add Bleed 5 to the attack.  While Bleed damage can be stopped by an average Heal check or any effect that cures hit point damage, this still feels pretty potent – especially in longer combats, and if you are fighting (and have ways to damage) multiple enemies.

3rd Boon: 1/day Empowered Blade Barrier, which you are immune to.  Not too shabby, though I question whether this is really worth much by the time you get it (even via Demoniac).

 

Overall: While I kind of like the theme of this Demon Lord, it’s going to be tricky to pull off her obedience under most conditions, and her granted powers are a bit lacklustre.

 

 

 

Angazhan – The Ravenous King

Demon Lord of apes, jungles, and brutal tyrants

Obedience: Get high on jungle plants, and pray to Angazhan while playing a song on a drum made of human skin and bones.  Oddly enough, the drum may be the simplest part of this obedience – you could either build one yourself, or get a party member to craft it for you.  The jungle plants may pose more of a problem: your best bet would be a party member or contact with underworld connections, but even that might not work all of the time.  If you do manage to complete it, however, you gain a +4 bonus on saves against diseases and poisons caused by being in the jungle, or caused by creatures native to jungles.

Consequences: Whereas the questionable bits of most other Obediences can be hidden behind a solid wall, this one might require you to come up with a cover story.  My first idea would be to pretend that you’re a Bard, or that you are carrying on an ancient tradition from your culture’s past.  Also, you might want to pump your Fortitude save, as hallucinogenic plants tend to be addictive.

 

1st Boon: All three of these spells are solid pickups, and free summoning is always good.

2nd Boon: More summoning; nice.  And as swift action, no less. Although you are restricted to specific types of creatures, those creatures are pretty solid – especially if you get this early via Demoniac.

3rd Boon: It’s… well, it’s okay.  Passive bonuses to stats/saves are nothing to sniff at, though these particular bonuses aren’t exactly earth-shattering.

 

Overall: With the right setup, in the right campaign, and with the right group, I could see this guy being a decent option.  Much like Aldinach, you really benefit from being in your patron’s home turf – in Angazhan’s case, the jungle.

 

 

 

Areshkagal – The Faceless Sphinx

Demon Lord of greed, magical and mundane portals, and riddles

Obedience: Carve several riddles into your skin using a knife made from a child’s rib bone.  While this Obedience might be a bit icky and painful to perform, once you have the necessary “tool” to complete it, it shouldn’t be too hard to do.  Unfortunately, the benefits aren’t great – a +4 bonus on saves vs. sonic and language-dependant effects.

Consequences: You’ll have some weird writing etched into your skin.  You can easily cover that with clothes, and IMHO that looks pretty cool.

1st Boon: A flavorful, if unimpressive, group of spells.  I could definitely see someone getting creative with Shrink Item.

2nd Boon: This Boon is held back by a couple of things: its once per day limitation, and the fact that it takes a bit of pre-planning.  That being said, you can’t really go wrong with swift action teleportation.  A great way to get yourself out of trouble – or into it.

3rd Boon: So let me get this straight: you gain the ability to use one of the most powerful spells in Pathfinder once per day – but only for its least powerful function.  Don’t get me wrong, planar travel is fun – and if you need this spell, you need it badly – but this Boon still feels underwhelming.

 

Overall: In my opinion, Areshkagal represents one of the safer options among the ranks of the Demon Lords.  That being said, riddle me this: when were the worshippers of fiendish powers ever interested in taking the safe route?

 

 

Baphomet - Lord of the Minotaurs

Demon Lord of beasts, labyrinths, and minotaurs

Obedience: Stay still for 55 minutes, and then spend the last 5 saying 50 things you noticed about your environment into a hollowed out bull’s horn.  Assuming your GM doesn’t have you make a check for that, and assuming you can find a hollowed-out bull’s horn, this one is pretty straightforward.  Upon completing the task, you gain immunity to Maze effects, as well as a +4 bonus on saves against Confusion and Insanity effects.

Consequences: None, really.  Even the item used in the Obedience could be carried openly without worry.

 

1st Boon: Almost any character could find several uses for all of these spells.  Mislead is particularly nice for a character that will, more often than not, be Evil.

2nd Boon: This is a nice, thematic boost to your close combat skill – though the penalty stings a little for CHA-based casters.  It also doesn’t say that you can turn it off early, so watch out for that.

3rd Boon: Maze is a solid spell, so this is a solid boon.  A quick word of warning, though: don’t cast this spell on a spellcaster, as they can use the time to buff up/just teleport out.

 

Overall: A surprisingly good option for more Melee-focused characters (and especially Demoniacs).

 

 

Cyth-V’sug - Prince of the Blasted Heath

Demon Lord of disease, fungus, and parasites

Obedience: Eugh.  Even by the standards we’ve established here, this Obedience is bad news.  You have to eat “moldering flesh” infested with worms, as well as drink “putrid alcohol” made from fungi.  In most adventuring locales, that’s not something you can readily buy, or even make – at least not in a timely fashion.  You also have to do this for 1 hour, which means, to me at least, that you have to have enough of this stuff to last you for an hour every morning.  That’s a lot of “food”.  The prize for following through with this is a +4 bonus on saves against disease and effects that cause Nausea – which you will probably get some use out of.

Consequences: Where to start – your lodgings will probably be a mess, you’re going to smell awful, and you’ll have to find a way to carry and store your “feasts” – and that’s all going off of the assumption that your GM won’t have you face any consequences from eating rubbish every day.

 

1st Boon: Warp Wood is, IMHO, an underrated spell.  That being said, you know you’re in trouble when it’s your best option among a group of three spells.  Ray of Enfeeblement just isn’t that impactful, especially at the time you would get this naturally, and Contagion is usually a bad option on account of its mediocre DCs and short range.

2nd Boon: Now this is what I’m talking about.  Long duration, strong effect, and the option to crank up the damage if you need to end the fight now.  Play havoc with the action economy and your enemies’ insides at the same time!  Only being once per day hurts it a bit though, as does the fact that it requires a Fortitude save.

3rd Boon: Once per day you can fire off a slightly tweaked, extra flavorful Destruction spell.  Not too shabby.

 

Overall: While his Boons are alright, I have a really hard time seeing this guy being worshipped by anyone other than NPCs.

 

 

 

Dagon – The Shadow in the Sea

Demon Lord of deformity, the sea, and sea monsters

Obedience: Pour a bowl-full of fresh blood into the sea, and the bowl used must be etched with symbols sacred to Dagon and worth at least 100 GP.  Unless your campaign is going to take place entirely on the coastline/on a ship on the ocean, this Obedience is going to be nearly impossible to complete.  For those who are able to, however, your reward is a +4 bonus on saves against the Supernatural and Extraordinary abilities of Aquatic creatures.

Consequences: If spotted, you might face some questions about why you are pouring blood into the sea every morning.  That being said, I think that obtaining the blood for this Obedience is the second hardest part of it: that’ll be up to you to figure out.

 

1st Boon: I’m sensing a theme here.  These spells are pretty underwhelming – and the first one borderline useless – unless you spend a lot of time near water.

2nd Boon: This boon gets a split rating because, on one hand, the given powers really make you feel at home in the water – on the other hand, you need to be constantly near water to make full use of them.  The passive +2 to Con is a nice touch, though.

3rd Boon: Once per day Dominate Monster, but only against creatures of the Aquatic subtype.  I’ve always been a bit skeptical of why this is a 9th-level spell, and the creature type restriction doesn’t help its case.  That being said, in a seaborne campaign, it could save your bacon.

 

Overall: The definition of a situational pickup.  If you’re in the right campaign, and if you have a way to harvest blood for your daily prayers, than this guy might even go up to Green.

 

Deskari - Lord of the Locust Host

Demon Lord of chasms, infestations, and locusts

Obedience: Meditate while covered in bugs, or if no bugs are available, lay face-down on some dirt and pray to Deskari while poking yourself with a sharpened stick or bone.  This one is a bit more reasonable – maybe that’s why he’s the one that’s getting things done.  Upon completion, you gain a +4 bonus on saves against Disease and on effects caused by Vermin.

Consequences: You may want to make sure you shake the bugs/dirt out before you go on with your adventuring day.

1st Boon: Some half-decent summoning options and Inflict Light Wounds; nothing earth-shattering.  (Pun most definitely intended)

2nd Boon: Now this is pretty neat – and it fits well with the powers given above.  Swarms do not damage or distract you, and you gain a pretty swish +4 to Initiative and all saves while standing inside one.  Keep in mind that this ability does not give you the power to control Swarms, only immunity to their effects.

3rd Boon: Swift-action Insect Plague 1/day.  Not as powerful as some of the other tier 3 Boons, but I’ll take it.

 

Overall: If built around, I could see Deskari (and more specifically, his second boon.) finding some use by adventurers who aren’t averse to creepy-crawlies. Other than that though, the Lord of the Locust Host is only alright.

 

Flauros –The Burning Maw

Demon Lord of fire, salamanders, and volcanoes

Obedience: Well then.  To complete this Obedience, you have to burn something worth at least 100 GP – or a living creature – and then eat the ashes.  From what I can tell, you have two options with regards to how you complete this Obedience: If you go the creature path, then you can simply scoop up some bugs and throw them in a lamp or similar object. If you go the object route, then you can try and get around it by burning something that is supposed to be burnt, like Incense.  Granted it would have to be some pretty expensive incense, but I imagine that you could probably find that sort of thing in your average city.  Alternatively, depending on how your GM interprets the wording of the Obedience, you may not have to eat the burned objects.  If that is the case, you could get away with casting Mending or Make Whole to repair your item, effectively making this a one-time investment of 100 GP.  For those who follow through with this, your reward is a +4 bonus on saves vs. Fire effects.

Consequences:  As I covered above, this guy’s unholy ritual is a pain in the ash to complete.  Also, I would be careful performing this Obedience in a wooden building (such as your average inn).

 

1st Boon: A pretty standard group of blasting spells.

2nd Boon: Resist 30 against the most common form of elemental damage is pretty solid.  You are also, in theory, immune to the effects of common flames – so you can stand in bonfires, put your face in torches, that sort of thing.  Who knew that worshipping a Lord of the Abyss would get you some sweet party tricks?

3rd Boon: A surprisingly strong and flavorful summoning ability with a decent duration.  Again, be careful around wooden things.

 

Overall: If it wasn’t for that darned Obedience, I would probably give this guy a full Green rating.  As it stands, however, I have a hard time recommending him to anyone without money to burn (literally), or an easygoing GM willing to let you use some of the tricks I’ve mentioned.

 

Gogunta – Song of the Swamp

Demon Lord of amphibians, boggards, and swamps

Obedience: Woo; another Obedience involving creative murder.  To gain Gogunta’s favor, you have to drown a living creature in muddy water, impale the corpse on a branch, and then meditate near it for the remaining time.  I think that this one edges out its murderous kin by a hair – if you have the ability to create/manipulate water, you can just add some nearby dirt or mud to make it suitable; as for the sacrifice, because it only specifies “living creature”, you should be able to get by with an insect – or at least a rat/squirrel/common verminous animal of choice.  You can also carry a sharpened stick (or several) with you, and if you are in the city, you can leave your sacrifice in a nearby alley.  Your reward for completing this convoluted ritual is a +4 bonus on saves against Disease and Poison caused by exposure to the jungle, or by creatures native to swamps.

 Consequences: You might smell a bit off, and you’ll probably want to put your sacrifices somewhere separate from where you sleep at night, just in case.

 

1st Boon: Decent utility spells (and pretty thematic), but not too exciting.

2nd Boon: A flat +3 Natural Armour Bonus is pretty good, even if it loses its lustre later on.

3rd Boon: Now this feels like a 3rd tier Boon – an awesome, flavorful summoning spell with a little added bonus.

 

Overall: The old frog’s Boons are actually pretty alright (and better if you are a Demoniac), but her Obedience is a bit of a hassle.  If you dig the whole creepy pseudo-Druid vibe, than you could do worse than worshipping Gogunta.

 

 

 

Haagenti – The Whispers Within

Demon Lord of alchemy, invention, and transformation

Obedience: Practice the procedure to turn lead into gold.  You don’t actually have to succeed (or even use gold in your Obedience) to qualify; the only thing that matters is “going through the motions”.  Cool, safe and fairly innocent. Your reward for doing this is a +4 bonus on saves against Transmutation effects.

Consequences: Nothing more than what your average Alchemist goes through on any given day: soot-blackened hands, odd smells, and carrying around a collection of alchemical accoutrements with you.  Other than that, this is a pretty safe Obedience.

 

1st Boon: Three solid self-buffing spells, and gold for a martial character.  I have a hard time thinking of many situations where at least one of these isn’t useful.

2nd Boon: I’m sensing a theme here.  While not being able to cast spells hurts, the bonuses it gives you are often worth it when you need it.

3rd Boon: Some sweet immunities and free healing whenever you are affected by a Polymorph spell.  I like it.

 

Overall: It’s hard to go wrong with Haagenti – he’s a solid choice for a lot of characters, and he works especially well with the Demoniac.

 

 

 

Jezelda - Mistress of the Hungry Moon

Demon Lord of desolation, the moon, and werewolves

Obedience: This Obedience has to be done at night and changes depending on the atmospherics – if the moon is visible, then you just have to pray in an area where you can see the night sky.  However, if the moon is not visible, than things get more complicated.  And by complicated, I mean that you have to ritualistically kill a creature – of your own race, no less – by tearing out their throat with your teeth and consuming some of their still-warm body.  Yep.  For all that work, you get a +4 bonus on all saving throws you make while the moon is visible in the sky.

Consequences: If you are able to perform the first, non-murder-y version of this Obedience, then you’re in the clear – other than the minor fact that, most of the time, you’d be doing your Obedience in the morning before heading out for the day.  If you are unlucky enough to have to do the second part, however, I can’t help much – other than saying that you should probably play a Human (or whatever is populous in the area you’ll be in).  Also, because of how the benefit works, it encourages you to play a more nocturnal character, which may or may not work with your party.  Ultimately, though, I feel that the biggest consequence of following this Obedience is that it forces your GM to take into account the phases of the moon + whether or not you will be able to see it from where you are.

 

1st Boon: Not the best, but not horrible – even if the summoning gets quickly outpaced.

2nd Boon: This is a weird one: you become an afflicted lycanthrope – which is different from a lycanthrope caused by the Curse of Lycanthropy – which gives you some interesting powers (that you can read about here).  In addition, if you were already an afflicted lycanthrope, you instead become a true lycanthrope; and if you were already a true lycanthrope, than you get a +2 bonus to Str and Con.  This ability gives you a bunch of options, and is also thematic as all get out, so it gets a Green rating.

3rd Boon: You become a better wolf.  If you were an afflicted lycanthrope, you become a true lycanthrope; and if you were already a true lycanthrope, you get +2 Str and Con – which stacks with the above Boon.  Also, you can now shapechange as a Swift Action (And because you are, either way, a true lycanthrope after you get this, you can change form without rolling for it as a Swift Action; which is pretty sweet.).

 

Overall: Jezelda’s later boons are fun and thematic, and if it wasn’t for her barbaric and oddly specific Obedience, she could have been a contender.  Also, on an unrelated note, I have developed an aversion to the word lycanthrope.

 

 

 

Jubilex - The Faceless Lord

Demon Lord of ooze monsters, poison, and sloth

Obedience: Pray to Jubilex while you submerge a severed human body part in a vial of acid, letting it melt and dissolve.  Pretty simple, once you obtain a vial of acid or two – just make a habit of slicing and dicing the corpses of any human enemies you come across.  Hey, if you budget out the pieces carefully enough, you might be able to make one corpse last an entire adventure (though it helps if you have some sort of extra dimensional space to store it in).  For this, you gain a +4 on saves vs. Poison.

Consequences: Other than the aforementioned after-battle activities, there isn’t much to worry about.

 

1st Boon: All of these spells are useful, though Grease 3/day is the standout for me.  So many uses.

2nd Boon: This is a pretty handy way to shut down a squishy character, and the fact that you can add it as a swift action when you attack is really nice, though it doesn’t specifically call out whether or not the ability is wasted if you miss. For that reason, it’s best to check with your GM before you use this Boon.

3rd Boon: Another Fort-dependent effect, but a nasty one indeed.  In this case, a pretty hefty save-or-suck with the added benefit of free “summoning” if you kill them.  I’ll take it.

 

Overall: I find it amusing that the Demon Lord of Oozes is, appropriately, green.  This guy is one of my favorites – his Obedience is fairly safe (while also being definitely evil), his Boons are pretty solid, and he’s just an interesting figure.

 

 

Kabriri - Him Who Gnaws

Demon Lord of ghouls, graves, and secrets kept by the dead

Obedience: Feast on a corpse that’s at least a week old, or eat any not-old corpse while on top of a grave.  Oddly enough, this might not be too bad to do – assuming you have a decent carry weight/an extra dimensional space to carry them in, you could just pick up some corpses while you adventure and then munch on them once they get old enough.  Unfortunately, the benefit gained isn’t great – a +4 bonus on saving throws vs. Paralysis and Disease effects from Undead enemies.

Consequences: You’re carrying around one or more dead bodies – not much more needs to be said.  And if you are doing the carrying old-school (i.e. not in a Bag of Holding or similar), then the smell might prove to be an issue eventually.

 

1st Boon: These spells are a mixed bag of “Meh”.  Deathwatch has been known to see occasional use, and I have a soft spot for Speak with Dead, but that’s about it.

2nd Boon: Create Undead 1/day, with automatic control of whatever you create.  Very nice, especially if you’re a martial/someone with sneak attack – flank buddies for un-life!  Also, you’ll likely be carrying around some spare corpses for your Obedience, so raising some of them to help you out in a pinch makes sense.

3rd Boon: This… this one is really weird.   After gaining this Boon, you are transformed into a Ghoul the next time you die – which isn’t a great start, seeing as you have to die to get any benefit from this ability.  What’s more, there’s no information for how this interacts with resurrection spells; does it only come into effect if you aren’t revived for 24 hours?  In addition, you are now Undead, which brings with it its own fair share of complications (and benefits).

 

Overall: While it might sound strange, I feel that this guy is best served by cultists with magically-enhanced carrying capacity.  That being said, Kabriri’s powers are pretty general, allowing many different types of characters to benefit from worshipping him.

 

 

 

Kostchtchie - The Deathless Frost

Demon Lord of cold, giants, and revenge

Obedience: This one doesn’t just involve murder, but murder in a specific environment.  You have spill the blood of a living creature onto snow-covered ground, and that creature has to die within one minute of the obedience ending.  I personally do not know of a way to create snow in Pathfinder*.  Even in snow-covered environs, this obedience is hard to pull off; while most of the others can be done in the seclusion of a tent or your room at the inn, this one requires you to be outside – it calls for “snow covered ground”, which most likely means snow covered earth.  Your given reward for doing this is a +4 bonus on saves vs. against cold.  Cold what, you may ask?  I don’t know for certain.

*With the exception of Sleet Storm or Polar Midnight, which is a tad overkill.

Consequences: Not only have you abducted and killed an intelligent creature, but you’re left a clue as to where the deed happened.  If I had a colour worse than red, this one would be it.

 

1st Boon: With the exception of Chill Touch, these spells are decent and useful to a wide array of characters.

2nd Boon: Cold Resist 30, changing automatically to Fire Resist 30 if you are (or become) immune to Cold.  Not as good as the flip-side Boon from Flauros, but still alright – especially if you are in a campaign taking place in the far north.

3rd Boon: Giant Form 1/day, with the added benefit of being able to shoot out an Empowered Cone of Cold while enhanced by the spell.  Not exactly world-shaking, but passable.

 

Overall: Fun fact: Kostchtchie’s name is pronounced either “kosh-TICK-ti-kai” or “Kosh-chuh-chai”.  Unfortunately, that’s one of the only fun things about worshipping him.  If you are playing an Evil campaign set in the far north, than this Demon Lord might be the one for you.  Otherwise, unless you have a brilliant plan to satisfy his Obedience’s requirements, I would stay away.

 

 

 

Lamashtu - The Demon Queen

Demon Lord of madness, monsters, and nightmares

Obedience: Hoo boy; this one might just take the cake for “worst obedience”.  So, to complete this obedience, you have to either 1: have intercourse with someone “with the sincere intention of impregnating your partner or becoming pregnant” – or 2: kill something that’s less than a week old.  Every day.  Ouch.

I do, however, have some suggestions: if you would prefer to do the first option, you could simply have your character be infertile – therefore making your obedience “have sex every day”, which is slightly more manageable.  If you would rather do the second option, then I would simply point out that most bugs have a lifespan of less than a week – meaning that, with an easygoing GM, you could get away with squashing a bug or two.  If not, then it’ll be up to you to find some baby alley rats or tiny squirrels to kill in the name of The Mother of Monsters (If you are particularly unscrupulous, you could also carry around with you a Familiar Satchel or similar container with some newborn/pregnant animals in it.  If you wish to go that way, I will once again direct you to this page of animal prices.)  Regardless of this Obediences’ squickyness, your reward for following through with it is a +4 on saving throws vs. Insanity, Confusion, and Polymorph effects.

Consequences: Let’s not mince words: if your GM isn’t willing to let you complete this Obedience as an infertile character, or if you can’t find any bugs or baby rats to kill and/or carry with you, you’re kind of screwed.  Either you go around killing bigger baby animals (which may take some work), or you run the risk of becoming a father/mother every time you complete this Obedience.  Neither of those are great for your average adventurer, so worship Lamashtu (via Demonic Obedience) at your own risk.

 

1st Boon: Not a great start – the first two spells are save-or-sucks that will be pretty useless by the time you get them, but Summon Monster III has some utility uses.

2nd Boon: This is surprisingly cool.  You get a beneficial deformity, giving you a customizable Natural Attack with an added benefit of your choice.  Options are always good, and this is a strong Boon for a melee character to build around.

3rd Boon: A suitably monstrous ability for the Demon Lord of deformation.  The Darkvision is nice (though a bit late to the party), but a passive +4 to Perception is always going to be good.  The Gaze attack is the main attraction though, and making someone permanently Confused on a failed save is just brutal.

 

Overall: If your GM is open to the tactics I suggested above – or you’ve come up with alternate ideas for satisfying the Mother of Monsters – then Lamashtu becomes a pretty strong choice.  Also, don’t forget that she counts as a God as well as a Demon Lord, meaning that she also has a Deific Obedience as well as her Demonic Obedience.  Here is a link to her Archives of Nethys page, which contains both Obediences.

 

 

 

Mazmezz - The Creeping Queen

Demon Lord of bindings, driders, and vermin

Obedience: You have to bind a living creature, leaving only a few choice parts of them uncovered.  You then have to “torment” those exposed parts with needles, knives, or poisonous vermin.  Thankfully, unlike Andirifkhu’s Obedience, you don’t need to kill your subject at the end, meaning that you could pull this off with a willing party member – assuming they don’t mind getting a bit kinky with the party’s resident demon-worshipper.  For doing this, you get a +4 bonus on Grapple checks and to your CMD, which is actually pretty sweet.

Consequences: Other than carrying around the necessary “equipment”, there usually won’t be much backlash from doing this ritual.  Unless, of course, you choose to perform it on an unwilling subject – or go full ham and utilize the “poisonous vermin” aspect.

 

1st Boon: My problem with this Boon is that all three of these spells do pretty much the same thing.  Web is alright, though.

2nd Boon: Poison and Vermin Shape II, once per day each.  Not too shabby, especially if you do your research about what you can to turn into.

3rd Boon: Cool; an extra-flavorful Heightened Temporal Stasis, with no Material Components needed (because it’s an SLA).  While it does require a Fortitude save, anything that can take someone out of the fight indefinitely is worth a pickup.

 

Overall: Mazmezz is another lesser-known Demon Lord with a surprisingly decent set of Boons.  I could see Grapple specialists getting use out of her Obedience benefit, and melee bruisers get some cool new forms to play with.

 

 

 

Mestama - The Mother of Witches

Demon Lord of cruelty, deception, and hags

Obedience: Observe a non-believer for an hour (preferably without them knowing where you are), then do something mean to them.  This action “must, at the very least, incite the victim to tears or anger”.  This one is a bit tricky: if you have an understanding group, you could theoretically watch them sleep for an hour, then do something to hurt or inconvenience them.  Perhaps you could steal a Bard’s instrument and threaten to break it, or tell someone hurtful lies about their family/friends.  I think it’s doable, but it requires a bit of foresight.  For successfully completing this Obedience, you get a +4 bonus on saving throws vs. Illusion spells.

Consequences: I think that the consequences from this Obedience are fairly obvious – I would not recommend regularly inciting anger or tears from the party Barbarian, for example.

 

1st Boon: Two good utility spells, and Bestow Curse, which is always nice.

2nd Boon: You age like fine wine – if wine somehow gained Intelligence, Wisdom and Charisma over time.  Also, if you are Venerable when you get this Boon, you die and become a ghost.  In addition, any Illusion spells you cast get a +2 to their DCs.  Very cool.  

3rd Boon: As much as I like Necromancy spells, this one is a bit of a letdown.  It certainly isn’t useless, but a successful Fort save flat-out stopping the damage is sad for a capstone ability.

 

Overall: Mestama is fairly unique flavor-wise among the Demon Lords, but her Boons are only ok.  I would probably recommend her for a caster-focussed Demoniac, as you really want to get to her second Boon as fast as you can for that sweet boost to your casting stat of choice.  

Small ramble: One thing that throws me off about her second Boon, though, is what happens if you don’t complete it the next day.  Do you go back to your original age?  Do you keep the age increase?  If so, do you start taking the penalties for being older?  What about the ghost thing?  So many questions.  Still a good Boon, though.

 

 

 

Nocticula - Our Lady in Shadow

Demon Lord of assassins, darkness, and lust

Obedience: Sex, drugs, and rock and roll – except without the rock and roll part.  Boo.  You have to ingest some form of psychedelic plant or fungi, and then perform one or more sexual acts by yourself or with others.  If that was all you had to do, than this would probably have been Orange.  However, there’s one more qualifier for this Obedience – during these acts, at least 1 pint of blood must be shed.  For those who are willing to do this, you get a +4 bonus on saving throws against Blindness and Charm effects.

Consequences: Depending on where you get the blood from, your partner(s) might be a bit miffed at you.  And if you are doing this alone, I would have to think that you’d take some damage from that.  Also, drugs; both obtaining them and regularly taking them may pose some problems.

 

1st Boon: A pair of Enchantment spells and Darkness.  Unless you can pump up the DCs, I don’t see this being terribly useful.

2nd Boon: Yes, yes please.  Even though it won’t work against all enemy types, and it’s based on a Fortitude save, Quickened Blindness/Deafness multiple times per day is really good.

3rd Boon: Permanent Dominate Monster, with a little bonus.  Very cool indeed – the nice thing about using a Dominate spell on a rampaging monster is that “Kill stuff” isn’t usually against their nature, so they won’t get another save.

 

Overall: The First Succubus’ Boons really reward those who can make them stick.  Because the DCs of SLAs scale off of Charisma, your best bet would be to worship Nocticula as a Sorcerer or Oracle.  If they do connect, though?  Hoo boy.

 

 

 

Nurgal - The Shining Scourge

Demon Lord of the deserts, the sun, and senseless warfare

Obedience: Pray to Nurgal with hitting yourself with a salt-encrusted whip.  This Obedience must end at noon, and if you do not complete this Obedience in an area “under the light of the sun”, you have to eat some salt mixed with sand.  For doing this, you gain – wait for it – a +4 bonus on all Fortitude saving throws against effects that cause fatigue or exhaustion, or that damage, drain, or penalize ability scores.

Consequences: This one has a variety of small drawbacks: you have to carry a bunch of salt with you (Which may get expensive – salt was a luxury back then.  And by a luxury, I mean 5GP for 1 pound of salt (Source: UE).), you have to do it at a certain point in the day (which may or may not be an issue, depending on your group and adventure), and if you don’t do it outside in the sun, you’ll also have to carry some sand around with you.

 

1st Boon: Thematic but only alright.  Daylight is nice if you know what you’re up against is gonna be blinded by the light.

2nd Boon: A cool little blasting spell – if you’re up against Undead or Oozes, this hits even harder.  Not bad.

3rd Boon: Horrid Wilting 3/day.  Another damage spell, but this time an AoE.  The area of effect for this spell is tricky to figure out, but the end result isn’t – also, it’s Fort half instead of Fort negates, which is nice.

 

Overall: Nurgal’s focus on blasting is both a strength and a weakness, making him a niche pick.  Certainly not the worst out there, though.

 

 

 

Orcus - Prince of Undeath

Demon Lord of death, necromancy, and wrath

Obedience: Crush up half a pound bones from a sentient creature, then mix it with some water to create a “gray paste”, which you have to eat after reciting prayers to Orcus.  Overall not too shabby – if you make it a habit to collecting the corpses of your enemies, as well as any old bones you find while adventuring, this shouldn’t be too hard to maintain.  The benefit from this obedience is pretty good, too – a +4 bonus on saves vs. Death and Negative Energy effects.

Consequences: As long as you are discreet with your bone-mashing – and you don’t show off the contents of your inventory to worshippers of Pharasma (or the city guard, for that matter) – you should be alright.

 

1st Boon: Three slightly underwhelming Necromancy spells.  Command Undead synergises well with the tier 2 Boon, however.

2nd Boon: The once per day limitation hurts it, but his has potential.  It’s especially powerful if you manage to land it on a spellcaster, as Juju Zombies retain the abilities they had before becoming Zombies – including spellcasting.

3rd Boon: While not the strongest at level 20, this ability packs a punch if you get it early via Demoniac.  All three options here are solid combatants with different sets of skills.

 

Overall: Orcus is a classic, both in theme and origins.  He’s also a pretty safe pick for a Demoniac – particularly for one who will be going into the higher levels.

 

 

 

Pazuzu - King of the Wind Demons

Demon Lord of the sky, temptation, and winged creatures

Obedience: Find a location that hungry birds might find, and then decorate it with the intestines of a freshly killed creature – note that you don’t have to kill this creature yourself, and that it doesn’t have to be an Intelligent creature (though I doubt that many bugs have usable intestines).  After doing this, you must then meditate on the offering (Though you don’t necessarily have to be near the offering to do so.).  Your reward for doing this is a +4 bonus on saving throws vs. flying creatures and Compulsion effects.

Consequences: Finding, catching, discretely murdering, and then disemboweling a living creature is a pretty tall order for a daily Obedience.  Even with the caveat that you don’t personally have to do anything except fling some intestines onto a high place, this isn’t very easy to pull off.

 

1st Boon: Fly 1/day brings this up a colour.  Otherwise, it’s only ok.

2nd Boon: This is really nice.  Magic Jar is legendary for the hijinks it lets you pull, and anything that upgrades that is good in my book.

3rd Boon: This one is a bit weird.  It definitely rewards careful planning/a good Bluff skill, but the payoff is a bit weak.  The ability to bypass Protection from Good and similar effects is really nice, though.

 

Overall: Pazuzu has a long history and some decent boons, but his obedience holds him back from being really recommendable.  For what it’s worth, though, I would say that he’s better off than Lamashtu.

 

 

 

 

Shax - The Blood Marquis

Demon Lord of envy, lies, and particularly violent murder

Obedience: Perform an autopsy on a creature killed within the last 24 hours, using bare hands over tools as much as possible.  Squicky and bloody, but not as bad as some of the others – assuming you have some privacy.  An extra dimensional bag would also help immensely with this – you can just pick up one of the corpses you or your party create on your travels, and examine it when you have the time.  For completing this Obedience, you gain a +4 bonus on saves against effects that cause Bleeding and Illusions with the Figment subtype.

Consequences: You may want to be careful you don’t end up with a literal “red right hand” from this Obedience, but as long as you stay secretive with your activities, you should be alright.

 

1st Boon: Three really sweet spells for a melee character.  Very nice.

2nd Boon: Though situational, this ability is really unique, flavorful, and gets you free healing in specific circumstances.

3rd Boon: A little late to the party at level 20, and depending on your campaign you might face few opponents who you can use this on.  Extra damage is usually good, though – especially for someone with lots of attacks/pre-existing Sneak Attack.

 

Overall: Shax is a really fun Demon Lord to mess around with, and his Obedience is just creepy enough to remind you that you’re the bad guy – without also reminding you that this is supposed to be a game about the good guys.  Melee bruisers (and especially TWFers) will want to keep an eye on ol’ bloody beak.

 

 

 

Shivaska - The Chained Maiden

Demon Lord of aberrations, clocks, and prisons

Obedience: You have to spend an hour bound with ropes, chains, a straightjacket, or other restraints.  During this time, you have to chant prayers to Shivaska while straining against your bindings hard enough to leave marks on your skin that will last the rest of the day.  In addition, you must begin this obedience at exactly the start of the hour.  For successfully doing this, you gain a +4 bonus to your CMD.

Consequences: As long as you have some privacy, the required materials, and a reliable way to tell time, this Obedience is quite easy to do.  The only thing that holds this back in my eyes is that you’re going to be in a bit of a bind (literally) if you/your party is attacked while you’re in the middle of it.

 

1st Boon: Not great – a trio of low-level, DC-dependent spells.

2nd Boon: A slightly-tweaked, streamlined Binding spell.  Pretty cool, and very thematic.

3rd Boon: 1/day Time Stop.  Yep, this is Blue.

 

Overall: If you stick around until the end, Shivaska may be one of the stronger options in the late game (and especially for a Demoniac).  Add to that a fairly unobtrusive Obedience with a usable benefit, and The Chained Maiden is in quite a comfortable position.

 

 

 

Sifkesh - The Sacred Whore

Demon Lord of hopeless despair, heresy, and suicide

Obedience: Normally I don’t preface these descriptions with warnings, as almost all of the Demonic Obediences are horrible in their own ways.  That being said, I am with this one: this Obedience – scratch that, this Demon Lord – is a very, very touchy subject.  Use your utmost discretion if you are contemplating choosing this Demon Lord as your patron, and be considerate towards the rest of your group when roleplaying as a worshipper of Sifkesh.

Now, let us look at her actual Obedience: you have to write a note “lambasting your enemies”, and then pretend to kill yourself in a ritualized suicide via strangulation or cutting.  Upon completing this obedience, you receive a +4 bonus against Mind-affecting effects, which is actually really good.

 

Consequences: Assuming that your group is fine with you worshipping Sifkesh and performing this Obedience, this actually isn’t that bad.  You’ll have to carry paper, a quill and some ink with you at all times (which can get quite expensive), and you’ll have to figure out a way to pretend kill yourself without actually killing yourself by accident.

 

1st Boon: Whispering Wind is a spell that I’ve always wanted to fiddle with, but the other two options are both save-or-sucks.

2nd Boon: Cool in theory, but you’ll have to have an idea what religion(s) you’ll be going up against regularly, and be within earshot of one of them in order to use this ability.  The unlimited duration is pretty sweet, though.

3rd Boon: Well then.  While it’s still a save-or-suck (with 2 required saves, no less.), it’s a particularly powerful and thematic one.

 

Overall: Even if your table is cool with you worshipping Sifkesh, her Boons are far from top-tier.  That being said, in the right hands, playing in the right campaign, I could see her given powers (especially her 2nd Boon) being fairly powerful – just make sure your Cha is high enough to make sure your SLAs connect.

 

 

 

Socothbenoth – The Silken Sin

Demon Lord of perversion, pride, and taboos

Obedience: Maybe this is just me, but I find this Obedience quite funny.  You must achieve sexual release – either by yourself or with a partner – and then defile a torn-out page from a religious text of a Lawful Good Deity.  Once you obtain your required “reading”, this is a really, really easy Obedience to complete.  And the bonus you get from it is pretty solid too – a +4 bonus on saves vs. Enchantment effects.

Consequences: As long as you don’t let a Cleric of Iomedae catch you with your pants down, there isn’t too much blowback to be had from this.

 

1st Boon: This group of spells is quite decent – especially if you are a Cha-based character.

2nd Boon: A cool little bonus, but Enchantment spells aren’t the strongest choice to specialize in.

3rd Boon: A surprisingly awesome Boon for melee characters – just make sure you keep in mind what options you have.

 

Overall: Who would have thought that the Demon Lord of perversion would be as solid of an option as he is?  Granted, he caters most heavily to Charisma-centered PCs, but there are still plenty of good options for the not-so-smooth supplicants of The Silken Sin.

 

 

 

Urxehl - Trollfather

Demon Lord of natural disasters, storms, and trolls

Obedience: Uh huh.  So, this Obedience has two options as to how to complete it – if it is storming in your area, you have to cavort naked on a hill, mountaintop, or rooftop.  If it isn’t, you instead have to mutilate a non-evil being by cutting off its fingers, toes, arms, and legs – burning each piece to ash before working on the next.  Note: it doesn’t say anywhere that the creature you’re hacking up has to be alive or humanoid, only that it has the applicable appendages/digits.  Upon completing this Obedience, you gain a +4 bonus on saves against weather-related effects and spells.

Consequences: If there isn’t a storm at your location when you go to perform your Obedience, then you have it fairly easy – unlike some of the others, you don’t even need to carry entire corpses with you; just their torsos.  If the sky has opened up, however, then I hope you aren’t the shy type.

 

1st Boon: Obscuring Mist 3/day is a pretty sweet pickup, and Call Lightning can bring the pain if you use it in the right conditions – for example, if your morning ritual involved some cavorting today.

2nd Boon: Nice.  Earthquake is dang cool spell, even with a halved area of effect.

3rd Boon: I would have been slightly disappointed if this spell hadn’t come into play somehow. A strong, thematic capstone for the Demon Lord of storms.

 

Overall: If you’re cool with performing his Obedience on a daily basis (and your GM is alright with figuring out the weather on any given day), then Urxehl is a really solid choice for a lot of characters.

 

 

 

Xoveron - The Horned Prince

Demon Lord of gargoyles, gluttony, and ruins

Obedience: Perch on a high place and survey the area around you.  If the area you are in is uninhabited, you just have to stay there for an hour.  If the area you are in is in a populated location, however, you have to make use that no passerby realizes you are a living creature.  Anyone who does realize your true nature, you must kill before the hour’s end.  For doing this, you get a +4 bonus on saving throws against effects that cause Sickness, Nausea, Exhaustion, and Fatigue.

Consequences: In my mind, it would be fairly hard to figure out if you were discovered without some form of mind-reading/very obvious signs, such as someone pointing at you or running away from your perch.  Aside from that, or if you are in a secluded location, this Obedience is fairly easy to do.  Also, put some ranks in Climb – you might need them.

 

1st Boon: An interesting handful of spells.  They aren’t bad, per say – just a bit disjointed.

2nd Boon: Unless you proselytize to/orchestrate with the rest of your group to all worship The Horned Prince, this ability effectively amounts to a single-target Hero’s Feast with a reduced consumption time.  That’s still pretty nice, though.

3rd Boon: This boon is unique, flavorful, and powerful – always a good combination.  This is another ability that really benefits from having a high Cha, especially because it’s based off of a Fort save.

 

Overall: You know, I have a theory about these Demon Lords – the less you’ve heard about them beforehand, the more likely they are to have cool Boons.  Xoveron certainly fits that description, with a colourful array of Boons that are both unique and decently strong.  Just make sure you talk with your GM about how they would adjudicate the second version of big X’s Obedience.

 

 

 

Yhidothrus - The Ravager Worm

Demon Lord of age, time, and worms

Obedience: Lie in a closed coffin partially filled with “worm-infested soil or leech-infested mud”, and meditate on The Ravager Worm.  During the hour, you have to eat or inhale at least a dozen of the creepy-crawlies.  Your reward for doing this a +4 bonus on saves against effects that cause slowness or magical aging, or anything that damages, drains, or penalizes ability scores.

Consequences: This Obedience has many problems, the base of all of them being the fact that you have to carry all the required materials with you wherever you go, or otherwise be able to create them when necessary.  Because of that, I have a hard time seeing Yhidothrus being worshipped by many adventurers.

 

1st Boon: Three fairly situational spells.  Gentle Repose is an interesting inclusion, as it’s typically seen as a fairly benevolent spell.

2nd Boon: This is pretty much the direct opposite of Mestama’s 2nd Boon – you can, 1/day, cast a unique Bestow Curse that dramatically ages your chosen target, with no benefits to their mental scores.  This Boon can be used effectively against most enemies – martial characters will have a harder time making the save (as well as being more strongly impacted by the penalty), and casters will become super fragile/clumsy/weak.  Overall a solid power.

3rd Boon: A pretty decent summoning spell, though you’ll get more out of it if you’re fighting Good opponents.

 

Overall: For your average (demon-worshipping) adventurer, Yhidothrus is a hard one to justify following.  His Boons are alright, but his Obedience is just inconvenient.  For NPC villains, however, I could definitely see this guy getting some use.

 

 

 

 

Zevgavizeb - The God of the Troglodytes

Demon Lord of vast caverns, reptiles, and troglodytes

Obedience: Find a cavern with a stalagmite in it, and impale a living creature on said stalagmite in such a way that it does not immediately die – though they do not have to survive the entire obedience.  You must then perform a creepy little song-and-dance around the stalagmite, stopping every ten minutes to push the impaled creature further down.  You must also time your dance so that by the end of the obedience, the creature has reached the bottom of the stalagmite.  If you manage to do this, you will gain a +4 bonus to saving throws against “special attacks and spells” originating from Reptilian creatures.

Consequences: Unless your campaign takes place entirely underground/in a cavernous area, you’re pretty much out of luck.  Even then, this Obedience is harder to do than most of the killing-related ones, as you have to murder this creature in a very specific way AND scream praises to Zevgavizeb while doing so.

 

1st Boon: As they say in the song, two outta three ain’t bad – especially if you use natural attacks.

2nd Boon: Okay, this is actually really sweet.  Summoning Dinosaurs never really gets old, even though they won’t be the kings of the earth at level 16.  Also, because they possess the Fiendish template, they hit hardest when facing Good opponents – making this an even better ability for an NPC Boss.

3rd Boon: I mean, I’ll take it.  A passive bonus to Con is good for everyone – as is an equal bonus to AC – but this still falls flat for a capstone.

 

Overall: Zevgavizeb makes a fun Demon Lord for Evil, subterranean NPCs to worship; player characters, not so much.  Also, for the love of the gods, never create a Svirfneblin worshipper of Zevgavizeb.  You’ll make your GM cry.

 

 

 

Zura - The Vampire Queen

Demon Lord of blood, cannibalism, and vampires

Obedience: Nice – another Obedience that can be completed with consent from a party member (or a helpful Familiar or Animal Companion).  For the first version of this obedience, you have to drink the blood of a willing creature, and then let them drink some of yours and meditate on the teachings of The Vampire Queen.  Cool.  The second version can be completed without the aid of others, but is a bit more problematic; you have to eat the flesh of a member of your own race “until you are full”.  For following through with either version of this Obedience, you gain a +4 bonus on saving throws against the Supernatural abilities of Undead creatures.

Consequences: Assuming you have a technique (or device) to drain blood without causing too much pain or damage to the target, this Obedience is, well, pretty painless.  And if you can’t convince one of your minions/party members to suck it up, you can simply grab some choice bits of corpses you find on your travels.

 

1st Boon: Spider Climb is a good utility spell, and the other two aren’t awful either.

2nd Boon: Gaining the combined benefits of Death Knell and Hero’s Feast as a Full-Round Action by drinking the blood of the fallen?  Yes please.

3rd Boon: I think that this, right here, might just be my favorite Boon.  For a few rounds per day (plus your Charisma modifier), you get to turn into a Vampire.  Not only does this increase your ability scores, but it gives you a wide variety of offensive and defensive tools and powers that are as awesome as they are flavorful.  That being said, however, it also comes with a wide variety of drawbacks – this is a power that really rewards careful research about how it works.  Because of its randomized duration and many drawbacks, it isn’t the strongest tier-3 Boon out there.  Is it still worth picking up (especially through Demoniac early access)?  I’d say so.

 

Overall: Looks like the demonic Vampires beat out the demonic Werewolves this time around – Zura’s given powers are all pretty solid, with tangible benefits for almost all characters, and her obedience is one of the more reasonable ones.  All in all a decent and thematic choice.

Enter the Demoniac

As I mentioned at the start of this guide, the Demoniac is a Prestige Class that revolves around the Demonic Obedience feat.

What I didn’t mention is that you also get a variety of other benefits as well, which I will cover, explain, and rate according to my personal opinion of them.

 

Note about Prestige Classing: there is now a wonderful feat for any spellcaster that is considering multiclassing – its name is Prestigious Spellcaster, and it is beautiful.  It allows you to spend two feats to ignore the first “missing” level of spellcasting in a PrC, thus making a number of PrCs more viable than ever before.  Also, it’s banned in PFS, so keep that in mind.

Also, it has not yet (to my knowledge) been confirmed as legal, but it’s possible your GM will allow you to utilize the Evangelist, Exalted, and Sentinel PrCs from Inner Sea Gods in addition to/in place of the Demoniac WRT accessing Boons early.  If that is the case, then I strongly urge you to check out the rundown on them in Walter’s guide, as they open up a wide variety of options that simply aren’t plausible for your average Demoniac.

 

As for the chassis of the class, the Demoniac has D8 Hit Die, ¾s BaB, 9/10 Spellcasting progression, 2+Int skill points per level & a good Fortitude save and bad Reflex and Will saves.

 

Requirements

Alignment: Chaotic evil – Chances are that if your GM has allowed you to take Demonic Obedience, they will probably be alright with you being CE as well.  Still, restricting options is never a good thing.

Feats: Demonic Obedience, Iron Will – For reasons that I will get to later, you would most likely be thinking of picking up Iron Will anyway, so this isn’t too bad.

Skills: Intimidate 7 ranks, Knowledge (planes) 7 ranks, Spellcraft 7 ranks – Intimidate is a bit of an odd inclusion, but since characters taking this PrC will probably have a decent Cha, it’s not too harsh of a tax.

Language: Abyssal – It’s probably a good thing to understand what Demons are saying, seeing as that’s kind of your thing.

Special: Must worship a demon lord.

That’s all fine and good.  However, there is one more step before you get to access your fun new toys:

Special: Must have been physically traumatized by a demon—either reduced to negative hit points by a demon’s physical attacks, taken a total of 10 or more points of ability damage or drain from a demon’s special attack (this total damage can be from multiple attacks as long as the total is 10 or more in all), or spent at least 1 day under a demon’s control (either via charm or compulsion effects or via demonic possession).

Thankfully, there is an aside that makes this part a little less problematic:

Note that this demon need not be one encountered during an adventure—many would-be demoniacs simply summon a demon and command it to aid in fulfilling this requirement.

 

While I may come back to this at a later date, the method that you choose for completing this is pretty much up to you.  It’s obviously a lot easier to do if you are a spellcaster (as you can summon the demon yourself), and being possessed by a Demon for 24 hours seems like a very bad idea on paper, but other than that I would simply recommend doing whatever you feel works best for you and your group.  Best of luck.

 

 

Now we get to the fun stuff – the profane gifts (aka Class Features) of the Demoniac.

 

Demonic Mark: This ability is really awesome – your Demon Lord’s unholy symbol appears somewhere on your body as an unholy brand, which 1: functions as an Unholy Symbol with regards to divine spellcasting, and 2: allows you to, 1/day, spend a free action when you cast a spell to have that spell not be expended – effectively a free Pearl of Power of any level you can cast.  Spells cast in this way gain the Chaotic and Evil descriptors, which may be a boon or a bane depending on your situation.

Obviously, this ability has little to no use for a non-spellcaster, but that’s not really the target demographic for this PrC.

 

Damned: You’re on a highway to Hell… or rather, the Abyss.  Basically, whenever someone tries to resurrect you, they have to make a caster level check; if they fail, they can’t try to raise you for 24 hours.  This is a bit of an inconvenience, especially in combat, but not game-breaking.

 

Obedience: In order to maintain the bonuses from being a Demoniac (including spell progression), you have to perform your Demonic Obedience every day.  How much of a drawback this is will depend on your Demon Lord of choice, but it’s something you’ll need to keep in mind when playing this Prestige Class.

 

Energumen: Aside from getting early access to your Demonic Boons, this is the flagship ability of the Prestige Class.  And what an ability it is.

When you activate this ability, your spirit is merged with that of a Demon, giving you +2 Profane bonus to any one ability score of your choice.

At level 5, it gives you a +4 bonus to any one ability score, as well as electricity resistance 10, and a +4 bonus on saving throws vs. poison.

And at level 7, you get a +6 bonus to any one ability score, as well as flat-out immunity to Electricity and Poison.

Now, you might be thinking “Wow, that’s a pretty neat ability.  Why does it have a split rating?”

First off, the duration is slightly pants: it only lasts a number of rounds equal to your Demoniac level, and can only be used once per day.

And, just like a lot of the abilities we’ve seen so far, this one has a drawback – and a fairly big one to boot.

Once the duration is up, the demon whom you are sharing your body with tries to press their advantage and take over your mind – you become Confused for a number of rounds equal to the amount of time spent under the influence.

Thankfully, there is way to counteract this: being affected by a Protection from Evil/Chaos spell stops the Confusion effect in its tracks – but it also cancels the positive effects if applied before they end.  In addition, at the start of every round that you spend possessed, you can try and make a DC 25 Will save to shake off the effect.

Because of that, I see this as being more of a last ditch effort type ability, as opposed to something that you will make use of every day.  Your best bet would probably be to use the stat boost to increase your casting stat, and then use the limited duration to lay into people with your best save-or-dies.  If you're more of a weapon-wielder, you could instead hulk out and raise your physical stat of choice - or boost your Con to avoid dying by any means necessary.  In any case, picking up a wand of Protection from Evil/Chaos might be a wise idea.  Best case scenario, you have a spotter who can stay near you and use it on you when the time is up (If you don’t trust your party members to do it, then a Familiar or Cohort (via Leadership or similar) could do it.).  Worst case scenario, you can use it on yourself - though that means that you’ll miss out on the last round’s benefits.

 

Demonic Boons: Just as a reminder, via taking levels in Demoniac you will get your Demonic Boons at level 10, 13, and 16, instead of levels 12, 16, and 20, assuming you enter into it at the earliest opportunity.

 

Summon Demon: Starting at level 4, once per day you may summon one Succubus, 1d3 Babaus, or 1d4+1 Brimoraks.  At 8th level, you can instead call forth one Hezrou, 1d3 Vrocks, or 1d4+1 Succubi to fight for you.  Or do other stuff.  I won’t judge.

 

Demonic Form: This is your capstone, and it is suitably awesome.  Once per day, you can transform into a unique Demon; you gain a bunch of nice benefits befitting your new form, as well as one ability chosen from a list – including a variety of natural attacks, the ability to breathe underwater, and wings (note that the customization, once chosen, cannot be changed afterwards).  You can stay in this form for a number of minutes per day equal to your caster level, spent in 1-minute increments.  All in all a pretty cool power, even if it’s not the most powerful thing in the world at level 17.

 

 

Ex-Demoniacs: Wanted to mention this quickly before we move on to the next section: much like a Cleric, if you act severely against the will of your demonic patron, or if your Alignment changes to anything other than CE, you can have your powers and class features stripped from you (except for Damned)*.  Yet another reason why you shouldn't tick off a Demon Lord.

*And, much like a Cleric, you can usually gain them back via a casting of Atonement.  

 

Build advice

Before we begin, I would be the first person to admit that I am not the most proficient at building characters.  These are simply my suggestions and general building advice, and should be taken with a grain of salt.

 

Class choice

Right off the bat, the Oracle seems to mesh well with everything the class has to offer; its 3/4s BaB and decent spellcasting, as well as its primary stat being Charisma mean that your Boons and SLAs will have higher DCs and/or more uses per day.  The Oracle also has 4+int Skill Points, meaning that it will be slightly easier for you to fulfill that part of the Demoniac’s prereqs.  The downsides of the Oracle are that your Will save won’t be as high as that of a Wisdom-based class, as well as the limited spell list of a Spontaneous caster.

The Cleric comes in as a close second, and is sort of the defensive/safe option of the bunch.  You’ll have a higher Will save, though your Boon DCs won’t be quite as high.  On top of that, you have all the standards strengths of the Cleric – Domains, Channel Energy*, and unfettered access to the second best spell list in the game.

*Though it will almost certainly be Negative Energy, which is slightly less useful than Positive IMHO.

And if the Cleric is the defensive option, and the Oracle is the balanced option, then the Sorcerer is the hyper-offensive option.  Coming in with d6 hit die instead of d8* and completely lacking native armour proficiency, the Sorcerer makes up for that with what will likely be the highest Charisma of any of the options presented.  They also have a wider array of spells to choose from, which may be of benefit with certain Demon Lords (Socothbenoth and Mestama come to mind).  Also, since you can’t really do melee very well (though you do have Bloodlines that specialize in it), some Demon Lords may be worse options for you than most (Haagenti and Jezelda, for example).  Overall the Sorcerer is a niche pick, but a powerful niche pick with some fun tools to play with.

*meaning that you will actually gain HP from taking levels in Demoniac.

In a similar vein, the Psychic would be cool to mess around with – while you lack the offensive punch of the Sorcerer, you bring more utility (and skill points) to the table as well as the ability to wear armour if you want to.  Also, because of the way that Psychic Disciplines work, you can decide if you want to emphasize your Wisdom or Charisma to have higher DCs or a better Will save.

 

Another slightly safer option would be the Shaman.  With the Shaman, you get a good mix of both worlds; because of the fact you have Hexes to play with, you don’t have to go into melee if you don’t want to – even though you have the BaB and armour proficiencies to do so if you build that way.  Because of this, a caster-based Shaman could have a 14 and above in all mental stats, giving you a solid Will save, good Boon/SLA DCs, AND a reasonable amount of Skill Points.  On top of all that, you have the flexibility of the Spirits – making you able to channel Positive Energy when needed*, throw some wicked debuffs on occasion, and even do a little bit of blasting if want to.  With a decent Intelligence, you could also use the Lore spirit to grab some choice Wizard/Sorcerer spells if you need them.

The downside of the Shaman is the fact that your base spell list isn’t the best (though that can be remedied by taking the Human FCB), and the fact that some of your best Hexes are dependent on your Shaman level, making them a bit weaker when multiclassing.

*something only a Life oracle or a Cleric with one of a few suboptimal archetypes could do in your place.

And last but not least, the Warpriest.  This flew under my radar when I started this guide, but the Warpriest is actually in a pretty good position to worship a Demon Lord – they also have d8 hit die and 3/4s BaB (though you’ll want to mitigate that as much as possible), so they fit in well.  The other options here have more magical clout, but the Warpriest has more physical oomph – working like the reverse of the Sorcerer above, you’ll want your demoniac patron to have Boons relating to buffing your martial prowess and touch-range effects.  In addition, you’ll have a slightly higher Will save than the Sorcerer and Psychic, and if you go into Arsenal Chaplain you’ll have more Feats to play with than any other class on this list.  

One more thing to note: if you play a Warpriest of Lamashtu, Shivaska, Sifkesh, or Zura, you can take the Madness Blessing.  While the Minor Blessing is of minimal use, the Major Blessing allows you to control which result of the d100 Confused creatures near you count as getting (including yourself).  This means that, after Energumen ends, you could choose to have yourself (and other confused creatures) act normally, and then use your standard action on that turn to cast Protection from Evil/Chaos on yourself and continue your day as normal.  Pretty nifty.

 

Quick tip: Regardless of which class you use, I would suggest waiting and picking up 8 levels of your base class, as most classes get cool/important stuff at level 8 (including your first iterative attack, which is especially important as a Warpriest).

 

 

Now that we’ve gone through my recommendations for what classes work best with the Demoniac, now I will give you a list of some classes that I would not recommend going into Demoniac with.

Any purely martial class: There just isn’t enough here for you to quantify losing that much BaB and HP.

Wizards/Arcanists: The fact that you don’t learn new spells per level when you multiclass really sucks for the masters of magic and their half-blooded cousins.  While they are strong classes, and you can always try to learn spells from scrolls and the spellbooks of others, they just aren’t the best option for a Demoniac.

Any 6th level Spellcaster that isn’t the Warpriest (Bards, Inquisitors, Alchemists, etc): Not progressing your class features is really rough for these guys, and the 9/10 spellcasting progression doesn’t do enough for them to justify the switch.

Witches: Even more so than the Shaman, your Hexes are your bread and butter.  Not having their DCs scale with you is kind of a death sentence for many Witch builds.

 

Feats and Traits for a Demoniac

Damnation Feats

If there was ever a feat tree that was made specifically with Demon-worshippers in mind, it would be this one.  As you take feats from this line, your soul will be pulled closer to the Abyss, making you harder to resurrect.  That being said, if you go into Demoniac, your soul will already be Damned, so there’s no downside to taking the first feat.

You can check out the link above for the full rundown, but going all the way culminates in being unable to be resurrected by anything short of a Wish or Miracle.  Also, with every Damnation Feat you take, your Alignment shifts one step closer to your patron’s - however, because you are a Demoniac, that isn’t a problem for you; your Alignment is always going to be the same as your patron.

The other unique thing about these feats is that their benefits scale depending on how many Damnation Feats you have - which, of course, tempts you into taking as many of them as possible.  Choose wisely.

Fiendskin: You gain resistances similar to those of your average Demon - Which means resistance to either Acid, Cold, or Fire damage.  If you have one Damnation Feat, you get Resistance 5 to one of the elements listed above.  With two Damnation Feats, you gain another 5 points of resistance to one of the aforementioned elements.  If you already have Resistance to the element you select, this stacks with it.  At three feats, you get Immunity to one of the three types of energy, and at four feats you become an Outsider (native), and gain Immunity to any energy type that a Demon would have Resistance or Immunity to.  I would only really recommend this Damnation Feat if you know that you’ll be taking a lot of elemental damage, and you won’t have Endure Elements or similar handy.

Maleficium: Now this is the one you want.  Upon taking this feat, you gain +1 to the DCs of any spell you cast with the Evil descriptor.  This is awesome for several reasons:

1: There are a lot of really cool Evil spells at all levels.

2: You can turn any spell into an Evil spell via your Demonic Mark.

If you only have one Damnation Feat, then that’s all it does.  If you have two Damnation Feats, however, you get a 1-level discount on Metamagic you put on Evil spells, to a minimum of +1 level above their original level.

If you have three Damnation Feats, you get another +1 to the DCs of Evil spells you cast, and if you have four Damnation Feats you treat your caster level as being 2 higher for any level dependant effects in Evil spells you cast.

While it does make you think about your spell choice a bit more than usual, I feel that it’s still a pretty strong Feat for a Demoniac.

Mask of Virtue: This ability is a bit more situational than the others, but it can be particularly strong in a campaign where enemies are likely to have mind-and-alignment reading magic, such as a more intrigue-heavy adventure.  With your first Damnation Feat, this ability makes Alignment-detecting magic return an inconclusive result - which may end up making others  more suspicious of you instead of less.  If you have two feats, you choose an Alignment one step away from your actual one, and register as that alignment instead (which will, more often than not, be CN).  With three feats under your belt, you can choose an Alignment two steps away from your own to masquerade as, and with four feats you gain the ability to instantly know when someone is trying to read your Alignment, and in addition to giving them whatever answer you desire, you also learn their name and Alignment.  Cool, but specific.

Soulless Gaze: Do you like being scary?  Like, really scary?  Well… there are probably better ways to do it.  But we’re here now, so let’s dig into this particular feat.

With one Damnation Feat, you gain a +2 bonus on Intimidate checks.  In a void, this is just a worse version of Skill Focus: Intimidate.

With a second Damnation Feat, you can use Intimidate to increase the potency of Fear effects you’ve already applied via Intimidate, instead of just extending the duration.  Not bad, but if your DM allows you to use the Skill Unlock system, then you’re better off just using that instead of endangering your soul with this feat.

Upon gaining a third Damnation Feat, you get another +2 to Intimidate.  You have now broken even with Skill Focus: Intimidate.

And if you get a fourth Damnation Feat, you gain the ability to demoralize foes with Intimidate as a swift action.  Alright, that’s actually pretty sweet.

Other Feats

Improved Iron Will: If you want to make that insane Will save from Energumen with any regularity, then I would recommend picking this up.

Traits

Dark Magic Affinity (Tiefling Only): When casting spells with the Evil descriptor, you act as if your caster level is one higher than it actually is.  Pretty sweet for a servant of darkness like yourself, and it stacks with the Damnation Feats I covered above.

Items

While your choice of mundane items are up to you, there are a few magical items that have a particular appeal to a Demoniac.

Orb of Foul Abaddon (18,000 GP): Expensive but good.  The Orb of Foul Abaddon has two powers: once per day, it can be used to fire a Dread Bolt at your enemies.  It is, however, it’s second power that makes it as useful (and expensive) as it is - if used as an additional spell focus, any spell you cast with the Evil descriptor is cast at +1 effective caster level.  As far as I know, this also stacks with the Dark Magic Affinity Trait mentioned above.  You never can have enough magic.

Padma Blossom (8,000GP): Now, when you think of crazed demon-worshippers, you probably don’t then immediately think of flowers.  That being said, if you’re playing a Demoniac, you’ll want to keep an eye out for this pretty little lotus.  When held, the Padma Blossom gives its owner a +3 bonus in concentration checks (which is pretty nice in itself) and also suppresses the following effects: Morale bonuses, Fear effects, and the Confused, Dazed, or Stunned conditions.  If you can obtain one of these puppies, utilizing Energumen becomes a much safer proposition - though you’ll want to watch out if you use a two-handed weapon that you have a hand free to hold it in, and that it doesn’t get squished during the rigors of adventuring.  Also, you can cast Calm Emotions with it 2/day; that might come in handy if you (probably rightfully) end up angering anyone during your travels.
