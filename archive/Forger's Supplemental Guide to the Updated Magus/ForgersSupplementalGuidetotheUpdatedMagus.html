Welcome

        I welcome you, my fellow Magi, to partake in the wisdom imparted to this simple scroll.  I am Forger, and inspired by Walter, Treantmonk, and even Rogue Eidolon, I too have taken it upon myself to present this simple introduction and walkthrough to Pathfinder’s base class, The Magus.

This guide was heavily inspired by Walter’s Guide to the Magus, as well as by the guides written by Treantmonk.  It is formatted based on Treantmonk’s work.  Walter’s Guide to the Magus is, sadly, no longer fully updated with all the goodies out there for the Magus to enjoy. Since this is the case, I’ve taken it upon myself to reintroduce the Magus, fully updated by books like Mythic Adventures, The Advanced Race Guide, the Advanced Class Guide, and the many great splat books running amok through the playerbase.  However, this is merely a supplement to Walter’s Guide to the Magus.  I strongly recommend you read his work first, before continuing on to this guide.  If you do not, you are going to be missing a significant amount of information, and will lack the basis to fully appreciate this Guide.

        While Walter’s guide has not, as of the writing of this guide, been updated recently, he has a lot of helpful hints and information.  While this guide will cover the basics of Race, attributes, and the Magi’s diverging build paths, once past there and the Magi’s class abilities, it will focus on the material NOT covered by Walter’s guide.  Some of this new material will override or replace suggestions by Walter, most of the rest will merely augment his work.

Introduction

        This guide serves as an introduction to the Path of the Magus, which is a long and complex path not easily understood by all.  Magi are beings of towering intellect, not unlike wizards, but unlike wizards they learn also the path of the sword, the spear, and even the bow.  Magi rely on a combination of sword and sorcerer, might and magic, to get the job done.  They strive to provide versatility, but their primary focus is on the art of war, how best to deliver harm to the enemy.  They don’t possess the raw fighting prowess of the Fighter or the Swashbuckler, but they posses fearsome spell power and a staggering intellect neither can match.  At the same time, they lack the sheer magical prowess of the wizard, replacing a portion of it with true martial skill wizards rarely possess.

Road Signs

        I too am borrowing Treantmonk’s color coding system.  I find it to be a most excellent and easily understood way to grade the various pieces that make up the Magus.

Red: Red is bad.  This is generally the worst option to take under almost, if not all, circumstances.  Seriously, don’t do this, my brothers.  

Orange: Orange is sometimes a good idea.  Occasionally.  Not very often, but for specific builds or ideas, or some situations, it is ok.  If you must take Orange, at the least your brothers will not be totally ashamed of you.

Green: Pretty good.  Decent, even great.  Green is just about always a good choice. You generally won’t go wrong with Green.  You can feel proud to be green.

Blue: Blue is the best choice.  Seriously go blue, always and forever.  Wear your blue with pride, my brothers.

The Path Divides

        The Magus is a cheeky blighter, daring to have quite a few different options for building him.  However, of all the “Good” ways to build the Magus, they generally spread out into two categories based on stats, and two more for “Signature” spell selection.

Strength

        This method, the Strength method, is good at later levels. Magi who focus on str prefer to rely on medium and heavy armor to protect them, something they don’t receive until level 7, and then level 13  The light armor they start with simply won’t get the job done.  Magi who utilize strength can deal much damage in battle, possibly slightly more so than their more dextrous brethren, but at the cost of being more easily injured at the start of their journey, being slower into battle (lower initiative) and having weak reflexes.  Still, at later levels their spells are more than up to the task of enhancing that str even further, to bring pain and terror down upon their enemies.

Dexterity

        Magi who follow the path of dexterity are hard to hit.  They almost always use either a scimitar or a rapier to slice or stab their foes, mixing magic and finesse to become masters of battle.  Finesse Magi are hard to hit early on, quick to the fight (higher initiative), and have excellent reflexes.  However, this costs them between two and three feats to achieve, and leaves them with two dead levels, Seven and Thirteen.  These are the two levels where they gain medium and heavy armor proficiency, respectively.  

Notations will be added through this guide to denote when something is for Strength and when something is for Dexterity.

Shocking Grasp*

        For forever and a day, Shocking Grasp has been THE quintessential Magus spell.  A level one touch attack spell, with a standard action casting time(more on why that’s important later), that scales 1d6 of damage with caster level up to level five, and throws in a +3 to hit vs anyone made of metal, wearing metal, or wielding metal.  Mix in Intensify Spell Metamagic, and it scales up to 10d6 at level ten.  It is useable and useful from level one, and its only flaw is that the spell is affected by spell resistance.  

Snowball*

        In 2013, Paizo published the Pathfinder Player Companion: People of the North.  Tucked away in the pages of this splat book was a level one magus spell, Snowball.  It looked alot like shocking grasp.  It did ice damage, sure, but it was the same level, just a ranged touch, and it scaled the same way.  1d6 per caster level, up to level five, just like shocking grasp.  Instead of a bonus to hit, it could stagger the target for up to one round, if they failed their saving throw.  Making their save wouldn’t affect damage.  But the final straw that made Snowball into the gem that would utterly replace Shocking Grasp was a single bit of text within the spell.  “Spell Resistance: no.”

Spell resistance is the bane of every Magi.  It stops their spells from hurting targets, foils their plots and schemes, and makes the fighter more useful than they are.  The magus has a few ways around it, at its later levels, but early on any kind of spell resistance could potentially stop the Magus cold.  It is then poetic, that a cold type spell is the solution to their dilemma.  

The Astute among you will have no doubt noticed a problem already, however.  Snowball is a ranged touch attack, so how are Magi to use it with things like Spell strike, or in melee?  Well, the answer is a bit annoying.  There is an item, spell strike gloves, that allows the Magus to use ranged touch spells as touch spells.  It works 3 times per day.  Some GMs would allow this item to function all the time, which makes it cost 13K instead of 8K.  

Calculations: To make an item useable a limited number of times per day change to having unlimited uses, one takes the original price, and multiplies it by the number of times per day over 5.  So ⅗ * X=8000.  Multiply both sides by 5 to eliminate the fraction, so 3x=40000.  Divide both sides by 3.  x=13333.  There’s your price for an infinite use version.  Many Magi shy away from this option because of of the cost and the reliance on an item.  Those that don’t will benefit from never having to be concerned by Spell Resistance while trying to kill the enemy with Snowball.

While both of these spell options are potent, there are clear cases of when to use which.  Shocking Grasp is much better for games starting at level one, because it starts good out of the gate, while Snowball takes many levels to be useable.  As such, Snowball will see much more use with Strength builds starting at later levels, and shocking grasp will show up on Finesse builds at early levels.  Obviously, for those with patience, either spell can work with either path, but these patterns will be most frequent.  

Notation: The Myrmidarch is an orange archetype, giving up much casting versatility and arcana for one big gain, Ranged Spellstrike.  Prior to Snowball, the Magus possessed a terrible count of good ranged touch spells.  With Snowball on hand, this archetype can now imitate the traditional Magi using Shocking Grasp, but with range (or the non traditional taking the time and effort to make Snowball useable in Melee), dramatically upgrading its effectiveness.  It becomes a Green Archetype.

Added Notation: The Eldritch Archer is a newer archetype that also grants Ranged Spellstrike.  However, it has none of the drawbacks of the Myrmidarch and a lot of benefits, and is the best way to make a range focused Magus.

Races of the Magi

        Now that you’ve got the basic options, Str vs Dex, Shocking Grasp vs Snowball, it is time to take a gander at what player race your Magi will fit best as.  

Amongst the Core races, the big three are Humans, Half Elves, and Elves.

Humans grant a floating +2 to any ability score, meaning you can buff Strength or Dex or Buff Intelligence.  The bonus feat is awesome, but Magus doesn’t really need the skill points, even if they are nice.  

Half Elves have that same lovely +2 floating about, and they can ditch skill focus for Exotic Weapon Proficiency to take things like the Falcata, or the Katana or the Wakizashi.  They also have a lovely alternate race option called Dual Minded, if you don’t need an exotic weapon.  Dual Minded gives you a +2 to Will Saves, which is always welcome.

Elves make excellent Magus along the Dexterity path, but poor Magus on the path of Strength.  They boost Dexterity and Intelligence, both of the Magi’s primary abilities, but have a weak constitution.  Strength Magi need their constitution badly because they are hit more easily early on, and elves do not boost their strength, giving two reasons not to take elves if you follow the path of strength.

Dwarves are sturdy, hard to kill, but neither smart nor stupid.  Half Orcs are inferior to Half elves, but superior to what follows, Gnomes, and Halflings.

Outside of Core, the Aasimar, The Fetchling, the Tiefling, the Drow, the Lashunta (male), and the Samsaran are powerful contenders.

The Fetchling is graceful, dextrous, and charismatic.  They lack in wisdom, but their many powerful racial abilities provide much added power to the Magus.

The Drow are in a similar position to the Fetchling, offer the same ability buffs, as well as built in spell resistance.

The Aasimar provides good ability buffs (though not to core abilities), as well as fantastic resistances and a 1/day daylight spell, lowlight AND darkvision.

The Tiefling is another good option for a Finesse magi as it boosts dexterity and intelligence, but penalizes charisma. The option of getting a tail in exchange for fiendish sorcery is good as there is a feat that allows the tail to hold wands and rods . The Tiefling can also switch around its ability score bonuses with a feat (though some GMs will allow you to do this without the feat) to gain bonuses to strength instead of to dex.  In addition to all this it has excellent elemental resistances.

The Samsaran provides a somewhat different option.  It has weak constitution, but boosts intelligence AND wisdom.  It resists death effects, and has some fun skill bonuses and spell like abilities.  Its real value though, comes by sacrificing its skill bonuses to gain Mystic Past Life. This grants knowledge of spells remembered from previous lives in which this particular samsaran was a spell caster.  They can be any spells of levels the Samsaran’s current class(es) could cast, and must match the type of spell (arcane or divine).  This dramatically alleviates some of the Magi’s greatest weakness, its limited spell list.  Note that you get a number of spells equal to 1 + your intelligence modifier at level 1.  Later increases to intelligence do not allow you to remember additional sells.

Lashunta (male): Male Lashunta are one of the only races to combine a bonus to strength and intelligence.  This alone would make them very valuable as a Magi, however they also gain bonuses to knowledge skills, limited telepathy, and even useful spell like abilities.  They have one interesting annoyance… they do not begin play speaking common.  However, as a Magi with a high intelligence score, this is easy to remedy.

The Distribution of Ability

For those who follow Strength: The Magi of Strength must focus most on their strength and intellect.  Follow this suggestion of priority: Str>Int>Con>Wis>Cha>Dex  Why is Dex black?  Because unless you want combat reflexes (which needs a 13) you will NEVER need more than a 12 in dex.  If you take a race with a con penalty, switch the priority of con and int.  You might also switch them if you’re particularly early on in your adventures, and your level is not near or past seven.  Otherwise, with your defenses shored up by medium or better armor, a higher intelligence will provide more spells and better save DCs on your spells.  

For those who follow Finesse: The Finesse Magus has a different set of priorities than the Strength.  Priority: Dex>Int>Con>Wis>Str>Cha With better consistent AC, you generally won’t have to worry about Con nearly as much as a strength magus, so focus on Int.  The reason str is orange, and trumps Cha, is that str is needed for carry capacity, which is especially important in early games.  An 11 or 12 is perfect in this regard, it gives you just enough capacity to wear light armor, wield a weapon, and carry your gear, but it really isn’t otherwise important.  It literally does nothing else… which still makes it more useful than charisma.  

If you happen to have the points to spare, or rolled very well, feel free to play around with this guide’s suggestions and consider higher Charisma for things like diplomacy, intimidate, bluff, or perform checks.  These flavorful skills frequently prove useful down the line.

A Lesson in Capacity

        The Magus has a number of class abilities to provide it power and versatility.  This is the part of the walkthrough where we discuss how each of these effects the Magi’s capacity to be what it can be.

        Spells: The most potent and versatile of the Magi’s abilities is spellcasting.  The vast majority of your spells are focused on either buffing or damage.  Necromancy, Transmutation, Evocation, some conjuration, a little illusion, a little abjuration, those are the kinds of things the Magus can perform.  Magus have the benefit of preparing spells, which is often considered the superior brand of spell casting.  For the Magus, it can be the better option if certain methods or “tricks” are utilized.*  

However, thanks to an archetype that appeared in The Advanced Class Guide, the Magus can now utilize spontaneous spell casting akin to that of the sorcerer.  Of course, to call this archetype the Magus is something of a stretch, since it effectively rewrites the entire Class.  Charisma instead of intelligence, no spell recall, no spell knowledge, instead Bloodrager Bloodlines.  It can use spell combat (covered later) only by spending points from what is, effectively, its arcane pool (also covered later).  It is, in short, almost an entirely new class, requiring a very different approach to the normal Magus.  As such, it will not be covered by THIS guide.

This Guide does not currently cover the Magus Archetypes provided in Occult Adventures, but consider the Esoteric sub-optimal.  Excellent if you want a monk type Magus, but expect to come up short compared to more traditional magi.

On the other hand, Mindblade is a very fun and much better method of being a spontaneous Magus compared to Eldritch Scion.

Arcane Pool:  This is probably the second or third most versatile ability the Magus has.  It can activate your arcana abilities, augment your concentration checks, and best of all, provide enhancement bonuses to your sword.  It can also provide things like Keen, which is frankly, fantastic and wonderful.  

Cantrips:* For most other classes, Cantrips would be, at best, green.  They’re nice, they never run out, they give you a couple useful things to do.  But they are weak, and limited in capacity.  Most classes cannot get more use out of them than that, but a unique combination of certain features allows a more frequent and powerful use of Cantrips, subject to GM discretion.  Note anything with an *.

Spell Combat: What makes spell combat so special?  Action economy.  We Magi were the only class who could make full attacks with weapons and cast spells at the same time without quicken spell.  The warpriest can cast spells on itself only, but the Magi?  We can target ourselves, our enemies, our friends… we can cast haste, fly, Monstrous Physique, elemental body, disintegrate, web, colorspray, vanish…  That gives us incredible versatility.  Spell Combat also has a nifty ability, if you’re willing to risk it.  You can take a penalty on atks up to your intelligence modifier, and gain a bonus to concentration checks when casting defensively. Be aware, you take a -2 penalty on all attacks when using spell combat, as though you were two weapon fighting.

Spell Strike: This is a nice little augment.  You give up the free touch attack granted by casting a touch spell, and instead you can deal damage with that spell by hitting the target with your weapon.  When doing so, you use the weapon’s critical threat range, but the multiplier is only x2.  This means it is harder to hit your target… but you can deal more damage to them.  You can even spellstrike as part of spell combat, to deal as much damage as possible to your enemies.  Read this: Brother Grick’s Guide to Spell Striking.  

Magus Arcana:  Magus Arcana range from red to blue, depending on which Arcana you select.  You can refer to Walter’s guide to the Magus for most Arcana, I will only cover the Arcana he does not.  

Aquatic Agility: If you are in a water based campaign or frequently around water, this could be a good Arcana.  Otherwise, there are better ways to breath underwater.

Arcane Dealer: For those of my brothers who wish to emulate Gambit, or utilize the myrmidarch archetype, thusly pursuing the path of the ranged Magus, this Arcana, which grants the Deadly Dealer Feat, could be exceedingly useful.  For others, it is an alright option at its best, and there are better choices out there.

Arcane Deed:  This Arcana, which requires you to take another Arcana that is anywhere from questionable to useful, ranges just as broadly. If you use a rapier (or take the slashing grace feat), you can gain the benefits a swashbuckler deed. Most are useable to the Finesse Magus, and a few are even useful to the path of strength, but they are in many ways a distraction from valuable Magus Arcana that are definitely useful.

Arcane Scent: This arcana, brothers, is frankly wasteful.  If you have no tracker, nor the power to summon one, it might be worthwhile, but it can only track other spellcasters, drastically limiting its power.

Divinatory Strike: When you truly need to know about your enemy, you may find a use for this.  Be wary though, for this ability will work fewer than one time in three, because you must first threaten, and then confirm, a critical against your foe to use it.  Inquisitors do it better, as do some rangers and many bards.  Your own knowledge skills should often be up to the task without this meager aid.

Flamboyant Arcana: This Arcana takes some effort to use, but will benefit any Magi who puts the needed effort into it.  Be careful of it, my brothers, for without that effort it is a trap of wasted Arcana and wasted arcane pool points.  It requires you to expend a point from the arcane pool, burn an attack of opportunity, and then defeat the attack roll of your opponent, something we are at a disadvantage for, being that we receive few innate bonuses to our attacks, and start behind with our 3/4ths BAB.  Arcane Accuracy can help make this usable, but do not ever expect to rely on this except to ward off the most dangerous of blows.  It is slightly more useful to our Finesse brothers who have taken Combat Reflexes.  It also allows us to obtain Arcane Deed, if we so desire.

Ki Arcana: If you multiclass, brothers, which you should not, and that multiclass grants you a ki pool, this arcana will be useful to you.  It remains yellow simply because it encourages you to do something you should not.

Natural Spell Combat: This arcana is useful to those possessing or wishing to focus on natural attacks.  This, like multi-classing, however is a tearful idea, one which wrenches at my heart, for to spell strike with natural attacks is such a waste, except at greatest need.  Tieflings should note that the they can use this with their racial alternates of claws and bite.

Rakshasa’s fortune: This might seem like a great thing, being that the Magus can improve the duration and benefits of polymorph spells like Monstrous Physique.  But it’s a game of chance, and the potential to halve the duration and lose some of the benefits of the spell simply isn’t worth it.  The spell’s duration and benefits stand on their own, and taking the chance you might cripple it isn’t worth the risk.

Scroll Mastery: Do you use scrolls brothers?  Probably you do.  Afterall, you only have so many spell slots, and Use Magic Device is on your class skill list.  This allows you to calculate the DC of your scrolls based on your intelligence modifier.

Spell Trickery: This… could be useful with the right spell, against the right foe… but you can only use it once a day.  If you like illusion or enchantment, you can go for this, otherwise, please don’t my brethren.

Spell Scars: This sounds good… but there is little wrong with actual scrolls, and there are other ways to get this same effect.  Avoid this, brothers.

Spell Recall: Reuse your spell slot.  Never need to double prepare spells again.  Just get that spell back, and use it again.  When knowledge pool hits, this gets better.

Bonus Feat: We always welcome feats.  We never have enough feats.  Metamagic, Item creation, and combat feats.  Enjoy, brothers.  Enjoy.

Knowledge Pool: Do you see it brothers?  The true power of this feature?  When we use it… we now have our entire spell list, and we can keep it.  One day at a time, we add spells we don’t know to our spell book, until we have every single spell on our spell list in our spellbooks.  All of them.  We spend points to treat a spell we don’t have as though it were on our list, prepare it for the day, then scribe it into our spellbook instead of casting it.

Medium Armor: My brothers on the path of Strength, this is for you.  Medium armor.  Finally, proper protection.  Grab some breastplate and have a ball.

Improved Spell Combat: Remember that attack penalty tradeoff for concentration check bonuses? well you don’t need it as badly now, since you get a free +2 now.

Fighter Training: You count as half a fighter.  Finally, a little recognition of our martial prowess.  Not as much as we deserve, but we’ll take it.  We can grab Weapon Focus, then GREATER Weapon Focus (we need that attack bonus), or disruptive if we really want to.  

Improved Spell Recall: Spell Recall was awesome.  There’s an improved version?  The cost is halved, and you can rapidly prepare spells from your spellbook that you did not prepare today.  You now have access to every spell you can cast, when you need it.

Heavy Armor: My brothers on the path of strength, you now have 3 more points of AC.  Your AC won’t get much better.  You’re almost unstoppable now.  Almost.

Greater Spell Combat: We appreciate these gifts that are given to us.  Our bonus to concentration is now double the penalty we take.  We will NOT fail concentration checks again.

Counterstrike: I admit my brothers, I am not impressed with this ability.  I see no need for yet another ability focusing on attacking our fellow spell casters.  Still, it does help us hurt the enemy, though our brothers on the path of strength, who will have the fewest attacks of opportunity, will find not all that much use for this power.

Greater Spell Access: A Magi’s true Capstone.  Two spells each of levels zero through six from the wizard’s spell list.  Grab Contingency, Permanency, and many more.  Power is within our hands now, brothers.  

True Magus:  We already beat our concentration checks.  Now we don’t have to make them.  We get a free +2 to attacks or to the DC of our spells, or to overcoming spell resistance (oh hey, if we use Snowball, we don’t really need that as often).

A Cornucopia of Skills

The MagI do not have a lot of base skill points (2+Int per level), but we derive our power from high intelligence, so we will never lack for skill points.  We have many skills to utilize and even the skills that are not our class skills we have the points to put in.  Of all the skills we do have… we don’t have Perception, the most used skill in the game.  Put points in it.  If you have traits (and hope to the gods you do, brothers*), grab the trait that grants perception as a class skill.

Fly:  Fly is on our spell list.  We will be flying.  We need this, we want this, we have this.

Perform (dance): This is only for our Finesse Brothers.  We need 2 ranks of this to qualify for Dervish Dancer, a feat I will detail soon.  If you use a Scimitar, you need Dervish Dancer, so you need 2 ranks here.  Also, Flavor.

Spellcraft: Every spellcaster worth his salt maxes this out.  They need it to prepare spells sometimes, to use scrolls, to identify magic and magical items, and to write spells into our spellbooks.

Knowledge: Many people underestimate the power of successful knowledge checks.  It’s a class skill, we have lots of points, and we’re an intelligence based class.  We got this.  We OWN this.  There is almost nothing we cannot find out inside our given knowledge skills.

Use Magic Device: Scrolls, wands, rods, and all manner of tools.  We can use them.  We have the intellect to put the points into this.  Make use of it.  

Climb: You will climb.  Frequently.  Not every session, but you will get a lot of use out of this skill if you have it.  You will wish you had it if you don’t.  

Intimidate:  You are probably looking at me like I am crazy, brethren.  Did I not instruct you to ignore charisma?  Well, there is a feat, and a trait that make this skill useful to us.  Intimidating prowess lets us use strength in place of charisma, while the trait Bruising Intellect lets us use our Intelligence.  Yes, you read that correctly.  Our Intellect finally starts earning the respect it deserves amongst these low minded drones.

Swim, ride, profession, and craft: These skills are all red in most campaigns.  There are a few, often those involving riding a lot, sitting in one place alot, or being on the water, where they become yellow, but otherwise, these can be safely ignored by most Magi.  If you have spare points, put some in Swim and Ride, and any craft, such as Craft Alchemy, that you find interesting.  

Feats, and how we use them

This section will, excepting a few essential feats, focus on the feats introduced after Walter created his guide to the Magus.  

Essential Feats:

Weapon Focus: This is good for us, as it provides us a boost to attack.  More importantly, if we want to use the Rapier, we need this to get another essential feat.  All brothers will benefit from this feat and its Greater version.

Weapon Finesse: For our Finesse brothers, without this they will not hit the enemy.  Dex to attack instead of strength.  Use this to get either Dervish Dancer or Fencing Grace.

Dervish Dancer: This lets you use dex to atk and to damage when wielding a scimitar.  It’s a feat tax, but a small one since you needed weapon finesse anyways, so it’s really only burning two skill points.  Still, it does constrain your available feats.  That said, it’s considered one of the best choices simply because the Finesse path of the Magi works from early to late.

Fencing Grace: This is like Dervish Dancer, but has a bigger feat tax.  You need weapon focus (Rapier).  That’s not so bad, since you benefit from weapon focus, but it takes longer to get it if you are not human, so be aware of that before choosing rapier.  That said, for those of you who want to use a rapier, take this.

Power Attack: Some might call this yellow, and it will never be more than Green.  Strength using brothers benefit from power attack’s added damage.  Our Finesse brothers need a 13 str to gain power attack, a high and wasteful cost.

Intensify Spell (+1):* My brothers, this feat is necessary to make Shocking Grasp or Snowball reach their full potential.  The extra 5d6 damage, only gained past 5th level, keeps those spells dangerous much longer than they otherwise would be.  It is a mere +1 level adjustment, so we can, at 10th level, deal 10d6 dmg with a 2nd level spell.  If we have traits (and hope to the gods we do, brothers*), we can make this lower leveled.

Empower Spell (+2): This feat can be a trap, but if you wish to prepare just one or two spells that will deal a much greater degree of damage, it might be worth the time.  Maximize is superior, but you can use Empower and maximize on the same spell, so be aware.  Keep it in mind, but don’t take it unless you have the room or really need that extra power, or to qualify for Spell Perfection.

Maximize Spell (+3): Maximum spell damage.  60 flat damage with intensified shocking grasp or snowball.  60 + an extra 5d6 if you add empowered spell.  That’s nothing to sneeze at.  It is a +3 spell level adjustment.  +5 with Empowered.  

Piercing Spell: I admit I am not certain this feat is green.  If you are an elf, there is a better feat for overcoming spell resistance, or just take Spell penetration and greater and you don’t have to adjust spell slots.  If you have snowball, this is red.  If you have shocking grasp, I’m willing to allow this to be Green.  

Spell Perfection: This feat will only affect one spell (Snowball or Shocking Grasp).  It negates the spell level adjustment caused by a single metamagic feat (typically either empowered or Maximized) when applied to that spell.  It will negate the adjustment of only one metamagic feat, so use it on the feat with the highest adjustment.  

New Feats: I’m only covering feats my fellow Magi can feasibly take (and nothing only available via Eldritch Scion)

Amateur Investigator: You gain a small pool of inspiration for use on knowledge, Linguistics, and spellcraft.  You won’t generally need it.  Or the studied combat taking this feat qualifies you for late down the road.  

Studied combatant and Improved:  My brothers, you do not need this.  Arcane Accuracy gives you all the attack bonus you need, so if you don’t have that, take Extra Arcana instead.

Amateur Swashbuckler: Flamboyant Arcana and Arcane Deed do this, and better.  Avoid this, brothers.

Barroom Brawler: This basically grants you a flexible combat feat once a day when you need it.  It can be worth taking over any other combat feat simply because you can then just go GET that other combat feat when you need it.  Still, don’t take this over a blue feat.

Battlecry: This is a feat for bards.  It uses charisma, we don’t have charisma, and we are not typically the party buff. Let the bard take this, avoid it yourselves brothers.

Befuddling Strike: Don’t take this, you shouldn’t even qualify for this feat in most games.

Believer’s Boon: You get a cleric 1st level domain power… once a day.  Not really all that helpful.

Believer’s Hands: Don’t have a healer?  This is a maybe.  Otherwise, no, not really.  We are not healers brothers.

Canny Tumble: Our Finesse brothers might have acrobatics.  If they do, they might find this feat benefits them tactically.  First they have to make a tumble, but if they succeed, they get a +2 bonus to attack.

Disheartening Display: If you would have taken Dazzling display ( a prerequisite) anyways, and have either intimidating prowess or Bruising Intellect, this feat is Green.  Otherwise, it is red.

Force Dash: Fact is, people underestimate speed.  Force Dash is relatively easy to qualify for, and it gives players a big boost in speed they can use to move around the battlefield or escape danger.  

Lunging Spell Touch: We can use Lunge with spell strike to the same effect, except all our attacks can also benefit, not just the touch attack with the spell, so please avoid this trap brothers.

Riving Strike: Since Arcane Strike is fairly useful to Magi, many of us have it.  Those of us who do will benefit from this feat, which makes it easier to successfully cast spells with saves upon our enemies.  By hitting them with our weapon while Arcane Strike is active, they take a -2 on saves against our spells for one round.

Seize Advantage: Requires the Parry and Riposte deed we can gain through Flamboyant Arcana.  We can use our enemy’s power attack against them.  Won’t come up too much, but power attack does make it easier for our parry to succeed, so this is a nice little bonus to have.

Slow Faller:  You have feather fall, or you should.  That said, our Finesse brothers CAN benefit from this (but our strength brothers likely never would), so it makes yellow for the Finesse Magi.

Stalker’s Focus: Most of the animal focus you could take, you can emulate through a spell more frequently and without costing you a feat.  This one is unnecessary my brothers.  A waste.

Steadfast Personality: We ditched Charisma.  This won’t help any of us.

Twinned Feint: We don’t bluff, we don’t feint.

Material Drawn from the Long Ago Ages

There are a trio of fun feats to consider, drawn from ages long past.  Drawn from a collection of Tomes of the third and one half edition of the oldest role playing game are these three beauties.  

The first is a simple feat called Elemental spell from Complete Arcane.  A +0 level adjustment that changes the energy type of a spell.  You select what energy type it changes the modified spell to when first selecting the feat, and this cannot be changed.  Select either Cold if you use Shocking Grasp or select Electricity if you use Snowball.  The true purpose of this feat is to qualify us for the next feat.

Elemental Admixture is a staggering +4 adjustment to the modified spell’s level, coming to us from Complete Arcane (use Spell perfection to negate this).  In exchange, when applied, it adds a number of dice of size and damage equal to amount of dice of damage the spell already deals.  Apply this to Shocking Grasp or Snowball to double our damage.  It stacks with both Intensified and Empowered Spell.  The new dice will deal energy damage matching the energy type selected for Elemental Spell, which is the prerequisite of this feat.

Last is a feat called Arcane Thesis, of the Player’s Handbook II.  Arcane Thesis work similar to Spell Perfection.  You select a single spell, as always either Shocking Grasp or Snowball. Whenever that spell is modified by metamagic feats, each metamagic feat adjusts the spell’s level one less than normal (to a minimum of 0).  Intensified cost nothing, Empowered costs 1,  Maximized 2, and Elemental Admixture 3 (and Elemental Admixture can be negated by Spell Perfection).

All Those Beautiful New Spells

I have already covered your essential spells* Snowball and Shocking Grasp.  From here, I will cover only the spells introduced after Walter’s Guide to the Magus was published.  Only one Magus spell was added in Mythic Adventures, so that will be covered in the Mythic Section.

Adjustable Polymorph: Alter Self isn’t worth it to you.  So neither is this.

Aggressive Thundercloud: This version could be fun for dealing with small fries and causing chaos, but its far from great.

Aggressive Thundercloud, Greater: Still not green, but better than before.

Air Geyser: I like this spell.  I like the imagery of blasting your enemy into the air, and letting them fall to earth.  You can make them take quite a lot of falling damage this way… if they fail the save.  That can make for quite a bit of damage.  Not fantastic, but one of the most fun damage spells I’ve encountered in a while.

Blade Lash: You want to trip somebody?  This is a good way to do it.  That +10 is pretty cool.

Blurred Movement: A level one Blur spell… when you move.  Use it when you need to get away, otherwise, don’t.  This is an emergency spell brothers.  Use it that way.

Disguise Weapon: … I don’t recommend this spell.  You are better off hiding the weapon if you need to sneak it in, or using a disguise self spell, or smuggling, or getting help from the Skill monkey that has disguise.

Euphoric Cloud: This spell in many respects replaces Fog Cloud, but only in situations where you won’t be placing it on top of you.  You are usually going to be a living creature, and as such are subject to the effects.  For containing foes or fleeing battle or blocking a direction at early levels, this is awesome.

Extreme Flexibility: When you need to escape sticky situations, this spell is great.  Put it in the spellbook, or grab it with Spell Knowledge or Improved Spell recall when you think you’ll need it.  Otherwise, leave it alone.

Flaming Sphere, Greater: Flaming sphere was an… alright spell for its level.  This one isn’t particularly better for its level.

Glue Seal: Think of this as a Web spell at first level, crossed with Grease. You can lock down enemy movements, stick things together, or just make it hard to move around.  

Heart of the Metal: This is an excellent spell.  Gets you around three different annoying kinds of damage reduction.  That’s really good in most major fights, especially as you work your way further along the campaign.  

Line in the Sand: Replace combat reflexes once per spell for a few rounds.

Long Arm: Gain reach.

Molten Orb: Deal splash fire damage that sticks around.  Kind of like Acid Arrow.

Monkey Fish:This spell can provide similar benefits to Aquatic agility, the magus Arcana.  Situationally useful, so keep it handy in the spellbook.

Nauseating Trail: Nauseate anyone who crossed your trail.  You can obliterate fast tails or enemies in combat, leave a line between you and them and they cannot charge.  I give it a green.

Phantom Blood:  This would be yellow for a bloodrager.  It’s red for you, since you so rarely meet the requirements of this spell.

River Whip: Create a whip made of water.  Extra damage against creatures with the fire subtype, or to put out fires.  If this was a level one spell, i’d like it better.  As is… keep it handy, you might find a use for it.

Silver Darts: Hit something that’s got DR/silver and low armor/natural armor.  Sometimes useful, but overall, there are better.

Sonic Scream:  The first cone is great… having to use the others to end the spell is not.  No on this spell unless you REALLY need that sonic damage.

Sunder Breaker: Orange.  Defend against sunder, otherwise, can’t even use it.  Strong when you can.

Sundering Shards: This spell is green at very early levels.  Give it to the heavy hitter and have fun. After that it’s red.

Stone Discus: Deal up to 12d6 against a single target, or some combination of 3 sets of 4d6 against other targets, and can count as magic, silver, bludgeoning, slashing, cold iron, and adamantium.  I like it.

Thunderstomp: Remember Bladelash?  This spell is at least as good, if not better thanks to its range.  It’s still only tripping, but hey, for a level one spell, this is pretty cool.  You’ll have to watch out for spell resistance, so this one might be a little less awesome than Blade Lash if they’re close to you.

Thunderstomp, Greater: At later levels, when you have more third level spells?  This is awesome.  Trip a whole bunch of enemies, all at once.  This is phenomenal. Still allows SR, but multiple trips for one spell is just fun brothers.

Time Shudder: Brothers, I cannot condone a gamble like this.  You can’t know who will be hasted or slowed.  Too many chances for this to go wrong instead of right.  

Wave Shield: Instant DR or fire resistance against one attack.  Sure, go for it.  It could save your life.

Vampiric Shadow Shield: This spell can be… useful.  Very useful.  Deal damage to attackers and heal… if you get hit, which is not ideal.  Use it against something you assume will hit you, like against major enemies.  Be wary of the Spell Resistance.

        Gear of the Magi

        Your weapon choices are limited.  
Longsword, Scimitar, Rapier, Katana, Falcata, Kukri, Wakizashi.  One handed weapons with good crit ranges.  Use the Longsword or the Falcata brothers, when you cannot expect to be able to use all that many spells for damage.  The more often you must deal damage without spells, the better the longsword and falcata serve.  Be aware, the Falcata and Katana and Wakizashi require exotic weapon proficiency, which only half elves and humans can gain at level one.

        Walter’s guide can advise you on all your other gear, for the most part.  Trust him, he is wise.

        Magus Traits

        The Magus has a variety of trait options, but I will list the strongest of them. I will include which trait family they come from, as one cannot take more than one trait from a given family.

Anatomist: A bonus to confirm criticals.  Combat Trait.  This is a huge boon, since we possess a broad crit range.  Anything that helps us confirm it is valued.

Bruising Intellect: Intelligence to intimidate checks. Social Trait.  This means we can actually use intimidate, brothers.  Time to shake the enemy.

Eyes and Ears of the City: Perception is now a class skill, and you gain a +1 Bonus.  Religion Trait.  I see where once I was blind, brothers.

Finding your Kin: Pick an NPC and a class.  You were raised by that NPC.  You gain a +1hp and +1skill point each time you take a level in that Class.  The chosen class is also a favored class(in addition to any other favored class).  Campaign Trait (Legacy of Fire).

Focused Mind: A bonus to concentration checks.  Magic Trait.  All those concentration checks we have to make.

Magical Lineage:* Pick one spell (shocking grasp or Snowball).  Whenever you modify that spell with a metamagic feat, the resulting adjusted spell slot is reduced by one.  (See Errata/FAQ)  Magic Trait. One of the best of all traits, this allows us to have first level intensified shocking grasp.

Metamagic Master:* Repeat almost verbatim with Magical Lineage.  However, See Lack of Errata with this trait. Regional Trait.

Reactionary: Grants +2 bonus to Initiative.  Combat Trait.

Seeker: Perception is now a class skill, and you gain a +1 bonus.  Social Trait.

        Drawbacks for my brothers

        Drawbacks are similar to the flaws from the long ago third and a half edition. They grant a bonus trait instead of a bonus feat, but the intent is the same.   As there as so few drawbacks, I will cover all of them.

        Anxious: you take a -2 on diplomacy, a skill you do not have, and speak slowly at a whisper.  There are no serious penalties to this trait.

        Attached: You have a randomly selected object of attachment.  when it is in danger or stolen, you take a -1 on will saves, and a -2 on saves vs fear. A reasonable Master of the Dungeon will give you something small and protectable, or difficult to destroy, as the object of your attachment.  This is red if the that DM will constantly threaten said object, and green if they are reasonable and will not.

        Avarice: This drawback is red if you need the aid another action.  For those among my brothers who almost never use aid another anyways (be warned, this includes assisting others with skill checks), simply take an equal share as always and accept the penalty, continue playing, and enjoy the bonus trait.

        Burned: A -1 on saves vs. fire.  Additional penalties whenever you are near any sort of fire.  Torches, camp fires, burning builds, fire balls, flaming weapons… This is not a good drawback to take my brother Magi.  

        Condescending: Being that we do not use diplomacy and are frequently condescending to those less intelligent, this drawback at first seems an excellent fit.  However, being that many of our brothers use intimidate, this drawback is terrible for them.  A five point penalty is harsh for anyone.  Use this only if you do not use intimidate, and you should use intimidate.

        Cruelty: This drawback will benefit some of you, while others will suffer from it.  It is for those who are, as the name implies, cruel.  Those who do not care for their brothers or their companions, and find joy in the kill.

        Dependent: I find this drawback to be the same as Anxious.  Being that we do not use diplomacy, we shall hardly, if ever, be affected by it.

        Doubt: This drawback imposes a -4 penalty on skill and ability checks that we fail, ensuring we will do so again.  Avoid this except when you need more drawbacks, or if you gain it from the results of another drawback, such as Attached.

        Envy: We are not adept at theft.  Still, some among us will find benefit to this drawback because of their basic nature.

        Family Ties: Surprisingly, this is green if family is part of the party as a PC.  If the family is an NPC, it is yellow instead.  It becomes red if the family is outside the party.  

Fey-taken: The Fey are not to be trusted, and neither is this drawback.  It’s penalties are far too common and costly to be worth any bonus trait.

Forgetful: Equipment of any kind is valuable.  Money is more so.  The chance to randomly lose any of these things is too much to bear.

Foul Brand: Place the brand on your face, and accept the penalties to skills we do not normally use, or can cover for with powerful magic such as Disguise self.  

Haunting Regret: A penalty to concentration, never mind the rest, is utterly unacceptable.

Headstrong: You must play your alignment to the hilt.  Contradictions are unacceptable, but in addition to the roleplay opportunities of this drawback, we have intimidate and magical and martial prowess.  An acceptable risk.

Hedonistic: We must have reward, pleasure, or entertainment.  Delicious meals, stories, good company, payment for a job well done… the ways to fulfill this drawback are endless.  Enjoy brothers.

Loner: I do not support this drawback.  We should work with our allies, not apart, and being unable to assist them properly will be detrimental to us and to them.

Lovesick: This drawback seems like Attached, at first, but is trickier.  You must stay near your crush, even if they hate you, to avoid gaining the doubt drawback.  If you can (such as a crush/lover within the party) this is perhaps acceptable.

Mark of Slavery: We take penalties to other skills when we fail a skill, unless we retry.  But the penalties only last one round.  Acceptable.

Meticulous: We are already meticulous prepared spell casters, and we have many skill points.  We can often bear this burden.

Misbegotten: If we do not anticipate making many dexterity based skill checks, or have a high dexterity, we might be able to withstand this burden as well.  Otherwise, we are better off with other drawbacks.

Naive: I have not seen nor heard of many using dirty trick combat maneuvers or frequent improvised weapons, brothers.  That tells me this is a gamble too risky, for such would come as a fast surprise and an easy advantage against those who take this drawback.  Risk it if you must, but I advise against it.

Nervous: Take 8 instead of take 10 for any check with a penalty for failure.  I would not unless my bonuses were incredible or I almost never used skill checks.  I would recommend the same to you, my brother Magi.

Oblivious: Perception is the most used skill in the game, and we are not good at it.  We are not bad, thanks to our plethora of skill ranks, but we are not good.  Even if we gained the trait to make it a class skill, we are not great.  The added penalty to sense motive, a useful skill to gain once we have the points to spare, makes this an unworthy choice.

Oppressive Expectations: Similar to Mark of Slavery, but the penalties persist longer.  They apply only to the skill in question though.  I would not take both, but one or the other should be fine.

Overprotective: A fine choice for ranged Magi, and potentially even more close range Magi.  Not incredible, but protecting our comrades is important.  A fine choice.

Paranoid: Trust no one, goes the axiom.  A DC 15 (very beatable) instead of a DC 10 for our friends to use Aid Another on us.  Considering this will most often either not come up, or come up in skill use (where bonuses are often highest), this is a wonderful drawback.

Power-Hungry: Flavorful, but dangerous.  Do not let yourself be controlled, and do not take this drawback.

Pride: If they insult us, and they are not friendly, kill them or avoid them.  If they are, they will apologize.  Either way, a -2 on diplomacy is more than acceptable.

Provincial: I dislike the flavor’s mix with the Magi, and I do not recommend the penalty to sense motive.  That said, this is otherwise in perfect line with my blue drawbacks.

Sentimental: penalties to perception and reflex… never.  As I said with Oblivious, this is bad.

Shadow-scarred: penalty on saves in dim light.  Would happen frequently.  I strongly advise against this.

Sheltered: Another I cannot recommend.  You are shaken at less than one half max health, which will also happen often.

Sleepy: Sleep effects on common early on, and rare late in the game.  There are also multiple races entirely immune to them.  Strictly speaking, nothing prevents those races from taking this drawback.

Spooked: Be shaken by a host of very common foes.  No brothers, don’t accept such silliness.

Superstitious: This is… acceptable.  Roll saves vs harmless spells.  You will sometimes not heal or sometimes not gain buffs.  Risk it if you wish.  It won’t affect your own magic.

Tainted Spirit: A dangerous gamble is this, brothers.  Cruel masters of the dungeon will throw you into combat, wait until you become fatigued, then hit you again.  Some may find it acceptable, if they have the fortitude.

Umbral Unmasking: A flavor and story drawback, you cast no shadow or a monstrous shadow.  You are not required to be evil, but many will think you as such.  

Unlearned: Flavorwise, this is atrocious for a Magi.  Penalty wise, many magi won’t be making untrained checks.  As they are not, this penalty is… not one.

Vain: You make no charisma based checks except intimidate or Use Magic Device, and for some of you, that no longer qualifies as charisma based.  Take it if you wish.

Warded against nature: Never be attacked by wild animals, and friendly ones must be coaxed to approach you.  Not particularly hazardous.  

Xenophobic: My usual diplomacy recommendations apply, as do my cautions regarding sense motive.

Zealous: Take a big penalty on attacks and do a tiny bit more damage.  Not worth our time or effort, brothers.

Archetypes

The Card Caster(link): If you’ve wanted to have a ranged Magus, this was it (see Eldritch Archer).  The range won’t be that good (20ft), but that can be augmented.  You get free ranged spellstrike that can strike all spells(touch and ranged).  Your Arcane pool provides brilliant energy for free (which is much better than spending an arcana for it), and you get Speed too.  You get Deadly Dealer for free, and you get a free bonus to your Harrow throwing.  Either an expanded crit range or that plus an improved multiplier.  Not on every throw, but often enough.  And no drawbacks for opposite alignment cards.  IF you wanted to go ranged, this was how you’d do it.   Take a rapier for a melee weapon, maybe burn a feat slot on weapon finesse.  Rapid shot and Manyshot are options to consider, even if they won’t work with spell combat.  And as with Myrmidarch, you’ll want Snowball over Shocking Grasp here.  You don’t need anything to compensate for its ranged touch attack, so bypass that spell resistance and intensify spell away.  

Cons: You lose ALL melee spell strike, but you’ve still got full spell combat, so if you WANT to spend the feats for a Dex build magus, you can still do bloody brilliant in Melee combat while tossing Harrow Cards at enemies to Spellstrike.  Or just full attack with arcane pool augmented Harrow cards.  Your choice.  Grab Arcane strike even though you don’t need it to qualify for Deadly Dealer, Harrow cards have low base damage, so every little bit helps.

Bladed Scarf Dancer: The Bladed Scarf Dancer uses a bladed scarf, a normally two handed weapon with trip and disarm, one handed.  They spell strike with it, and they attack with it, and they ward off grapples with it.  It has a number of abilities based on rolling criticals with the bladed scarf… which crits on a natural 20 only.  It is meant for Dex builds, and grants Canny defense using Magus levels, so you can be assured of fairly good AC, but your damage output is significantly weaker than a normal Dex build magus.  Worse, as noted, it’s special abilities largely rely on something that won’t happen very often… critical hits.  Even worse you give up one spell per level of your spell casting.  It barely replaces anything, however, and therefore stacks with almost any Magus archetype.

Eldritch Scion: This archetype, as noted previously, practically rewrites the Magus into an entirely new class, a much greater change than normal archetypes.  It converts the Magus to a charisma based spontaneous caster.  Replaces many of its abilities (spell recall, knowledge pool, Bonus Feats), for Bloodrager Bloodlines.  This switches up the skills, spell priority and selection, feat selection… it changes a lot and makes Shocking Grasp or Snowball much less powerful than it was before because of how metamagic mixes with spontaneous casting.  That is why I say it would require its own guide.  That said, it’s fairly strong and consistent in and of itself, and I’m certain those who want to can make excellent use of it.

Greensting Slayer: This archetype is incredible.  Most Finesse Magi should consider this archetype unless they desire to be a Bladebound.  This archetype grants half power sneak attack (capping at 5d6), and replaces medium and heavy armor proficiency with Evasion and Improved Evasion.  

Spire defender: This Archetype is for dex magi only.  You get two free bonus feats, a change to weapon proficiencies (including a free exotic weapon proficiency), but lose all armor.  You replace Spell Recall with special temporary skill bonuses to a variety of useful skills.  Fairly good for Dex Magus who don’t want the armor proficiencies, and want to replace them with something.  You can cast in light armor if you pick up light armor proficiency (letting you still wear Celestial Armor).

Eldritch Archer(link): Before this came out, Card Caster was the best way to be a ranged Magus.  Now it’s been replaced by this gem.  There is everything to love and nothing to fear from this archetype.  It’s wonderful.  Everything you could want for being a range based Magus.  It even replaces the fairly worthless Counterstrike ability with a much better one.

Racial Archetypes: There are three racial archetypes for the Magus.

Elemental Knight:  Suli are not good Magi.  That said, if you are going to go Suli, this is a good archetype.  Enhances and augments the Suli’s natural abilities, and grants several Magus Arcana to further enhance them and mix them with Magus.

Fiend Flayer: This archetype is potentially very powerful for Tieflings, but self sacrificing.  You take con damage to restore arcane pool, and can gain an arcana that ensures you are never unarmed, and will always have the right magical weapon to hand.  They also get an evil outsider hate arcana, bypassing their DR once per round by spending points from their arcane pool.  You literally give up nothing for this archetype.  And Tiefling is great for Magi.  Plenty of Good reasons to select this archetype.  If you go ranged, take this too because you’ll not have to worry about getting hit as often, so you’ll probably be able to spare the Con damage more often.

Spell Dancer:  This is a fairly strong Magi Archetype for our elven brethren.  They lose armor beyond light (something most Elven Magi don’t use), and gain insight bonuses to AC that almost makes up for it, as well as movement bonuses and bonuses vs attacks of opportunity.  They gain skill bonuses, and perform dance and Acrobatics, which is excellent  Those ranks in perform dance for Dervish Dance are alot more potent now.  They give up only one painful thing that keeps this archetype from blue status.  They lose the ability to augment their weapon with their arcane pool, instead gaining speed boosts.  Clearly, this is meant to be a mobile Magi.  Despite the loss of free Keen, this is quite a potent Magus Archetype and worth due consideration amongst our Elven Brethren.

        The Mythic Magus

        Mythic Adventures is a tome of power.  Incredible power.  It is rare and hard to obtain, but when the myth becomes real, brothers… you must know the path to take.

Of the many mythic paths open to the Magus, three possess what the Magus needs.  The Champion, the Archmage, and the Trickster.  I’ll cover the bare essentials of these paths, and how to get them.  The rest is up to you, brothers.  find your path, and stick to it.

Be Aware brothers, Mythic power is attainable only if some games, and is not guaranteed.  It is gained solely at the discretion of the Master of the Dungeon, and will mean a massive spike in the power of your enemies.  

Universal path abilities:

        Divine Source (3rd tier): This ability grants the ability to cast spells from 2 domains so long as they are less than your tier once per day as a spell like ability. The second time you take it (after 6th tier) you can add 1 more domain and 2 sub domains to the list. The 3rd time (after 9th tier) you again add 1 more domain and 2 sub domains to the list. That is potentially 30+ spells. Could possibly be more powerful than the mythic ability Mythic spellcasting.

        Legendary Item (Tier 1): You empower an item, granting it various special powers.  You can take this ability again at Tier 3, then again at tier 6.  You need only take it twice to gain the best of the available abilities. You can have a total number of abilities equal to your tier.  The second time you take this ability, the Legendary Item becomes a Minor Artifact.  The third time, it becomes a Major Artifact.

                Foebiting: this doubles your damage on a single hit (this would also double spell strike damage), and increases your critical modifier on the attack.  The Legendary Item must be a minor artifact, and a weapon, to take this ability.

The Archmage: +3 hp per tier, and you can nab Wild Arcana (to swift cast any spell on your spell list) (even if you didn’t prepare it today), and add metamagics to it up to an adjusted spell level equal to the maximum spell level you can cast.  If you have serious issues with Spell Resistance, Arcane Surge might be better, as it makes the target roll saves twice, and gives you bonuses to beat Spell Resistance.

        Path Abilities:

        Competent Caster (Tier 1): Never fail a concentration check to cast defensively again.  Ever.  It does negate some of the Magi’s built in abilities, but never failing a concentration check?  Why would you NOT?  Brothers, please, nab this early!

        Crafting Mastery (Tier 1): gain all crafting feats.  Sweet!  Not amazing for a Magus, yet still strong.

Elemental Bond: Gain resistance to an element, and a +10 bonus to caster level when casting that element.  Electricity or cold for your signature spell, obviously.

Enduring Armor: for Dex Magi, or even strength Magi, this is an amazing path ability.  Armor equal to 3+ your tier.  So that’s a permanent Mage armor that improves over time up to a 13.

Energy Conversion: switch any elemental spell to another element.  Combine this with elemental bond, and watch the magic happen.

Perfect/Rapid Preparation: These are both Green.  Rapidly prepare spells, and you don’t need a spellbook to prepare spells.  Be careful, you still need a spellbook to LEARN spells.

Arcane Potency: Gain more spell slots.  Yes,  that’s a cool feat, brethren.

Mirror Dodge: Avoid an attack entirely and leave behind an illusionary replacement.

Channel Power:This is the best Archmage has to offer, in many respects.  This Path Ability grants a 50% boost in damage when applied to a spell(stacking with things like empower spell), doubles the duration of spells whose durations are greater than a single round, and brothers, it allows that spell to ignore spell resistance, the bane of our existence.  Non-Mythic characters take a -4 penalty on saves, mythic characters half that penalty.  Truly, a trump card (in addition to True Archmage, the path capstone).

The Champion: +5hp per tier, and you can nab Fleet Charge (to gain a move and attack to get you into spell strike range).  The champion only has one big thing our brother Magi need… the 1st tier Always a Chance ability.  It means you do not automatically crit fail on a natural one.  If the roll WOULD hit, despite being a 1, it is NOT a critical failure, and succeeds instead.

The Trickster: +4 hp per tier, and you can still nab Fleet Charge (again, to gain a move and attack, getting you into spell strike and spell combat range).

There is one primary Path Ability you need from trickster.  There is a second beauty you can hunt down.  Nab Fickle Attack 3 times.  Every time you roll for dmg when making an attack with a weapon (note it does not say weapon damage), you treat any 1s as max damage on that die.  Take it twice, treat 1s and 2s as max.  Take it 3 times(the max) and treat all 1s, 2s, and 3s as max.  This applies to both your weapon damage… and any damage from spell strike.  This is almost like a free Maximized Spell, without costing a feat slot or needing to adjust spell slots. It changes the average damage on shocking grasp or snowball from being 3.5 to being 5.5.  In essence, instead of dealing  35 dmg on average with an intensified shocking grasp at level 10, we dole out 55.  20 more points of damage, passing the threshold of massive damage, and potentially killing the foe in a single blow.

In addition to Fickle Attack, take Path Dabbling to get Always a Chance from Champion.

Mythic Feats:

Mythic Spell Lore: You can learn mythic versions of many of the best Magus Spells, making them even better must haves.  

Extra Path ability: Gain an extra path ability.

Dual Path:  Take Dual Path at 1st tier, you can now take from two paths, I suggest Trickster and Archmage, brethren, and you pick up their special path ability as well.

Mythic Weapon Finesse: If you are a Finesse Brother, you can take this to gain dexterity to damage with any weapon that can be used with Weapon Finesse, negating the need to take Fencer’s Grace or Dervish Dancer.  That leaves more regular feat slots available.

While there are many other Mythic Feats, I do not not believe that beyond these four you will need my assistance to grab hold of them.

        Mythic Spells

        Mythic spells are something new.  By Taking Mythic spells (the universal path ability) or Mythic Spell Lore(The mythic feat), you gain a number of Mythic spells equal to your tier, and a new spell at each new tier.  These spells can, by spending points from your Mythic Power, be enhanced with better effects, high caster levels, or higher save DCs.  I will list the variant effects of some of the better Mythic Spells for the Magus.

If you have Shocking Grasp, grab the mythic version to really pour on the pain when you need it, making it deal d8s instead of d6s.  Strength Magi will get Lightyears of mileage out of Mythic Enlarge Person, especially early on.

Mythic Invisibility, Grease, and Web are good as well.

Mythic Haste grants an additional move action.

Mythic Lightning bolt and Fireball deal d8s of damage.  Lightning bolt staggers anyone who fails their saves, while Fireball sets them alight.

The Ability score bonuses of the Monstrous Physique Spell line are boosted, and can now be shared between friendlies.

Mythic Black Tentacles deal acid and are tougher to get away from.

Mythic Dimension Door can bring your friends along after you.

Mythic Stoneskin gives fort save bonuses and medium fortification.
