Face_P0lluti0n’s guide to Weapon Finesse

Introduction

Finesse combatants, like swashbucklers, ninjas, sneaky dagger-wielding assassins, and graceful martial artists are some of the most beloved archetypes of fantasy. I would almost go so far as to say that outside of fantasy literature specifically developed inside of the tabletop roleplaying paradigm, these characters are more iconic than the giant fighter with the olympic weightlifter physique wearing full-plate. Outside of fantasy fiction specifically developed by gaming, I usually only see those characters in two roles. Knights and big dumb guys that aren’t usually sympathetic characters. As a culture, we tend to like fiction where clever everymen (or women) defeat the bruiser with agility or wits. It’s not even a geek vs. jock thing. People were rooting for David to beat Goliath long, long before there were lockers for geeks to be stuffed in. Finesse fighters are an incredibly popular icon of fantasy. They should not be so underpowered that they are laughed away from the table.

The purpose of this guide is to show that even with the deck stacked against them, Finesse combatants can show BSFs how it’s done, in true plucky underdog fashion. However, we need to get a few things out of the way.

Like most of the other guides I have seen, I’m only going to be referencing Paizo products that are meant for use with the Pathfinder RPG. I won’t limit myself to Pathfinder Society resources only, though the guide will emphasize choices or issues that would come up at a PFS table, because a lot of readers are probably going to care about that.

The other reason I’m focusing on Paizo/PFS is because there’s a lot of 3.5 and third party publisher material out there that makes things WAY easier for the Finesse combatant. I played in a 3.5 game where Finesse combatants owned every combat, thanks to feats like Shadow Blade from the Tome of Battle and the 3.5 Swashbuckler class which adds Int to damage in addition to Strength (or in addition to Dex, if you were using Shadow Blade too), and the fact that Pathfinder had not yet arrived and therefore wearing heavy armor was basically death by armor check penalty and reduced movement speed every time.

Format

I’m going to stick with the accepted forum standard format for recommending or un-recommending build options, the Treantmonk color scheme. Blue means the option is either amazingly good or it’s a cornerstone of the entire build - essentially, blue means “must have”. Green means that it’s worth a feat slot (or the gp, or whatever the cost is) most of the time but it’s either one killer flaw away from perfect or something you can wait on until you have resources free. Either way, green means good but not essential. Yellow means it’s either a mediocre or extreme niche choice. There might be one build in which a yellow option is the very best, but approach with caution under normal conditions. Red means “stay away!” this is almost always a choice that will not help you in any way.

“It’s a trap!”

Myths and facts

Yes, I know. Weapon Finesse is a bad player trap, a feat tax, a dangerous step towards making Dexterity the one stat to rule them all and thank [insert your deity of choice] that there’s not a flat Dex-to-damage feat, etc. I don’t agree with most of these. I’ll take them one at a time.

Myth #1: It’s a bad player trap: It shouldn’t be (see above for rant) and it doesn’t have to be. At this stage in the Pathfinder RPG, there is a critical mass of sourcebooks providing extra feats, classes, archetypes and tactical options. Complex builds are possible, and some of those are either able to substitute Dex for Strength to a degree that it is worth the inconvenience of feat or class level tax, or maximize the advantages of a high Dex in a way that outdoes the flat DPR factor of high-Strength builds. However, I want to lay down a few ground rules, some things that I think are still traps, even when a Finesse character is built right.

Trap #1: Strength cannot be your dump stat unless you can use Dexterity for damage by second level.

Yeah, this is a little bit of personal interpretation, so I’m going to defend it.

1a. I assume you will be starting at first level. Like playing 3.5, starting above first level means that you have some truly absurd options open to you. Dervish Dance, Agile weapons, and other things make it so that if you don’t need to worry about surviving to the level where you “come online” you have many more toys to play with. I’m going to assume that you are playing PFS, an Adventure Path, or a home game where the GM makes you play the whole zero-to-hero arc. Not doing so would be unfair to those of you who are.

1b. If you can’t deal decent damage by second level, you can’t melee. At first level, many of your enemies will be very weak, with single digit hit points. The two-handed Fighter or Barbarian can get a kill in one hit. You can probably get a kill in two, even if you’re just rolling an unmodified d6. At second level though, most of your enemies are going to move into the 2-digit HP range. Two-handed Strength builds will still be killing many things in one shot. But you’ve just moved up to three or four hits, maybe five if your dice are cold. At that point, you’re not contributing nearly as much to the party. If melee isn’t you deal, that’s cool, you’re an archer or a caster, providing some other service to the party. If you say your role is melee and then don’t contribute, though...well, at that point, it makes you an ineffective character.

1c. Power attack. You really want this more than you want Piranha Strike unless you are a Two-Weapon Fighter. For those of you who are Dervish Dancing or using the Elven Curve Blade, Piranha Strike does not help you. Yes, I know it technically applies to a Curve Blade, but you don’t get x1.5 damage. You still do with Power Attack.

I will go forward assuming that unless stated otherwise, Finesse builds still have a minimum Strength of 13. None of the builds or options I cover here will require a Strength of any greater than 13, though some may benefit from higher Strength mods.

Trap #2: “Agile” weapons do not fully even the gap between Strength builds and Finesse builds.

Strength characters, already running even with Finesse builds in the To-Hit department and having a major advantage on damage, will be able to buy another +1 to hit and damage, which you are trading out for the Agile enchantment. I’m not saying it’s a bad idea. If you’re not Dervish Dancing, you probably want that enchantment on at least your main hand weapon ASAP. What I am saying is that it doesn’t make the two ability scores equal.

In addition, unless you are using an Amulet of Mighty Fists, you have to have a +2 equivalent weapon. In PFS, or going by the normal wealth-by-level chart, you’re not going to have that sort of access until around 6th or 7th level. Unless your GM let you start at that level, you really can’t make Agile weapons a vital component of your build. It’s just a nice bonus to make the transition into the gonzo damagefests of the high levels go more smoothly once the BSFs start playing with 22 or 24 Strength, and your 20+ stat is your Dexterity.

Trap #3: You will never deal as much damage as a Strength-based melee fighter (unless you are a Dervish Dance Magus)

It is not possible to wield two weapons, or a two-handed weapon, and deal as much damage per round with your Dexterity as a Strength-based combatant deals. The closest you can get is using a two-weapon style with a pair of Agile weapons, and the Strength fighter will still get +1 more to hit and damage on his or her weapons. Dervish Dancers are forced to leave their offhand empty, which means that there’s no hope of out-damaging two-weapon and two-handers until you are deep into the Duelist class. Even if you are playing an AP that goes to level 15, you will spend most of the game waiting to reach parity with the Falchion Barbarian who has been playing at full strength at every level. There is only one other exception to this rule, and that is the Dervish Magus. (To be added: Dervish Dancing with a Swashbucker from the ACG playtest will also involve vast quantities of damage, but it is another feat-heavy build)

Myth #2: If you could add Dex to damage, Dex would be a totally cheesed up unfair god-stat: I must respectfully disagree. Weapon Finesse characters are already one feat behind, just by taking Weapon Finesse. Let’s say, hypothetically, that there was a feat called “Greater Weapon Finesse” that allowed the user to add Dex modifier to damage in place of Strength with Light or otherwise Finesse-worthy weapons. So to completely replace Strength with Dexterity as far as combat is concerned, you need to eat two feats. If this feat applies to Finesse weapons, Strength-users still get to use bigger kill-sticks with better damage dice, power attack with two-handers to apply x1.5 Strength mod and Power Attack mod to damage, and then they could go with the Two-Handed Warrior archetype, which lets them add double Str mod to damage. In a world with Greater Weapon Finesse, I believe Strength is still king of melee, it’s just that, at that point, I think other viable options would exist that give benefits that are worth giving yourself only 85-90% of the DPR of Falchion Fred or some other maxed out two-handed warrior. I accept that total AC turtling is not a great idea, but having decent AC and Reflex saves means your damage mitigation goes up, and higher Init means a better chance of going first and controlling more aspects of the battle, and there are some other benefits as well, such as better touch AC, wearing lighter armor, and having higher modifiers on Dex-based skills.

Why Dexterity?

The mechanical argument

Dexterity, as an ability score, modifies the following things:

1. Your Armor Class and touch Armor Class

2. Your Initiative modifier

3. The skill check modifiers for Dexterity based skills, of which there are several

In addition, there are two feats that make Dexterity modify additional rolls.

1. Weapon Finesse, the subject of this guide, allows Dexterity to modify your roll to hit rather than your Strength modifier.

2. The Agile Maneuvers feat allows you to use your Dexterity modifier instead of your Strength modifier when calculating your CMB. Yes, even for Grapple and Bull Rush checks.

“Why is Agile Maneuvers yellow?”

Because two of your best maneuvers don’t need it. See “Combat Maneuvers” below. I would still take Agile Maneuvers in the later levels though, because I will not be so feat starved, and because what I believe is the best maneuver for the later levels, Dirty Trick, can’t be Finessed with a weapon.

Your Arsenal

What can I do to make Finesse good?

The main problem that Finesse fighters have really boils down to this: Weapon Finesse makes you use one stat to hit and a different stat for damage.

Anything that makes Finesse good needs to leverage Dexterity and the feats and class abilities at your disposal to either match the damage that Strength-based melee builds can deal, or find something to do that is better than dealing damage, and cannot be done better with a high Strength.

That’s it. Those are the two goals. Do as much damage as a BSF, or do something better than damage.

But first, a word about books.

There are a few books I am going to draw heavily from. This is because there are a couple of Pathfinder campaign world books that contain feats or other abilities that really go the distance for Finesse warriors. One of these is the Inner Sea World Guide. This book contains the Dervish Dance feat. It’s vital to probably a third to a half of the builds in this guide, including the one that IMO is the best argument we’ve got for Finesse warriors.

The other must-have book for Finesse Warriors is the Pathfinder Society Field Guide. That book contains the Agile weapon enchantment as well as the Lore Warden Fighter archetype. The Lore Warden in particular is the center of several other builds I am going to heavily endorse. If you are not playing in the Pathfinder Campaign Setting, you need to ask your GM to include these optional rules - they are probably ¾ of what keeps things fair for Finesse warriors.

Sargava, the Lost Colony contains two feats that are helpful to TWF fighters in particular, Piranha Strike and Desperate Battler.

How to do damage:

1. Dervish Dance

Dervish Dance is a feat from the Inner Sea World Guide. It allows you to use your Dexterity modifier to determine to-hit bonus and damage bonus, instead of your Strength bonus. This sounds great, right? There’s one killer drawback. You must fight with a Scimitar in one hand and you may not have a weapon or shield in your other hand. Not even a buckler. This limits your options. At this point, there are three ways to use your empty off hand for something good enough to justify leaving it empty.

1a. Cast spells with it via Spell Combat.

The Dervish Magus is a big contender for best Finesse Fighter. Once you have the Dervish Dance feat, you can do almost as much damage as a Strength-based build. Strength builds have wielding their weapon with two hands as a fallback plan, and have two more feat slots open. On the other hand, Dex builds get to derive more of their important combat stats from a single ability score. Personally, I think that avoiding MAD and having better Initiative and defenses in the low and mid levels is worth the two feats, but both approaches are optimal - just optimal at slightly different things. Still, this is a finesse guide, and the Dex/Dervish Magus has one of the highest damage outputs of any character using the Weapon Finesse feat.

1b. Never use it and become a Duelist

Duelists also have an ability that requires you to not use your off-hand. It’s an ability that adds large portions of damage. This makes it acceptable to not use your off-hand - you’re basically using your off hand to add 1 point of damage per Duelist level to your main hand damage. The only drawback is that Duelist is a Prestige class, and using your off-hand to fight will give you more damage until you get your Duelist level much higher.

1c. Unarmed strike with it

The feat requires you to have nothing in your off-hand. It doesn’t say you can’t use it. If you take one level of Monk and the Two-Weapon Fighting feat, you can make your second weapon a punch - or kick, or headbutt, or whatever. The point is, you will add your Dex to damage with your Scimitar, and then you also get a second attack. Wear an Amulet of Mighty Fists and add your full Dexterity to damage with both attacks.

1d. Use it to deflect attacks (compatible with 1c)

Deflect Arrows and the Crane Style feat Crane Wing allow you to negate certain kinds of physical attacks (Ranged and Melee respectively). Both are much easier to get if you take one or two levels of Monk, and both require that you have at least one hand free, but they don’t ban you from using that hand for an unarmed attack - just like Dervish Dance. While this doesn’t increase your damage output, if you combine it with the idea in 1c - using your “empty” hand for punching - you can increase your damage output and powerfully decrease the amount of damage you take. You can’t win with defense alone, but negating the scariest attack each turn is a pretty attractive option.

3/3/2014: (Crane Wing has been errata’d in a pretty extreme way, and as far as I can tell, it doesn’t have a shadow of the build-defining power that it used to. I can’t comment on exactly how OP it was - I know some people were saying that it broke game balance so badly that characters and monsters had to be built around compensating for it. I’m not going to comment on that here except to say that I believe that it is no longer strong enough to justify single-hand fighting on its own. It may very well still have its uses - but it needs to be approached as a new feat and likely one that supplements a one-handed style rather than enables it where it would otherwise not work.)

1e. Carry a flag

The Flag Bearer feat states that you can carry a banner or flag for basically any group you belong to and other members of that group within 30 feet get +1 to attacks, damage, and saves against fear and charm, including the flag-holder. The only downside is that it requires a 15 Charisma, which is a bit of an investment. Sadly, the best classes and builds to take advantage of this are Bards, who already perform, which doesn’t stack. Still...somewhere, someone will make this work. It’s a legitimate option.

2. The “Agile” weapon enchantment

The Agile weapon enchantment appears in the Pathfinder Society Field Guide, which is a treasure trove for Finesse warriors belonging (or not belonging) to any faction or society, not just Pathfinders. The ability rather simply states that you use your Dex modifier to calculate your damage bonus rather than your Strength modifier. Yup, that’s it. Dex to damage. Right there. Your problems, however, are not yet fully solved.

2a. You can’t afford it until 6th or 7th level.

It’s a +1 equivalent weapon enchantment, meaning the minimum you can put on it is a +2 equivalent weapon, costing 8000gp for the magic alone. In the average by the books home game, or PFS, you won’t be able to afford that until 6th or 7th level. In PFS, that means that it comes online roughly halfway through your character’s PFS career. You need to be a viable build that is valuable in some way to your party until you reach the level at which you can afford an Agile weapon or two. Having the bonded item class feature cuts this price in half, but the minimum caster level is still 7.

2b. Your weapon is +1 less

A Strength build can pay 8000gp and get a +2 weapon. You pay 8000gp for a +1 weapon. Losing that +1 to hit and damage becomes a big problem when you are making many, many attacks with that +1 less (on each hand!). At least if you are burning feat slots on catching up in the damage race, you can still reach the same maximum. With Agile weapons you’ll always be +1 behind. Not good when you’re trying to justify your existence to DPR fiends.

2c. An Agile Amulet of Mighty Fists is way, way cheaper than a weapon

To the tune of 5000gp, because the text of Amulet of Mighty fists states that it does not require a +1 enchantment before adding on other abilities. You’ll be able to afford that a couple levels earlier. In addition, if you Flurry of Blows with it, you will be adding your FULL Dex mod to all damage rolls. Otherwise, Agile weapons suffer from the same off hand damage cut as using your Strength mod with your off-hand. Not so for Monks. You get full Dex to damage each time.

Granted, this comes with some serious drawbacks. One is that it doesn’t really help anyone who is not a full Monk fighting unarmed with Flurry of Blows. The other is that you lose your amulet slot, so no Amulets of Natural Armor, a vital component of AC at the higher levels. Definitely worth considering, though, if you can find some way of making up for that missing armor.

2d. It doesn’t give x1.5 damage for using a weapon with both hands.

3. Wield an Elven Curve Blade

It does 1d10 damage with an 18-20 crit range, and can be used with Weapon Finesse. The only weapon that can make a case for being better is the Falchion, and that’s not really a Finesse weapon. But 2nd place for biggest most damaging weapon in the game is still a pretty good finish for Finesse. There’s nothing stopping you from using a Curve Blade along with Weapon Finesse, Power Attack, Furious Focus, and all of the other nastiness that usually makes two-handed warriors claim that they are the best. Well here’s an idea - take all of their toys, and then go first in the Initiative order.

4. Fight with two weapons

Two Weapon Fighting is a great way to close the gap, because TWF maximizes the bonuses that you will get from non-Strength related sources. Fighters can take Weapon Specialization and Weapon Training in a light weapon, and many other bonuses do not halve the damage on the off hand.

4a. Fighter bonuses to weapon damage.

Weapon Training and Weapon Specialization add a flat amount of damage to each attack, making it the case that more attacks translates directly to more chances to apply the damage and more opportunities to crit and multiply all damage. It’s not easy to reach, but there is a critical mass of direct damage bonuses that makes it worth it from a DPR perspective to take the hit to weapon damage dice.

4b. Paladins’ Smite Evil

4c. Cavalier’s Challenge

4d. Ranger options (Favored Enemy or Guide archetype)

4e. Desperate Battler feat

4f. The Brawler archetype of the Fighter has a Weapon Training variant that adds +3 to damage (and +1 to-hit) with weapons from the “close” group, including unarmed strike, a couple of Monk weapons, and some other martial and exotic weapons. This begins at 3rd level rather than 5th, and increases at the same points that Armor Training normally does. Considering that TWF usually involves weapons with a d6 damage die, dropping to a d4 to get a few more points of static damage seems like a net gain. This may be better than the Weapon Master, which also gets Weapon Training at 3rd level, but is restricted to a single weapon and only gets +1 to damage when the ability is gained. Brawler remains 2 points of damage ahead of Weapon Master for his or her whole career, and the other abilities are arguably better for a TWFer.

5. Fight with natural weapons (analysis will go here, credit goes to XMorsX and StreamOfTheSky)

How to do better than damage:

1. Combat maneuvers

Using Weapon Finesse or Agile Maneuvers, it is possible to be 100% as effective at maneuvering as a Strength build. There’s only one roll involved in maneuvering, not two, and that roll can be turned into a roll modified by Dex. Some Maneuvers can be absolutely devastating. At low levels, the scariest maneuvers are Trip and Disarm.

Trip: Succeeding at a trip attempt makes the target prone. This means two very important things. The first is that attacking a prone target gives the attacker a whopping +4 to hit. The second is that standing from prone, which will generally be required to rejoin the combat in an effective manner, provokes an attack of opportunity from anyone who threatens. Meaning a successful trip results in you getting your attack anyway, and all of your allies get a free attack at their highest bonus. This makes you a BSF’s best friend, because you give him or her yet another attack with the greatsword or falchion or whatever, and you still get to take your attack at your target on its way back up from prone. If you are a two-weapon combatant, tripping only counts as one of your attacks! If you successfully trip with your primary hand, then your off-hand attack will get a +4, along with any of your allies that take their turns before your target does.

That. Is. Insane.

But wait, there’s more!

You don’t need Agile Maneuvers to use your Dex when tripping!

The reason is because the trip maneuver uses a weapon, and therefore, all weapon-related bonuses, including masterwork/enhancement bonuses, weapon focus, and Weapon Finesse all apply.

(This has been clarified to be a valid reading of the rule by a Dev)

This is one of the reasons Trip and Disarm are good at low levels, when you are still waiting to get the good maneuver bonuses. The target still only gets normal CMD, but your CMB is based on Dex if you have Weapon Finesse, and adds most of your relevant melee bonuses.

If you want to focus on tripping, and maneuvering in general, take Combat Reflexes. You won’t be sorry. By 6th level, when you are able to use Greater Trip and Greater Disarm, you can completely ruin a humanoid’s turn, and then take more attacks of opportunity against them then you would have been able to take with a full attack...and Combat Reflexes works from your Dex modifier.

You can’t make the most of Combat Reflexes with a mediocre Dexterity

Who needs iterative attacks at a low BAB, when you can take a handful of attacks on an opponent, all of them at your full BAB!

Trip does have a weakness, though, and that is that there’s a lot out there that can resist or just completely ignore trip attempts. Any creature currently in-flight can’t be tripped, along with any creature that doesn’t have to stand up. Plus, quadripeds get an extra bonus to their CMD on top of any bonuses they might be getting for being bigger than you. That makes tripping less of a auto-win at high levels, but right around the time that trip is becoming less reliable, Dirty Trick becomes worth using. For that reason, I’m considering Improved Trip and Greater Trip to be Blue in the low and middle levels, and demoting them to Green at the top end of the single digits and going into the double digits.

Disarm: It’s almost as devastating as Trip. What makes it not blue is that the pool of targets is much smaller. However, it retains the advantage of being usable with Weapon Finesse, because you use a weapon to Disarm. In addition, these two are devastating when used together. A successful Trip, followed by a successful Disarm (at a +4 due to the target being prone) means the target must spend two move actions to get back to combat capability. That’s a target’s whole turn, and you get two attacks of opportunity - and so do all of your friends, summoned creatures, eidolons, and animal companions. Ouch. Greater Disarm makes the whole experience even more painful for your opponent. However, unlike the Blue maneuvers, Disarming will still fail against a decent portion of your enemies, so it’s not feasible to make it your primary combat maneuver. However, for someone with some extra feats to burn, such as a Lore Warden or Maneuver Master, I believe that Disarm is a fantastic backup plan, especially if you’re playing in a campaign that involves a lot of humanoids or other weapon-users. Urban campaigns, as well as epic wars against orc hordes and the like, are great places to show your Disarm finesse.

Dirty Trick: IMO, this is one for Maneuver Master Monks, and anybody high-level enough that they can dip into 1-2 levels of that class or pick up Dirty Trick Strike. The reasons are twofold. The first is that DT is not very good until you can get Greater Dirty Trick. The second is that it’s a Standard Action, not a replacement for an attack - unless you’re a Maneuver Master Monk, in which case you can full attack, and then do one maneuver at -2 BAB, even if that maneuver would normally be a Standard Action. Most of the conditions you could inflict are pretty nasty, but none are quite worth one of your attacks - except Blind. You basically force the enemy to take an action to remove the condition ASAP, unless they would really love to lose their Dex bonus to AC, grant attackers +2 to hit, suffer a 50% miss chance, and be incapable of aiming spells and spell-like abilities. Brutal.

The reason Greater Dirty Trick really makes this a winner? Takes a Standard Action to remove. Congrats. If you win the CMB check, you just killed their turn dead. If you’re a Maneuver Master, you killed their turn dead IN ADDITION to taking your full attack.

Think about that.

As a 7th level character. Attack twice and then roll to see if your target loses the most important part of their turn.

While creatures of size above medium do get size bonuses to their CMD, this is not as much of a lost cause as Trip or Disarm when you’re tangling with Giants and Dragons. Especially because by the time you get to the level where you are fighting Giants and Dragons, you should have lots of bonuses to CMB from Greater [Maneuver] feats and lots of levels of Lore Warden.

Another bonus of this feat is that unlike Trip and Disarm, you do not suffer some sort of penalty if you roll way lower than the target’s CMD. Other than that action that you take to attempt the maneuver, you stand to lose nothing other than the action.

Plus, if you have any Rogues or Ninjas in the party, they will really appreciate anything that negates a Dex bonus, like, say, Blindness.

The other way to gain access to Dirty Trick without losing your attacks is Dirty Trick Strike. It’s specifically a late-game option, but Dirty Trick isn’t really Blue until you can get Greater Dirty Trick, as the debuff is too easy to remove until then. However, if you want to go straight Lore Warden (or maneuver Magus), DT is, IMO, the best maneuver to invest in during the late game, when you’re likelier to encounter flying creatures or others that are immune or highly resistant to being tripped.  

(I’m going to add a section on Dirty Trick Master when I get some time. It seems like Dirty Trick is increasing in its utility as a top-tier, late game combat maneuver)

Grapple: This is better for Finesse characters than you might think. Yes, it requires Agile Maneuvers, and it doesn’t work out so well against big monsters (hence it is not blue), but it completely shuts down both casters and anybody with a big weapon, while Finesse characters, with Light weapons, are allowed to retain the use of all of their weapons and attacks in a grapple.

In terms of size issues, it does work against big monsters, unlike Trip, though those big beasties get a size bonus against you as well as the fact that they usually have absurd Strength scores. Still, it’s an option. You’ll need it someday!

Bull Rush

Drag

Reposition

All three of these maneuvers do basically the same thing - they alter the position of the enemy on the battlefield without weakening the enemy or dealing any damage to it. The greater version of each of them provoke Attacks of Opportunity, but each one costs two feats to get to that point, each of them takes a Standard Action, not an attack, and it’s almost always a better choice to use one of the maneuvers above. Positioning counts, but negating whole turns counts more. If you have lots of people that use the precise strike teamwork feat and/or sneak attack, upgrade to green. Otherwise, Blind ‘em instead, and leave Bull Rushing for tanks.

If you are wielding a weapon with the Trip feature, you CAN use Weapon Finesse to add your Dex mod rather than your Strength mod to your CMB for Drag and Reposition, which makes both of these maneuvers slightly better. The main problem with these maneuvers is that the effect could be better. Not horrible though, if you’re already all set on your other maneuvers.

Steal

Two problems here. The first is that the best thing to take away 90% of the time is the thing that lets the enemy take action against you - the weapon, which is covered by another maneuver. Secondly, Steal does not use your weapon, meaning you need Agile Maneuvers to make the most of it. It might be a nice thing to have if your Lore Warden has feats to burn later in his or her career, but there are usually better things to do.

Feint

Totally not worth it unless you have the Sneak Attack ability, and then, only because you have no other choice if you want to sneak attack without a flank at low levels. Just ask the Wizard to cast Improved Invisibility. Give him/her scrolls, buy the wand at really high levels, or be a Scout.

Sunder

Overrun

Please don’t do these if you are not a BSF. Your low damage bonuses and smaller weapons will make these all but worthless.

2. Win Initiative

In almost every game (Role-playing, board, video, CCG) I have ever played, going first is a good idea. If it’s a game with a life point or damage mechanic, you’re always one turn ahead in dealing damage. In any sort of strategy game, going first means you get to decide many of the terms of the battle - you act and your opponent must react. Magic: the Gathering theorists have a pretty good analysis of why it’s good to be on the offensive that I won’t go into in great detail here, but suffice to say, I don’t think Pathfinder is any different - you are more likely to win a damage race and to sufficiently control the battlefield if you went first.

This is only possible by maxing out your Dexterity!

A starting Dex modifier of +3 or +4 will only mean a 10-15% greater chance of going first than the Strength warrior with 12-14 Dex. However, as the BSF pumps his/her Str into the twenties, you will be raising your Dex by similar amounts. So when you hit the double digit levels, you will be looking at a +5 to +7 modifier, meaning 20-30% greater chance of going first. If you have a build that really cares about going first, take Improved Initiative for another 20% chance, and the Reactionary trait for another 10% that stacks with everything else you have. Now you’re 40-50% more likely to go first than the BSF. Now Trip him! : P

The major issue here, of course, is that going first is pointless if you have no way of affecting the combat in a meaningful way. That said, going first is vitally important, but so is having something to do with it. Casting Magus spells, dealing vast amounts of damage, and leaving your enemies prone, blind, and disarmed are some really good ways to take advantage of turn one.

The point by point breakdown

Ability Scores

“But this guide is all about Dexterity, right?”

Yes, but other ability scores are often necessary to make the most of your Dexterity enhancing options.

Strength: As I mentioned earlier, Power attack is a really good idea. It’s also a bad idea to completely dump stat Strength because you need to have some sort of damage bonus until you can get Dervish Dance or Agile weapons. I would recommend a Strength of 12-14 for most builds. That’s really not that expensive.

Intelligence: Two of your best options are combat maneuvers and Magus spells. Each of these requires a decent amount of Intelligence to get right. Trip and Disarm require 13 Int to get their “Improved...” feats, and Magus spells want as much Int as you’re willing to give to them. It’s only appropriate, really - Finesse warriors in fantasy are usually clever as well as quick. Not all are geniuses (though I suggest that the Magus ones should be), but I know of very few stupid Finesse warriors in fantasy literature. Usually, if they have a dump stat, it’s Widsom  : P

That said, Maneuver Master Monks can pick up combat maneuver feats as Monk bonus feats and don’t need to fulfill prerequisites if they use bonus feat slots to pick up Combat Maneuvers (and they really should use every bonus feat slot for that purpose!). However, bonus feats for MM Monks come in at level 1, 2, and then every four levels afterward (6th, 10th, etc). If you want to pick up a better selection of maneuvers as an MM Monk, you’ll still have to pick up Combat Expertise at some point, but it is ultimately optional for a MM Monk. Intelligence is nice for an MM Monk if you can make it happen somehow, but Monks, especially finesse Monks, are going to be pretty MAD as it is, so consider it very optional. MM Monks can still be top-tier maneuverers without high Int, you’ll just have a lower selection of maneuvers available. If your maneuvers are Trip and Dirty Trick, you might not care about the others, anyway.

Constitution: You’re playing a melee character, and unless you go with the Crane Wing/Deflect Arrows build (and get Evasion from Monk levels), you are going to be taking damage. Lots and lots of damage. Your AC, wearing Light or Medium armor, will be comparable to that of a Two-Handed warrior in heavy armor, not better, so you still need to be ready to take punishment.

Wisdom: Wisdom has lots and lots of uses, and if you can get away with having a high Wisdom, go for it. The reason it’s yellow here is that none of those uses contribute directly to maximizing your Dexterity-based melee abilities. It boosts your Will save and if you are a Monk, adds to your AC if you’re not wearing armor. However, any build that isn’t 20 straight levels of Monk will end up with a better AC by wearing a Mithral Chain Shirt or casting AC buffing spells. Many of the builds I will suggest have good Will save progressions, and there are also feats and traits, like Iron Will and Indomitable Faith, to shore up your Will bonus. If you try to have a high Wisdom in addition to three physical ability scores and Intelligence, you will likely end up too MAD to survive.

Charisma: See above, but Charisma doesn’t have the bonus of adding to Will saves. The problem is that many Finesse warriors of literature do, in fact, also have a lot of personality. However, you can do just fine with a 12 if you can find ways to make social skills into class skills with traits or careful multiclassing. Raise Charisma for roleplaying purposes, just don’t expect it to make your combat build any more effective...unless you’re a Bard, in which case, you’ll have to dump stat Widsom. Go to town. You get the good Will save chart anyhow!

The exception to the rule is Swashbucklers, for whom Charisma is a minimum of Green. It might be Blue for them, but Dex is still king. The question is whether to go for Intelligence or Charisma after your physical stats are squared away, and IMO there are decent arguments for both, though Charisma provides many more mechanical goodies for the Swashbuckler.

Feats

Weapon Finesse: The entire point of this guide. You can’t be a Finesse warrior without it.

Dervish Dance: The Holy Grail of Finesse builds - the ability to add you Dex mod to damage rather than your Strength. Don’t take this without thinking though - you have to fight with one hand totally empty. Find something to do with it. If you can, you win at Finesse with this feat.

Combat Expertise: It’s not so useful for its effect as it is as a prerequisite for the feats you really want, the Improved/Greater versions of Trip, Disarm, and Dirty Trick, your three best maneuvers as a Maneuver Master or a Lore Warden (if you are playing one of these, there are benefits to becoming both).

Power Attack: Let’s get this out of the way. In Pathfinder, unlike 3.5, you may Power Attack with any weapon. Including Light weapons. There is no restriction in the rules against Power Attacking with a weapon that you are also using Weapon Finesse with. Also, unlike Piranha strike, if you are two-handing it with a Curve Blade or Aldori Dueling Sword, you do get the x1.5 damage, even though you are using Weapon Finesse with it.

Vital Strike: Vital Strike (and the reast of the feat chain) is generally regarded as a subpar feat. For most of these builds, I agree. I’d consider it, though, if I were doing two-handed combat with an Elven Curve Blade. That’s an extra d10 for each level of the feat chain, and two-handed warriors are not as feat starved as maneuvering and two-weapon builds. I’m not saying it’s great, just that it’s not quite as bad as normal.

Two-Weapon Fighting: If you have a lot of static bonuses to damage, then it will be more important to hit often than to hit hard.

Improved Initiative: Initiative is something you can never have too much of, especially if you can start a fight by doing something really devastating. See the section above for my rant about Init though - you can’t just win Init, you then have to do something awesome with it.

Piranha Strike: The finesse version of Power Attack, usable only with light weapons, and it still includes the off-hand damage decrease when you are TWFing. The sad part is that it’s not compatible with Dervish Dance or non-light finesse weapons like the Rapier or Elven Curve Blade. The big winner here is not needing 13 Strength. If you can’t pick up Power Attack, pick up Piranha Strike.

Classes

“You’re playing a what Monk?”

Magus: The class that can get the most out of Dervish Dance. Once you have spent the two feats and two skill ranks to gain Dervish Dance, there is no difference in the amount of damage an optimal Strength Magus can do and the amount of damage an optimal Dervish Magus can do when attacking with a one-handed weapon, and the Dervish has better AC, Reflex, Initiative, and ranged attacks. The Strength Magus still has a better backup plan for when the spells run out - go two-handed on their melee weapon and dish out the 1.5x Strength and Power Attack damage. However, no other class brings Dex as close to dishing out completely equal damage as Strength. Spell combat is simply mind-blowing once your spells per day and Concentration check are decent - say around level four when Spell Recall is gained. I’m personally putting my vote behind Dervish Magus as the current best Finesse build as far as pure optimization goes - you can melee, you can cast spells, you can do both at the same time, you have good initiative, and many tactical and movement options.

Even without Dervish Dance, the Dex Magus is still decent. I’d make it Green if you can’t get Dervish Dance for some reason. A lot of this is because even though the Dervish is a melee combatant, weapon damage is really at the bottom of the list of factors that contribute to the class’ usefulness. Hitting at all (for the purpose of Spellstriking some absurdly painful touch attack) and getting a lot of critical hits (again, for Spellstrike) are vastly more important than the difference of two or three points of melee damage. In addition, the Magus wants, very very badly, to go first and have a decent AC. With a low Dex, the Magus will spend a significant amount of his/her career getting hit. With a d8 hit die, that’s not an attractive option. This is one situation where AC will be so suboptimal that squeezing out another point of two of DPR is not as important as a 2-4 point AC boost. Your big damage comes from spells, not from the metal stick you are using to deliver those spells. The weapon will likely just be one more d6 to roll for damage. However, the non-Dervish Dex Magus will have a harder time with the low levels, and with monsters that have lots of energy immunities. Get an Agile weapon ASAP - you can make up the lost +1 enhancement bonus with your Arcane Pool ability. As it stands, it looks like Strength Magi are better at buffed combat and long-term resiliency, but Dervish Magi make fantastic nova damage dealers because they can win initiative and the extra couple of points of AC, touch AC, and Reflex save make it likelier that they can survive the one more round they need to take an enemy out of the running.

Fighter (Lore Warden): The big blue here is based on the idea that you will be taking the Lore Warden archetype. If not, see below. As I explained above, it’s a lot easier to make a Dex-based maneuvering character equal with his/her strength based buddies. Gaining a means of attacking or maneuvering more than once in a turn, through two-weapon fighting or the Maneuver Master Monk archetype, means that you will spend many of your turns making more of a difference than the big bruiser - you get to attack and maneuver. Everyone else needs to wait until double digit BABs to even think about that.

The reason that I regard Lore Wardens as a Finesse option are the same as the Magus. Light armor proficiency is a good reason to have a high Dexterity, and Lore Wardens do not have defensive spells to make up the difference like the Magus does. Lore Wardens are also the best class at maneuvering that there is, because they get a full BAB progression, bonus feats, and an untyped bonus to CMB that scales up with their level. I’m pretty sure that there is no other class that can grant the sorts of class-given bonuses to CMB that Lore Warden does at a full BAB progression.

In addition, the Lore Warden wants to go first as badly as the Magus does. Unlike a TWF damage machine, the Lore Warden should be perfectly happy going first, moving to an enemy, and tripping or disarming them. At minimum, you still get an attack, and they lose the option for a full attack. If your friends go first with you, they get to walk over, take their attacks at +4, and then take another full table’s worth of highest-bonus attacks when the unlucky target tries to stand up. Can anyone survive that?

Fighter (Brawler): I’ve yet to test this out myself, but the Brawler archetype gives out some serious static damage bonuses that TWFers love, and since it begins as early as 3rd level, the Brawler Fighter is even borderline dippable. The close weapons group includes some Monk weapons, so this could also be a major damage boost to Flurry of Blows attacks - the Martial Artist and Weapon Adept archetypes seem to me like they are not only mechanically sound multiclass options, but also pretty flavorful. Someday I will play a Brawler who decided to learn the ways of disciplined combat after he/she hit 3rd level, or at least that character will show up as an NPC in a future campaign I run. Alternately, the damage bonuses continue to increase, and 4th level enables Weapon Specialization, making it viable to have extremely high static damage bonuses on both hands, which is what TWF needs in order to compare favorably to two-handed fighting, which is typically the most powerful and feat-light fighting style.

Fighter (Other): Static damage bonuses are really nice for the two weapon Fighters. The problem is that unlike Rangers, dual-wielding Fighters will have a bit of MAD (multiple ability dependency) because they need to have high Dexterity to meet the feat requirements, and if you’re Finessing Two-Weapon Fighting, you don’t have the static damage bonuses to justify dumping your Strength until very, very late in your career. You will also always be +1 behind because you are dumping one point of your weapons’ enhancement bonuses into Agile. However, all of that said, a Finesse Fighter using Two-Weapon Fighting or an Elven Curve Blade is still likely to be effective enough to justify inclusion in an optimized party.

Rogue: I would ask if World of Warcraft created this expectation that Rogues ought to be dual wielding damage maniacs, but I don’t want to start another TT vs. MMO flame war. Suffice to say, it’s what most people expect out of Rogues, but it’s just not true. The traditional dual-wielders have always been Rangers, but honestly, Fighters and Paladins are better at it now.

There are several problems that hamper a Rogue’s ability to deal effective amounts of damage. They start with having no reliable ability to make a full set of Sneak Attacks against an enemy without a friend. Usually this means flanking, though Improved Invisibility certainly doesn’t hurt.

Paladin and Cavalier: I lump these two classes together because their claim to fame in the finesse department is almost the same - each of them has the ability to gain a bonus to damage that is so large that it is worth giving up Strength-based damage in order to wield two weapons and deal that static damage twice in a turn. For Paladins, this damage is Smite Evil, and for Cavaliers, it’s the Challenge ability. Their armor proficiencies and general combat style don’t play particularly well with finesse builds, and when not using their special abilities, they won’t be that special in combat, but these classes tend to shine in “boss” fights and other tangles involving a small number of really scary combatants. Their big weakness is that they get only so many uses of their big special power per day, meaning that finesse Cavaliers and Paladins spend a lot of time being medicore, and then become utterly game-breaking during the special fights. If you’re okay with that, go for it. Your party will thank you when you take down the impossible boss monster in two combat rounds.

Ranger: Blasphemy, right? Not really. The big advantage of TWFing as a Ranger is that you don’t have to Finesse because you get the feats without qualifying for them, which means that you don’t need a 19 Dex to max out your TWF chain. The only reason a melee Ranger needs Dex is for init and because they’re probably wearing substandard armor. I’d assume most TWF Rangers have Dex in the 12-14 range. It’s just not enough to justify dumping Strength. Dexterity for Rangers is really more of an archery thing.

Monk: The common optimization widsom is that Monks should just suck it up, accept their low AC, and max out Strength and Wis. Ok, I can accept that, but Monks are also decent Finesse warriors, and one of my favorite Finesse dips. For unarmed Monks, flurry means using your whole Str bonus on each damage roll because you never attack with your “off hand”, and that applies to Agile Amulets of Mighty Fists too. It won’t let you out-damage a Strength Monk, but you might live a lot longer than a Strength Monk.

Monk also makes a good dip for Dervish builds, allowing you to make attacks using your free hand if you take the Two-Weapon Fighting feat. It’s highly recommended that if you dip Monk this way (1-2 levels), you take either the Maneuver Master or Master of Many Styles archetype. The reason for this is that Flurry of Blows can’t be used with the Scimitar - it is much better to just lose Flurry in favor of something that will actually help you. The best for Dervishes is two levels of Master of Many Styles, to pick up Crane Style and Crane Wing. This allows you to fight with two weapons (Scimitar in main hand, Unarmed Strike in off-hand) while simultaneously using your off hand to automatically negate one successful melee attack against you each turn. Not a bad tanking strategy.

Also notable is the Maneuver Master Monk. We’ve gone over how Finesse characters are much closer to their Strength based cousins in the maneuvering department, thanks to the ruling that the Weapon Finesse feat applies to any maneuver that uses a weapon: Disarm, Sunder, Trip, Drag, and Reposition. The reason this is important is that the Flurry of Maneuvers ability does not require you to use a special Monk weapon, and can be used as part of a full attack which adds one maneuver on to your full attack without penalizing the attack rolls of the normal attacks. The -2 only applies to maneuvers. Finally, the maneuver used does not have to be a maneuver that replaces an attack - you can add a Standard Action maneuver onto a full attack without detracting from your full attack sequence at all. Why is this special for Finesse characters? Lore Wardens! Maneuver Master Monks make a stunningly good two level dip, and IMO, it’s the only way to get the absolute most out of maneuvers in Pathfinder.

One nice thing about any 2-level Monk dip is the +3 to all saves and Evasion, which, really, is only appropriate for any truly dedicated Finesse warrior.

Swashbuckler Swashbucklers are a little MAD, which makes me a little sad (that they are not SAD), but aside from Dervish Magi and Feral Slasher Alchemists, might be one of the strongest options for handing out vast quantities of damage for those with more Dexterity than Strength. It can really end up being worth the cost, though Swashbucklers likely require a point buy of at least PFS-level (20 points) to do well.

However, if you can make the stats work for you, Swashbucklers are pretty much all upside for finesse warriors. Free Weapon Finesse at 1st level, 4 skill points a level, and a d10 hit die are a good way to start - that’s already better than what a 1st level finesse-based Fighter would get, and that’s not even taking into account Panache and Deeds. Deed use will be extremely limited in the early levels, but they’re just one of many toys the Swashbuckler gets to play with. The list of good things easily keeps pace with Fighters for most of the early levels, but they’re all geared towards boosting a one-handed, finesse fighting style, and they do so better than a straight Fighter (even with the one-handed archetype) could do.

The dependence on Charisma hurts, because the Swashbuckler, and finesse fighters in general, want high marks in every other ability score, but even with a mediocre Charisma, Swashbucklers do more with finesse than most classes. The biggest boost is Precise Strike, which allows a one-handed finesse fighter to have damage that is very close to that of a two-weapon or two-handed warrior. The Barbarian and Magus are still the kings of burst damage, but Swashbucklers get yet more that makes up for lagging a few points of DPR behind the winners of the DPR Olympics.

Swashbucklers are extremely difficult to match in defensive ability and mobility. A high Dexterity, combined with Nimble giving a dodge bonus to AC at the same rate that Armor Training would increase Max Dex for a heavily armored fighter, means that the Swashbuckler can keep up the same sort of AC that a full-plate wearer has. While using a buckler and a single weapon, thanks to the wording of Swashbuckler Finesse and Precise Strike.

The Swashbuckler need only invest in a single weapon, like the two-handed warrior, but doesn’t need to choose between a decent AC and keeping a 30ft movement speed. And can invest in a buckler for even more AC than their full-plate-wearing, greatsword-wielding buddies.

That’s not where it ends, though. Opportune Parry and Riposte take it further. Opportune Parry lets the Swashbuckler roll an attack by spending an AoO, and if that attack is higher than the opponent’s melee attack roll, spend an Immediate Action to counterattack at the highest iteration of the Swashbuckler’s BAB. This doesn’t get much use at the low levels, when the Swashbuckler doesn’t have much Panache, but through Charisma-boosting items or spending spare feats on Extra Grit/Panache, at the middle and high levels, the Swashbuckler can force melee opponents into a particularly nasty catch-22 - attack someone else and allow the Swashbuckler to rip you to shreds in the meantime, or attack the Swashbuckler and risk enabling them to rip you to shreds anyway.

Slayer Slayers appear to be Paizo’s answer to all of the people who wanted Rogues to be more combat-viable. A more urban, Rogue-esque skill list has been welded onto a Ranger chassis, Trapfinding is an option, spellcasting has been pitched in favor of Sneak Attack +1d6 per 3 levels, and Favored Enemy is replaced by an ability that lets you burn a move action to “mark” a target and gain +1 to hit and damage (+1 more per 5 levels of the class) against that target until you choose a new target. Spending a move action really hurts two-weapon builds, but taking 7 levels in the class is rewarded with an upgrade to swift action. While I don’t quite expect finesse Slayers to outpace a traditional Strength-based damage build of the same class, any sort of static attack/damage bonus is useful for two-weapon builds, and the Slayer gets two. Slayers also have the benefit of having access to Sneak Attack, but not relying on it for decent damage. Combined with two-weapon attacks, the Slayer might be a class that wants to get into a flanking position, but can do just fine if that’s not possible or the enemy is immune to SA - which has always been a major strike against the Rogue. The Slayer talents are definitely not anything to complain about - at very least, you get Ranger combat styles available at the same level that Rangers would get them, plus completely viable options on the other even levels, such as Trapfinding and several of the Rogue’s more combat-focused talents, including Weapon Finesse.

I think the main problem is the same as Rangers - “Gain TWF without the prerequisites” enables Strength-based TWF. Still, I have to hand it to Slayer for having a lot of combat bonuses and abilities that would still be relevant for a finesse-based TWF warrior, though the biggest rewards come from investing all of your levels in the class. At the very least, it’s a solid choice to recommend to someone who always wanted to play a two-weapon thief/assassin sort of character and found the Rogue class disappointing, even if finesse Slayers are out of the running for gold medals in the DPR Olympics.

Barbarian (Urban Barbarian) Controlled Rage is a pretty serious boon to anyone who likes the Dexterity ability score. You can split the boost from Controlled Rage any way that you want, each time you begin a Rage, you don’t get the drawbacks of Rage (except for the fatigue), and you’re still eligible for all of the Rage powers, most of which are scary and awesome, to say the least. This is also a very dippable class, as two levels of Barbarian gives you a starter pack of rounds of Controlled Rage and access to a Rage Power, meaning that you can begin buying the Extra Rage Power feat if you want some other classes but need the Rage Powers. However, straight Urban Barbarian, or Urban Barbarian into a PrC like Duelist, might be the strongest option, as the really good Rage Powers require lots of Barbarian class levels.

A word about color coding the builds - I’m saving Blue for the builds that I actually believe are as good or better than Strength builds that aim to accomplish the same thing. Green is for anything that I still find to be usable in a high-powered, optimization heavy game. Anything below Green might still work, but anybody who wants to pick on you for not choosing completely maxed-out, optimized choices will have a lot of ammo to work with for builds below the green level. If you need armor against the really rabid, optimize-or-die crowd, choose a Green build to minimize arguing, or a blue build to just keep them quiet.

Dervish Magus

Really, all it boils down to is this:

Level 1: Weapon Finesse

Level 2: Two ranks of Perform (Dance)

Level 3: Dervish Dance

After that, wield a Scimitar for the rest of your career. Because of the specific interaction of Dervish Dance with the Magus’s Spell Combat and Spellstrike class features, your damage will be exactly the same as a Strength-based Magus. This is because only one hand will ever be used for dealing damage - the other hand is used for spellcasting, which, of course, uses neither Dex nor Strength.

I personally think that the Dervish build of the Magus is slightly superior to the Strength version. Magi have an AC problem, especially early in their lives, and high Dex solves that handily. Most importantly, though, having a Higher Dex gives a better Initiative, which, IMO, is even more essential for Magi than for other classes. Magi are good at buffs, debuffs, and burst damage, all of which are much better when done proactively as opposed to reactively. A Magus going first could mean the difference between the BBEG being able to full attack or not, or in a battle with many enemies, a Magus going first could mean one of those enemies is at 0 HP before the fighting even really starts. Stacking high Dex on top of Improved Initiative and the reactionary trait means almost always being first. That’s really nice when you lead with Haste on the whole party.

IMO, Dervish Magus is the #1 way for a Finesse warrior to prove themselves the equal of a Strength warrior, followed closely by the Lore Warden. What I mean by this is completely maximum optimization - there are many other Dex builds that are so close to a Strength warrior’s effectiveness that it’s likely that nobody’s going to notice if your DPR is a couple of points lower in actual play, especially if you have other advantages (Init, Dex skills, touch AC) that make up for it. Just don’t play a TWF Rogue...

Lore Warden

The other big star of the show. The Lore Warden archetype is a big win for Dexterity-based Fighters, because it adds some really unique and useful bonuses while stripping out all of the reasons a Fighter doesn’t need Dexterity. No shields, light armor only, no Armor Training. Instead, at all of the levels where you would get armor training, you get +2 to CMB and CMD. Plus, at level 2, free Combat Expertise feat, which really cuts down on the feat starvation.

My basic skeleton for the Two-Weapon Tripper Lore Warden looks something like this:

Human Fighter

Level 1: Fighter 1, Weapon Finesse, Two-Weapon Fighting, Weapon Focus

Since you’re waiting for Combat Expertise at level 2, you’re starting out as an underwhelming Two-Weapon Fighter. Level 1’s not so scary, though, and most builds really haven’t “come online” yet anyway. Level 1 will always be ruled by the BSF with the big sword.

Level 2: Fighter 2, Combat Expertise (Bonus), Improved Trip

Now you get maneuvers. You’re not going to be doing so great yet - beware trying to trip big scary things, you might end up dropping your weapon if you fail the roll by 10 or more, unless you’re using a pair of Sickles, the one light, TWFable weapon with the Trip quality. I’m not sold on that one, I might just stick with Short Swords.

Level 3: Fighter 3, Lore Warden bonus to CMB +2, Iron Will

The important part of this level is the Lore Warden’s bonus to CMB. Iron Will is just a must for Fighters, might as well get it early.

Level 4: Fighter 4, Weapon Specialization

Not really important to maneuvering, but very important to dealing damage.

Level 5: Fighter 5, Weapon Training class feature, Combat Reflexes

One level until the really good maneuver stuff comes online! However, Weapon Training, as an attack roll bonus that involved your weapon, will apply to Trip and Disarm rolls. Nice.

Level 6: Fighter 6, Greater Trip

Level 7: Monk (Maneuver Master) 1, Improved Dirty Trick, Agile Maneuvers

Level 8: Monk (Maneuver Master) 2

Level 9: Fighter 7, Greater Dirty Trick

Ok, now we get to the point where trip is probably starting to have limited usefulness - many monsters are going to fly, have many legs, be really big, have high Strength, and probably several of these things in combination. While Trip will be utterly devastating when it works, you need a backup plan.

Greater Dirty Trick. With Flurry of Maneuvers, you can add a Dirty Trick to a full attack sequence - and you can take that Dirty Trick attempt at the beginning of your attack sequence, so that if it is successful, the enemy loses its standard action cancelling the blindness, and you get +2 (and denied Dex AC mod) to all of your attacks later in the sequence as an added bonus.

Trip is still a great way to generate a lot of extra pain, but the later part of a Lore Warden’s career is likely to focus on using Dirty Trick to lock out the standard action of whoever is most dangerous at any given moment. PFS Wardens will probably get more opportunities to Trip, but at least, with Dirty Trick, you have a one-size-fits-all maneuver that is really devastating unless you’re fighting a mook horde.

After level 9, the feat tree kind of opens up. I’d suggest going for the critical strike chain and improved crit so you can stack Dirty Trick conditions, or trip + blind some unlucky target. Actually, you can technically do that with a FoM + TWF attack sequence - DT, Trip, Attack. Ouch.

An alternate build for the feat-starved might be to go with an Elven Curve Blade instead, to cut out the TWF tree dependency. This isn’t really economical for anyone other than Elves and Half-Elves though, since anybody else needs to spend a feat buying ECB proficiency. A good way to make the Lore Warden build work with non-humans though.

Swashbuckler Swashbuckler isn’t finished yet (we’ll see the final form in the Advanced Class Guide this summer), and others on the forums have brough up a few glaring weaknesses with the Swashbuckler as written, but the class’s ability to make finesse combat work out-of-the-box is hard for me to deny, especially since not only does it stand amongst those with highest finesse-based damage potential, defense, and mobility, but Swashbucklers also look, flavor-wise, exactly like you would expect a finesse combatant in an early-renaissance fantasy game would look. They look to me like they stepped right out of 7th Sea and into Pathfinder. Whether or not the class still needs a few fixes is up for debate (I’m not going to take sides here), the Swashbuckler as written is probably the best option for someone who wants to play an effective, reasonably-optimized finesse warrior with 100% straight duelist/swashbuckling hero flavor and very little build complexity. If I were going to build a pirate or noble-born fencer PC tomorrow, Swashbuckler would be my choice.

(that said, I can’t wait to see the archetypes, as I’m personally hoping for an “erudite warrior” archetype that derives Panache from INT rather than CHA, and a lot of people are clamoring for a two-weapon Swashbuckler archetype. I would love to see archetypes for both, though I do hope that the core Swashbuckler is still a damage machine with a rapier in one hand and a buckler in the other).

One thing to keep in mind - there is a feat that allows the Swashbucler to use a one-handed slashing weapon with all of the Swashbuckler abilities, including Swashbuckler Finesse and Precise Strike. Since the damage boost relies on a one-handed fighting style, this means that Dervish Dance is running at peak efficiency here, allowing the Swashbuckler to add their level to damage on top of using Dexterity to derive to-hit and damage modifiers. Aside from Magus-based nova antics, that’s pretty much the best possible Dexterity-to-damage potential that I know of, and its attached to a full-BAB class with acceptable skill points and some terrifyingly good defensive and mobility options at the mid-to-high levels, so IMO, Swashbuckler certainly deserves to be at least part-Blue. I haven’t run the numbers myself yet, but Swashbuckler might be finesse’s best contributor to the DPR Olympics. For those sick of Dervish Dance, there has been a promise from the devs that a Dexterity-to-damage feat which is usable with a larger selection of weapons will be included in the ACG alongside the completed Swashbuckler, and the class is certainly viable without Dervish Dance, especially since, right now, Dervish Dancing as a Swashbuckler is a heavy feat tax, it might be better to wait for an Agile rapier and use your feats on other things because the Swashbuckler’s damage output will still be respectable even if its not maxed out.
