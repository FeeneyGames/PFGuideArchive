An Extraordinarily Thorough Guide on using Handle Animal

Handle Animal is one of those skills that nobody wants to deal with. The rules seem overly convoluted, muddy, and tedious. This is not a false perception, however the rules do actually make sense, and work, and this is one of the most powerful skills in the game, rivaling many class-abilities. The only thing it requires from you is time and gold. And GM approval ;)

The purpose of this guide is to walk you through the steps of obtaining and training a combat animal through the use of Handle Animal. Combat Animals are different than a Druid’s Animal Companion, having slightly different requirements to obtain, train, keep, and employ.

*Art sourced from Heise on DeviantArt.

The Steps needed to obtain and prepare your combat animal are explained below, and summarized here:

  1. Rear or Purchase your animal(s)
  2. Train your animal(s) for a specific purpose. Most likely they will be customized.
  3. Understand the mechanics of Handle Animal when employing your combat animal.
  4. Pick, Add to, or Replace your combat animals from the comprehensive list.
  5. Keeping and Feeding your animals.

We will need a base line reference character for this guide. Assume any class with a Charisma of 16, Handle Animal as a class skill with max ranks, the Animal Affinity feat, and the Halfling Outrider Trait (or some other means of gaining at least a +1 bonus to one's Handle Animal skill at level 1). This means that our starting character will have a total Handle Animal bonus of +11.

Lets get started :)

STEP 1: Obtaining a Combat Animal

Lets first understand some basics:

Which stats do I use for my Combat Animal, those in the Druid's entry, or those from the Bestiary?

A Druid's animal companion is a class-mechanic that operates under the assumption that the Companion begins as a smaller, juvenile animal, which then rapidly matures. The Druid's Animal Companion also gains huge Natural Armor increases, Feats, and bonus tricks. Handle Animal does not grant a Druid's Animal Companion, it allows for the domestication of animals as they are listed in the Bestiary. In this way, Combat Animals incur a purchase cost, time investment, and even as they begin fully mature at level 1, their stats never become more powerful than as they are presented in the Bestiary. This means that most combat animals of 7 HD or less fall behind the Druid's companion by levels 4 or 7, and become untenable in combat before level 10. One must domesticate larger and more bizarre combat animals if he wishes to continue using them into the latter half of the game, and given the time it takes for these animals to mature, it may be impossible to find or rear one before the adventure is over.

There are variant versions of this animal described in its stat block, which can I choose?

Since animals do not have levels, one cannot simply apply a template to Advance his animal unless variants are permitted within a specific animal's stat block. Some variants do not change the animal's HD, and in these cases your animal will be the standard version for an animal of its type at maturity when captured within the listed Region.

However, if an animal comes in more than one size within the listed Region, and the variant sizes include their adjusted HD, so long as one can auto-succeed on his Rearing checks, then you are able to domesticate that version of the animal. It matures at the same rate; the principle difference when rearing and keeping a larger sized animal of the same species is that they cost more to feed.

Which method should I use to obtain a Domesticated animal?

There are two means by which you can obtain a Domesticated animal. 1) Purchase one that has already been Domesticated, and maybe even trained as described in its stat block (ex: Riding Dog). 2) Obtain an infant version of that animal, Rearing it and having it trained as it matures. Ultimately you will employ both means throughout your adventuring career. Based on your region, obtaining pups may be restricted to one or the other.

What is the price difference between Purchasing and Rearing?

Purchasing an already domesticated (and possibly trained) animal is done at the listed market price. If no price is listed in any Pathfinder resource, assume there is no standard market price and your GM will have to provide one that he feels is reasonable. Haggling prices down falls under Diplomacy, and is covered under the Request to Give Simple Aid. Haggling is otherwise not covered by the rules. As with all Profession/Craft skills, the market price is double the creation or basic service cost. One can assume (this is the realm of assuming) that the cost to rear an animal is half the market price. This is only an advantage if a market price is listed. Otherwise, the cost to rear an animal is what your GM decides it will be.

My character is only 1st level, which is the better method?

As a 1st level character, you can purchase an already domesticated animal, and then train it accordingly. Since this animal's combat ability is not part of a class feature, but rather the result of a skill, a balanced approach is to treat them as equipment. Otherwise, it is up to your GM to allow you to have raised an animal from infancy (rearing it), which will open up your options to more exotic choices. In either case, you'll be spending gold from your starting allotment, and feeding your combat animals may incur a weekly fee as well (more on this below).

Some non-Rule considerations: These animals must also be kept from instinctively roaming or running away, and so housing/penning, exercise, and loyalty/affection building might be factors. Set aside time each day for these activities, if anything to satisfy the friendship that you'll want between you and your animal.

Rearing:

If you are permitted to rear your combat animal, Handle Animal allows for three of the same kind to be reared at the same time. Rearing is the act of raising an animal from infancy into maturity and the result is Domestication. Domesticated animals have a base attitude of “Indifferent.” Once play begins, any animal that you wish to rear will take months to years to reach maturity, and so you must actively seek out exotic pups during your adventures or downtime in order to have them ready by a higher level.

The time required to rear an animal to maturity is found in the beta release of the Epic Handbook:

How much time each day/week does it take to Rear an animal?

Rearing has no specific daily time investment. Rearing has no specific time investment. I repeated that line on purpose :P Pathfinder makes a distinction between Rearing and Training. For both GMs and players interesting in rearing, if Combat Animals have already been approved as a feature of Handle Animal, then this point must be discussed and settled before play begins. Domestication isn't a only matter of training as it is also a matter of acclimation. An animal must learn to be comfortable around groups of people, and must also learn its place. Even a ferocious animal, such as a Tiger or Bear, can be reared into relative docility.

Let me suggest this: Handle Animal requires a character to spend 3 hours every day per animal when Training them. Since some of the more exotic choices involve apex predators (like bears and tigers), and eventually magical beasts or any non-Animal with an int of 1 or 2, domestication should not be assumed an easy occurrence without some heavy-handed behavioral programming. Gorgons aren't inherently domestic, neither are Owlbears ;) And so if you are ambitious enough to steal an infant, be prepared for your GM to require lots of time spent raising it. In these sorts of cases, 3 hours per day per pup seems fair. However, most animals and some magical beasts shouldn't require nearly as much attention, and some will require a lot more (Purple Worms anyone?).

When do I roll my Rear check? And what are the DCs?

As described in Handle Animal, you must spend half the time required to complete the action before making the roll. And so, for example, a Leopard which matures in 18 months will require you to devote 9 months to rearing it before you make a roll to determine if it has been domesticated. Because of how we have set up our character, we should auto-succeed at domesticating up to 6 HD animals at level 1. You are allowed to also train your pup as you're rearing it, so make those checks around the same time.

Rearing is either a Success or Failure. If one fails his Rear check, that animal cannot be domesticated, it become impossible to domesticate (feral), and all of the time and resources you put into raising it has been wasted. This is why I am suggesting that you only select from those animals or magical beasts whose Rear DC is automatically successful under the Take 10 rule.

The DC to rear an animal is 15 plus its HD at maturity. The DC to rear a non-Animal with an Int of 1 or 2 is 20 plus its HD at maturity.

If you are taking 10, this means that you must have at least a total Handle Animal skill bonus of +6 to domesticate a 1 HD animal. As was explained above, as a 1st level character with a Charisma of 16, your level 1 skill bonus should be +11. With Take 10, that means you can auto-Succeed at rearing animals of 6 HD or less (10 plus 11 minus 15 equals 6). Rearing multiple animals does not increase this DC. Each must be 6 HD or less, and each takes time out of your day until mature.

Handle Animal also allows you to rear non-animal creatures (magical beasts, or anything really) with an Int of 1 or 2, but the DC increases by 5. And so with a Rearing DC of 20 plus HD, this allows you to rear a non-Animal with up to 1 HD. That would be either a Stirge or a Crawling Hand.

How do I obtain pups/eggs/cubs?

Finding Exotic Animal pups is no easy task. It requires both Knowledge: Nature, as well as Survival. If you lack one or both, you will have to employ someone who can provide their services. You will need to know what sorts of animals or non-animals exist in your region, and so must make a Knowledge: Nature check, the DC may vary:

Check: Answering a question within your field of study has a DC of 10 (for really easy questions), 15 (for basic questions), or 20 to 30 (for really tough questions).

You can use this skill to identify monsters and their special powers or vulnerabilities. In general, the DC of such a check equals 10 + the monster's CR. For common monsters, such as goblins, the DC of this check equals 5 + the monster's CR. For particularly rare monsters, such as the tarrasque, the DC of this check equals 15 + the monster's CR, or more. A successful check allows you to remember a bit of useful information about that monster. For every 5 points by which your check result exceeds the DC, you recall another piece of useful information. Many of the Knowledge skills have specific uses as noted on Table: Knowledge Skill DCs.

Based on this passage, the DC to know if an animal is in your region is 15 plus its CR. A Leopard, for example, would have a DC of 17. Remember that Knowledge: Nature is an important skill, and you will want to max it out. There is no consequence to failing a Knowledge check, and so at 1st level with Take 20, your result will auto-succeed up to about DC 24. This is more than enough to know every major type of 6 HD animal in your region. This will also give you insight into the type of terrain where you can find your quarry, and once in the appropriate area, you must track one to its den.

Something to consider, you must track them down during their birthing season. The requisite Survival DC should be determined by your GM considering all of the factors, but the best way to track is by Scent. Purchase and train a Dog for Hunting and employ someone with Knowledge: Geography and Survival. Once you have found their den, you'll want to inspect it for cubs. At this point, its up to you to decide how you'd like to pilfer said cubs, any parents present are likely to fight to the death to protect them.

Can I Domesticate an Adult Animal?

The short answer is: There are no hard rules on this (yet). Yes you can, but there's no specific DC given for this situation, and so its up to your GM. Consider the following passage:

An axe beak requires training before it will bear a rider. Taming an axe beak requires 6 weeks of work and a DC 25 Handle Animal check, after which it can be taught tricks as normal.

Assume that not all animals can be domesticated as adults, as they are too feral, and that some are simply easier to domesticate than others.

Purchasing an animal:

Paying to obtain an animal is the other option. Some animals come trained, which is explicitly mentioned in their name (Cat, Hunting), or in their description (Dog; lists 3 types including Guard). All animals can be domesticated and trained for a general purpose—even a lap dog or house cat, Beware: Guard Chihuahua, even if the end result is ineffective to that purpose.

The listed price is the Market Price for these animals, and that cost can be negotiated down via Diplomacy. Refer to the comprehensive list of animals and others that have been listed with a purchase price in the Animal Statistics block below.

STEP 2: Training Your Combat Animal

Once you have obtained your combat animal(s), you'll need to train them. Its reasonable to assume that the animals that you begin with at level 1 have either already been trained by your hand, or were a trained animal at purchase.

The rules for training are easy to follow, and so I'll simply give this advice: if you intend for them to be used in combat, teach them individual tricks. The reason is this:

Attack (DC 20): The animal attacks apparent enemies. You may point to a particular creature that you wish the animal to attack, and it will comply if able. Normally, an animal will attack only humanoids, monstrous humanoids, giants, or other animals. Teaching an animal to attack all creatures (including such unnatural creatures as undead and aberrations) counts as two tricks.

General Purpose training doesn't teach them to attack all creatures, and so you'll have to do it trick by trick. The great thing is that this doesn't involve more time, nor cost, it's simply a matter of extra Training Checks. The toughest tricks have a DC of 20, and since we can Take 10 to achieve a result of 19 we'll almost always auto-succeed on each weekly check. We need only spend the required 3 hours daily, over the number of weeks listed.

When do I make my Training checks?

Each trick takes only 1 week to train (21 hours), and you make your check on day 4 (at hour 12). If you fail, you must begin the training from scratch, which can begin the next day.

Which Tricks should I Teach my animal?

For a combat animal with an Int of 1, teach the following tricks:

Attack, Attack (so that it will attack any target), Down

For a combat animal with an Int of 2, teach the following tricks:

Attack, Attack (so that it will attack any target), Defend, Down, Heel, Seek

If your combat animal has an Int of 3 or greater, and if you're comfortable with the mechanics of Handle Animal, tailor your animal to your liking. However, because no animals have an Int above 2, and because you cannot domesticate a non-Animal with an Int above 2, this can only be achieved by magical enhancement. Not a big deal, but be prepared to spend a lot of gold.

Your combat animal should know the Defend Trick. With this trick, once combat begins, your animal will automatically take action if its personally threatened, if you are threatened, or if you command it to. You may also command your animal to Defend other specific people, which only requires a Handle Check. If your GM is feeling generous, he can allow your combat animal to defend anyone that both you and it are familiar with.

What is my best choice?

Your best choice will be limited by what your GM grants you access to. Among those choices, there are some factors to consider. Animals and Magical Beasts with Int scores of 1 tend to have special abilities, but are more difficult to control during combat. If you are of a level and have access to magical items which boost their intelligence, then they become a viable choice. However, the most powerful straight killing-machines all have an Int of 2, which can also be raised, and so you'll generally want to pick combat animals with an Int of 2.

Once your pet has been acquired and trained, you'll want to employ them effectively. Keep in mind that these animals are not invulnerable, and should be used with the understanding that they may end up taking mortal wounds. At that point, the pet is gone and must be replaced. I will explain how I would choose to use them in a later part of this guide, under Combat Scenarios. But the gist is to use them opportunistically.

Which would you choose, Mr. Cynic?

If this were my 1st level character, my wish list by priority would be:

Purchasable (Assuming 100g budget): Leopard, 2 Aurochs, 3 Constrictor Snakes, 3 Ram, 3 Guard Dogs

Rearable: Tiger, Lion, Deinonychus, 2 Bison, Rhinoceros, Elk (Megaloceros), 3 Thylacines (Blood Cougar)

STEP 3: Using Handle Animal during Combat

How do I use Handle Animal in combat?

There are only two checks that one can make in Combat: Handle, and Push. This is where a Druid or Ranger has an advantage over you. They can Handle as a Free action, and Push as a Move action. As an Animal Speaker Bard, you'll follow the standard rule of Handle as a Move action and Push as a Full-round. “Handle” means to command it to perform one specific Trick.

Because of how we've built this character, Handling will be an auto-success even at level 1, and so simply spend a Move action to change what your pet is doing. However, it isn't entirely necessary to Handle your pet during combat if it knows the Attack or Defend Tricks. It will automatically attack the nearest target that threatens you or it, and then move on to the next nearest threatening target, etc.

Will my pet react on its own during combat?

Your combat animal will automatically attack the nearest apparent enemy if it knows the Attack or Defend tricks, so long as your animal can perceive the threat you need not make a Handle check for them to engage. Animals respond when they hear your commands, and so you need not Handle each animal individually so long as you want them all to follow the same command. Handling becomes complicated when you want to finesse what each animal does during combat. In this case, you can only Handle one animal at a time, and so if you're one of those 'menagerie' types, you may end up losing control of your pets in situations that would require otherwise.

Can I take 10 during Combat?

You cannot Take 10 during combat, and so to Handle successfully you'll want your Handle Animal skill bonus to be at least +11. Because skills do not auto-fail on a natural 1, your minimum skill check result will be 1 plus 11 = 12. You'll auto-succeed even if your pet takes normal or non-lethal damage, or ability score damage (each of which increases the DC to 12).

When will I want to Push my animal?

Pushing your animal is the tricky part. If it cannot learn enough tricks, its behavior will cause a bit of chaos for the party. For example, an Animal that doesn't know the trick Stay, will begin to roam about when you're not present, or even when you are. One must be in firm control of his animals, or else they might attack harmless passersby, or simply roam away never to return. As far as your animal is concerned, all the world's a fence post, and so that's a lot of territory to mark ;)

Pushing requires a Handle Animal check DC 25. If successful, your pet will perform a trick that it does not know, that it is physically capable of performing, and the instructions of which it can understand. If have the ability to Speak with Animals, and if they have an attitude of Helpful via Charm Animal, or some other class ability, they'll be willing to do anything that you can explain to them, so long as you don’t need Push them to get over their instinct to avoid complicated or dangerous tasks, or to calm down once riled up which is also against an animal's instincts. You can take 20 to Push an animal when out of combat, and so if you have 2 minutes, this will allow you to auto-succeed on every check, even at level 1. If you are in combat, this will be a difficult prospect for you throughout the early levels, and given the full-round time for Pushing, one shouldn't bother unless the situation is dire.

STEP 4: Information on available Animals, Magical Beasts, and Others

So which Animals are available?

All of them, or few to any of them depending on the campaign and the balance judgments of your GM. As I said above, given no restrictions, and regional availability, you can rear a T-Rex, and once domesticated and mature, have him run around swallowing all of your enemies whole.

Just to perform some mental exercise, lets consider what level would be required to do this. The DC to rear a Tyrannosaurus is 15 plus its HD at maturity, which is 18. The DC is 33. Taking 10, you'd need a skill bonus of 23. At level 5, with a Wealth By Level of 10,500g, I could have a magical item with a magical +7 Skill bonus to Handle Animal, and a Circlet of Persuasion which adds another +3 to all Charisma-based checks. My maxed out skill bonus will be +25; by Taking 10, I auto-succeed. According to the Nova website on pbs.org, it takes 14 years for the T-Rex to become a 1 ton Juvenile (which is equivalent to the Young Template), and 18 years to become a fully mature adult.

But realistically having an 8 Ton, Gargantuan, 18 HD combat animal at level 5, is probably not going to sit well with your GM. And feeding it... you'll need virgins, lots of them ;) So please take this list for what it is: Comprehensive, not permissive.

Those animals listed with a GP value are those commonly available for sale. Animal “Kinds” is not clearly defined by Pathfinder, and so I have generalized grouped “Kinds” as a suggestion. Not all animals were listed with a Market Price, as they are not typically sold. All Magical Beasts reach full maturity in 1 year. The maturation time that I have listed for each animal is based on a web search, and are not official. However, there isn't anything official, yet it is a very important part of Rearing. As for the list of Other creatures that can be domesticated, your guess on maturation is as good as mine. However, I would suggest 1 year as they're closer to Magical Beasts in regards to growing up fast and furiously.

STEP 6:

What does it cost to Keep and Feed my pet?

From the book:

Feed (per day)

Price 5 cp; Weight 10 lbs.

Horses, donkeys, mules, and ponies can graze in some regions to sustain themselves, but feed provides more reliable nourishment. Riding dogs and other carnivores can be fed meat for the same cost. Except for horses, other Large creatures need to eat twice the feed of other animals each day. Huge creatures need four times the feed.

Cage

Price varies; Weight varies

This secure, portable enclosure is used to hold creatures—generally animals, but a large enough cage can hold anything. Cages are made from iron, wood, or bamboo, depending on local customs and available materials. One cage holds one animal of that size, or five animals of a smaller size. The weight of the creature adds to the weight of the cage. Cages come in the following sizes: Diminutive or Fine, Tiny, Small, Medium, Large, and Huge. Cages for aquatic creatures cost twice as much, and weigh four times the normal weight when filled with water.

Stabling (per day) 5sp

What if my character owns a Range, how does that affect the cost of Keeping and Feeding?

If your character owns property, he can set up fenced areas in which his animals can roam, graze, and maybe even hunt. However, we're now completely in GM discretion territory ;)
