Why be a Monster Summoner?

The obvious answer to why you want to summon monsters is because it’s cool. You get to summon succubi. You can ask creatures to do your cleaning (take care - last time I summoned a gelatinous cube to clean my tower it ate the carpet and chairs as well as the dirt) and as for homework, what can’t you ask? But being serious, Summon Monster X (or Summon Nature's Ally X) is a great spell for several reasons:

  • Flexibility
  • Expendability
  • Speed
  • Stat Agnosticism

Flexibility

Because Summon Monster is one spell per level with half a dozen different options, it has more flexibility than just about any other spell in the game. Meet fliers you can’t reach, summon eagles. Meet things with protection/good, smite them. This makes it perfect for spontaneous casters with limited spells known such as sorcerers - or just for indecisive wizards. Prepare Summon Monster and you know you’ve never prepared the wrong spell.

Expendability

Summoned monsters will vanish in a few rounds. So you can take insane risks with them without being too worried. And any time the enemy’s attacking a summoned monster they aren’t attacking one of your guys so it’s effectively a wasted attack. Even the Summoner’s Eidolon isn’t this expendable. Just watch for area of effect attacks.

Speed

Monster summoning is fast. Or more accurately it shatters the action economy. If you spend your turn summoning a monster and the monster then casts a spell on its turn you’ve traded a productive action for a productive action. If the monster casts a second spell, the round after, this spell is effectively quickened. And if you summon more than one monster and they all cast spells you’re twinning spells at the very least.

To illustrate, Summon Monster 5 can summon a Bralani Azata, and one of its abilities is to be able to cast Lightning Bolt twice at caster level 6. I’d rate a level 6 lightning bolt from a level 9 caster as about equivalent to a second level spell. An awful return and just one reason Summon Monster is often thought to be weak.

Now, let’s turn crazier. We want to summon the Bralani Azata in a 7th level slot so it can lightning bolt. We have the Superior Summons feat and are slightly unlucky to end up with four Bralani. Each Bralani immediately casts a lightning bolt. We’re up to 24d6 - or almost the raw damage output of a Meteor Swarm. And they do it again the next turn. The Evoker meanwhile turns green with envy as, after doing over 150 damage across a wide area, the Bralani Azata all turn away and heal people. To match just the lightning bolts with a metamagicked lightning bolt of his own he’d need something like an Empowered (+2) Maximised (+3) Repeated (+3) lightning bolt for a level 11 spell slot (8 with the Arcane Thesis feat). And this is just something you can do almost as a throwaway spell - the only feats it took apply to all your summonings.

Stat Agnosticism

Summoning is a very low-impact activity. With the exception of feats (which it is unfortunately very greedy with, especially at lower levels) you can slap this specialization on a lot of characters. It's not just SAD, it's practically NAD; a summoner that has the bare minimum of stats to cast their spells and 8-10 in everything else can still be a contributing party member.

So not only do you have the flexibility to still play a strong character with a poor CharGen ability roll or restrictive point buy game, but you can branch off into other specializations to make yourself more versatile - or even just pick up roleplaying hooks that you normally wouldn't be able to. Did you want your Herald Caller to break the usual trend of DEX 12/INT 7 caster clerics? You can get away with putting a 14 in WIS and pumping up DEX and INT, using your traits to pick up Disable Device, Stealth, and Trapfinding, then playing your cleric as a trap-master while still being effective in combat. This kind of versatility would be impossible with Battle or Bad Touch or Blaster or even Archer clerics thanks to demands on their stats.

What are the drawbacks to summoning?

Summoning is very powerful. Encounter-wreckingly powerful. Especially if you’re a primary spellcaster like summoners tend to be. But it’s not all good.

Summon Management

We’re not just talking about how summons often have a problem of getting in other party members’ way, though that is a thing that frequently happens. One of the biggest drawbacks to summoning is that if you have too many summons on the board it can clog the flow of the game. There’s the obvious example of having to declare the actions of, say, 8 lantern archons but also the more insidious example of doing things like calculating 8 saving throws or track 8 separate hit point counters. Even if it’s not as objectively powerful as dropping a Dazing Admixture’d Fireball with metamagic reducers or Envy Variant Channeling + Confusion + Charming Smile, it will still tick people off if you have poor summon management.

Here are some tips to keep the other players and DM happy:

  • Let other players control your summons. If there’s one thing you can do to stop the other players from getting bored or, worse, jealous it’s this. When you summon 4 small earth elementals and trivialize an encounter, it will feel less like the Raistlin "Mary Sue" McCutsuptinyanimals show and more of a team effort. But not only does this create buy-in from your other party members but it also cuts down on the number of things you need to track. One of the writers never plays his own summons anymore unless they're doing something really complicated. Yes, this will take quite a bit more effort on your part. We recommend having index cards or printouts of your summons and giving them to your buddies.
  • Try not to summon more than four critters at once unless a TPK is on the line or you’re playing with a group of hardcore optimizers who don’t mind a little (well, a lot) of gameplay inconvenience for raw power. In fact, summoning just two top-level summons will make combat go a lot more smoothly than 5-7 almost top-level summons.
  • If you have multiple summons out, try to have them acting on the same turn and doing the same thing. Again, unless your party is cool with it, have them act on the same initiative count.
  • Space permitting, roll all of your dice at once. And we do mean all of them. Do you have 3 celestial eagles trying to dogpile an ogre? Roll 9 d20s AND all 9 damage dice at once. If you could have them color-coded for each eagle, even better. Yes, it can be a pain, but it’s your job as a player fiddling with 2 to 6 (or more) tokens to make things more convenient for other players at the table.
  • Also, it goes without saying: summoners who don’t have the monster statistics on hand and with common calculations already made (templates, augmented summoning, Evolved Summon monster, etc.) are jerks who don’t even deserve to play such a versatile role.

The CR-Gap

The CR-Gap is the biggest downside to focusing on summoning. The monsters you summon will get behind on CR and associated goodies such as HD and special abilities and the gap will only get larger as you gain more summon monster spells. You will really start to notice the CR-Gap once you get to SM6, where even the bruiser monsters only have about an unaided 50-60% chance of hitting on their primary attack against level-appropriate opposition. Even worse is how quickly summons become obsolete if you cast them out of spell slots. A Suggestion or Phantasmal Killer can still be useful at level 12. Summon Monster IV or even V at that level is much dicier.

The CR-Gap is manageable. For one, CR is assigned kind of arbitrarily. For two, CR is assigned based on how difficult a monster is for PCs to defeat, not how much butt a monster can kick against other monsters. A shadow demon isn’t super-difficult for PCs to take on but it’s a terror against most monsters of its CR. Finally, AC doesn’t rise as quickly as attack bonuses. The Dire Tiger has a CR of 8 but has 14 HD, with  attack bonuses so high that it can reliably hit creatures that are CR 12+. Still, the gap is big enough that it shuts out dabblers for the higher level spells and forces people to specialize in summoning or stick to the creatures that are overpowered for their CR such as Tyrannosaurus. You will also seriously need to consider a way to buff your creatures without losing too many actions. Get used to flanking bonuses and attacking from higher ground and if you have a familiar you should strongly consider putting them on buff duty, even (especially) if it means UMDing wands of haste and animal growth.

Unusual Environments

“Creatures cannot be summoned into an environment that cannot support them” is a very ambiguous clause. A shark or a whale can last for a significant amount of time on land, but many DMs won’t let you summon them out of water. And what about other environments? Can you summon a flyer in an area with strong winds? Am I not allowed to summon a huge Earth Elemental if our party is fighting on the rooftops? If you’re on the Elemental Plane of Fire, can you summon a creature without fire resistance? What about a creature with fire resistance but not enough to avoid scratch damage? What about a space choked with a Wall of Fire or Acid Fog? What about extremely unusual environments such as psionic battlefields or dreamscapes?

This is the biggest way a DM can use Rule Zero to unexpectedly screw your schtick over. Have a nice long discussion with your DM with what this means and familiarize yourself with second-tier summons that might be able to get around this clause.

*Spell Effects

There are a lot of spell effects that can really mess your summons up such as Banishment and Plane Shift. However, this is generally less onerous than you think; a Charm Monster that targets your T-Rex is a Charm Monster that’s not aimed at your party fighter or yourself. And if you’re summoning multiple creatures, this is a race that your opposition is bound to lose. Nonetheless, there are a number of game effects that can bone your summoner if you’re not careful.

Area of Effect Spells: The most common answer a creature has to the action race. Thanks to the CR-gap, your summons will have fairly low saves, especially will and reflex saves. Illusions and enchantments can really mess your day up and there’s not much you can do about it. If your summons get targeted by a Mass Suggestion or a Confusion, remember that your summons are dismissible! If you’re a Summoner or an Occultist or a class with a limit on summons, don’t be afraid of summoning a new set of creatures to get you out of your current jam.

Anti-Magic Fields: The good news is that these are rare. Most spellcasters are very reluctant to use this spell and there aren’t many creatures who can use this effect natively. The bad news is that this can completely screw you over. There aren’t really any easy summoning counters; about the best you can do is summon creatures with a lot of mundane ranged attacks such as giants or Azata and plink away.

Protection/Magic Circle Spells: These spells will be the bane of your existence as a summoner. Nothing like summoning a horde of celestial lions but then having them not able to approach your target because they’re warded with a Magic Circle Against Good. A particularly problematic spell is the Communal Protection spell line, since they ward a good number of creatures and can be adjusted on the fly to counter your critter selection. To get around these spells, familiarize yourself with the ranged options and don’t be afraid of summoning a TN monster. Versatile Summon Monster, discussed below, is also a great way of getting around this restriction with animal summons.

Dispel Magic: The regular dispel magic spell is not a super big deal, since it can only target one creature at a time. However, it is a very common spell-like ability for outsiders to have, so don’t get cocky. Greater Dispel Magic is much more problematic since it can wipe out all of your summons in addition to whatever battlefield control effects you might have. The standard trick of thwarting an area dispel (cast one long-lasting but unimportant buff at a higher CL than your more important ones) is very hard to implement due to the low CL of summoned monster abilities and the short duration of summons. Be sure to grab caster level boosts whenever you can, but most of the time you’ll just have to grin and bear it.

Communicating With Summons

Sometimes, a little finesse with your summons might be required. Such as: don’t step on those glowing runes over there. Or: don’t bother with the small fry, go dogpile the bandit leader. Unfortunately, a large portion of your summoning list have a hard time understanding common languages and a number of those creatures can’t understand a language at all.

This is a bigger problem at low-level than at high-level. At higher-level, not only do monsters that understand common languages show up more often, but you also have ways to communicate with monsters that have obscure or no language.

Before you get to that point there are a few ways you can deal with this problem.

  • Select the Herald Caller cleric archetype and call it a day.
  • Handle Animal checks. This is less than ideal, because the DC is devilishly hard at lower levels to push an animal (25-32, depending on the target and other modifiers!) and it takes a full-round action regardless.
  • Slap an elemental template onto the monster with Versatile Summon Monster and cast Elemental Speech.
  • If you have a familiar and you summon the right kind of animal (i.e. a feline familiar directing lions and leopards) you can relay orders to your summons via your familiar.
  • Train up Linguistics for languages that you know that you’re going to use. For example, Small Earth Elementals are a very strong pick for their levels and you can grab Terran if need be.
  • The Speak with Animals spell. If you have a way to spam this spell, it can get you out of a lot of jams. Unfortunately, it’s not comprehensive; it does nothing to help you communicate with magical beasts or aberrations. Fortunately, the vast majority of non-sapient summons at low levels are animals.
  • The Voice of Monsters trait. Unlike Speak with Animals, it also works with Aberrations and Magical Beasts. A strict wording of the feat allows you to do this whenever you cast or use Speak With Animals, not just the 1/day use of this feat. Alternatively, the Pelt of the Beast will permanently take care of that problem, but it’s a bit pricey for this one function.
  • If you really have money to burn, Gloves of the Commanding Conjurer will take care of all of your problems. Of course, by the time you can painlessly afford the 30k gp price tag you're kind of beyond languageless summons.

Intraparty Jealousy

Don’t laugh, it’s something that summoners have to think about on a metagame level. Summons can do a lot of damage. Soul-crushing amounts of damage. 4 Celestial Dire Tigers, easily achievable at level 13, easily dish out 300 damage in a round. It’s not a huge deal at low and very high levels (the CR-Gap really starts to hamper your effectiveness) but around levels 7 to 15 it’s very likely that one casting of your spell can completely outclass all but the most optimized of DPR-characters for an entire combat.

Unless your party members are cool with this or they have some other useful fallback tactic such as ranged DPR or control spells of their own, take it easy with the megadamage. Save it for when there’s a TPK on the line and try not to bore people by summoning the same monsters over and over again. Pathfinder changed summoning from the stupid pet trick it was in 3.5E D&D to a viable and even dominating tactic and we don’t want to remind people about CoDzilla.

Building your Monster Summoner

Races

Humans - As usual, humans get the long end of the stick. The bonus feat is a godsend to such a feat-starved chassis. And because summoners are generally stat-agnostic, only having one +2 isn’t that bad of a deal.

Acadamae Graduate wizards might really appreciate the Heart of the Fields ability at low level, what with its ability to ignore a source of fatigue or exhaustion once per day. People of the Summoner or Cleric class likewise might really enjoy the Fey Magic trait, not so much for the spell-like abilities (though having Aspect of the Falcon/Entangle/Snowball/etc. is pretty damn boss) but for the ability to pick two skills as class skills, no questions asked. Yes, I would like to have Perception or Use Magic Device or Diplomacy on speed dial very much. Wayfarer is also quite helpful for the human looking to snag as many languages to communicate with summons as possible.

Elves - Elves with the Overwhelming Magic trait can snag Spell Focus as a bonus feat, immediately giving them parity with Human summoners, save for summoner wizards in Pathfinder Society Games.

Summoners

Perhaps unsurprisingly Summoners make excellent Monster Summoners - in fact I’d say point blank that they make the best Monster Summoners in the game as long as their Eidolon isn’t getting underfoot.  This is for two reasons:

  • Summoners get more high level summons than any other class in the game (3+Cha Modifier of the highest level summons anyone gets.
  • Summoners summon monsters as Standard actions, meaning monsters act the turn you cast the spell. Almost the equivalent of quickening it.

For these two reasons the Summoner is almost certainly the best Monster Summoner in the game even if they only get to do this trick once per encounter. Speed is life and they are simply the fastest. Their summons also last for one minute/level, meaning that at low level they aren’t quite so “use-and-throw” and might make a couple of fights at high level.  Even the supposedly nerfed “Unchained Summoner” has the Summoning part intact.

The Master Summoner specialist class can summon top tier monsters for two rounds rather than one, further leveraging this speed advantage and cutting down his stamina to “only” about five fights per day going flat out. That said, at a potential average of nine allies in a fight people might complain. This archetype is also banned in PFS, so expect people to use that against you if they’re getting salty over your build.

Summoner Dirty Tricks: 

Double Summons: If the Summoner uses his Summon Monster SLA when it is already active, the previous set of summons are dismissed. But Summon Monster as an SLA is only a standard action. So if you summoned your monsters on turn 1, you activate them on turn 2, have them attack, and then use your Summon Monster SLA again - dismissing the old set, but activating the new set after you’ve gone. This does lower your endurance significantly.

Low Level Spells: Summoner summoning spells are lower level than normal, which allows them to use cheaper Metamagic Rods when casting spells off their spell list. If you aren’t using Metamagic Rods Summon Monster 7 is only a 5th level spell on the Summoner Spell List it’s entirely possible to use Spell Perfection to quicken it at the cost of a lot of feats - but that means you can summon d4+2 Quickened Bralani Azatas - each one producing a repeated 6d6 lightning bolt (as mentioned above). It’s enough to make any Evoker turn green with envy. (This is the only substantial nerf the Unchained Summoner has if they aren’t getting their Eidolon underfoot).

Note that to clear up doubt, Jason Bulmahn has ruled Augment Summoning applies to the Summon Monster Class Feature of Summoners - this is because other than reduced casting time and not having slots (so no metamagic works on SLAs) the SLA functions in all ways like the relevant spell.

Spell-Like Ability Metamagic: This trick is so dirty that we’ll warn you ahead of time that you should expect a demand from the DM to remake your character if you do this. Only do this if you’re playing in a really high-optimization game. But basically, there’s nothing stopping you from grabbing the Metamagic feats for your Summon Monster SLA other than caster level. The limiting factor on your cheese is caster level: so be sure to grab effects like Orange Prism Ioun Stones, the Mage’s Tattoo feat, UMD + Prayer Beads of Karma, so-on.  It is important to note that you MUST have  something to give you additional caster levels or the feat does not work.  Since your summon monster SLA automatically scales with your level you can never for an example cast summon monster IV as a 10th level summoner because it has already scaled to V so you no longer have the ability to cast summon monster IV as a spell-like ability.  This needs to be really stressed or someone could end up with a worthless feat.  Even then you’re giving your gm license to steal, dispell or otherwise hijinx whatever is giving you the bonus thus killing your use of the feat.

Prestige Class: Evangelist. This prestige class falls under the heading of “Mick, the, are you taking it?” But it’s the designers not you who are at fault. As a second level PRC feature you gain all the abilities of your base class. Plus a list of new ones. For the cost of a single feat. For a guide on Obediences, see Walter’s Guide.

Sorcerers

The problem with the Sorcerer-as-summoner is that it comes out second best to the Summoner. It looks like a great option for the Sorcerer, making them really flexible. But one level in two the Sorcerer is a whole level of summons behind and doesn’t have the Summoner’s standard action summons. Also a Master Summoner has about as many uses of Summon Monster as a Sorcerer can cast out of their top two levels of spells.

It’s a decent way of playing a sorcerer - but the Summoner is better at what the Summoner is focussed at. On the other hand, making your first spell known to be the summons and training it out when you can take some variety is a good default option. 

Note that an Abyssal Bloodline Sorcerer at level 15 gets to summon an extra demon or fiendish creature (most of the standard monsters) every time he summons one for some very nasty work at Summon Monster IX. This is extra powerful when combined with the Superior Summoning feat which lets you summon an extra monster whenever you are summoning more than one and if you have a Robe of Arcane Heritage you would obtain this power as early as level 11. If you have the Wild-blooded Bedrock bloodline, your summons would also obtain DR/Adamantine equal to half your class level.

Wizards

It’s a trap at first glance... though wizards, being wizards, have ways to turn a trap option into something dominating. The major advantage the wizard has over the cleric is quality of spells. This means that any time you restrict yourself to spells on the cleric list you would probably be better off as a cleric.

Of course we’re talking about Wizards here; the Academae Graduate feat allows standard action summons - and fatigue can be dealt with.

Wizards, or rather Academae Graduate wizards, have two other major advantages over Summoners and Occultist Arcanists: the ability to use the supremely awesome Master’s Staff of Necromancy and the Rod of Giant Summoning on standard-action summons at all. The efficacy of these options cannot be denied; they by themselves make wizards a top-tier choice for summoners, with only Master Summoners being able to reliably beat them on speed. Finally, Pathfinder Society makes people trade out their Scribe Scroll feat for Spell Focus: Conjuration. Check with your DM to see if they’ll let you use that option for non-PFS games.

Wizard Specialities and archetypes

Strongly consider going Sin Magic Specialist if you’re going a wizard summoner (Evocation is fairly droppable and you still have one of the mind control schools). Keep in mind that per the FAQ Sin Magic Specialists still get their base arcane school abilities, even if they can’t specialize. Sin Magic Specialists can be played in PFS, but require you to complete a certain adventure path first. Be warned that it’s easily one of the most difficult APs ever published (comfortably up there with infamous APs such as the Red Hand of Doom and even Tomb of Horrors), with TPKs a very high possibility. And since you can’t apply it to the character that wins, you’ll either have to run it as a DM or get another character up to level 7-8 first.

Another option for those interested in using your Conjuration (summoning) Wizard in PFS is the Student of Thassilon Boon from the Chronicle sheet for Season 4, Session 26.  It is the ending of a long story relating to the Runelord of Sloth, Krune.  

The Boon reads: “Through the study of Krune's sanctum and personal effects, Pathfinder sages have deciphered many of the secrets of Thassilonian magic and how to avoid the sinful excesses that twisted Xin's magic. When building a new character for Pathfinder Society Organized Play, you may make use of the Thassilonian magic rules on page 17 of Pathfinder Campaign Setting: Inner Sea Magic. As with other rules outside the core assumption, you must provide a legal copy of the book to use this option.”

Using this option, you can become a Thassilonian Wizard using the Conjuration (Sloth) specialization in PFS.  “A Thassilonian specialist wizard receives two additional spell slots of each spell level he can cast. These bonus spell slots must both be used to prepare the same spell from the wizard’s school of specialization, allowing the wizard to cast that spell twice (as he has prepared the spell twice). The wizard cannot use these slots to prepare two different spells, even if they are of the school he is

specialized in.” So basically It nets you +2 spells per spell level, but the big drawback is loss of Evocation Spells (including the cantrip Light!) and Illusion spells.  Meaning, they aren’t even on your spell list.  But, trust me, you won’t need to bother with Evocation with this specialization.

You can still make a regular specialist wizard work with the build, even if you don’t pick up the Item Arcane Bond. In fact, at later levels a familiar will be more useful than the Item Arcane Bond if you put them on UMD/magic item activation duty. Some of the school abilities are quite nifty and you don’t have to endure the pain of having an entirely banned school. As far as banning schools go:

  • Abjuration: The weakest school in the game. Many of the good spells in the school (Magic Circle, Communal Protection From Energy/Evil, Dispel Magic, Dimensional Anchor) the cleric can already cast - however, there are exceptions such as Mind Blank. Unless you’re the only one in the party with access to these spells feel free to ban this school.
  • Conjuration: You’re not banning conjuration. What are you, crazy? You wouldn’t ban this school even if you weren’t a summoner wizard. The school abilities are great, too. An extra round or two of summoning really helps at low level.
  • Divination: This school is your ‘win the fight before it even starts’ school, made better in Pathfinder with the proliferation of d20 boosters and REALLY good school abilities. The school abilities are super-boss even if it’s something of a waste of spell slots. It’s a fairly droppable school for regular wizards (since many of the spells are generally cast in downtime anyway) but unless you have another wizard in the party you don’t want to ban it entirely. Fortunately, SMS doesn’t allow you to drop Divination.
  • Enchantment: Enchantment is full of power but lacks somewhat with versatility. And the usefulness of the school really starts to taper off at around level 14 of  of the game -- admittedly, many of the D&D games end long before this point. If you’re starting at higher level you can probably get anyway with banning this school.
  • Illusion: True seeing and truesight isn’t super-common even at the high levels, but it’s enough to ruin your day. Again, if you’re starting at higher level you can probably get anyway with banning this school. If you’re starting at low level, think long and hard about banning this school; honestly, color spray and silent image will win your team more encounters than SM1 and SM2. This school also contains Simulacrum, which is hideously powerful.
  • Evocation: This used to be the weakest school in 3.5E, but Pathfinder made it a lot more useful. Nonetheless, it’s mostly still just straight damage effects with sometimes a status effect thrown in and as a summoner the only spell you’ll really miss is contingency -- which is usually cast as a downtime spell anyway.
  • Necromancy: This is the grab-bag of game effects school. You can safely ban it without hurting your core competency, but specializing in necromancy gives you a lot of versatility. If your DM will let you, having an undead horde to go with your summons is the most boss thing ever.
  • Transmutation: Transmutation has a crapton of filler in it. It’s the biggest school and about 2/3rds of the spells you don’t care about, especially since you’re a summoner with short-duration summons. There are a few too many gems to ban this school, though.

One last thing: if you’re an elf, a half-elf, or a human or half-orc with the Racial Heritage feat, you may be interested in the Spellbinder wizard archetype. The real purpose of this archetype is to use Spell Bond to replace your prepared specialization spells with something else. You do lose out on Arcane Bond (though humans with the Racial Heritage feat and half-elves can use their bonus Skill Focus feat to get Eldritch Heritage: Arcane Bloodline) but it allows you to prepare top-tier Summon Monster spells while having another school ability. Conjuration, especially its subschools, is such an excellent school for regular specialists as-is that this probably won’t interest you. But if you’re a Spellbinder that picks Sin Magic Specialist going Abjuration, Enchantment, or Necromancy hurts you less on banned school choice while still letting you prepare Summon Monster spells in your specialization slots.

Pact Wizard is worth picking up if you’re not going Sin Magic Specialist (as it requires banning another school - namely illusion) and you really want Improved Familiar without spending a bonus feat. Unfortunately, the archetype comes with a build-in clause where your familiar tattles on you and your DM imposes a code-of-conduct. But if you don’t mind that then, hey, free feat. You don’t really care about having the ability to pick up Sacred Summons unless your DM is blocking you from picking Academae Graduate. But if that applies to you (and you can’t get into another game) then this archetype works as a last resort of last resorts. Sacred Summons does have a trick you can pull with Balanced Summoning to greatly expand the list. See the writeup on Balanced Summoning for more details.. This can also help a lot with communicating with summoned monsters (see the truespeech ability)

Spell Sage is normally something you’d never do -- why give up your arcane bond for a 1-3/day +4 CL boost and your school specialization for the ability to cast cleric/druid/bard spells so slowly that it’s not a combat option? You need those spell slots pretty badly! However, Spell Sage is also one of the few ways for a wizard that doesn’t want to multiclass/variant multiclass with a cleric to be able to (technically) spontaneously cast cure spells. With the Believer’s Boon feat for the animal or plant domain, you can pick up Spontaneous Nature’s Ally. Note that, with Acadamae Graduate, this is one of the only ways to cast SNA spells as a standard action -- and unlike druids, you have access to the entire list.

If sacrificing the arcane bond is a bit too much for you, however, there’s still the Spirit Whisperer archetype. If you select the Nature spirit, you can spontaneously cast Summon Nature’s Ally spells, no additional feats or expansion options needed.

Familiar Adept, or rather the Summoned Shell option of this archetype, isn’t really worth it until higher levels thanks to the cost in bonus feats unless you’re having a lot of trouble communicating with your summons or you’re doing something cheesy such as having a multi-hit die homunculus with useful combat feats using Summoned Shell. Regardless, since it requires a feat payment upfront and doesn’t shell out the Summoned Shell until level 10, it’s only an option for high-level games anyway.

Summoned Shell does have one really powerful use if you’re willing to endure the feat loss: transferring the buffs from your familiar over to your summoned monster. Some examples:

  • Contingency + Any other spell. If you’re really into cheese, pair it with Paragon Surge (thank god for that clause in share spells, eh?) and snag Quicken Spell-Like Ability. Or if you’re just sort-of into cheese, Power Attack or Multiattack.
  • Form of the Dragon + A High-STR monster (looking at you, Mr. T-Rex).
  • Permanency + Greater Magic Fang. Or just have the druid cast it at the beginning of the day.
  • Communal Stoneskin. Or Defending Bone if you’re a cheapskate.
  • Statue for the 8 hardness.
  • Monstrous Extremities for a cut-rate version of Evolved Summoned Monster.
  • Amplify Stench. As if Hezrou needed more reasons to be OP.
  • And there’s always the simple, long-lasting buffs. Mage Armor, Magic Circle Against Evil, False Life, Heroism, and Greater Scale Spikes.

Arcanists (non-Occultist)

Arcanists are hybrid Sorcerer-Wizards. The default Arcanist manages to combine all the reasons wizards don’t make good summoners with all the reasons sorcerers don’t make good summoners. That said, a Blood Arcanist might be slightly better than most wizards, especially if your DM lets you use School Understanding to snag Acadamae Graduate. See below.

Brown-Fur Transmuters, however, do have one saving grace: the ability to cast personal-range touch spells onto other targets. It’s still not good enough to make up for the massive advantages that Occultist Arcanists get (and the archetypes are mutually exclusive) but if you’re looking to replicate the rather powerful trick of polymorphing a creature with great stats but few or terrible natural attacks into a buzzsaw monstrosity, Brown-Fur Transmuters is 4-6 levels ahead of the competition.

Arcanists (Occultist)

Occultist Arcanists get a summons equal to the Summoner’s, but limited uses of it - it costs 1+level/2 reserve points to use, and they only have 3+level/2 reserve points. On the other hand this is a top tier spell, effectively giving you the majority of the benefits of a Quickened Summon, and letting you drop spell slots between fights to do this again. With the normal Summoning feats the Arcanist gets more raw power than the wizard at odd levels while having the utility of the wizard and the ability to swap spells with a round’s notice for spells they haven’t prepared. If you  want to both lead with summons and be a wizard, the Arcanist is probably the way.

Unfortunately, Arcanists copped a rather rough nerf that limited the amount of times that they can recharge their arcane pool in a day by CHA uses -- making it much harder to recycle low-level spell slots for more Summon Monster uses and blocking them from using the dirty trick of Double Summons unless they regularly do one-encounter workdays. If your DM lets you use the unerrata’d book, though, you’re golden. If not, you’ll be facing some rough choices if you’re not okay with using your arcane reservoir for nothing but summons. A 10th level Arcanist with CHA 14 and is willing to set two 3rd-level spells on fire has 16 points to play with; enough for three castings of SMV while leaving their fifth and fourth level slots untouched.

School Understanding is a first-class exploit (hint: grab the Foresight or Void schools) but sadly RAW won’t let you pick up Acadamae Graduate with it -- you’re a specialist, but you’re not a first level-specialist wizard. Your DM may let you do so anyway, however, so check with them!

Bloodline Development: Arcane, combined with the Familiar exploit, is one of the very few ways for a character to have an Item Arcane Bond and a Familiar at the same time. If for some reason you feel the urge to multiclass sorcerer (perhaps for the Abyssal bloodline?) this will let your levels fully stack.

Potent Magic should be one of the first exploits you pick. +2 to caster level and DCs of spells you cast in exchange for a mere point? Oh my.

Spell Tinkerer is a remarkable arcanist exploit on its own merits, but making summons that would normally last for 10 minutes at 8th level (assuming certain traits or Potent Magic) last for 15 minutes makes it much easier to enter combat with your summons already out. And speed is everything to a summoner.

Clerics

Clerics are as good at baseline summoning as a wizard - which means that this is a better option for them than it is for a Wizard, and they can also use Sacred Summons to summon a few monsters at Summoner speed. But they don’t have the advantage Summoners do, and make better Necromancers. If you want to play a caster that summons rather than a summoning caster the Cleric is highly recommended. 

Cleric Domains and Archetypes

The Herald Caller archetype was literally made to be a clerical summoner, and gives a good grab bag of goodies including spontaneously summoning monsters and the Augment and Superior summons feats for free. Unfortunately the archetype has three drawbacks

  • Restricted list of summonable monsters; your summons must share at least one part of the alignment with their deity (get the Summon Good/Neutral/Evil monster feats - but that’s only a partial solution). Note that Sacred Summons works best for ‘extreme’ alignments and this archetype works best for neutral alignments. Neutral Good, Neutral, and especially Neutral Evil characters work best with this archetype.
  • The Herald Caller only gets one domain rather than two, and loses medium armour and shields (later you just get a Mithral Buckler and ignore the second part).
  • Augment Summoning and Superior Summoning come on a little later than you’d probably like, at levels 4 and 8. It’s not a complete loss, but unless you can retrain or waste feats you’ll have to go without.This isn’t a bad deal if you plan for it. Just take another feat you will need anyway (many don’t have Augment Summoning as a prereq anyway) and once you hit 4th you get Augment for free and you’re on your way.

To sum up, the Herald Caller with Sacred Summons is a very effective Summoner when it’s good but much more tightly focussed than a normal one. Which probably means even more prep work rather than less; looking down your chosen monster domains. It’s a mixed bag depending how much you like the expanded list of options.

The Ecclesitheurge is another option for cleric summoners. Unlike Herald Caller, it doesn’t boost your summons directly. It’s still a berserkly powerful archetype as it allows you to mix-and-match domains from your deity (and if you have some downtime, lets you mix and match spells in your domain slots) and spontaneously cast any cleric spell known without consuming a slot. This is game-changingly powerful both coming and going.

If you really, really must have a particular domain the Separatist archetype is a great alternate pick. The only real downside of Separatist is that you can’t combine it with another cleric archetype, so you miss out on some goodies. That said, it’s pretty much the only way to have certain domains that get restricted to one end of the alignment spectrum. Try finding an evil deity that grants the Community or Liberation domain or a good deity that grants Dark Tapestry.

Finally, there’s the Evangelist archetype. Getting inspire courage/greatness and eventually being able to use them as move and swift actions is incredibly helpful for closing the CR gap.

There’s also the Exalted Prestige Class as an upgrade on the basic cleric. It’s an acceptable option, but its biggest weakness is that it requires two feats up-front and the summoner is already feat-starved.  Moreover, you’ll be behind a level on summoning and the Exalted doesn’t really give anything to help summoners until late in the PrC.

Domains

Some domains are good on their own merits, some are great for cleric summoners. Some of them are so great that they are by themselves, reasons why you would pick an Ecclesitheurge over a Herald Caller. While this guide just goes over the domains of special interest to a summoner, don’t be shy about picking only one or even none of these domains! No one is going to blame you for picking up the powerful-in-their-own-right Charm or Flotsam domains.

  • Community (Cooperation): Do you have Teamwork feats? Like, say, Improved Feint Partner? Then your summons have Teamwork feats thanks to the domain spell. Also, Unity replaces the increasingly substandard saving throw bonuses of your summons with your much better ones. It’s likely that you have the best Will saving throw of your party and there’s nothing quite like going ‘lol no’ to an otherwise-deadly Mass Suggestion or Confusion.
  • Good (Redemption): If for some reason you’re able to get this domain while being forced to summon evil monsters (i.e. you’re an evil Separatist cleric of a neutral deity with Summon Evil Monster; try justifying that one in RP!), this makes Smite Good much more usable.
  • Liberation: Aside from its usual benefit of pulling your team’s butt out of the fire when everyone rolls poorly against a Fear spell, it temporarily suppresses the grappled condition for your allies only. A lot of your summons grapple. Get the picture?
  • Luck (Fate): One of your summons has a great ability but kind of a naff DC for it? Give them another crack at it.
  • Glory (Heroism): Swift-action heroism to all of my allies within 30 feet? Where do I sign?
  • Void (Dark Tapestry): That’s right, kids, slap that advanced template onto your monsters good and hard. Unfortunately, Dark Tapestry tends to be the domain of cosmic horror deities so you’ll probably have to be a separatist to snag it. Make up some story to the DM about you being a Lovecraftian astrologer.

Variant Channeling

If you’re using Variant Channeling, you want to heal. The variant channeling options for harm are useful, too, but are beyond the scope of this guide. The biggest reason why you want to focus on the healing ones is because it’s very difficult for non-Herald Callers to exclude your summons (not to mention fellow PCs!) from the harm effect without further magic item or feat investment. Healing is easier; you just need to stay in the backline. Herald Caller lets you exclude your summons from the harm effect, so it IS an option. If you’re able to shell out the money for it (10k gp) a Malleable Symbol will take care of targeting problems.

  • Cities - Give your allies a channel bonus to attack rolls when flanking and to AC when flanked.
  • Envy - If there's one harm variant channeling you want, it's this. Forces targets to make a steal check if it lands. Not only do they lose their turn but they also draw AoOs.
  • Justice/Law - Lawful creatures get a channel bonus to perception, sense motive, attack rolls, and saving throws.
  • Rulership - Creatures gain a channel bonus on Diplomacy checks and to the DC of abilities that are charm or language-dependent. Your succubi will love you for this.
  • Self-Perfection - Creatures may ignore one temporary condition of their choice until the end of your next turn. Read the glossary carefully because condition has a well-defined meaning in Pathfinder. But who can say no to a round of ignoring paralysis, petrification, or stun?
  • Strength - Channel bonus to STR attacks, maneuver checks, STR skills, and strength checks. Read the creature entries carefully as a lot of monsters use Weapon Finesse for melee attacks.

Expanded Summoning For Priests

For the most part, these creatures aren’t very good.  They come online at the same level or later as creatures granted by other feats or even natively by the Summon Monster spell. However, there are some monsters that range from ‘slight power-up’ to ‘outrageously good’. You’ll probably only get one of these monsters, if that, so unless your campaign is going to center or end at a certain level don’t worry about what deity you pick when designing your cleric summoner.

SM2

  • Asmodeus - Hell Hound: It’s on the SM4 list normally. SM2 turns it from ‘underpowered’ to ‘situationally potent’.
  • Cayden Cailean - Celestial Cayhound: At first glance, appears to be a slightly inferior Hyena. They have more strength (but not a higher mod) but are slower and have 1 less AC. However! They have Acrobatics at a decent modifier (+14 when jumping). So in tight spaces, they can jump through enemy lines for flanking or just to get by people in a way fliers sometimes can't. This actually happens more often than you think given how cramped battlefields in Pathfinder APs can be.
  • Erastil - Celestial Elk: If attack rolls aren't an issue Celestial Elk are another great option. They have worse attack rolls than small earth elementals, but have three attacks. Augmented Summoning + Versatile Summon Monster makes that +5/2d6+4 ,+0/1d6+1d3+2, +0/1d6+1d3+2, with an average damage if all three attacks hit of 26 versus the Small Earth Elemental's +7/1d6+11 for an average of 14. The Earth Elemental is much more likely to hit, however.
  • Irori - Shikigami Kami: 22hp, Fast Healing 2, DR5/Cold Iron, and the Statue spell. Tougher than most of Summon Monster 4, never mind SM3.
  • Zon-Kuthon - Augur (kyton):DR 5/good or silver and Regen 2 and three uses of Inflict Light Wounds. Tiny isn’t such an issue when you can summon them where you need them, though keep in mind that they will suffer AoOs from entering a foe’s space and are too weak to control an area

SM3

  • Abadar - Resolute Hippogriff: Excellent ‘air’ monster with three attacks rather than the Dire Bat’s one. Often worth changing the template if you don’t mind losing the DR.
  • Erastil - Celestial Dire Boar. They're a beatstick monster through and through, but what a beatstick! Augmented Summoning and Smite make their damage an eye-popping 2d6+17, average 24, with DR 5/Good and a hit point stack of 52 to go with it. They're almost guaranteed to outdamage the party melee characters when you first get them -- so be careful about stepping on their toes.
  • Cayden Cailean - Satyr (without pipes): Ridiculous at Summon Monster 3; 52 hit points and DR 5/cold iron) and have access to at-will Suggestion at DC 17 and 1/day Fear at DC 18! If your DM can tolerate the stink of cheese, carry some extra bagpipes to toss to it since there’s mechanically nothing special about the bagpipes in of themselves.
  • Rovagug - Cythnigot: Soften Earth and Stone is a very useful spell at CL6 (a summoning level before you get the Earth Mephit), especially when you can summon 1d3+1 of them to completely upend a dungeon. Also entangles at DC 11 on a bite and has DR 5/cold iron or lawful.

SM4

  • Abadar - Resolute Griffon: Another excellent air monster, being a strict upgrade from the Hippogriff and almost as strong as the land-bound muscle the Lion. It comes with pounce! (You may want to change the template)
  • Pharasma - Catrina: Swiss-army knife of psychopomps. Calm Emotions aura breaks up fights quickly, Dominate Person acts as a slow-acting (but action-negating) save-or-die, and it even has unlimited Major Image (L3, DC20(!)). Jaw-droppingly durable, too, with a DR 5/adamantine, SR 16, and 63 HP. Would be a top pick even at SM6, having it at SM4 is just unfair.
  • Norgorber - Celestial/Fiendish Giant Black Widow Spider: Immune to mind affecting effects, Tremorsense, Fort DC 21 Poison for d3 CON can ruin a wizard’s day. Template gives it some badly-needed durability. Better than the Spider-Summoner feat, oddly.

SM5

  • Cayden Cailean - Rust Monster. Yes, really. Not as potent in Pathfinder as they are in a lot of D&D games due to the Reflex 15 save for attended objects. On the other hand, Rust Monsters will eat through metal barriers and fortifications (even magically treated adamantine ones) lickety split. Losing the object does indeed suck, but then again Pathfinder Society doesn't directly award magical equipment anyway.

SM7

  • Irori - Zuishin: No one dies tonight when this monster is summoned. As a swift-action, it can imbue a fired arrow by consuming one of its spell-like abilities of: breath of life (3 times a day!), cure light wounds, heal, neutralize poison, remove curse, or remove disease. It can also shoot up to 4 arrows a round and also adds the ‘holy’ weapon property with each arrow. If you can boost their rather mediocre attack roll, they roll over non-ranged enemies and any undead might as well go home.

Oracles

Oracles have the same relationship to Clerics that Sorcerers do to Wizards - but don’t have anything to kick their summoning ability up a notch other than the Nature domain giving their summoned animals (frequently Dire Tigers) a bit of extra magical protection.

Druids

This is another trap. Druids get Summon Nature's Ally X rather than Summon Monster X. This means they have a lot of good beatsticks, but you only need one. What they lack are the utility monsters as those are almost all outsiders - I therefore can’t recommend Summon Nature's Ally X in Pathfinder, although the much more fey D&D 3.5 version had such things as Unicorns on the level 4 list. Finally the Druid is missing the Celestial and Fiendish templates for their ‘natural’ monsters, which significantly lowers the damage per attack. The animal totems let you summon their type of animal as a standard action; most versatile is the Lion but it’s still not great.

If you absolutely have to summon as a druid, grabbing the Versatile Nature’s Ally feat is an absolute must-have. You lose out on Augmented Summoning bonuses (making Harrowed Summoning a mediocre feat instead of a completely useless one) but you get to add templates at no extra cost. Trading out 1-3 damage on a natural attack for a 2d6 bonus thanks to the Fiery Template goes a long way towards making Summon Nature’s Ally viable, though you still have the problem of Summon Nature’s Ally lacking versatility and the fact that smite damage + Augment Summoning bonuses outpace template damage at high levels.

Saurian Druids

If you don’t mind having access mostly to beatsticks, though, Saurian Druid is by far the best option of Druid Summoners. Not only do you get standard-action summons, but being able to summon dinosaurs and reptiles in and of-itself gives you access to great options at every level starting from level 5. Combined with their ability to summon monsters (if you slap the young template on them) a spell level early and they’ll never hurt for options past level 5. Summoning Young Ankylosaurs at level 7? 1d3+1 Young Fire Drakes (with the proper ring of Natural Attunement) at level 9? As long as you only consider beatsticks, you’ve very easily closed the gap in raw, unadulterated summoning damage compared to Summon Monster users. You’re still behind on versatility.

Alchemist (Preservationist)

A Preservationist is a surprise entry; the Alchemist is a six level caster and the Preservationist has the same core problem as the Druid of using Summon Nature’s Ally rather than Summon Monster for their summoning spells - fortunately for them this can be fixed by means of the Planar Preservationist feat. They also get their summoning spells slightly late; Summon I at level 2, Summon II at level 5(!), Summon IV at level 8, Summon V at level 10, Summon VII at level 14, and Summon IX at level 18. This is, on the face of it, far from ideal. But the Preservationist casts the Summon spell by imbibing the infusion - i.e. as a standard action.

Combining the Standard Action with the ability to summon monsters (rather than Natures’ Allies) means that the Preservationist is admittedly no Summoner, but once you’ve got past what I believe to be a typo at 5th level (it should be Summon III) and have hit Summon 4 at level 8 as a Standard Action then there’s a good case to be made for the Preservationist being a top tier summoner.

Warpriest

The Warpriest is another surprise entry. By using the Alignment blessings for Battle Companion, the warpriest can get an aligned (thankfully not subject to the limitations of Sacred Summons!) creature to help them out starting at level 10. Unfortunately, there are a few drawbacks. The biggest one is that unless you’re using your regular spell slots to summon you’re not going to be able to summon any monsters until level 10 -- and as a 2/3rds caster, at character level 9 you will be stuck at Summon Monster III if you do it this way. Coming in at a close second place is the delayed acquisition of your summons. You get Summon Monster V at level 12 and Summon Monster IX at level 20. And a close third place drawback is that you can only summon one monster with this spell. No Superior Summons shenanigans.

Why bother with Warpriest at all, then? Because they have the supremely awesome Quicken Blessing feat. So at level 12, they can drop 2 Dire Lions or Shadow Demons onto the field at the cost of 3 blessings. That goes a long way towards letting them play with the big boys; they’re still no nowhere good as actual summoners but good enough that you can make a warpriest that grabs the summoning feats (Augment Summoning, Evolved Summoned Monster) without feeling too bad. Think of the character as a melee DPRer that can summon instead of a summoner.

Inquisitor (Monster Tactician) 

As of this current guide, the third and final surprise entry. And unlike the Warpriest and Preservationist Alchemist, this archetype is the real deal when it comes to summoning. You get the summoner class’s key feature in terms of uses per day, duration, upgrade schedule, and above all else casting time. And all you trade for it are your judgments, ability to discern lies, and true slayer.

The real moneymaker of this summoning build is Summon Tactics. It’s simple but brutally powerful: all of your summons get one of your teamwork feats known at 5th level, then two at 8th level, then three at level 16. Giving all of your summons, say, Improved Feint Partner or Broken Wing Gambit will turn fair battles into embarrassing beatdowns for your enemies.

The biggest downside to going Monster Tactician is the spell list. Unlike the Summoner, the Inquisitor’s spell list isn’t weighted towards the idea that they’d still be a primary caster. And casting Summon Monster III at level 9 is just downright embarrassing. There’s no real way to layer on additional pain if your summons aren’t doing the trick, but if you’re okay with that then you have a flavorful and surprisingly effective summoning option.

Variant Multiclassing - Cleric

Picking Variant Multiclassing for cleric does come with a nice selection of goodies, but the real draw is the aura -- which allows you to pick up the Sacred Summons feat. The issue of wasting feats still remains and you will lose the ability to cast [Alignment]-tagged spells of the wrong alignment.

This option is probably the best pick for an Abyssal-Bloodline sorcerer or for a Blood Arcanist. Unfortunately, you will be really cramped on feats. A human will have to wait until level 5 to get Superior Summoning, while a non-human will have to wait until level 9! In order to make this build work you would need to either:

  • Go as a cross-blooded sorcerer that has Spell Focus as one of the bloodline feats and at level 7 start retroactively retraining your feats such that you swap out Spell Focus: Conjuration/Augmented Summoning/Superior Summoning for Summon Evil Monster/Sacred Summons/Evolved or Versatile Summon Monster at levels 7, 9, and 13.
  • Start in a game of at least 13th level and take Summon Evil Monster/Sacred Summons/Evolved or Versatile Summon monster in your 1st and 5th level slots and then at levels 9 and 13 backfill with Augmented and Superior Summoning.
  • Just be content with not being able to get Superior Summoning until level 13.

If you can make this convoluted but rules-legal build to work, however, you will be one hell of a fiend summoner.

Variant multiclassing with a cleric has one other upside: it allows you to pick up Spontaneous Nature’s Ally. Unfortunately, it has all of the same baggage with respect to feats as the Abyssal sorcerer does. Your build will almost certainly be a human that has the PFS Spell Focus replacement option that picks up Acadamae Graduate, Spell Focus: Conjuration, and Augment Summoning at level 1, Superior Summoning at level 5, and Spontaneous Nature’s Ally at level 9 or 13. If you really want to cast Summon’s Nature Ally spontaneously, you’d be better off as a Spirit Whisperer wizard with the Nature spirit.

Variant Multiclassing - Summoner

This is less of a class option and more of a character add-on. Variant Multiclassing is an option introduced by Pathfinder Unchained that allowed you to give up your feats at levels 3, 7, 11, 15, and 19 and give you feats that simulated the class features. The Summoner option, like a lot of caster options, is weak when you first get it but gets substantially powerful over time. First, the bad: your SLA is two levels behind that of a summoner in summoning critters and they only last a round per level. Moreover, you get a vastly weakened eidolon that only has half of the expected evolution points. You will never be summoning this eidolon for combat, making shield ally and the 1 evolution point you get completely worthless. Even worse, despite being a SLA the casting time is 1 round! Finally, you only get one use per day of your summoning ability. And all it cost you was a lot of feats, something a summoner is already starved of.

That really hurts and almost takes the option out of consideration. But! It’s not all bad. First of all, at level 11 you get 2 more uses per day. Second of all, there’s the otherwise useless Extra Summons feat. If your DM will let you, you can always retrain out of the feat once you get to level 11. Finally, at very, very high level (level 19) the level gap ceases to matter at all.

Still, the disadvantages are pretty stark. Who can use this option? Pretty much no one other than a Herald Caller that has the Sacred Summons feat. Despite all of its disadvantages, Variant Multiclassing goes together with that archetype like peanut butter and jelly. Sacred Summons/Summon Evil Monster takes care of the casting time disadvantage and Herald Caller gets a lot of summoning bonus feats to go with it. You gain the ability to spam your highest (or rather, near-highest) level summons while retaining a lot of your durability. In fact, because a Herald Caller can spontaneously cast Summon Monster, they don’t have to prepare the spell at all and can have untold day-to-day versatility for a summoner.

This option really isn’t for everyone. There will be cleric builds, even Herald Caller builds that don’t want to give up the feats. But it’s quite usable if you know what you’re getting into.

Prestige Class - Envoy of Balance

This is even easier to enter than the Tattooed Mystic. The feat (Augment Summoning) is one you already have and the Spellcraft/Knowledge (Planes) skills you probably want to know anyway. The biggest (and only) sticking point is that you have to be neutral to take this PrC. Unfortunately for you, Summon Neutral Monster is generally the weakest of the alignment feats and there is no way to use Sacred Summons with neutral monsters - LN/NG/NE/CN monsters are not an option due to the PrC requirement that the deity must be neutral.

If you can tolerate that, however, the 2nd-level Endowment feature for Planar Parity is golden: not only do the protection line of spells no longer stop bodily contact with your summons (very nice) but you also get to add the counterpoised template to your summons as long as they’re neutral in alignment. This is a godsend for SNA, but it’s still excellent for SM users on the condition that they have Versatile Summon Monster. Convert the alignment template to something like, say, Fiery and then slap on the Counterpoised template anyway. You’re dang right I’d like a lion that did a minimum of 1d4+2d6+12 damage on all five attacks against a lawful/chaotic evil monster. Or, hell, monsters that are natively neutral anyway (like elemental) get to join in on the smite action.

Prestige Class - Tattooed Mystic

This PrC isn’t all that difficult to get into: you need to be able to cast second-level spells, have two skills, one of which (Spellcraft) you were going to level anyway, and it requires you to pick the mediocre Mage’s Tattoo feat. It’s a prerequisite for the much better Tattoo Attunement feat, at least.

Unfortunately, Pathfinder’s philosophy towards primary-caster PrCs is that you should rarely be able to get into one without sacrificing caster levels. If you absolutely MUST play a primary caster that can summon as a standard action while not having one of the primary tricks, this PrC is your absolute last resort. It’s much easier to tolerate if your game is going to end before level 11, since you will be giving up one caster level instead of two and still be able to cast up to Summon Monster 5 as a standard action.

If you can tolerate that, though, the PrC offers some weregild. First is the Pouncing Summons ability; you can cast SM3 and SNA3 as a standard action (as long as the summons start next to you) and each time you take the discovery you increase the max level by two. You also gain a familiar if you don’t have one, Inscribe Magic Tattoo, the ability to inscribe tattoos at half the time, and the ability to craft tattoos at your caster level and spellcasting modifier instead of the default. Is it still worth it? Generally not, but if you’re absolutely dying to play a Witch or a Shaman summoner that can summon the entire list as a standard action for roleplay or convoluted cheese reasons you have an option.

3.5E Prestige Classes

Despite the claims of backwards compatibility with 3.5E D&D Pathfinder used to get a foot in the door back in 2009-2011, modern Pathfinder has amnesia towards its predecessor ruleset. Don't expect to be allowed to use any 3.5E D&D material. Not that it’s really a big deal, because summoning was mostly a joke back then, but there were a couple of goodies. Nonetheless, a surprising number of home games allow 3.5E material, so for completeness's sake we'll talk about a couple of prestige classes here.

Malconvoker:

This PrC used to be the gold standard for Summon Monster characters back in the 3.5E D&D salad days, and for good reason. It's nowhere near as good as it used to be (mostly because most serious summoners have Summon Monster as a native class SLA rather than a spell) but it's still worth talking about.

The bad thing is that it immediately robs you of a caster level. Major ouch. It gets better from there.

But first: you get Deceptive Summons and Unrestricted Conjuration. Unrestricted Conjuration, you don't care if you're not a cleric. In fact, it's kind of a kick in the pants because it promulgates the stupid folklore houserule that casting [Evil] spells is in of itself evil (when casting the Magic Circle Against Good necessary to bind an Angel is an [Evil] spell, natch). But it's a huge effin' deal if you're a cleric.

Deceptive Summons can be good, but it's also extremely risky. You get sizable bonuses (especially that 2xHD bonus to hp) but if your Bluff check isn't solid as steel, your summons will turn on you. And a lot of devils have pretty damn huge Sense Motive bonuses! Use with extreme care.

Adding Planar Binding to your list is like being able to take Leadership as a bonus feat. When it works, it's a game-changing effect that can get you out of a ton of binds. On the other hand, DMs have a tendency of looking for loopholes in the spell (or just making up some offscreen drama you can't know about -- that angel you called was going to deliver medicine to orphans, who just died of plague BTW, and now the celestial lords are pissed and are seeking revenge!) to screw you over. Like Deceptive Summons, it's great when it works but catastrophic when it doesn't.

Fiendish Legion is the money shot of this prestige class. Summon an additional evil creature when you summon one or more evil creatures. Note: Superior Summoning didn't exist when this PrC was published. And now you get to replicate the Abyssal Sorcerer's best trick but better.

The other levels: meh. They help out calling more than summoning. Deceitful Bargain is a pretty huge bonus for an opposed ability check (-5 to the opponent's roll) but the caveat about the DM using it to screw you still applies. Increasing the HD limit is always nice, letting you call things like Balors. And Safe Summoning lets you dismiss evil creatures as an immediate action instead of a standard action -- but at this point you should be juicing your Bluff skill to auto-succeed anyway.

Bottom Line? This PrC is still useful, but nowhere near as necessary as it was in the 3.5E D&D bad old days. The loss of a caster level really and truly hurts. You get so many more monster summoning expansion options in Pathfinder (better lists, better feats, better spells, etc.) that you'll probably end up even more powerful than a Malconvoker. Yes, being able to drop the mic with 3 succubi at level 12 is indeed awesome. But next level, an Acadamae Graduate wizard can not only do that but picks up your mic and hits you in the groin with it several times sneering 'I summoned 4 celestial Anklysosauruses at that level -- I AM NOT IMPRESSED.' Really, the PrC serves more of a milestone on how much more powerful summoning is in Pathfinder than in 3.5E D&D than something that will really boost your power.

Thaumaturgist

You don't give up a caster level. Yay. But it requires you to be able to cast planar ally and a lot of abilities revolve around it. ... not so yay. Pretty much only clerics and shamans can qualify for it. Spell Sage wizards can qualify for it if they want to if your DM decides that their ability to poach cleric spells count. So hey: a reason for wizards who want to cast Summon Nature's Ally to go Spell Sage instead of Spirit Whisperer.

As far as the Planar Ally goes... personally, I was never too big into this spell. Even though it dodges many of the 'expect your DM to plot offscreen revenge against you' issues that plague Planar Binding, it also eats into your funds like starving squirrels burrowing into your peanut stash. The cost reduction really does help, but it’s not really enough to be anything more than you’d use at the end of a long campaign.

You also get Augmented Summons at level 2 of this PrC. Considering that you need to be level 7 minimum to enter, that's darkly hilarious. Yes, let's go eight levels as a summoner without the feat that unlocks most of the better summoning feats. Then again, if your DM will let you retroactively retrain Augmented Summoning (Pathfinder Society does) it is a free feat. Then again then again, Herald Callers (the archetype most likely to spring for this PrC) don't care.

Contingent Conjuration is nuts, though. Technically, you can't use Contingency to cast Summon Monster and even if you could, the level limit and/or caster level division would make it a moot point. This feature is the only way that anyone aside from Staff of the Master-abusers can beat Master Summoners on speed.

Planar Cohort may be awesome and it may suck. If your DM holds you to the ECL limit (a thing that doesn't exist in Pathfinder, it must be said) then it's actually kind of crappy, since ECL is exponential. If they don't hold you to it, however, then you can get a Movanic Deva Angel following you around right away. Aw yeah.

Bottom Line: If your DM lets you take this PrC, you qualify for it, and you anticipate the campaign going for that long then it's a nice little addition to your summoning arsenal -- especially if your DM lets you retrain old feats, has a liberal interpretation of Planar Cohort, and you ignore those pangs of guilt from using Contingent Conjuration. But it is surprisingly hard to qualify for. Only clerics really have an easy time snagging it. Fortunately, Herald Caller stops giving out goodies (level 8) just after the level when you can qualify for this PrC (level 7).

Regular Multiclassing

This guide generally takes the view of ‘friends don’t let their full-caster friends multiclass’. Every level you delay improvements to Summon Monster really, really hurts. That said, if you simply have to multiclass for a level or two (i.e. you’re planning on going Mystic Theurge) but still want to summon as a primary tactic, you have a couple of options.

Cleric - This gives you the aura ability for Sacred Summoning, some domains, and a couple of other goodies. The Crusader archetype also hands out a bonus feat. Finally, you can pick up the Spontaneous Nature’s Ally feat if you’re absolutely dying for a way to use Summon Nature’s Ally spells as a character who doesn’t get it on their spell list.

Sorcerer - An Occultist Arcanist can take the Bloodline Development exploit along with a level of Abyssal Bloodline Sorcerer to get the additional demonic summons feature at the exact same level the sorcerer gets it. Because Occultist Arcanists get their Summon Monster SLA upgrades a level ahead of schedule, you can make a good case that Sorcerer 1/Occultist Arcanists X make better summoners than pure Sorcerers.

Wizard - You get the ability to take Acadamae Graduate. Witches, Summoners, and any other arcane preparation spellcasters that get published in the future may now rejoice. Note that taking a level of wizard is the only way to qualify for this feat: Variant Multiclassing, School feats, and even the School Understanding exploit won’t actually make you a specialist wizard. If your DM will let you use the Pathfinder Society variant rules, you can also swap out Scribe Scroll for Spell Focus.

Feats

The original version of this guide had the feats at the front because there were so few of them in the core rules, but this has changed drastically in the last year and a half.

Core Feats

These feats are Core both in the sense that every Summoner should have them (they are that good) and that they are in the core books rather than specifically focused books.

  • Augment Summoning (requires Spell Focus (Conjuration)) is the only Summoner feat in the PHB. It also makes all your summons stronger and tougher and is well worth the two feats.

The situation is so bad that my Summoner who’s allowed just the PHB and APG is burning feats Pathfinder Society lets people trade out their Scribe Scroll feat for Spell Focus: Conjuration.rk towards Spell Perfection which allows him (with Summon Monster VII in a 5th level slot) to cast Quicken Summon Monster VII at level 15. Hard to get and a terrifying ability - but the campaign looks as if it will end at level 14.

General Feats

These feats are useful to any monster summoner although they aren’t in the core books. The first two are awesome and worth getting as soon as possible - i.e. straight after Augment Summoning.

  • Superior Summoning (Ultimate Magic) - When you summon multiple monsters, summon an extra one. A 50% increase in the strength of summoning monsters 1 level lower than your maximum can be awesome. Note that some DMs house rule that if you roll a ‘1’ on the 1d3 for a lower-level list, you won’t be eligible for this feat and you will only get one monster instead of two. If this is the case, consider the Balanced Summoning discovery or contemplate using the
  • Evolved Summoned Monster (Advanced Class Guide). Adaptability on the fly is one of the hallmarks of monster summoners - and this kicks it up to the next level. A free bite is an excellent combat default for monsters, or Sticky for your grappler1 or 1d4+1 options.s - but at the right time Climb, Swim, Pull, Push, Resistance, or even Skilled are incredible. And you can stock up on multiples of this feat for more flexibility or combat power. Note that by RAW your summons don’t possess limbs as an evolution and therefore don’t qualify for claws (although can get extra attacks from a bite or tentacles)
  • Expanded Summon Monster (Monster Summoner’s Handbook)  is a feat to take late if at all. Until SM5 creatures start becoming available, you’re completely safe skipping this feat -- especially if you have Summon Good/Neutral/Evil Monster since most of the passable monsters this feat provides show up on those lists, but there are some pretty good summons from level 6 onwards.
  • Versatile Summon Monster  (Monster Summoner’s Handbook) - At first blush the Celestial (and Fiendish) template does more damage and has better DR. But the key to an outstanding summoner is knowing how to exploit versatility; not all foes are evil (or good - especially for Evil summoners) and this feat brings options in spades. This feat can also be enhanced (abused) with the use of Wysp Improved Familiars. Yes, I would like to give my horde of Fire templated Celestial Lions +2 to attack and damage very much.
  • Aerial Template - Fly at base land (or swim) speed, what more do you want? A little extra damage? You get that.
  • Aquatic Template - Most aquatic monsters are great - but this is a nice niche template.
  • Cthonic Template - Burrow’s a fun niche ability especially at low level (although you can normally summon Elementals). Even more fun with the Deep Guardian trait to give it a +1 bonus to attack and AC.
  • Dark Template - Concealment isn’t generally useful enough to justify this.
  • Fiery Template - Fire immunity helps sometimes. More useful is the extra damage for HD5+ monsters - if you want to hordebreak (or just face neutral enemies) this is far better than Smiting. The Goblin Fire Drum goes quite nicely with this template, though amusingly enough a Fire Wysp probably won’t be able to play the drum.
  • Primordial Template - The damage buff and the spells are both weak. Not at all worth it.
  • Tattoo Attunement (Monster Summoner’s Handbook, costs a pre-requisite feat) - lets you store one of your summons for a few hours and bring it out as a standard action. Not so hot for Summoners, but allows you to pre-buff Summons, play at being a Summoner, and even use a Ring of Sustenance to hold a spell from one day to the next. It also goes quite nicely with the Contingent Action spell. Two feats is a little steep, however, even if Mage Tattoo/Craft Magical Tattoo are some of the better feats

Alignment Feats

There are three feats that affect Summoners of a given alignment - one in each of Champions of Purity, Balance, and Evil

  • Summon Good Monster should be called “Summon really good monster”. Or possibly just “I’m Angel Summoner.” An extra 20 or so HP for when you’re using your summons to protect the party and soak bad guy attacks is nice. But superb is the list of angels you can summon. This works really well with Sacred Summons, pushing the good summoner ahead of the evil one.
  • Summon Neutral Monster - this feat in Champions of Balance is the Neutral version of Summon Good Monster. Instead of Die Hard it gives +2 Will, and the smites from the Counterpoised Template affect anyone with an alignment in one of the corners. A useful boost to expand your options - but not as good as Summon Good Monster.
  • Summon Evil Monster - another way to get access to summons-as-standard-actions, one of the holy grails of Summoners - but they don’t get to act the turn you bring them out. Which is annoying - and what you summon fast is mostly outsiders, meaning few beatsticks. You probably need to combine it with Sacred Summons (for speed) and Evolved Summoned Monster (for additional attacks).

Guardian Spirits

These are complicated and could potentially take a full guide themselves. Summon Guardian Spirit allows you add any one fey or outsider Improved Familiar to your Summon Monster III list - it levels up with the summoning spell you use for it, and can’t be resummoned for 24 hours if it’s discorporated. It can also use all the tricks and cheese on a Summoned Monster.  So your wizard with Augment Summoning, Evolved Summoned Monster for 2 wing attacks, this ritual pegged to Summon Monster 6, and a Rod of Giant Summoning (an otherwise pretty typical build for wizard summons) can drop a Mephit that, when using Smite Threat, has four attacks at 1d6 +7 (str) + 4d4 (electricity) + 13 (smite threat), a bunch of other spell-like abilities, and lasts for 1/min level. There’s a lot of cheese potential with this feat, but be prepared to spend several hours hashing over ability permutations - and to do so is outside the scope of this guide.

One last thing: Summon Guardian Spirit is one of the few reasons to take the Balanced Summoning discovery. By RAW, you can use Summon Guardian Spirit and grab one or two other monsters of the same level -- something you can’t normally do just by delaying the upgrades because you can only summon one Guardian Spirit, ever. Moreover, all of the summons will last 1 min/level to boot, allowing non-Occultists/Summoners to duplicate one of their best tricks. However, this requires you to deliberately manipulate the upgrading rituals such that you only do it right before you level up.

Cleric Summoning Feats

  • Sacred Summons lets clerics summon monsters matching their alignment type as a standard action - an extra turn of monster action is incredibly useful
  • Beacon of Hope - OK, so it’s a mass buff not an actual summons. It’s an awesome mass buff and mass buffs work really well with summons anyway. You may also want to take Quick Channel or the Ring of Protected Life to trigger the buff more easily.
  • Spontaneous Nature’s Ally - Hey, look, you get to spontaneously cast Summon Natural Ally spells! Seldom useful for clerics (Sacred Summons doesn’t work with SNA), but potentially potent for Acadamae Graduate wizards with Cleric-style Variant multiclassing (see above).

Druid Summoning Feats

  • Versatile Summon Nature’s Ally is possibly more flexible than the Versatile Summon Monster Feat - at the cost of the Augment Summoning Bonuses (so you’re still behind a non-druidic monster summoner but have closed a lot of the gap).

Wizard or Sorcerer Summoning Feats

  • Academae Graduate effectively gives wizards one to three fast summons per day at lower levels and the drawback becomes less and less of an issue at later levels. Although printed in Paizo’s 3.5E days before the official release of the Pathfinder core rules, keep in mind that this feat is legal to use in Pathfinder Society. When you can you’ll want to pick up a Cord of Stubborn Resolve or a Wayfinder plus a Cracked Pale Green Prism Ioun Stone to deal with the effects, or a Tireless Wayfinder to mitigate them.
  • Resilient Illusions - just for those clever wizards who are using Shadow Conjuration to fake being summoners (now that’s flexibility). Resilient Illusions with caster-level boosts is one of the most unfair things you can do to the DM.

Racial Summoning Feats

  • The half-orc’s Ferocious Summons, which gives Summoned Monsters Ferocity (the equivalent of the Die Hard feat) alas has two pre-requisites, meaning you can’t take it until its use becomes marginal. It would be very very good if you could take it at low levels - but it has two prerequisite feats
  • The Drow’s Spider-Summoner means that you really need to know the Web rules - which are useful utility when you want to capture someone alive. The only time spiders should ever be mainline summons is for Summon Monster 1 purposes, although you might well keep them as your meat wall up to level 4. Interesting flavour and utility, but not that great overall (and for a power build you want Augment Summoning first of course).

Trap Feats

Some feats are for summoning but just aren’t very good.

  • Moonlight Summons and Starlight Summons both require Summon Nature’s Ally (which compares badly to Summon Monster) and are both very situational.
  • Skeleton Summoner makes your summons worse.
  • Proxy Summoning is very marginal and there are by now too many feats that are good for summons to waste one (you can always use an extra Evolve Summoned Monster). If you’re able to make your summons last for a long time (i.e. Occultist Arcanist with Spell Tinkerer, a Wizard with a Rod of Extend Spell + Summon Guardian Spirit + Balanced Summoning) this feat becomes significantly less bad. But it’s still not very good.
  • (Proxy Summoning is essentially the ‘Brown Fur Transmuter’ of feats - as they already do not have 1min/duration summons - giving any potential summon the ‘Share Spells’ feature, in order to cast polymorph spells on them and turn them into buzzsaws of doom. - In edition to silliness such as making Laser Raptors from Kung Fury. Share Burning Gaze + Expanded Summon Monster: Raptor choice)
  • Scouting Summons to be useful needs a DM with a liberal interpretation of Magic Jar. It has some potential although it opens a whole new can of worms.
  • Harrowed Summoning does not alas stack with Augment Summoning (both are Enhancement Bonuses) - but gives you an almost 1/3 chance of helping your summoned spellcasters. Much more useful with Versatile Natural Ally, but it’s still a pretty big gamble.
  • Balanced Summoning (Wizards only) gives you just a touch of versatility and protects you against summoning only 1 monster out of D3 (just get Superior Summons; 2-4 is never less than two). There may be buffer-and-brute combos that are useful here but it’s so niche that it’s barely worth it. Probably the best use is an artifact of the cramped quarters most Pathfinder Society fights take place in; you might not be able to summon 3 dire bats into the same quarters, but you can summon 1 fiendish dire bat and 2 lantern archons. Much better with Summon Guardian Spirit; see the above section for more information. Probably worth it if your DM rules that rolling a ‘1’ on the 1d3 summoning roll doesn’t trigger Superior Summoning.
  • Balanced Summoning has one other really good, if obscure use: you can use it with Sacred Summons to summon a much more diverse array of creatures than you otherwise could. For example, a wizard with this feat, Sacred Summons, and a lawful and evil aura could throw out a Hell Hound and two Celestial Lions as a standard action. Granted, that’s still generally worse than Acadamae Graduate but if you can’t get that particular feat then it works as a reasonable backup option.
  • Finally, Balanced Summoning has one minor cheese loop with . See that section for more details. In fact, it’s not a bad idea to combine this feat with that feat so that you can get three good monsters with a summon instead of two good monsters and a potentially mediocre one.

Familiars

The big question: familiars or item bonds? Item bonds are better at lower levels, when you’re more limited on resources, and familiars are better at higher levels, when you can make better use of the action advantage. However, the gap in utility isn’t big in either case. And it’s rarely a bad idea to grab both if you can.

Item Bonds:

  • At low levels, the 1 extra spell a day is a huge help, especially if your DM of the type to run 3 or more encounters between rest periods. If you’re summoning with your actual spell slots, you’ll go through them like candy.
  • At higher levels, the ability to cast a spell without having it prepared is a huge help. Spellcasters have a lot of situational spells that they don’t want to prepare nor put on reduced-CL/DC scrolls. But a well-timed Banishment or Water Breathing will make you the belle of the ball. And it’s good for saving money, which is at a premium in PFS.
  • Note that strict RAW makes it so that a lot of classes won’t be able to make use of a particular item bond, due to references to spellbooks or arcane spell slots.
  • You can use an obscure item creation feat without spending feat slot on it along with getting it several levels early. The utility for a summoner getting a Staff of the Master: Necromancy at level 8 or a Ring of Invisibility cannot be denied.
  • Item arcane bonds are noticeably harder to get than familiar arcane bonds.

Familiars:

  • They give action advantage. What’s usually missing in this discussion, however, is the quality of that action advantage. At high level, having a familiar that has access to wands of Haste and Invisibility is a big help. At low levels, when your budget limits you to four tanglefoot bags for an entire 12-battle quest, not so much. And there, as always, borderline cheesy uses. Stick a Greater Hat of Disguise on your buddy and give it a cute little set of bow and arrows.
  • If you’re worried about your familiars getting killed, remember the R2D2 rule: the DM and thus the enemies generally won’t target your familiar as long as it’s not actively getting involved in combat and/or it’s by itself in a perilous situation. If you’re just using it for an extra set of eyes or for passive bonuses, you probably don’t have to worry about it. Once you start giving it wands or bags of caltrops, however, you should keep a close eye on it.
  • Familiars are an extra set of skill checks. You can always use an extra spotter or historian in the party. Even beyond that, Familiars know all of the skills that you do. Easy Aid Another bonuses as long as you plug some loopholes (like taking the Linguistics skill if you want help with Knowledge: Arcana).
  • Familiars can give some pretty darn good bonuses. The +4 to initiative is the one that gets everyone’s blood boiling, but don’t feel limited to that one. Imagine getting a Raven familiar that can do Gather Information checks across town or give you an extra crack at Diplomacy checks.
  • Improved Familiar is somewhat of a shot in the dark. While some of the Improved Familiars are excellent additions to your arsenal, such as the Homonculus and the Silvanshee Agathion and Imp, many of them are straight downgrades thanks to losing the familiar special ability. Use Magic Device is generally the major reason people pick Improved Familiar -- though granted, it’s a pretty huge reason, as any summoner with a Wand of Haste or Alter Summon Monster will tell you.

Good familiars to pick include:

Familiars:

Bat: Blindsense is very useful, especially at low levels. Not only does it give you a fighting chance against (Deeper) Darkness but it’s quite nice for ferreting out hiding and invisible enemies. And because the bat can fly, you can have it safely sweep the area. It also has a very large Perception check.

Monkey: Monkeys have hands. Some DMs might get snide and ask you how exactly is your familiar using those scrolls and wands you gave them.

Goat/Pig: Being small, Goats and Pigs don’t provoke Attacks of Opportunity for delivering touch spells.

Hare/Composagnus/Greensting Scorpion: Most people pick these familiars for the +4 bonus to initiative. The Greensting Scorpion is probably the best of the pack, having a huge stealth modifier and immunity to mind-affecting effects for some serious cheese with the Emissary familiar archetype.

Ioun Wyrd: Ioun Wyrds have blindsight out to 30 feet. Not blindsense, blindsight. They're also constructs, so they're packaged with a cocktail of immunities -- making it quite safe to take them into places where AoEs are being thrown about. Along with allowing Emissary cheese. Share Ioun is also a really nice feature to have. Not only does it make your ioun stones marginally safer from being sundered/grabbed, but both you and your familiar get the bonuses. Unfortunately, they can’t actually see with their native senses, which may make item manipulation and especially reading a huge problem.

Rat: Not only do they give a bonus to a save squishy wizards rarely see, but they make great ‘hide in plain sight’ scouts. If you use a hawk or monkey for scouting and they get caught, people will get suspicious. But given that the default campaign setting of Pathfinder implies that people are still doing their business in chamber pots, there are very few locales where a rat would be out of place -- they blend in just fine at the market, the tavern, the forest, a dragon’s lair, the enemy’s swampland camp, etc.

Rhamphorhynchus: Yes, they do only get +2 bonus to initiative. But their perception and/or stealth bonuses are much higher than the +4 initiative bonus familiars, they fly, and they have scent. It can even use sudden swoop to safely deliver touch spells. If there’s a ‘Jack of All Trades, Master of Most’ familiar, it’s this one.

Improved Familiars:

Wysps        - Wysps’ Resonance ability lets them provide a whopping +2 competence bonus to the attack and damage rolls of creatures that match its elemental subtype. Versatile Summon Monster exists. You do the math. The Aid Another bonus is also very boss. If you’re picking a Fire Wysp, be sure to fireproof anything you want it to touch.

Faerie Dragon - They cast spells as a 3rd level sorcerer. You don’t care about the spells except out of very low levels, but you do care about the ability to use wands with minimal fuss. You can also feed it Pages of Spell Knowledge.

Ceru - Free rerolls that can target pretty much anyone in the party and even some monsters. DC to force negative rerolls is decently adequate, being 10 + ½ character level + CHA mod vs. Will. Would be close to an auto-pick if its reroll could affect caster level checks, but can’t have everything, yes?

Imp - Augury, Beast Shape, DC 15 Suggestion, 1/wk Commune, ability to go invisible at will, and if you pick a consular telepathy and access to the entire list of animals to change into with Beast Shape.

  • Familiars also have archetypes. As usual, this could take up a whole guide by itself, but to summarize for the discerning summoner:
  • Figment Familiar give you mutable evolution points to use on your familiar.
  • Emissary gives you an extra crack at will saving throws and limited use of a domain ability. An Acadamae Graduate wizard will love a familiar that uses the Restoration domain.
  • Sages have many knowledge bonuses and have the intelligence to use them.

Traits

  • Genie Caller is a life-saver at low levels, allowing summons to last more than a couple of rounds - a potential game changer.
  • Diabolocal Dabbler adds a small but well-appreciated amount of durability (+1 hp per HD) to any fiendish animal you summon. Unfortunately, fiendish animals tend to be the weakest…
  • A dwarf user of summons (i.e. cleric or monster tactician inquisitor) will probably find the Deep Guardian trait nifty but a bit limited. Its value goes way up with Versatile Summon Monster: Cthonic.
  • Voice of Monsters trait lets you give more detailed orders to not only animals but INT 1-2 aberrations and magical beasts. Very handy at low levels.
  • Outsider Ties lets you choose a subtype of outsider, and every time you summon them via summon monster X, (less/great) planar ally, planar binding, etc.  they gain the spell level in temp hitpoints.

Metamagic Rods

  • Quicken rods allow you to use your summons as swift actions and therefore the Summons to act on the turn you cast it. They’re extremely expensive, however, and you probably won’t be able to get level-appropriate summons with the rods unless you craft them yourself.
  • Rods of Echoing are much, much cheaper than pearls of power or similar gear. 3 level 6 pearls of power would cost you 118k gp. 3 level 5 pearls of power would cost you 75k gp a rod of echoing is a much more affordable 54k.
  • A somewhat liberal (but not tortured) interpretation of Rime Spell allows your summons with cold damage (as usual, Versatile Summon Monster helps a ton) to entangle creatures on a hit.
  • Rods of Maximise and Empower help you summon multiple monsters. Maximize and Empower don’t stack anymore, unfortunately.
  • The Rod of Giant Summoning (a quasi-metamagic rod) applies the Giant Simple Template to all creatures summoned with a conjuration (summoning) spell and is priced as a +1 boost. It doesn’t work with the Summoner’s (or similar Summon Monster abilities) SLA alas. Changing a monster from small to medium size is good as it is (it’s like doubling up on Augmented Summons) but going from Medium to Large or Large to Huge is a ‘huge’ deal. Like X-Box Huge. Like Atari 5200 Huge. For example, a Foo Lion goes from:
  • 52 hp; bite +10 1d8+8 (grab at +16 CMB), 2 claws +10 (1d6+8)
  • 62 hp; bite +12 2d6+12 (grab at +24 CMB), 2 claws +12 1d8+12. So 16 more points of damage assuming all attacks hit.

If you cast summon monster spells out of spell slots you want this rod like yesterday. Unfortunately, this rod is not available in Pathfinder Society. Sadness.

A Suzerain Scepter hands out a blanket bonus to hit - and your Summons start having trouble with that at level 12 or so, therefore this helps a lot.

The Staff of the Master (Necromancy) is almost an auto-include for any character that has access to Vampiric Touch. This magical item does evil things with Empower, Quicken, and Familiar Spell and is the main reason to want metamagic on a summoner (unless you’re core only and going for Spell Perfection).

Summon Slave Crystals are mostly trap options unless you have a way to protect your body (hint: Emergency Force Sphere) and you’re a competent enough melee combatant to make the reduction in action advantage worth it.

Golden Eagle Epaulets are extremely inexpensive at 3000 gp, giving the wearer a +2 competence bonus to Diplomacy and one other skill in addition to a reflexive sanctuary and bless 1/day. While you could wear it on yourself, the summoner would be much better served giving it to their familiar and having them cast ‘bless’ on your summons.

A Goblin Fire Drum is a great way to squeeze out a bit more damage out of summons with fire attacks, such as the Fire Drake. Or you could just slap on the Fiery template with Versatile Summon Monster and call it a day. Goblin Fire Drums are also quite useful with the normally lackluster ‘burn’ ability monsters like Fire Elements get, as you can use it to trigger Pyrotechnics.

Books of Extended Summoning - single use and 1 round/level spells aren’t helped much by extending

Rings of Summoning Affinity (Archon), (Aeon), (Kyton), (Angel) (a whole lot more to add) - more creatures you can summon are good. More creatures with spells are better. Pick your alignments……

A Serpent’s Band is one of the few ways to converse with non-sentient reptilian and magical beast creatures. It’s a bit pricey for that purpose at 9,000 gp but if your DM is really strict about the communication limitations on ordering your summons this may be one of the few ways around that.

If you’re thinking about summoning creatures at all but you’re not able to do so as a standard action (i.e. you’re a sorcerer, a cleric trying to go beyond Sacred Summons, etc.) you absolutely need two magical items: a Quick Runner’s Shirt and Sandals of Quick Reaction. Full-round summoners are badly screwed by inter-round damage and the ability to make a quick getaway is vital. Full-round summoners are also screwed over by being limited to only a move action in the surprise round. Fortunately, both items are very inexpensive.

A Robe of Gates is very expensive, but makes an excellent capstone item what with its ability to act as a pseudo-Maximize Spell on your summon spells three times per day. For SM1-6, it’s not as good as an actual Metamagic Rod of Maximize, but for SM7-9 it’s a massive price savings. Works extremely well with an actual Metamagic Rod of Empower/Master’s Staff + Empower Spell; dump up to 8 creatures onto the battlefield with a single casting.

Spells

Again, breaking the spells down by what they do.

Modifying summons

As a rule single target buffs on your summons aren’t that great compared to simply summoning more stuff - but flexibility is the lifeblood of the Summoner and there are a couple of spells to do this. When you start to get higher-level spell slots, sometimes SM6 plus a 3rd-level buff or battlefield control spell will be just as effective and less taxing on your durability than SM5 + SM6.

  • Alter Summoned Monster might seem nice but it’s actually a surprisingly versatile spell. If there’s one spell in your arsenal that you want to grab Quicken Spell and metamagic reducers for, it’s this one. Yes, even more than haste, which you can put into a wand and give to the rogue or your familiar. Heck, there are plenty of reasons why you would want a familiar to have a wand of this particular spell.
  • RAW, this allows you to bounce between the Summon Nature’s Ally and Summon Monster list without knowing both spells. Every now and then, the SNA list will actually have a monster that’s competitive or even superior against the SM options, like the Stone Giant for SNA6 for ranged damage.
  • It’s a great Will-based SoD for the foe’s summoned monsters; just hotswap that Dire Tiger that took 20 points of damage for a celestial eagle. Or just hotswap for a crappy summon when it’s at full hit points.
  • Switch out a monster suffering from  various conditions such as paralysis with a monster that has an immunity to said condition.
  • Use it to recycle a monster’s ability to use spell-like abilities.
  • Use it to get around spell-list restrictions -- or if you can cast this spell as an off-action, to swap a ‘fake’ monster you can get quickly with Sacred Summons or Summon Evil Monster with one you actually want. It’s great with Summon Evil Monster because the swapped creature can then take a full-round action.
  • Use it to get more actions. Ready a use of this spell to go off as soon as a particular summon gets an action, then hotswap it out.
  • Summon out-of-combat utility monsters and trade them all in before the duration expires for combat monsters. I’ll trade 3 castings of a level 2 spell to preserve a level 5 spell slot.
  • Cheesy use: Use SM2+ to summon 1d3 or 1d4+1 monsters. Use this spell to hotswap in a higher-level monster, i.e. use SM2 to summon 1d3+1 dogs and cast this spell twice to trade them for small earth elementals.
  • Finally, there’s the super cheesy, ‘no way in hell you munchkin’ use: cast a Heightened Mount and hotswap it with a ‘real’ monster so you can have a top-tier summon that lasts for several hours
  • Master’s Mutation gives the option of adding grab and constrict to all of your summon’s natural attacks. If used on the right kind of creature (e.g. T-Rex with Evolved Summoned Monster) this can outright double your summon’s damage. Its high level unfortunately makes it an endgame spell. It does go quite nicely with:
  • Animal Shapes. This spell at high levels can turn a set of animals with massive stats but only one good attack (such as the aforementioned tyrannosaurus) into a monstrosity with a bunch of natural attacks and an even greater stat boost. Great endgame spell.

Staying Alive

  • Invisibility. Every summoner should use invisibility; Summon Monster isn’t an attack so doesn’t break it. Let your monsters do the fighting while you stay invisible (and the Summoner’s SLA doesn’t have verbal or somatic components as it’s an SLA)
  • Emergency Force Sphere is a lifesaver - set your summons up then hide in the sphere to wait the fight out (although you can’t summon more monsters through the bubble)

Other Spells

  • Haste is a great spell (and Summoners get access to it at level 4). It’s especially good on Summons with one awesome attack, like the Ankylosaurus with its DC25 Stun.
  • Elemental Speech (in conjunction with Versatile Summon Monster) is an inexpensive way to get around the whole ‘how do you give summons more complicated orders?’ issues stricter DMs might hold you to. Better than Tongues in some ways because it allows you to communicate with INT 1 and 2 creatures, which nearly all of the *-templated monsters are. Prime candidate for the Imbue With Spell Ability + Familiar/Animal Companion combo, if the cleric has an elemental domain.
  • Speaking of Tongues, this spell exists and unless you have a lot of ranks in Linguistics your summoner will need it. It’s not perfect (the communication isn’t two-way and you still can’t talk to non-sapient creatures), but it’s inexpensive and long-lasting.
  • Communal Air Walk is one of the earliest ways to get mass flight. Even if your summons can fly thanks to Versatile Summon Monster or Evolved Summoned Monster, your party (remember them?) will probably be able to make use of it for the rest of the mid-levels.
  • Litany of Righteousness is how Monster Tactician Inquisitors (and Samsaran clerics) drop the mic when full-casters start taunting them about spell access.
  • Shadow Conjuration (and upgrades) is one of the few spells more flexible than Summon Monster (by covering argument). But Summon Monster is the most flexible use of Shadow Conjuration. The crossover is beyond this guide, but this guide should be useful for Shadow Conjurers.
  • Create Pit (and upgrades) are even more effective for Summoners than most casters due to Summoned monsters loving attacks of opportunity.
  • Dazzling Blade - Action Economy? What action economy (immediate action buff)
  • Spellcasting Contract or Imbue With Spell Ability let summons and familiars break the action economy by passing on pre-cast spells. This deserves its own guide.
  • Death Knell has the problem ‘summoned creatures disappear when reaching 0 hp’ - Summon Good Monster and Ferocious Summons get round this - although in the former case it’s an Evil spell. Then again, if you have a Sacrificial Dagger of the Blood Mother or a Pallid Crystal (only 2700 and 3300 gp) you can get around this even if you’re good-aligned.

Shadow Spells

Shadow magic is really powerful; you cast conjuration and evocation spells as a standard action and without consuming components. So its utility for summoner character is obvious. However, it’s problematic as a primary option for l reasons.

  • A lot of tables think that shadow magic is in fact outright broken. Not because it’s the most powerful game effect (it’s up there, though) but because shadow magic is a nightmare to adjudicate. We won’t even get into what happens when you have something that’s 120% real, quite possible in Pathfinder. Even if people can agree on what happens, nothing’s quite as hilariously sad as people repeatedly taking 60% of a celestial lion’s damage.
  • Ironically, using shadow magic to summon is probably its least effective use. From an optimization perspective, the only reason why you would use shadow magic to replicate Summon Monster over Planar Binding is if you weren’t confident on the charisma check. However, it’s also the least controversial use from both a rules and game balance perspective so it you want to avoid an on-the-spot nerf it’s a useable option.
  • That reduced effectiveness of shadow magic hurts summons more than other conjuration effects. While you can manipulate the DC with effects like Resilient Illusions, there’s no getting around the reduced hp. The Shadowcaster archetype by RAW doesn’t increase summon durability. This means that the 4th level Shadow Conjuration spell is practically useless in combat. Shades and Greater Shadow Conjuration fortunately make it a lot more viable, but you’re still behind a level on summons. Solid Shadows and Shadow Master unconditionally make your spells more real, but the latter option only comes online at level 20 and the former option requires two feats and a +1 level spell adjustment or for your DM to let you create a rod that needs to be extrapolated from existing price guidelines.
  • There’s a bit of controversy with deciding what exactly you can summon. Can a good-aligned cleric use Shadow Conjuration: SM3 to summon fiendish animals? Can a sorcerer use it to snag creatures off of the Priestly options lists? Etc. You can always fail conservative, but it’s at the cost of losing the biggest advantage of shadow magic.
  • The biggest problem with using shadow magic to replicate summoning magic is that there are way too many level gaps. You have Shadow Conjuration (4th level, SM3), Greater Shadow Conjuration (7th level, SM6), and Shades (9th level, SM8). Even if you don’t mind being behind a spell level or the inherently reduced effectiveness, you’ll frequently have to go without.

Summoning Tactics

Summoning tactics rely on three central principles.

  • Expendability
  • Flexibility
  • Positioning

Expendability

I’ve mentioned before that summoned monsters are expendable, and there’s no reason not to mention it again. If you drop a summoned monster into the middle of an enemy group and they take a round to turn round and shank it, you’ve effectively used up all their actions for the round. You might as well have stunned them - and the monster got to do something on the turn it was summoned. A bargain much of the time. (Yes, I am recommending summoning monsters in the middle of blocks of enemies).

Flexibility

The huge advantage of Summon Monster is being able to pick the right tool for the job. Summon Monster isn’t just one spell, it’s often a choice of half a dozen. Know your enemies and guess your targets. Do they look big and beefy (in which case you want to attack their reflex defence) or small and scrawny (attack their fortitude) - you have plenty to do both, and even some monsters to attack Will. Know your enemy. And have your favourite monsters either printed out or on index cards. It’ll save everyone a lot of time.

Positioning

The range of Summon Monster X is “close (25 ft. + 5 ft./2 levels)”. Which means it’s often possible to drop a multi-attacking monster like an eagle right on top of the enemy. Or on the far side of a shieldwall so it can attack the enemy caster. Also summoning a herd of aurochs onto the flank of the enemy so they can stampede straight down the line is not only funny, it’s extremely effective. (As is summoning someone with lightning bolt onto a flank, or a Vrock or two with a stun attack into the middle of the enemy and just out of range of your fighters). Look at the angles or just block the doorway. And remember that single attackers and pouncers want to charge - the -2AC doesn’t matter to you anyway. Position your monsters as outright awkwardly as possible for the other side.

Monsters to Summon

Unlike most guides I’m not rating the monsters specifically - the key to being a good summoner is to pick the right tool for the job which might not be the best tool in the abstract - so best approach is to list the tools. I am, however, picking two monsters at each level - one as meat and one as muscle. Meat refers to a large, hard to kill monster - you put it somewhere and the enemy will need work to get past. Muscle on the other hand just does as much damage as possible. And remember for both that there’s both more meat and more muscle in several lower level creatures than one being summoned at the first level you can. Also that the Summon Good Monster feat adds a lot of meat even to muscle and utility monsters, especially at lower levels. The Air and Water monsters are specialties to indicate monsters you wouldn’t otherwise summon - but knowing how to fill niches is part of a summoner’s stock in trade (although for water other than the dolphin, in an “all in” game you’re often better summoning an ordinary monster with Evolved Summoned Monster or Versatile Summon Monster and having it swim).


Almost all the “mundane” monsters gain either the Celestial Template or the Fiendish Template or (more rarely) the Resolute Template or the Entropic Template- the most important part of which is Smite: 1/day, the summoned monster can declare an enemy of opposing alignment their smite target, and gets to add damage equal to their hit dice to all attacks against them. With a monster like a Dire Tiger (summon Monster 6), this is five attacks and 14 hit dice for a potential 70 extra damage. Note that unlike a Paladin your smite doesn’t overcome Damage Resistance - and Celestial creatures do not have the “Good” subtype.

As a rule of thumb, the optimal number of augmented summons to drop is d3+1; if you drop two levels you may get a wall of meat but it often has trouble hitting if the enemy is a single target. But you’re about at break even even if you only get two summons.

I’ve also linked the d20pfsrd for all the monsters I’ve picked out. Their stats for monsters with Augment Summoning are frequently incorrect; they almost never take account of the rule that says “a creature has only one natural attack, it is always made using the creature’s full base attack bonus and adds 1-1/2 times the creature’s Strength bonus on damage rolls.” This means that their Augment Summoning stats frequently do 1 point too little damage.

Also in the below lists, I assume that by default people only use the core summonings.

  • Italics indicates a non-core summoning (normally with the source)
  • Strikethrough indicates a third party monster (often broken)
  • Monsters that require a feat or item to summon have that feat listed (e.g. “(Summon Good Monster required)”)

Summon Minor Monster

Summon Minor Monster is broken as written; the wording allows you to summon any tiny animal. There is an equivalent version for Summon Nature’s Ally called Summon Minor Ally, which is, of course, a direct downgrade due to no Alignment template. Stick to the list of examples and it’s fine - but that is guidance not rules and some of the following are sick, especially with Augment Summoning.

  • Skunk, which has Musk 2/day, with ~70% Sicken for 1d4 rounds, or ~40% Nauseate for 1d4 rounds, then Sicken for 1d4 minutes per use of Musk ability.
  • Hagfish, which has 17 HP, great DPR with its +4 attack for d3+1 damage, and is aquatic (from AP 56). A somewhat ridiculous amount of meat - and won’t suffocate before the summons ends on land. Completely broken.
  • Owl/Hawk for airborne DPR (2 talons at +5, d4-2). Stronger than the Summon Monster 1 winner - the Eagle.
  • Armadillo, with its 21 possible AC including protective ball and total defense. With no offence, however, smart enemies will just ignore them. Interestingly, the rules on Moving Through a Square prevent Medium or smaller creatures from moving through the square of a Tiny creature, so these little guys can stop a charge or hold a chokepoint if nothing else.
  • the poison creatures: Blue-Ringed Octopus, Platypus (clearly created by a demented wizard), Compsognathus (Dinosaur), Viper, and Poisonous Frog. The Poison DC is Con-based, so Augment increases it. Seldom that useful, however, as things weak enough to be hurt by the poison normally have very few hit points.
  • Bat and Platypus for locating invisible creatures, in air and underwater (lets see your Dire Bat swim, Druid!). In other words, this is one of the cheapest ways to detect invisible creatures underwater
  • Rabbit (Can move at 250 ft/round with its 50ft move speed and the Run feat)
  • Porcupine (Tail Slap +2, 1d3) - not bad DPR for if your DM won’t allow you to summon a Hagfish out of water.

Note that Tiny Aquatic Animals are usually quite powerful in comparison to any other tiny animal, and that arguably they can function just as well out of water as in it due to Suffocation not applying to them until long after the summoning spell’s duration runs out (the GM might question whether it’s an environment that can support them).

Summon Monster 1

At only 1-2 rounds, these are use-and-throw monsters - and under most conditions Mount is simply a better spell than Summon Monster 1 (1hr/level and the horse shows up on Summon Monster 2) although Summoners still use Summon Monster 1 of course. For others this is generally too weak and too short duration - and poison isn’t much use against enemies who won’t survive anyway. Note that early printings had the Riding Dog (single attack with trip and 17hp) - this has been errata’d out for good reason.

Meat: Pony - it might have sucky attacks but at 17hp the enemy are going to take at least a few attacks to get through it and if it ever hits (at -1) it hits surprisingly hard. Any creature summoned with Summon Good Monster will qualify as meat - the pony might go up to at least the mid 20s hit points, but the Eagle will have at least 18 whereas Summon Evil Monster makes it a standard action.

Muscle: Eagle - triple attack with fly - really benefits from Augment. That said it needs the triple attack as each of the attacks has a hard time with anything bigger than a kobold. But three attacks at +3 to hit and d4+3 damage (including Smite Evil) is nasty at this level.

Air: Eagle - it’s the muscle choice and it flies. What more do you need?

Water: Dolphin. If that thing weren’t restricted to the water, it would be a solid second level summons. If you have the Versatile Summon Monster of course flying dolphins may be the best level 1 summon available

Ranged: Curiously, while Summon Nature’s Ally I has ranged options, there aren’t any for SM1. Not that summons and especially flyers like eagles aren’t de facto ranged attacks.

Bloody Skeletons are an optional monster from Carrion Crown that get two attacks and fast healing - but keep coming back until hallowed. Depending on how your DM interprets the rules and how much you expect plans to bite you, this might be a strong monster or a very bad idea. Know your DM!

Sprite (Summon Neutral Monster required) DC11 colour spray, constant detect good and detect evil. DC10 Daze At Will is almost certainly more useful than the 1hp their weapons can do.

Spider, Giant Crab (Spider Summoner required) - 2 hit dice means that augmented it has 15hp and an AC better than the Pony and an acceptable bite attack.

Stingrays (Summon Evil Monster required) are almost as good as Dolphins in the water - recommended aquatic if you are using Summon Evil Monster to make summons a standard action.

Vultures (Summon Evil Monster required) are again a second rate choice to Eagles but Summon Evil Monster makes them a standard action

Badger (Expanded Summon Monster): Slightly second rate against eagles, being landbound and doing a point less damage with each attack without blood rage. However, they have scent and 50% more hp (11 vs 7), making them situationally better. Especially if blood rage triggers.

Summon Monster 2

Meat: Lemure - DR/5 Good or Silver is the toughest - but eagles hit harder (Summon Evil Monster summons it as a standard action). Like the Pony at level 1 they hold somewhere but the enemy might as well take the opportunity attack (+4 to hit, 1d4+2 damage) and walk round them. If you don’t want a demon or you want to hurt the enemy a Small Mud Elemental can’t be ignored because it attacks at +7 to hit with a DC14 entrapment effect.

Muscle: Hardest hitting is the Small Earth Elemental which when augmented and power attacking people on the ground attacks at +8 to hit for 1d6+11 damage (and can take advantage of charging) - this will be useful right up to Summon Monster 4. A flock of eagles is also a pretty good choice unless the enemy has serious AC.

Air: For damage you want a flock of eagles assuming soft targets. For armoured targets, the Small Lightning Elemental is very accurate and can push people down into reach - or the Small Air Elemental can pull slow targets into reach (depending on how you rule the whirlwind’s effectiveness).

Water: Pure water at this level is multiple dolphins unless you’ve a real use for whirlwinds - while the Small Ice Elemental can come out of the water if the targets leave or run out.

Ranged: Giant Spider, has a +5 ranged touch web attack that will entangle if it hit which means -2 to hit, -4 to dex (ie AC) and half movement (ie no 5ft step), great support for challenging melee threats.

Elemental (small) - Elementals are all beatsticks with some secondary use. Generally earth for beatsticks, air for scouting, and hybrids for making the monsters’ lives miserable.

Small Earth Elemental - Burrow and Tremorsense. A.k.a. Hide and Seek. Can bash people on the ground with Power Attack at +8 to hit, 1d6+11 damage on a power attack. Ouch.

Small Air Elemental - Fly 100ft/round, stealth +11. Useful as scouts and for a DC14 whirlwind - but please note that there is a dispute about whether you use the size of the elemental or size of the whirlwind for what you can pick up.

Small Fire Elemental - Nope. Burning isn’t strong or fast enough. No other uses

Small Water Elemental - in water (dolphins are better) or for putting out fires only. The underwater whirlwind is subject to the same argument that the air elemental’s is.

Small Lightning Elemental - It flies, and gets a bonus to hit against people carrying metal. Also has an excellent charging trip or disarm as a one-shot tactic (against people carrying metal)

Small Magma Elemental - Turns a square into molten magma for a few rounds - nice to hold the line. They also burrow, but again edge case. Unless on magma go for one of the others.

Small Mud Elemental - On land a second rate earth elemental other than a DC14 entrap rider on its attack (entrap is admittedly very good). In the water a water elemental is faster and gets a vortex. But earth elementals suffer badly in the water and water are worse on land.

Small Ice Elemental - Like Mud elementals they are amphibious (as well as excellent on snow) - but are better underwater than on land. Also its Stagger works while entrap washes away - but Stagger doesn’t threaten Helpless, and it doesn’t get tremorsense or earthwalk.

Small Aether Elemental (Bestiary 5) can fly and use telekinetic throw +5(d8+2) which will damage also thrown object or creature and has point blank shot (+1 to attack rating and damage when 30 feet or closer). Also has telekinetic invisibility, blindsense and darkvision (both 60 feet) and Intelligence score 4, so you can direct it and it speak all elemental languages :)

Small Gravity Elemental - blame Frog God Games for this cheese. If your DM allows you Reverse Gravity with a second level spell from a third party publisher, the DM deserves whatever the DM gets.

Small Cold Iron Elemental - Super Genius Games aren’t as bad as Frog God Games were. But it’s made of cold iron, and has tremorsense and burrow. Like an Earth Elemental but better.

Psionic Elementals, Negative Energy Elementals, Positive Energy Elementals. Not as silly as Gravity Elementals. But if it’s from Frog God it should probably be banned. And Quasi-elementals aren’t real elementals anyway.

Hell Hound - Allowed by the Council of Thieves AP, and whoever thought a CR3 monster with a breath weapon and 4 hit dice should be allowed by Summon Monster 2 fails at math. And didn’t see that it’s normally on the Summon Monster 4 list

Grig - the Fiddling Grig is allowed by the Jade Regent AP or Summon Good Monster and is an excellent distraction monster when you don’t mean to fight. Pyrotechnics and Entangle.

Reefclaw - in water only, allowed by the Skull and Shackles AP. The Reefclaw swims, has a free grab, and won’t stop until it’s dead (-Con hit points). In the water it’s excellent and really annoying meat.

Celestial Elk - Allowed by Rivers Run Red AP 32 for Erastil Worshippers. They have worse attack rolls than small earth elementals, but have three attacks. Augmented Summoning + Versatile Summon Monster makes that +5/2d6+4 ,+0/1d6+1d3+2, +0/1d6+1d3+2, with an average damage if all three attacks hit of 26 versus the Small Earth Elemental's +7/1d6+11 for an average of 14. The Earth Elemental is much more likely to hit, however.

Pseudodragon (Summon Good Monster required). Blindsense, Telepathy, Stealth +19, flight. Doesn’t fight terribly well although it has a save-or-sleep sting - but a really useful utility monster.

Faun (Summon Good Monster required). It can cast spells with a DC 16 save. Notably one use of Sleep (a first level spell) and one use of Hideous Laughter (a second level spell). DR2/cold iron and Die Hard combined with an AC of 16 means that it makes for surprisingly good meat.

Celestial Wolf (Summon Good Monster) can take a lot of damage before going down and can trip. Viable (don’t use the non-celestial version - the Hyena is strictly better).

Atomie (Summon Neutral Monster required). Invisibility and a 1d6 Sneak Attack. Also can reduce person at will at DC 15, or 1/day (i.e. 1/Summoning) cast Shrink Item (a third level spell)

DR2/Cold Iron and pretty good scouts (Stealth 20 without their personal Invisibility)

Brownie (Summon Neutral Monster required) - 1hd makes them not combat useful, and their spells aren’t great.

Damned/Larvae/Haunted Petitioner (Summon Evil Monster required) - more mechanical trouble than it’s worth. Great RP potential to pull someone you knew away from the gates of the abyss for a few rounds

Pugwampi Gremlin (Summon Evil Monster or First Worlder Summoner) - level 2 monsterer that contains the level 2 Shatter spell. Also contains an Aura of Unluck which debuffs everyone near it - useful for messing up archers even if it’s in heavy cover.

Vexigit Gremlin (First Worlder Summoner Only) - You won’t often need to trash non-magical metal things in a hurry. But when you do, the vexigits each carry the L4 rusting grasp spell. More useful to set up Snares.

Summon Monster 3

This is the level where monsters really start getting powers of their own - and the Lantern Archon’s ability to fly and ignore DR is worth a call-out.

Meat: Aurochs - Large makes for more meat, and brings a DC19 trample attack for crowd control. At higher levels, a stampede of Aurochs (requires 3) is going to be nasty - and funny. For crowd control of course Versatile Summon Monster has extra damage to all targets rather than a smite. However, if your DM lets you use the alternate summoning option of Erastil clerics from AP 32, Dire Boars contemptuously outclass even Aurochs. Packing a whopping 52 hit points with DR 5/aligned, it’s almost unfair. Hippies make the best summoners, apparently.

Muscle: Leopard - First of the big cats that can Claw/Pounce/Rake for five attacks at +7 to hit, d3 (d6 on the bite) +8 damage when smiting per attack. Unless you need aligned attacks, multiple small earth elementals are probably more effective at damage although casters hate being grabbed.

Air: Dire Bat is the best melee flier - and is really useful for blindsense to find invisible enemies so you can glitterdust them. There is an argument that it’s also a riding animal.

Ranged: Lantern Archon has unresistable 2 x 1d6 ranged touch attacks, flies, and is very durable. 4 of them summoned with SM4 are shockingly good at wearing down much higher-level mobiles. They also come with a smorgasbord of goodies.

Dretch - Stinking Cloud. It’s not that hard to resist from the Dretch, (DC13) but still a very useful spell. And overlapping stinking clouds or multiple stinking clouds mean that people will fail saves in the end. Summon Evil Monster works.

Lantern Archon - useful against creatures with low will or with DR you otherwise can’t break as a Lantern Archon’s ranged touch attacks ignore all DR although only do 1d6 damage. They also have DR10/Evil for the meat. Also the Aid spell At Will almost makes this a pocket healer. And they fly. (But then so do Air Elementals - but Air Elementals don’t get True Speech)

Dire Bat (Summon Evil Monster works) - Radar - if you have the spells to spend on it (but the Bestiary 5 B Small Aether Elemental has same radar ability and is one level lower). Also a riding animal that flies.

Constrictor (snake) - A really obnoxious grappler - although nothing like as scary as a leopard

Cheetah - generally inferior to the Leopard, the Cheetah can be summoned as a ballistic missile capable of charging at least 500ft (arguably 1000ft) to bite and trip enemies who thought they were well out of range.

Wolverine Multiattack + Augment Summoning + Rage + Smite gives the wolverine 3 attacks at +8, doing d4 or d6 +9 damage when raging and smiting. The problem is that if the enemy doesn’t attack it (triggering rage) it’s strictly worse than the leopard, and it doesn’t have pounce so provoking means you drop two attacks as well as taking damage. Better in theory than in practice.

Azata Lyrakien (requires Summon Good Monster) - Need a +16 bonus to an obscure knowledge skill in a hurry? Combo this creature with Evolved Summoned Monster: Skilled.

Dire Boar (AP 32 - Rivers Run Red, option for Erastil followers) If your DM lets you summon this monster at this level, count your blessings. Comfortable winner of the meat award even over the Foo Dog and Auroch, with its huge hp stack and DR. It also does really good damage as well, only really outclassed by the Leopard and that’s if you don’t use Evolved Summoned Monster to give it two claw attacks. Unlike a lot of alternate summoning options, Erastil is a pretty easy and thematic deity to snag.

Foo Dog (requires Summon Good Monster or Expanded Summon Monster). DR 5/Bludgeoning. Can be Hardness 8. Die Hard. Two of them means they both get Protection from Evil. If you have the necessary summoning feats it’s the comfortable winner of the Meat (or rather stone) award - although a herd of aurochs is funnier and has a charm all of its own.

Silvanshee Agathion requires either Summon Good Monster or Ring of Agathion Summoning or is an optional monster from AP50 (Night of Frozen Shadows/Jade Regent). Unfortunately you can’t use it to Commune as that would require an expensive material component, but between a 90’ fly speed, the ability to understand (and speak with) any language or animal, a gaseous form a +19 stealth, and +10 perception, it’s a handy little utility monster.

Arbiter Inevitable (requires Summon Neutral Monster or Ring of Inevitable Summoning). About the only thing it can do that’s useful is release a 3d6 10’ radius electrical burst and then be summoned. But if used in a higher level slot this can stack nastily.

Nosoi Psychopomp - requires either Summon Neutral Monster or Ring of Psychopomp Summoning or is an optional summons from AP 44 (Night of the Beast/Carrion Crown). It allows a 6 Question (i.e. CL12) Speak with Dead (but with casting time of 10 minutes), and can ward off spirits. Useful for a random utility spell.

Voidworm Protean (requires Summon Neutral Monster or Ring of Protean Summoning). Even if this was good as anything other than meat I’d really recommend not using it because it’s both a protean and a shapeshifter and they take time and focus to use.

Paracletus Aeon (requires Summon Neutral Monster or a Ring of Aeon Summoning). 3/day Calm Emotions at DC13 and some buffing auras for whole party which effects last for 10 minutes :) - our spell list is expanding. Otherwise pretty average.

Augur Kyton (requires a Ring of Kyton Summoning or Summon Evil Monster). It’s got spells. Such a pity none of them are useful.

Cassissian (requires a Ring of Angel Summoning) - not much use in combat. Do you want either the Aid or Daylight L2 spells? Because it can do both.

Harbinger Archon (requires a ring of Archon Summoning) - not much use in combat but 3/day uses of both Cure Light Wounds and Protection from Evil (CL 6). Handy as a utility.

Remember that Commune takes extensive material components so may not be used even as an SLA

Human Natural Wererat Rogue 2 - from Carrion Crown. Nice skill list there - it’s a second level rogue so why wouldn’t it have one. In hybrid form it has AC 19 and DR10/silver as well as a sneak attack - if you don’t qualify for the Foo Dog, this is pretty good meat. Also inflicts Lycanthropy and Filth Fever with its bite. Probably best for evil PCs.

Cacodaemon (Summon Evil Monster or Expanded Summoned Monster required or Ring of Deamon Summoning) - invisibility, perfect flight, Stealth +14, Perception +7, Knowledge (The Planes) +18. Telepath, speaks Common (can damn recently deceased innocents..)

Div, Doru (Summon Evil Monster required or Ring of Div Summoning) Invisibility at will, Minor Image 3/day, Suggestion 1/day, Perfect Flight, DR10, Perception +7, Stealth +17. Doesn’t speak common but is a telepath.

Cythnigot (requires Ring of Qlippoth Summoning) can cast Soften Earth and Stone (before SMIV Mephits) which can be creatively used (in favorably conditions) for making cave-ins, avalanches or big pools of mud. Also able to cast Warp Wood which has some utility (leak a ship, deal with wooden doors and gates, warp pillars in tunnels to start cave-in). At last has also constant law a and magic detection.

Howler (Summon Evil Monster required) 5HD makes this the emergency combat choice of the discerning Evil Monster SUmmoner

Nuglub Gremlin (First Worlder Summoner Only) - heat metal, shocking grasp, snare. If you want to prepare snares, you can summon more Vexigits.

Summon Monster 4

The utility monsters here are the Mephits. It’s where the templates start getting good by bringing DR5/Evil

Meat: Hound Archon - if the foe is evil it has continual Magic Circle against Evil. If not it’s DR10/Evil. If you’re lucky, both apply. Remember you can use its Beast Shape for Dire Wolf form for +4 Str and a trip attack, and AC 21 at the cost of the slam.

Muscle: Lion - Grab, Pounce, Rake. And more to the point a Celestial Lion has DR5/Evil - making it pretty durable.

Air: Pteranodon - this thing is a lot meaner than it looks. It has ten foot reach (always handy for the opportunity attacks) - but the best thing about it is the way everything stacks on that main attack; when augmented and Smiting Evil it’s only +8 to hit, but 2d6+12 damage all in.

Water: Don’t... just don’t. Sharks are bad, and that leaves the Ice and Water (and Mud - but it takes a nerf in water) elemental, and they all struggle to deal with d4+1 small elementals. The best candidate is Ice as it has Cleave.

Bison - an upgraded Aurochs. Probably most useful at summon monster 6 - at SM 5 it has a 1 in 3 chance of not triggering the stampede. And the summon monster 5 monsters are generally awesome. At this level it also has DR5/Evil

Brown Bear - not only large and tough but grabs, making it that much harder to ignore.

Tiger - The Tiger is marked as an alternative summons in AP53 (Tide of Honour/Jade Regent) and has 6d8 hit points, meaning its Celestial template gives it DR 5/Evil. It also has Pounce and Rake - if your DM allows it the Tiger is the standout monster at this level.

Choker - allowed by the Carrion Crown AP. No use in combat, but makes for a decent assassin that can keep the victim quiet and in place.

Gibbering Mouther - allowed by the Legacy of Fire AP. It’s an interesting grappler that messes the scenery up.

Cerberi – allowed by the Council of Thieves AP. It is better meat than Hound Archon and muscle than Tiger if you are fighting true neutral creatures. Also it’s bite curse the victim to not be able to teleport and is able to scent (and trail) even undead and incorporeal.

Mephits - all Mephits get a weak breath weapon and either two second level spells or a second and a third level spell at CL6 - often useful for utility. They also have 15ft cone Breath Weapons; the d8 damage ones are pretty useless, but about half of them do only d4 damage, but sicken (2 point debuff) for 3 rounds. Notable ones (i.e. third level spells) are below:

Dust Mephit - Blur and Wind Wall, sickening breath.

Earth Mephit - Soften Earth and Stone. Might come in useful and no one will have learned or prepared it (if you have Ring of Qlippoth Summoning, you can get the same spell from SMIII Cythnigot).

Fire Mephit – Heat Metal to 5 rounds of fire damage to up to 3 creatures in metal armor (will save negates).

Ice Mephit - Chill Metal, 3 magic missiles. Sickening breath. Scraping the barrel

Ooze Mephit (or Water Mephit)- Acid Arrow (6d4 ranged touch, no save or SR), Stinking Cloud, and ooze has sickening breath. The combat option - but I’d still use a combat creature rather than a mephit. That said, d3+1 Mephits dropping a DC15 stinking cloud followed by a 6d4 acid arrow are probably more than a match for d4+2 dretches dropping a DC13 stinking cloud then trying to cause fear. Either way smells revolting.

Salt Mephit – Glitterdust, sickening breath and 20 feet radius dehydration = 2d8 damage (DC 14 half).

Lightning Mephit - once again Frog God Games have managed to blow the power levels. The lightning mephit can cast a 6d6 lightning bolt and when you’re summoning either d3+1 or d4+2 of them you can blow any evoker out of the water.

Elemental (medium) - Elementals are all beatsticks - but the medium elementals would struggle to make the Summon Monster 3 list of highlights. Unless you have a weird use for them (the abilities are similar to those of the small elementals), these are probably best avoided. +3 to hit and twice the HP certainly doesn’t make up for being able to summon d4+2 (or even d4+1) of them.

Pixie (Summon Good Monster required) - it can cast a Permanent Illusion (although only sound and sight). Permanent Illusion is a level 6 spell, the summoning a pixie is level 4. Also has special arrows (Charm (L4), sleep, Modify Memory (L6) - DC15), permanent invisibility, and DR 10/Cold Iron.

Foo Lion (Summon Good Monster required). Alas poor lion. Claw, rake, pounce, made out of stone, 10ft reach (for the opportunity attacks).

Magmin (Summon Neutral Monster required). This is more battlefield control than a monster; it radiates heat and burns weapons that attack it. But isn’t really worth it.

Satyr (Summon Neutral Monster required) You get this one level before a First Worlder Summoner for some reason. DC17 Suggestion (third level spell), DC18 Fear (fourth level spell), and the pipes are a Mass Suggestion. Very handy utility monster.

Giant Skunk (Expanded Summon Monster): They’re pretty okay attackers in melee, what with having DR, +5 smite damage, and three natural attacks. But the real reason why you summon them is for their 2/day Fort DC 17 Nauseating ranged attack for 1d6 rounds + 1d6 minutes of sicken. Or to upset NPCs out of combat.
Shae (Summon Neutral Monster required) At Will Lesser Shadow Evocation for first and second level wizard/sorcerer evocations. Darkness you can disbelieve is always fun - and the Shae can keep firing evocations until everyone gets bored (although there is the save to disbelieve). A lot here depends on how your DM rules on illusions.

Vidus Psychopomp (Summon Neutral Monster required) - it’s a creepy monster, but in practical terms the most useful thing about it might be the Mind Fog it creates when killed.

Theletos Aeon (Ring of Aeon Summoning required). It’s CR 7! By the standards of this level it’s a fast healing tank - >90hp with Augment Summoning, Fast Healing 5, and has crowd control every d4 rounds.. It’s also got a nice list of spells like Dispel Magic (CL9) 3/day, Charm Monster and Lesser Geas (both 4th level - pity about the DCs) 1/day and most importantly Remove Curse 3/day. Summon Neutral Monster makes this Summon 6 - I think it would be about right at Summon 5.

The latest errata to the Advanced Class Guide puts the creature on the SM6 list via the ring. It’s still not a bad creature to summon, but loses a lot of the OMFG factor.

Giant Black Widow Spider (Spider Summoner required). The highlight of this spell is the poison - not generally something that’s as useful against monsters as against PCs - but Fort DC 21 for d3 Con damage can really ruin a wizard’s day. As can immunity to mind affecting effects and tremor sense. Niche but useful in that niche. +8 to hit is struggling against anything bigger.

Kelpie (Summon Evil Monster) Edge case but DC17 charm save or be drowned is possibly worth thinking of

Aghash (require the Ring of Div Summoning) some good SLAs and Su: at will bestow curse (will save DC16, but when it works it is deadly) combined with at will spectral hand; also can target fortitude (DC16) and choose some debuff with cursed gaze.

Catrina (require the Ring of Psychopomp Summoning) 3/day Major image (will DC20); can lure creatures to kiss her (will DC17) and kiss the to death (fortitude DC17; 5d6 damage per round or death after 3 rounds).

Unicorn (First Worlder Summoner Only) - Magic Circle against Evil like the Hound Archon makes this a good summons. But beyond that it has Cure Poison, Cure Moderate Wounds, and two uses of Cure Light at CL9. A useful defensive summons.

Summon Monster 5

Demons and Devils - spells FTW. And the Ankylosaurus is an outstanding monster.

Meat: Ankylosaurus - it’s not just the 95 HP and 15’ reach here, it’s that the tail (+16 to hit) inflicts a DC25 (!) one round daze as well as 3d6+25 damage when smiting. And it has DR5/Evil from the Celestial template. Evolved Summoned Monster with an Ankylosaurus is just plain evil, dealing an additional 2d8+15 with two more hoof or claw attacks. For evil summoners the Babau with DR 10/Cold Iron or Good and three attacks with Sneak Attack is a standout.

Muscle: Large Earth Elemental - their feats include Greater Bull Rush so foes they bull rush provoke opportunity attacks. They have a CMB (augmented) of +18 - for +23 when Bull Rushing someone on the ground. Or they can just smash people with their two slam attacks - power attacking at +14 for 2d6+13 damage each (again at people on the ground). And DR5/-

Air: The Bralani Azata is an excellent flying monster - but it’s a monster that can fly (and lightning bolt) rather than an air superiority monster. For air superiority, Large Air Elementals can inflict a lot of DC20 reflex saves and drag the enemies where you want them or multiple Pteranodons are your best single target attacker.

Water: For dealing with chaff the Large Water Elemental’s vortex can scythe through the enemy, or the Large Ice Elemental can shut people down on fortitude. Grapplers like the Giant Moray Eel are especially effective against enemies who get too close to the water (and are a choice of the Summon Evil Monster summoner) while the Orca is simple and very very effective. I have no right answer.

Ranged: If you need mass range damage and you need it now, the Bralani Azata has your back. However, if you’re only targeting one enemy at range, you expect an extended combat, or you just want something that can stand up to the enemy for awhile the Large Aether Elemental is a better choice. If you want ranged control with a side order of ranged damage and an option to tag into melee combat, the Ceustodemon can be even better than those two.

Bralani Azata - Two 6d6 lightning bolts, cure serious wounds, and wind wall at will. For those of you keeping score that’s at least four level three spells - but if you want wind walls fast you can summon multiple dust mephits. Evocation is generally weak - but see above for how these things work at level 7. Made a whole lot tougher by Summon Good Monster

Babau (a Summon Evil Monster choice)- dispel magic at will. And See Invisibility. Keep it dispelling until it succeeds - a utility monster although Sneak Attack (2d6) and 3 attacks at +14 is pretty effective. Probably the Summon Evil Monster muscle of choice, and DR10/cold iron or good make it pretty meaty.

Wooly Rhino - however hilarious a herd of aurochs are, this is an expert (DC 25) trampler with a powerful charge for whatever’s left

Dire Lion: It’s a pretty good cat. Pounce, Rake. You know the drill. +5 to hit over the normal lion and it starts getting DR.

Elemental (Large) - Elementals are all beatsticks and at this level gain 2 attacks and DR 5/-. See the small elemental list for details. Mud is a whole lot nastier at this level as with two attacks one can render a low-fort enemy helpless in a turn, if you are using the harsher possible reading then the air elemental’s whirlwind is finally useful against medium creatures by the stricter interpretation, and the Elementals are all contenders as meat although the anklyosaurus rocks just too much. Feats are useful, with Water, Mud, and Ice elementals all getting Great Cleave - but the comfortable standout here is the Earth Elemental who gets the Muscle nod for this level

Vulpinal Agathion (Summon Good Monster, Expanded Summon Monster, or Ring of Agathion Summoning) also allowed by Jade Regent, the Vulpinal Agathion is a utility rather than combat monster with Detect Evil, Major Image, Remove Disease, Calm Emotions, Charm Monster, Dispel Evil, 3* 3d8 Holy Smite, and Flame Arrow. And 6 uses of 3d6 Lay On Hands. Just the thing to break an angry mob whipped up by foul magics. Also has +21 to all knowledges. Not often that useful but on the rare occasions it is it really is.

Ceustodaemon (Expanded Summon Monster or Ring of Daemon Summoning) - The evil counterpart to the Bralani Azata. Comes with a cocktail of immunities (death effects and mind-affecting are the big ones), some very good utility magic at CL8 such as See Invisibility (L2), Dispel Magic (L3), Fly (L3), Hold Monster (L5), and Slow (L3). The big thing is the 1d4/round breath weapon at 6d6 damage. If you’re lucky, you can REALLY rack up the damage. Unfortunately, it’s not as guaranteed as the Bralani Azata’s’s 2/day Lightning Bolt… but on the other hand, Ceustodaemons still have Hold Monster, Slow, and Fly. Probably a better choice than Bralani Azatas if you’re doing 1 min/level summons and have a rolling skirmishing fight rather than want to create as much damage as fast as possible.

Incubus (Expanded Summon Monster) - It’s a Summon Monster V option that can cast Suggestion (L3) at-will. It doesn’t need to do anything else.

Djinni (Summon Good Monster). Summoned by a level 5 spell, it can cast: Gaseous Form (L3), Major Creation (L5 with a bonus), Wind Walk (L6 - three targets), and Persistent image (L5). A very useful utility monster that fights in combat as a whirlwind that’s about as big as it needs to be.

Unicorn (Summon Good Monster) - it’s been a bit outlevelled - but with Cure Moderate Wounds, three uses of Cure Light Wounds, and Cure Poison (L4) it’s still able to give some nice healing to an arcanist.

Catrina Psychopomp (Summon Neutral Monster, or you can summon it with Summon monster IV if you have Ring of Psychopom Summoning) - this is a creepy effect. Speak With Dead takes too long to cast for anyone other than a Summoner. Major Image is nice, as is Calm Emotions, and there’s the DC17 three round assassination effect. Very nice.

Ogre Spider (Spider Summoner required) - compared to the Ankylosaurus it’s a waste of space except at creating webs.

Shadow Mastiff - allowed by Carrion Crown or Summon Evil Monster. The bay of the mastiff makes everyone within 300’ make a will save at DC 16 or panic and flee for 1d4 rounds. It’s a complete hordebreaker, although watch for blowback as you and your allies also flee.

Cloaker – allowed by AP41. Can automatically debuff everybody (who can hear and is mindaffectable) in 60 feet by his Moan extraordinary ability – which cannot be dispelled, antimagiced, spell-resisted and even doesn’t provoke attacks of opportunity :D

Pixie (First Worlder Summoner Only) - this isn’t a combat summons. The Pixie has the L6 spell Permanent Image. Also Charm Monster (L4) and Modify Memory (L6). Even useful at this level although Summon Good Monster summoners had them a level before.

Satyr (First Worlder Summoner Only) - Suggestion and Fear effects if you want them. The Summon Neutral summoner had it earlier.

Summon Monster 6

Here’s where you start getting an actual spellcaster as a summoned monster. Unfortunately it’s a bard with all the wrong spells known. But still useful. Also, Summon Monster 6 is where Expanded Summon Monster starts to become significantly more useful.

Meat: Triceratops - It’s a triceratops. What more do you want? Celestial or fiendish template? Almost 150 hit points? Huge - so it takes up a lot of room? And a nice trample attack doing 1d8 + 28 damage (including Smite) on their primary target for taking out a herd large or smaller creatures in the way? That said, the Dire Tiger is better most of the time.

If you have the space for it (and unfortunately, you often won’t) you can also spring for an Immense Tortoise. They're the first Colossal animal you can summon and boy do they bring the pain. Not only are they a gigantic wall of meat and area control but they do a ton of damage. Would you like a Smite-Power Attack-Trample for 2d8+45 damage? You sure would!

Muscle: Dire Tiger Pounce, Rake, Grab. 77 damage on average rolls if all attacks (all at +20) hit and you succeed at a grab once. And that’s without Smite Evil for +14 to damage on each of those attacks.

Air: Plenty on this list can fly - but almost all utility casters. Huge Elementals might qualify, but only get +3 to hit over large ones and the same DR. Probably go for multiple large elementals - or multiple Bralani Azata is never wrong against something that isn’t immune to electricity.

Water: Giant Octopus. This is just unpleasant; the point about the Giant Octopus is that it has a bite and 8 tentacles each of which gets first a free grab and then a free constrict damage roll if the grab succeeds. If smiting each of these attacks can get +12 damage. That’s an absurd amount of scaling. As they can move on land this is probably the Summon Evil Monster summoner’s meat and muscle of choice.

Ranged: You’re spoiled for options this time around. If for some reason you can get your hands on a Mothman (note that Mothmen are an option for Groetus worshippers -- and Groetus is probably the best deity for Pathfinder Society blaster clerics) and can sweet-talk the DM into explaining how throwing out Delayed Blast Fireballs corrects fate, they completely blow away all other ranged options. Summon Good Monster summoners can pick up the Wood Giant. They’re hard to work with given their frankly mediocre attack rolls, but they can do a ton of ranged damage if you can manage to boost it. Suzerain Scepter is, as always, highly recommended. If you can’t get either, Shadow Demons are an okay backup. If you’re looking for something more cerebral (at the cost of doing raw damage) the Erinyes have your back. Huge Aether Elementals are still a good pick, but their damage is starting to stall out at this point.

Last but not least, there’s the Vanth Psychopomp. It’s slightly weaker than the other options, but Searing Light remains relevant much longer, even against non-undead.

Invisible Stalker - it’s always invisible. And... er... that’s about it.

Lilend Azata - it’s a 7th level bard with a bad spell selection. If you want an unoptimised 7th level bard here’s where to turn - can be pretty useful in the right circumstances (buffing and healing party or speaking with animals and plants).

Shadow Demon - Shadow Conjuration 3/day, Shadow Evocation 3/day, Telekinesis At Will. Shadow Conjuration is level 4, the rest are level 5. And this is only one option for a L6 monster summon. I hope the sorcerer knows you’re treading on his toes. At level 11 he can, after all, only cast 5th level spells. A Summon Evil Monster choice.  There is also an exploit that can be used with Shadow Conjuration if your DM allows the Shadow Demon to deliberately fail it’s own saves against it’s Shadow Conjuration.  Alter Summoned Monster is a Conjuration(Summoning) spell, and thus a valid use of Shadow Conjuration.  At SMVII and up, when you can summon multiple Shadow Demons, they can then change themselves into creatures from the higher level list at the cost of a single turn.  None of your own actions, and no spell slots.

Succubus - the bane of many summoners. What more do you need to say other than that it’s a succubus or that some summoners will summon d4+2 of them just because they can? Possibly Charm Monster (L4), Dominate Person (L5), Cha 27 (so very high DCs), Bluff +27, for quick infiltration / interrogation use combination of tongues + alter self + detect thoughts. A Summon Evil Monster choice.

Erinyes – some Su and SLAs: constant true seeing SL6, has darkvision and see in darkness; at will unholy blight (SL4, DC 19; evil AOE damaging and potentially sickening); entangle (Su; DC 20); also fear DC 19 and minor image DC17. Because it has Minor Image AND True Seeing, it can be almost as hard to hit as Aether Elementals if you’re feeling creative.

Mothman (Shattered Star AP). Technically only CR6 this is a utility monster through and through. Ignoring its Agent of Fate ability (any spell of L5 or below to steer fate in the right direction) it can cast: Mind Fog (L5), False Vision (L5), Shadow Walk 3/day (L6), Phantasmal Killer 3/day (L4) Nightmare 3/day (L5) – but casting takes 10 minutes, Modify Memory 3/day (L6). And, of course, by being from a summons, the Mothman’s Mind Fog does horrible things to the action economy.

Chaos Beast (Shattered Star AP) Very, very unpleasant if it hits someone - the claws causing corporeal instability. Friends don’t do this to each other - but it’s more efficient to hit harder.

Vanth Psychopomp (AP44 or Ring of Psychopomp Summoning) – DR 10/adamantine; spiritsense; bestow curse DC 17); locate creature; searing light.

Animate Dream (Expanded Summon Monster) - Despite having no damage reduction, it’s still ridiculously durable; 12 hit die and incorporeal to boot! It also hits pretty hard and accurately with its 6d8 incorporeal touch -- try to get it some Haste action. The real money of this summon, however, are its spell-likes: Deep Slumber (L3, DC 18), Fear, Confusion, AND Phantasmal Killer (all three L4, DC 19) on speed-dial? Yet another of those ‘not too bad for PCs, nightmares for other monsters to fight’ monsters.

Naunet Protean (Expanded Summon Monster or Ring of Protean Summoning) - Stymied by damage reduction? You have your choice of overcoming Magic, Chaotic, Silver, Cold Iron, or Adamantine Damage reduction with a decently hard-hitting monster. Also his tentacles (2 attacks +13) can cause Confusion (will DC 21) for 1 round (and it stacks) – could be useful for controlling crowd of nonchaotic enemies.

Tylosaurus (Expanded Summon Monster) - Grab attack, +29 grapple bonus, Swallow Whole. Chomp! The Tylosaurus is also an option for the Jade Regent Adventure Path, but that version doesn’t come with an alignment template.

Kirin (Summon Good Monster). It’s a 6th level sorcerer specialising in evocation spells. As its only third level spell is a 6d6 lightning bolt there’s the Bralani Azata that takes a lower level. On the other hand it has both Break Enchantment (L5, CL9) and Lesser Restoration (L2), and 8 uses of Cure Light Wounds. Oh, and it also has Major Creation (L5). Not generally better than you find on the Summon Monster V list, but when you need Break Enchantment you need it!

Wood Giant (Summon Good Monster) Does considerable ranged damage if you can get all three attacks to land. 6d6+21 damage is the equivalent of 12d6 and it can do it every round. Point Blank Shot and Beacon of Hope can make the attack routine a much more reasonable +12/+12/+7.

Chaos Beast (Summon Neutral Monster) - Corporeal Instability is a messy way of killing people. But if you want them dead the Dire Tiger is far more useful.

Theletos Aeon (Summon Neutral Monster or Ring of Aeon Summoning) - mind control is always useful. Pity about the DCs. Then again, since it has the ability to use two DC 19 charisma damage attacks (and if it hits, it lowers their saving throw bonus) if can kill, say, high-HP low-CHA creatures with bad will saves quite handily. 3/day Dispel Magic and Remove Curse is handy to know about.

Legion Archon (Summon Good Monster or Ring of Archon Summoning required). It’s not a bad Archon with DR10/evil, but as a beatstick it’s good but no Dire Tiger -- few things are, of course. Unlike Dire Tigers, however, you can fit several of them into a tight space. And they fly. Perfect for tight corridors. Its spells are mostly self-buffs, alas. But it’s one of the few monsters that’s able to pierce multiple types of damage reduction.

Lamia (Cyphermage Prestige Class) - Has a nice full-attack that includes wisdom drain, and a few low-DC Illusion and Enchantment spells

Adhukait Asura (Ring of Asura Summoning) – Spike growth (SL3) for battlefield control in outdoor areas with vegetation.

Hydrodaemon (Ring of Daemon Summoning) – Control Water for whirlpooling enemies ships or flooding shores.

Shoggti Qlippoth (Ring of Qlippoth Summoning) – first summoned creature with Protection from law (so late); Braincloud damages wisdom and it needs only successful touch attack.  

Efreeti (Summon Evil Monster required). Fortunately Wish has expensive material components so this isn’t a cheap way to get wishes. You do however get Permanent Image (level 6), three walls of fire (level 4 or 5), and Pyrotechnics At Will. A utility summons

Kalavakus (Expanded Summon Monster) - It has Haste and Dominate Person (L4, DC 18) at 3/day, at-will Telekinesis (L9, DC 19). If that wasn’t juicy enough, it also has a swift action ‘enslave soul’ ability that staggers a foe on a save or gives them a -6 penalty against the Kalavakus’s effects if they fail. It’s win-win!

Vanth Psychopomp (worship Pharasma, or Ring of Psychopomp Summoning)- They have 3 CL 10 Searing Lights. Unless you’re facing undead (but let’s face it; undead are a very common mid-level enemy) they kind of pale against other options… on the other hand, Searing Light has no saving throw, so they can remain relevant until the endgame as long as their foes don't have spell resistance. Seventh level spell slots reliably blasting CR15 foes with 20d8/40d6 damage for 3 rounds? Now that's some deep hurting!

Elemental (Huge) - Elementals are all beatsticks and at this level gain 2 attacks and DR 5/-. See the small elemental list for details. Which means, of course, that they are little better than the Large elementals - a few more hit points and about +3 to hit and damage. Not worth it although Magma and Mud elementals join the Greater Bull Rush club. However…

Huge Air Elemental - This is the level in which Air Elementals start getting extremely good. They have a whopping DC23 Reflex Save to put people in whirlwinds. And because they’re huge they can suck in multiple large creatures. Whirlwind is not only very good crowd control but it’s also a greater mobile barrier / visual screen.

Summon Monster 7

This level’s a little disappointing after the high casting of the last one; you’re probably better off with d3 monsters from the previous list.

Meat: The Brachiosaurus is a gargantuan trampler (DC 34) with Great Cleave and Greater Overrun (CMB 32). A hordebreaker. (It probably needs errata - as things stand it has the Improved Bull Rush feat rather than Improved Overrun)

Muscle: Multiple Dire Tigers are still your most effective choice - or there’s the Roc - Grab and fly off (and possibly let go) is a simple approach. But when a gargantuan creature grabs it grabs. And the Roc can both fly and flyby attack and has DR10/Evil and does quite a lot of damage. Also a Summon Good Monster option.

Air: I just said the Roc was muscle. It’s a strong flier - use it. If dealing with chaff the Greater Air Elemental is normally +6 over the Huge one.

Water: Greater Water Elementals are a huge step up on huge ones - for clearing chaff or moving people. For single targets the Dire Crocodile with its Death Roll and Swallow Whole combined with Smite can do a lot of damage. Note that for the Dire Crocodile’s special attacks look at the Crocodile entry.

Ranged: Unfortunately, this is the level where Aether Elementals, even Greater variants, start to permanently fall behind. Their ranged attack roll is still quite good if you’re up against monsters with good saves but bad ACs -- like a lot of outsiders. But since they only get one attack/round at the equivalent of 10d6 damage, Shedu and Jyoti are better choices for ranged damage, especially AoE ranged damage.

Bone Devil - it has a Dimensional Anchor if no one remembered to prepare one.

Vrock - A DC 23 30 ft radius stun is really quite nice. But it’s a one shot wonder which is then best off just using Telekinesis for a second shot. Either that or you can have the Vrock dance somewhere expensive. No, it’s not going to complete the dance against competent opposition. But that’s the point - it’s a bag of hit points that the enemy must take down. An Evil summoner can Vrock Bomb with the best of them

Tyrannosaurus - a contender for meat. It’s huge and bulky. And the Dire Tiger does more damage. But being gargantuan that bite has one hell of a reach and some really nasty criticals. A tyrannosaurus is a prime candidate for Evolved Summoned Monster, especially multiple copies of that feat -- nothing quite like dropping 3 Tyrannosaurs each with an bite/claw/claw attack routine of 4d6+44/3d6+31/3d6+31.

Bebilith – okay for fight with 174 hit points, 22 AC and three +21 attacks dealing approximately 50 damage (or 3×+16 for 80 damage with power attack). Best are special effects after successful hits: dismantle armor (if hits with both claws) or consecutive 5 rounds of constitution damage (after biting). Also can throw really strong web to entangle opponents (on successful touch ranged +11 attack with DC25 to escape). His strikes count as chaotic and magical, and against demons also cold iron and good.

Angel, Movanic Deva (Summon Good Monster or Ring of Angel Summoning). Our summoner has at last become Angel Summoner - with an angel with the angelic laundry list of resistances including two low level spells, an SR of 21, and an AC of 24. I don’t know whether it can actually use its antimagic field. In addition to its resistances , the Movanic Deva can Remove Curse (or other ailments) at will until it finally succeeds, and carries a significant amount of healing juice. Also (at will) can discern lies and can cast holy smite to attack area of evil outsiders.  Alas its Holy Aura has an expensive component. Warning: The d20PFSRD has mistakenly linked the Angel, Monadic Deva - which is significantly more powerful and a Summon Monster 8 option.

Shield Archon (Summon Good Monster or Ring of Archon Summoning) - with AC 29, SR21 this deserves a meat award of its own. Oh, and Disrupting Weapon to make melee attacks against Undead Save or Die. And can cast shield other and become HP battery.

Shedu (Summon Good Monster) - A dismissal spell, a 12d6 Flame Strike, AC24, SR20, and some hordebreaking.

Treant (Summon Good Monster) If you go into the woods today... Treants basically have two tricks. The first is that they are vulnerable to slashing rather than evil, meaning that the enemy might be unprepared unlike for your normal run of celestials. The second is that they trample interlopers (although can’t animate trees, being summoned monsters).

Dragon, Young Bronze (Summon Good Monster) It’s a dragon, and a pretty respectable one at that. Repulsion Breath is always handy, as are half a dozen attacks. But mostly It’s a Dragon! Young Bronze Dragons have somewhat mediocre attack rolls and average damage rolls, but they get a ton of them. If you’re able to boost that somehow, with, say, a Rod of Giant Summoning then they’ll completely shred their foes.

Jyoti (Summon Neutral Monster) - an 11d6 60’ cone fire breath usable 1/round. But more importantly, they have 1/day Breath of Life, meaning they can resurrect someone who died in the previous round (and wasn’t too battered). It’s mostly a Summoner trick; 1 full round is too slow to summon them - but a Summoner can with their Summon Monster SLA.

Sacristan Kyton (Ring of Kyton Summoning or Summon Evil Monster required) It’s a scary beatstick with a nasty insanity-causing crowd control effect. Really worrying against PCs - not so much against monsters where you kill rather than turn insane most of the time - although the 25’ radius DC18 Deeper Darkness + Confusion can be crippling.

Giant Tarantula (Spider Summoner required) It has frankly terrible offence and no webs. Its one redeeming feature is the Nausea touch attack and reaction to being hit by a non-reach weapon. As Nauseated might as well be out of the fight for a while, the only question is whether DC 24 saving throws on Ref and Fort are going to be enough. Know your enemy!

Bogeyman (Summon Evil Monster required) 3/day it can cast both a Hold Person DC20 and a Quickened Phantasmal Killer (DC21) That’s a lot of save-or-losery. And the fear aura and 6d6 sneak attack claws don’t help anyone either. This is a summons to eliminate anyone with low will saves. DR15/Cold Iron and a fear aura make it a semi-decent meat choice.

Nucklavee (Summon Evil Monster required) - a 10d6 30’ cone of bad breath once every 1d4 rounds and DR10/Cold Iron. Can upset trash.

Leukodaemon (Expanded Summon Monster required) - Two effects that really bring the pain. The first is a 1/day harm at CL 10. The other is a Contagion that strikes every time the Leukodaemon hits a foe with an arrow… and the DC is constitution-based! Having an unsaveable 50’ aura that slaps on a -4 save penalty to this is just unfair -- 2 shots of Blinding Sickness at DC 25 per round on top of arrow damage make this a powerful horde-breaker.

Ghawwas Div (Ring of Div Summoning) – really good in combat and could be very useful in water: control water at will, boil water at will. And if you want to rescue victim of fire execution (quench).

Nyogoth Qlippoth (Ring of Qlippoth Summoning) – acid fog 1/day.

Elemental (Greater) - Your elementals now have DR 10/-. See the small elemental list for details on other effects, but the semi-mundane creatures are all on DR10/Good or Evil anyway. 

Daughter of Urgathoa (a.k.a. Daughter of the Dead) - do you actually want a level 6 evil Cleric with a constant Desecrate that causes Bubonic Plague and who doesn’t heal? If so this Carrion Crown option offers it. Technically not evil.

Nymph (First Worlder Summoner Only) - the Nymph is a nice way to open a fight with a bang, with Blinding Beauty. Unfortunately her best spell is Summon Nature’s Ally IV (which you can’t use) - and for blowing stuff up she has Call Lightning - or simply can unleash her Stunning Gaze on anyone who doesn’t get blinded. A good alternative to the Vrock as a control-bomb summon.

Summon Monster 8

Only three options here. And like at level 7 I don’t recommend any of them. You’re probably better off sticking with flooding the place with Summon Monster 6 - or Vrock-bombing (an average of three attempts to stun everyone in 30ft for the turn can be pretty effective). Both meat and muscle here are Elder Elementals, and they aren’t a lot better than the Greater Elementals last level. I also don’t honestly see any casting here that beats Summon Monster 6.

Gorgon - Kingmaker allows you a squishy monster that offers a DC21 (23 if augmented) vs Fortitude Save-or-be-petrified 60 foot cone. I’m sure there was an army round here?

Leonal Agathion (Expanded Summon Monster required or Ring of Agathion Summoning) - good beatsticks are often pouncers, as the Leonal is (Summons don’t need it as much as you summon them straight into melee). A CL14 holy word is useful for weeding out chaff, and 10d6 fireballs at will never go amiss - and neither does a heal spell. Still, despite the DR10 evil and silver, its main advantage over multiple Movanic Devas is 3* Wall of Force/day. When combined with some of the other tricks, you’re doing 60d6 Fire Damage (although DC15 means everyone will take half that) Every round for the duration… Evokers eat your heart out.

Angel, Monadic Deva (Summon Good Monster or ring of Angel Summoing) Call forth the angelic hordes! This thing’s CR12 for a good reason. Angel immunities and SR23. Spells including Heal (L6), Holy Aura (L8), Holy Word (L7, DC21, pity about 10th level), Hold Monster (L5), and is a strong beatstick.

Dragon, Young Gold (Summon Good Monster) A slightly more powerful dragon, especially when under Augment Summoning. And it’s a dragon.

Imentesh Protean (Summon Neutral Monster) 1/day Polymorph Any Object! Also a DC22 Warpwave when summoned (may be used as a swift action), Break Enchantment and some other goodies. It loses in combat to a Dire Tiger (what doesn’t?) so keep it out of combat - and that way the Protean rules won’t get underfoot.

Baregara (Summon Evil Monster) - a really nasty grappler (+23 to hit, free +29 grab with sfx to keep the victim there). Which makes it a utility thing.

Kolyarut Inevitable (Expanded Summon Monster or Ring of Inevitable Summoning) - Aside from being surprisingly decent in a straight-up slugfest, it also has three shots of a Quickened Suggestion. It’s at DC 16, but at three uses you might still be able to nail it. But can cast enervation at will!

Aghasura (Ring of Asura Summoning) – if enemies are not immune to poison, there is big chance she will kill all of them. 3 attacks (2×+28 2d6+14/15-20,1×+21 2d6+8+grab) all with poison (d4 constitution damage, fortitude DC23, 1/round for 6 rounds, cure 2 consecutive saves!) with 15ft reach. And also can cast cloudkill (d4 constitution damage/round, fortitude DC19 halves) and allure creatures in 50 feet towards her (will DC21 negates).

Maledaemon (Ring of Daemon Summoning) – some utility spell against plants.

Chernobue (Ring of Qlippoth summoning) – constant arcane sight, at will chaos hammer (will DC18)  and 3/day confusion (will DC18). Otherwise not so good.

Summon Monster 9

I’ve been complaining about level 7 and 8 monsters. But the level 9 ones are all good. All the celestials can fly - but only the Hamatula can swim.

Astral Deva - Holy Aura to start. Then Blade Barrier and Heal. And it’s also a beatstick with an array of ways to deal with evil creatures, and almost a full curing spectrum.

Ghaele Azata - always on Holy Aura, then casts like a 13th level cleric. Plus Prismatic Spray and Chain Lightning to boost the offence. And not that bad a beatstick.

Glabrezu - Reverse Gravity At Will. Fun! (Also Power Word Stun). And hits pretty hard. Summon Evil Monster speeds this up

Ice Devil - At Will Ice Storm and Cone of Cold (13d6). Also Wall of Ice to keep people in the area. Still, if you wanted evocation damage, you were better off at Summon Monster 7, summoning an average of four-and-a-half Brelani Azatas who would all throw 6d6 lightning bolts for the first two turns for 27d6. Summon Evil Monster speeds this up

Nalfeshnee - Greater Dispel Magic and Feeblemind At Will and a dazing unholy nimbus.

Trumpet Archon - flying 14th level cleric with a Magic Circle against Evil. I’m more impressed by the Azata.

Young Celestial Storm Giant - Serpent’s Skull. Not a bad beatstick, but also gains Control Weather and Chain Lightning.

Summon Good Monster, of course gives all the angels the die hard feat. Not that it matters much by now.

Leonal Agathion (Summon Good Monster required) - good beatsticks are often pouncers, as the Leonal is (Summons don’t need it as much as you summon them straight into melee). A CL14 holy word is useful for weeding out chaff, and 10d6 fireballs at will never go amiss - and neither does a heal spell. Still, despite the DR10 evil and silver, its main advantage over multiple Movanic Devas is 3* Wall of Force/day.

Akhana Aeon (Summon Neutral Monster or Ring of Aeon Summoning required) all its best spells take expensive material components so can’t be cast thanks to the rules for Summon Monster. Like Raise Dead.

Morrigna Psychopomp (Summon Neutral Monster required). Casts like a 12th level Inquisitor. At least it had the sense to take the Spell Immunity spell.

Valkyrie (Summon Neutral Monster required) - Heal, Breath of Life, Geas, surprisingly little combat damage - I think their CR involves them being able to summon a Slepinir which, of course, they can’t as a summoned monster. Weirdly you summon them to prevent them taking the souls of warriors.

Interlocutor Kyton (Ring of Kyton Summoning required) - Breath of Life is your main reason to summon this.

Ankou (Summon Evil Monster required) 1/day Circle of Death and 1/Day Prismatic Spray. Dimensional Anchors, and Ray of Exhaustion At Will

Derghodaemon (Summon Evil Monster required) free 30’ radius DC20 Feeblemind and a bit of combat potential.

Marut Inevitable (Ring of Inevitable Summoning) – muscle with two + 29 [augmented] slams of damage: 2d6+14 plus 3d6 electricity or sonic plus blindness or deafness (fortitude DC28 negates), has reach 10 feet and Power attack :) Also meatshield with 246 hp, 30 AC, 10 regeneration (chaotic), 1 DR/chaotic. Can cast earthquake (8th level cleric spell), at will greater dispel magic (CL16 – better than Nalfeshnee) and some other spells (chain lightning 1/day, wall of force 1/day).

Imentesh Protean (Ring of Protean Summoning) – some utility SLAs: dispel law (to get rid of any enchantment which have been cast by evil creature), polymorph any object (wizard 8th level spell).

Morrigna Psychopomp (Ring of Psychopomp Summoning) – okay-ish combat statistics, but some investigating utility SLA’s: confess, blood biography.

Augnagar Qlippoth (Ring of Qlippoth Summoning) – better muscle than Marut with 1 bite +25 (2d6+13 plus d8 bleed and rotting curse fortitude DC28 d6 Con damage) and 3 claws +25 (d8+13 plus d6 bleed) [augmented], can fly and has 15 feet reach, 227 hp with DR 10/lawful and some immunities and resistances :). Also can use its Horrific Appearance to confused for d3 rounds and damage 2 points of Charisma (will DC21 to negate charisma damage). Can also cast area of effect SLA waves of exhaustion (7th level spell), and has blindsight (30 feet), darkvision (60 feet), scent and true seeing.

Summon Nature’s Ally

Note that a lot of creatures and elementals show up on the Summon Monster list - especially Elementals. For the Elementals see the Summon Monster lists.

Summon Nature’s Ally 1

Mite - The first summon with ranged attacks available! And it’s not very good at its job. Even with Point Blank Shot and Augmented Summons, they still do a measly 1d3+2 damage at a +3 to hit with thrown darts. 1d3+1 of these creatures plinking away with nuisance damage isn’t a bad use of a second-level spell slot.

Gourd Leshy (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Leshy)- These little twerps actually do a decent amount of ranged damage if you slap the Fiery Template on them. DC 12 reflex entangles onto their ranged attacks is just gravy. Unfortunately, the levels in which these would be useful you’re probably not going to be able to afford a 2,800 gp ring. Watch out for how the DM rules upon Verdant Burst for your summoned monsters. You may get more than you bargained for, in both the good and bad sense.

Summon Nature’s Ally 2

Fungus Leshy (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Leshy)- Aside from doing the same amount of ranged damage as Gourd Leshys, they’re reasonably potent in melee if you can find a way to boost their three attacks. Their spores and puffball attacks can cause a surprising amount of trouble for enemy archers and spellcasters.

Summon Nature’s Ally 3

Celestial Dire Boar- If you’re using AP32 path shenanigans, you can get this onto your list, too. Aside from being one of the very few ways to summon celestial creatures on the Summon Nature’s Ally list, you can also add the fiery template. Imagine summoning 1d3+1 Celestial Fiery Dire Boars that can do 4d6+14 smiting damage each.

Shikigami (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Kami)- 22hp, Fast Healing 2, DR5/Cold Iron, and the Statue spell. Very tough, though might have some trouble holding monsters’ attention even with the Fiery Template and metal improvised weapons -- which fortunately doesn’t sink the Shikigami’s already questionable attack roll even further, since it uses Cha.

Summon Nature’s Ally 4

Griffon - Fiery Griffons completely outclass Celestial Pteranodons with Augment Summoning on the offense and those were a pretty good aerial monster.

Satyr - It’s just as good as it was on the Summon Monster list. Probably even better, because you can slap templates onto it without reducing its offense. (Remember it doesn’t come with pipes)

Tiger - You straight-up get a tiger, no questions asked. Since Summon Monster users ‘only’ get an alignment-templated lion without resorting to questionable AP-path picks, someone using Versatile Nature’s Ally can actually out damage Summon Monster users at this level even by being out on the Augment Summoning bonuses.

Summon Nature’s Ally 5

Ankylosaurus - Yes, it’s noticeably weaker than the Summon Monster option. However, you generally use these creatures to chain-stun anyway so losing the +10 smiting damage bonus doesn’t hurt their effectiveness too much.

Cyclops - Would be a mediocre beatstick for this level if not for Flash of Insight. You can use this ability to guarantee a critical hit with a x3 weapon. With Augment Summoning and Power-Attacking for the moon, you can make the first full-attack you make (hint: use Flash of Insight for the second attack unless they have a really high AC) do an astonishing 12d6+64 points of damage.

Ettin - Note: the Monster block does NOT include the extra free attack it would get from fighting with two weapons.

Flame Drake (Ring of Drake Natural Attunement - Drake required) - The Flame Drake is a pretty good monster even compared to Summon Monster 5. The fireball breath only does 5d6 compared to the Bralani Azata’s 6d6 lightning ball, but it’s more likely to hit targets. Unlike the Bralani Azata, it also does pretty decent damage in melee as well. It’s ability to take a move action as a swift action gives it pseudo-pounce.

Girallion- While slightly outdamaged by pouncing Dire Lions, these things have an incredible 10-foot reach. Nasty.

Manticore- Surprisingly, its ranged damage completely outclasses the Azata’s… assuming all of the tail spikes hit. With the Fiery Template, can do a whopping 12d6+20 damage if all four spikes land. Starts off strong, grows rapidly weaker since that +8 attack bonus makes it incompetent at actually hitting anything. Augment Summoning doesn’t even help since spikes use Dex.

Kodama (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Kami) - It has some plant-related spell like abilities and is decently tough, what with its grab-bag of immunities and DR. The real money of this monster, however, is its staggering gaze. As long as you’re mindful of your range and allies, this can give a group of monsters a really bad time.

Summon Nature’s Ally 6

Stone Giant - Does a lot of ranged damage with its rock-throwing. Augment Summonings gives you two attacks at 1d8+15. Also very good in melee.

Summon Nature’s Ally 7

Fire Giant - With three attacks per round and power attack with a two-handed weapon, they do huge amounts of melee damage per round and have a good chance to hit with all of their attacks.

Zuishin (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Kami) - No one dies tonight when this monster is summoned. As a swift-action, it can imbue a fired arrow by consuming one of its spell-like abilities of: breath of life (3 times a day!), cure light wounds, heal, neutralize poison, remove curse, or remove disease. It can also shoot up to 4 arrows a round and also adds the ‘holy’ weapon property with each arrow. If you can boost their rather mediocre attack roll, they roll over non-ranged enemies and any undead might as well go home.

Summon Nature’s Ally 9

Toshigami (Requires Ring of Natural Attunement - Kami) - Holy crap, look at that attack routine! Too bad about the rather pitiful damage, but since each hit forces a DC27 ‘save or lose from physical or mental stats’ it’s quite workable. It also makes very strong meat, too, with its relatively high AC, hit points, and DR. If that’s not enough, it also gets some killer spells, too: time stop, finger of death at DC 24, and True Seeing.
