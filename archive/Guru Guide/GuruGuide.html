Akashic Mysteries Guide 2[a][b][c]: The Guru

The Guru is the second class of Ssalarn’s excellent port of 3.5’s Incarnum subsystem. It is the more rogue-like and skilled class among the initial three. They use their Essence to power class features in a very unique way, by burning it and dealing themselves damage to their essence pool. They also gain access to the only fighting style of its kind in the game, the Gentle Touch.

Long story short on the function of Akasha, for those who don’t know (and who haven’t read my previous guide): You create effects called Veils, tied to certain slots on your body called Chakras. Only one of any given Veil, and only one at any given Chakra. You can charge them with bits of your soul, called Essence, to increase their power. As you grow in power, you learn how to step up their effects even more by increasing their capacity for Essence, gaining more Essence in your total pool, and by tying the Veils closely to their respective Chakras. This process is called Binding. The biggest advantage of it all is flexibility, you always have the ability to change a large chunk of your build overnight, and you can shift your bonuses around to meet whatever challenges you are facing with a Swift action to reallocate your Essence where it will be most beneficial.

Now that the introductions are over, here’s how to read the guide. I will, like everyone else, be using a color-based system to rate various aspects, features, feats, veils, etc. I’m a bit colorblind, though, so Treantmonk’s original doesn’t work so well for me. Here is what I’ll be using in this guide:

RED is a terrible option, and should be avoided in nearly every build. If it’s a class feature, expect to not use it.

ORANGE is generally bad, but might be useful to some builds.

YELLOW is medium-range. Something to take if you don’t have anything else to do. If it’s a class feature, then you will likely be using it whenever your main deal is offline for whatever reason.

BLUE is good, something most builds will want to take a look at and/or use frequently.

SKY BLUE is pure excellence, and/or should be taken by nearly everyone at first opportunity

The Guru Class:

The Guru, more so than the Vizier and Daevic, is defined much more by his class features than by his choice of Veils. Said Veils are more for backing his natural abilities up, rather than the other way around. All Gurus gain the Gentle Touch ability, which allows them to deal greatly amplified amounts of damage if they’re willing to let it be Subdual. Finally, their chassis lends itself very heavily to Scout and Skillmonkey roles (traditionally occupied by Rogues and Monks)

Chassis:

Hit Die: d8 hit die isn’t great, but it is enough to stand up to a couple of hits while the Guru goes about what they need to do. You will need to make sure to use your abilities to keep yourself going.

Skills: 6+Int is very good, and the highest of any of the Akashic classes. The Class Skills are likewise solid, lending themselves well to sentinels and sneaks alike. Major standouts are Perception, Stealth, Disable Device (more on this later), Acrobatics, and Diplomacy.

Proficiencies: Like the Vizier, the Guru gains proficiency with Simple Weapons and Light Armor. Gurus don’t gain proficiency with shields, and combined with Light Armor only and their preference for melee combat it can make them a bit squishy if you aren’t careful. They do, however, gain bonus proficiencies from their chosen Philosophy, some of which can be quite nice.

BAB: Gurus have  Medium Base Attack, equivalent to the classes they run alongside. This can be an issue when engaging in direct combat and qualifying for certain high-priority feats, but isn’t actively bad. Just remember that you have to lose four levels to get that last iterative attack.

Saves: Good Reflex and Will, poor Fortitude. Beware poisons and other potential SOS/SOD coming at your weak save, though thankfully Con is enough of a priority for you to bolster this a bit.

Stat Priorities:

As usual, this will change depending on Philosophy and build. Strength is a good stat, but can be dumped by Sineaters and Vayists who take certain feats or dips. Dex is critical for all Gurus, given your light armor and midrange hit die (not to mention Finesse builds will be common). Con is important for the same reasons, plus your poor Fortitude and desire to use Akashic feats. Int should be kept at least decent unless you’re making a combat brute (doable, don’t get me wrong). Wisdom is just as important as Dex, since it feeds your veil DCs and many of your class features (and adds to attacks via Gentle Touch). Charisma is dumpable, you don’t really use it and those few times you would are mostly covered by veils. So, for example:

Akasin Melee: STR=WIS>CON>DEX>INT>CHA

Vayist Rogue-like: WIS=DEX>CON=INT>STR>CHA

Class Abilities:

Veil Shaping: The Guru is decent at this, but it is not their main deal by a long shot. While their capacity increases just as high as a vizier, they don’t get anywhere near as many bind locations or Essence for their effects. You will be using these to supplement and round out your other abilities, not the other way around.

Gentle Touch: This awesome ability is one of the biggest defining features of the class, and something that only the Guru gets. With investment, you can massively increase your damage if you are willing to turn it all into Subdual. This adds dice of damage, and also your Wisdom mod, to all attacks from a Philosophy weapon. Think of it as a Sneak Attack for slightly less that’s WAY easier to set up (at level 20, Wis of +8 will even them up on average). This also makes the Guru absolutely unparallelled in his ability to bring his target in alive. The only drawback is that it only works on attacks from Philosophy weapons… but then again it is NOT limited to melee weapons. Watching a shuriken deal over 40 subdual is one of my more memorable playtesting moments. Also notable is that, like a Rogue, it affects every qualifying strike, so the more you are getting the better.

Stunning Fist: The Guru gains this, and gets uses like a Monk does. They can also channel these through their Philosophy weapons automatically. In addition, some Philosophy abilities use up Stunning Fist attempts to fuel themselves.

Philosophy: Three choices here, and they dictate almost literally everything about how you will be running your character. Each gives a set of proficiencies that define your Philosophy weapons, along with seven abilities plus an Essence recovery method for when you burn through your reserves. Remember that all paths can restore burnt Essence by meditation out of combat.

-Akasin: These are the more martial-oriented Gurus. Kind of like Paladins, actually, since they get a lot of Light and Healing abilities. Standout proficiencies include the Greatsword, Glaive, and Longbow. Their Essence recovery method is proactive, meditating in the light to gain a reserve of Temporary Essence to use. They ALSO gain temporary HP when they voluntarily burn Essence as well, making them surprisingly tough.

--Akasins learn how to Remove Blindness and Raise Dead, along with personal-range Daylight and eventually a move-action Teleport ability

--They also gain two potent offensive abilities, one that lets them effectively make their weapon become Brilliant Energy (starts at level 7) and another that lets them burn tons of Essence at once to deliver mighty single strikes.

-Sineater: These are hard to quantify, but are much more tanky and dynamic than the other paths on their resources. They tend to burn a LOT of Essence, but they restore it quickly by attacking their enemies. Standout proficiencies include the Spiked Chain and Unarmed Strike (and they get Improved Unarmed Strike for free).

--Sineaters gain a slew of defensive abilities, using Essence burn to soak HP damage, gain DR, Fast Healing, and Regeneration.

--They also gain a Grab attack, a Whirlwind attack, a Paralyze, and the ability to use Atonement.

-Vayist: Free-spirited and roguelike, these Gurus can force opponents to try to attack them alone or suffer penalties in order to let their allies roam free. Any time their designated enemies miss them, they regain Essence. Standout proficiencies include the Whip, Shuriken, and Scimitar.

--Vayists can use Blur, Mirror Image, Breath of Life, and end up gaining continuous Freedom of Movement.

--They can also stand from prone, enhance their ranged attacks, and gain a short-ranged teleport.

Chakra Disruption: For when Stunning just isn’t enough, all Gurus gain a wide range of debilitating status effects they can inflict on opponents they strike while using Gentle Touch. The DCs for these effects scale very quickly, the only drawback being that an attempted attack using one for these is a Standard action. They also have low durations, so be ready to rush in to capitalize on them. Do note, though, that you have a couple of abilities on your Move action (Seal Wounds and the Feet bind of the Stalker’s Tabi come to mind), so using one of these on the Standard becomes a good option.

-Hands: Reduce enemy attack rolls

-Feet: Reduce enemy land speed

-Shoulders: Reduce Dex and Shield bonuses to AC

-Headband: Blinded

-Head: Stunned. Really, if you wanted to do this in the first place, just use Stunning Fist. It won’t cost you iteratives. The only advantage here is a likely much higher save DC

-Body: Exhausted

Binds were covered under Veilweaving

Sunder Veil: Get used to using this… a lot. It lets you take out Undead, and also shred enemy buffs by Stunning them out of existence, which can become critical against any enemy that likes using them.

Improved Essence Capacity: Just like the Vizier, you gain a huge edge by being able to stack your Veil bonuses to the roof. Combined with your far greater skills, you can do some very impressive things on your skill checks.

Seal Wounds: Unfortunately locks Essence, or it would be very nice. This can provide significant healing on the Move action, but you don’t have so much Essence that you can afford to have that much of it locked away all day.

Crack the Shell: Sundering with extra oomph can come in handy, but the big benefit is being able to strike Constructs with your main combat ability

Sever the Flow: An on-call DISJUNCTION starting from level 16, and all you have to do is tag your opponent with a Gentle Touch attack. It leaves items alone, which your allies will thank you for.

Immortal Essence: Lackluster for a capstone, and you can miss it without really suffering. Healing and bonus essence are nice to have for free, tacked onto your abilities, but it’s a thin bonus.

Notes: The Guru is a vicious combatant, tough as nails in any of his incarnations with a very wide assortment of combat options baked into every path. Their massive skillset supported by Veil bonuses gives them the versatility to land low on T3.

Guru Veils:

Unlike the Vizier, your Veils support who you are rather than defining you completely. On the flip side, when properly applied, they can make you into a wide variety of threats. Whether in direct combat via DR and bonus health or by enhancing your Rogue imitation skills, you always have to take your role into account when choosing them. Unlike the Vizier, you can’t change out in the middle of the day, so if you’re caught without an appropriate Veil you’d best hope your class abilities (or teammates) can carry it.

If you read the Vizier guide first, some of these will look very familiar to you… but then again, some of the shared Veils between the two classes work out much differently on this chassis.

Hands Chakra (Lvl 2): You have a wide range of effects for your first Bind available, ranging from direct combat to distraction to skillmonkey roles.

Forcestrike Knuckles: We lead off with a solid combat veil for the Sineaters. This greatly increases your damage output and notably deals Force damage, allowing you to strike incorporeal foes from level 1. Binding gives you that missing Shield bonus to AC. Anyone going Unarmed or using natural attacks pretty much WILL shape this and keep it loaded.

Gloves of the Master Thief: If you wanted to be an Akashic Rogue, this is how. Significant skill bonuses to Disable and Sleight, and binding gives the all-important ability to disarm magical traps.

Hands of the Bard: Pity the winning streak couldn’t keep up. You have no native abilities that would require a Concentration check, and the Fascinate is difficult to maintain while you’re trying to be sneaky.

Immaculate Touch: You have better things to do in combat than heal, but the amount can be significant over time and out of combat. When bound you can pick up a Paladin’s Mercy. This can become critical.

Lashing Spinnerets: This is for mobility rather than straight offense, but boy can it move you. The base effect, with all of one Essence invested, can shift you 60 feet as a Move + Swift action. Works in all three dimensions as well. This is my slot default, assuming I don’t have a reason to use the others. Bind effect here won’t be all that useful immediately, but can provide a good place to rest by the time you’re able to put 3-4 essence into it. Has a Wrist bind, which I will cover there.

Loyal Paladin’s Spear of Light: This lets you generate a light weapon out of nothing, one that gets very solid amounts of damage and tons of reach over time. Also lets you use your Veilweaver level instead of BAB, which gets valuable over time. The Bind effect is a Line of Light, letting you get in on the areas of effect early. Good for ripping chunks out of formations or enemies in tunnels.

Plaguebringer Gauntlets: Immaculate Touch’s evil twin. Although the damage is only ever going to be mediocre, it can add up. The Cruelties on bind are where it’s at, though, there are some real winners here to add to the slew you already have access too, eventually including a Bestow Curse, Blind, Paralysis, or a multi-round Stun. Just remember that you need to meet the prerequisites for anything you take, so you can’t use this before level 3 and also will be unable to access the Exhausted, Frightened, or Nauseated effects (unless you have an Antipaladin dip for some reason).

Snakehandler’s Gauntlets: The Base and Essence effect is REALLY nice early on, but fade out fast. Aligned attacks are common once you start facing Outsiders regularly. Poison resistance is also nice. The Bind effect is great just as long as enemies aren’t immune, free poison should not be overlooked.

Feet Chakra (Lvl 5): Four options here, and unlike many slots all four can be very useful at times. This will mostly just depend on the role you have decided to fill for that day.

Coward’s Boots: Untyped bonuses to base movement speed is a valuable effect. I default to this Veil if my Feet are open and I have any extra Veils to be shaped. The Bind effect is just as good, since if you’re moving you’ll likely have the Veil stuffed with Essence for a really good AC bonus. Unlike the Vizier, you will have a hard time using this to qualify for things like Spring Attack.

Immovable Boots: Frontliners like the resistance to two of the more common combat maneuvers, but EVERYONE loves having a rare movement form when bound.

Lavawalker’s Boots: Another bit of alternate movement, this one over any liquid. The ability to pump Essence for Fire and Acid resist is a nice touch, in case your DM believes in physics while you attempt to walk on lava. The Bind effect is fun, though, just as long as you can find (or make) the terrain to take advantage of it.

Stalker’s Tabi: Another Skill booster, this one turns you the rest of the way into a solid skillmonkey. Acrobatics will be a common roll for you, especially in light armor trying to get around the battlefield. The bonuses from this will let you hit the CMDs of your opponents very reliably, even deep into the game. Stealth is also very nice to have when you really aren’t where you’re supposed to be (perhaps using the Gloves of the Master Thief to unlock something). Feet bind is CRAZY good as long as you aren’t out in the daylight, allowing you to get out of hairy situations or into locked rooms with ease, at will, and as a move action while retaining your ability to act effectively for the remainder of the turn. Has a Belt bind, which I will cover there.

Wrist Chakra (Can’t Bind): Just one Veil here, because you have it for the Hand slot. Well worth considering, though.

Lashing Spinnerets: Rather than putting together a place to sleep, this now generates a powerful and VERY large-area Web effect. Only usable a few times per day, but it can cover enormous amounts of real estate and limit lesser enemies to futile struggling while you take out bigger threats. Note that this is also great place to put the Veil if you don’t intend to bind it, since literally nothing else you have goes natively into the slot.

Head Chakra (Lvl 8):Only two options natively here, but both are good to have in their given situations.

Diadem of Pure Reflection: If your opponents like using these status effects, this can and will be a lifesaver. All three are on the SOS list, fight enders that you want to avoid succumbing to. Binding this to the Head gives you the ability to reflect Dazed, which can be a nasty shock. Also has a Headband slot, which I will cover later.

Mask of Elemental Adaptation: I may be slightly biased, due to recent frustrations with energy resistances, but I digress. This Veil allows you to convert incoming energy damage to a type of your choice, allowing you to heavily stack one resistance and have it affect everything. Many races have resist 5 to something natively, and this lets you utilize it against anything that has energy damage. Shape Veil for the Bralani’s Brooch off the Vizier list makes this MUCH nicer, on top of it all.

Shoulders (Can’t Bind): Only one Veil shapeable here, and it’s only because it can also form on the Neck where you have the native ability to Bind. If you feel the desperate need to Bind it here, you can take Access Middle Chakra Slot to open it up.

Courtesan’s Cloak: Whether or not you decide to Bind this, it is a very strong defensive veil. AC against ranged attacks is nice, but the save bonus goes to everything and stacks with common bonuses to them. Binding gives Evasion in this slot, though it has a Neck bind as well.

Headband (Lvl 11): Again, there are a wide variety of effects here. Not all will be frequently useful, but many will.

Circlet of Brass: It has to deal fire DAMAGE, which is unfortunate. On top of that, you really don’t do much that deals fire damage in the first place unless you bind this (which is decent, but not great for you).

Diadem of Pure Reflection: This one gets better in the Headband slot, since being able to reflect Blind and Stun is evil if your opponents are using them. If your DM likes throwing these status effects out, this goes blue.

Dreamcatcher: There are few better uses for downtime than this. Just remember the seven P’s (Proper Prior Planning Prevents Piss Poor Performance), and then remember that this can give upwards of +18 to Knowledge skills. Just note that it locks Essence for 24 hours, so if you get ambushed on an off day you could be short nearly half your essence. Unlike the Vizier, you don’t have a way to rapidly reclaim this. The Bind is gravy, letting you snoop on thoughts with a highly-boosted DC, and forcing those who save to keep on saving. Put it this way, taking 20 will take 6 minutes of conversation.

Eyes of the Hawkguard: Very niche, but when you can apply it this is very good. Note that the increase on ranged weapons is set number, not proportional, so that 10-ft boost that is barely noticeable to daylight vision or a Longbow increment is dramatic when applied to a limited-range Darkvision or Blindsight, or to the range increment of a thrown shuriken. The Headband bind is nice to have, though you should have other means to defeat invisibility by now. This has a mighty Body bind, which I will cover there.

Neck (Lvl 14): Three here with widely varying effects, but all can be useful once again depending on how you want to play things.

Collar of Skilled Instruction: This one is available to anyone, but your uses tend to be much more focused and roguelike. Giving as many allies as you can a +2 (or more with Teamwork or certain racials) to nearly any action can be good, especially since you can blanket the battlefield with the effect at need. Binding grants a Teamwork feat you qualify for and the ability to share it like a Seer, so if your team is all in on the same task it can get really nice.

Courtesan’s Cloak: We meet again. A solid Veil to begin with, getting Improved Evasion out of it as well is going to make you hard to pin down.

Metabolist’s Scarf: If you have a splash of psion, this can be very nice. Same if you know bleed damage is a possibility (Shape Soulmeld feat for a Bloody Shroud, perhaps). Adapt body, while nice to have, is also usually going to be niche here. The ability to share with your buddies will be appreciated if it comes up, though.

Belt (Lvl 17): These three are mostly defensive in nature, and until your class defenses come online this will be important.

Guardian Sash: The standard and Essence effect isn’t all that impressive unless you know your opponent likes to crit-fish. The Bind, however, is a nonaction triggered parry whenever you WOULD be taken down. This, depending on stats and investment, can be made with over a +30 to the die, which would make it remarkably difficult to punch out your last few HP.

Stalker’s Tabi: Distinctly lackluster compared to its awe-inspiring Feet bind, but useful nonetheless if you find the opportunity for it. You will find more use for this than the Viziers did, if only because you like the Stealth skill more, but this still isn’t the slot for it unless your Feet are occupied by something more important.

Stone Giant’s Girdle: This provides a good buffer of temporary hit points, not to be trifled with (especially in early levels, given that it regenerates relatively quickly).  This falls off fast, but is still a good default for this slot if you aren’t doing anything else. It’s still lots of health that you didn’t have before, and critical in the early game before all of your defenses come online. The Belt bind isn’t all that great for you, but it’s decent if you expect to face incoming thrown rocks a lot.

Body (Lvl 20): You only get access to these alongside your capstone, and the power shows.

Aerial Nimbus: This is all for the Shape effect, which is incredible from the moment you get it. Short-distance Air Walks greatly enhance your mobility and will let you get into and out of places both in and out of combat that you wouldn’t believe. The Bind is decent, allowing constant Air Walking and giving a potentially versatile minion to control (though not at the same time).

Eyes of the Hawkguard:  Arguably the strongest effect of any Bind in your arsenal, constant True Seeing is an incredible power to have. Don’t even bother investing Essence unless you need to ping an enemy hiding a half-mile away with a Longbow, you don’t need to.

Martyr’s Toga: You have much better ways to heal allies than this, the only time you’ll use it is if your Hands are taken or Immaculate Touch is used up. The Body bind is decent, especially when paired with the main ability, basically lets your group start every conflict at full health with no resources expended but time. Don’t count on it in battle, though, even at max it’s only 7-8 points of fast healing.

Stone Giant’s Girdle: If you are a Strength-based build, you probably want this at full capacity in the Body bind slot. Though the stat bonus won’t stack with other size bonuses if you have them, the reach still will.

Races:

Akashic: Three have been released with two sub-races each for a total of nine. These are all up a notch from what they otherwise would be for one simple reason: each gives a point of Essence as a racial feature, and there is no such thing as having too much Essence.

Gamla: Not so great on a Dex build, but an Unarmed Sineater build basically gets nothing but benefit. Dipping into Monk or Deadly Fist to support yourself plus large size and bonuses to two critical stats is not to be ignored… especially when you can arrange to ignore the race’s main penalty. Still good for Akasins as well, since the Glaive weapon plus large size is worth 20 feet of threat from level one. The Spit is also very useful to you in clutch situations.

-Feelkha: Directly worse than a base Gamla, this moves stats in the wrong direction and removes an emergency backup trick that you care actually good at.

-Alqarn: This variation makes for a very dangerous Akasin, especially of the Polearm variety. You really won’t miss the Wis with the strength bump, and the horn will cover your blind spot cleanly

Sobek: Losing Wis hurts less than you might think, the Strength bonus is good for Akasins, and the Bite is a useful backup for enemies who get inside of Polearm range. Speed loss hurts, but this option goes up in a huge way if your campaign is around water a lot (or if you do a lot of Stealth in one particular natural environment, I’ve seen modifiers go into the 60s without magic items used)

-Nameer: Boost to Dex and quick land speed, plus a hit to a dump stat, should have made for an awesome Vayist. The problem is that you won’t ever use the claws (since they aren’t on any Philosophy list), and the bonus to Cha goes nowhere on this chassis.

-Solhofaat: +4 Con is HUGE, and lets you access powerful feats for nearly no point-buy investment. The natural armor is nice as well, and can add up to +10 eventually. Losing Dex, though, hurts it for many builds. Still good for most applications, it just won’t often be your first choice

Suqur: The wings from level one are an awesome thing, as is low-light vision and the Dex and Perception bonuses, but losing Con and gaining a bonus to a stat you don’t use as much, plus the lowered ground speed, hurt this.

-Hibkha: This is all wrong for you. heavily invested in the wrong kind of bonus, losing important stats, with perks that shouldn’t be seeing use. All you like here are the wings and Essence, and there are better ways to do that.

-Nisir: HERE we go. Dex/Con bonus with a penalty to the dump stat. Trading Perception and Fly bonus away can hurt, but the Disease and Poison resistance is a good replacement, and more importantly can be invested in. Since those effects do their damage over time, you will be able to invest after the condition is inflicted and still benefit.

Core:

Human: Stat can go anywhere, the feat gets you into your style quick, and the skills help you out anywhere… what’s not to love?

Dwarf: As long as you’re not trying to be a Spring Attacker, these guys are surprisingly good. Wis and Con boosts with a dump stat penalty, and if you ever get heavier armor they aren’t slowed. Rock Stepper is a nice alternate trait as well, and Darkvision is awesome.

Elf:  Think of them as Suqur without the wings and you’re very close. Dex is nice, Int is meh, and losing Con just sucks. The weapon proficiencies won’t help, since you want to be using Philosophy weapons.

Gnome: Eh… penalty to Strength hurts, especially combined with Small size. Con bonus is nice, though Cha isn’t really useful to you. Basically, anything these guys can do, Halflings will do better.

Half-Elf: Really can’t complain here, especially if you’re dipping something else to top off your Guru. Floating stat point plus Elven immunities, and the ability to select two favored classes is clutch.

Half-Orc: Floating stat bonus, darkvision, and Ferocity is good. Unfortunately, any good alternate trait usually replaces Ferocity. A decent choice, but one that moves up noticeably if you want to take certain Teamwork choices.

Halfling: For those who really just wanted to be Akashic Rogues, the Small size doesn’t hurt you as bad here. Charisma still doesn’t help, and losing Strength is no fun unless you have Deadly Agility or similar somewhere, but otherwise these guys are gold.

Psionic: If you have access to Akashic Mysteries, then Psionics are probably on the table as well. These guys have a couple of edges to them, notably the ability to take Psionic feats and other Psionic effects without requiring a dip. Several also have good stats and abilities for you as well.

Blue: Rating is slightly comedic, all things considered. Small size plus Dex/Int and losing from your dump stat are solid benefits… if and only if you are going for a roguelike skill-build. Bonuses to Perception and Stealth augment this, as do Small size and Darkvision. These guys are no good for Akasins, so don’t even think it.

Dromite: Not quite as good as a Blue, and in the same roles. The stat mix isn’t so hot for physical skillmonkeys, and Gurus are mediocre at social style.

Duergar: Think of these guys as Dwarves, which have no alternate racials and light sensitivity in exchange for a couple of Psi-like abilities and natural psionics. This can work, just be careful to have some way to protect your eyes in most settings.

Elan: As a general rule, you aren’t using your Swift much after the first round or two of combat. If you plan on gaining even a few more PP (for example, dipping PsiWar or Gifted Blade Soulknife), Elan are walking around with permanent Immediate-action counters and go Sky-Blue. Floating stats and Aberrant Blood are icing on the cake (the latter mostly for Bloodforge feats)

Forgeborn: Definitely decent. Half-construct resistances along with the boost to strength are nice for Akasins, though Int isn’t usually their thing.

Half-Giant: Powerful Build is valuable to people who want to be weapon-based for damage, and the stat mix also greatly supports the Akasin role (though it’s also good for the Unarmed and Dangerous setup, below).

Maenad: Floating stat is good, but everything else is mediocre. Outburst will usually be only a marginal help at best, since it drops Wis. Energy Ray will be rarely used, and the other abilities aren’t going to pop up too often for you.

Noral: Losing Dex hurts, though gaining Wis/Con is very nice. The main reason to pick these guys is to basically have a hero point to spend each day, which helps offset things (slightly). Your DC for Mind Thrust is going to be too low to matter.

Ophiduan: Excellent stats, natural armor, and darkvision make these guys a good pick. The bite won’t matter too often due to not being a Philosophy, but can be nice to have.

Xeph: Nothing horrible, the Dex and speed bonuses are nice, but really just mediocre overall.

Bloodforge: The Bloodforge book, also by DSP, presents a large number of additional races to use. The power levels here tend to be high, so make sure to clear it with your DM first. I’ll present five good picks here, but there are lots more possibilities in the book itself.

Half-Gnoll: Stats are generally good for any Philosophy. You won’t use the Claws except in an emergency, but literally everything else on the list is a nifty bonus and good to have. In particular, stealthy teams with a lot of Sneak Attack (or really, any team with lots of melee) will absolutely adore Pack Hunter. Hounding Advance is really nice on the alternates as well, though you have to give up Pack Hunter to get it.

Kijin: Although the stats are a hair off, Sineaters love the accelerated restoration of Essence burn, which will allow much more mileage out of your slew of defensive abilities. Sin Soaked for Heart of the Wight, plus Invisibility, make for awesome Alternate Racials, as well.

Lasher: Losing Dex hurts… unless you go for heavier armor. Akasins like the stat mix, and extending your reach by an eventual 25 feet (!) makes you incredible at area control with a Polearm. Grab the Bite alternate racial to cover the gap, and remember that Aberrant subtype opens up a couple of very nice feats

Watcher: The stat arrangement is good for Gurus of any Philosophy, but that’s just the start of it. Natural armor helps you make up for your lack of heavier armor, Sheltering Wings helps protect allies with you more able to take hits or avoid damage, plus several other nifty tricks. Just avoid the Claws racial, they don’t fall under a Philosophy.

Woodborn: Stats are perfect for an Akasin, and it just gets better from there. Natural armor plus resistance to death effects and negative energy help out. Low-Light and Darkvision are good to have, plus Keen Senses as well. Destructive alternate trait plus Crack the Shell lets you do terrible things to any object in your way. Finally, the Guru alternate favored class bonus means you’ll be ripping buffs off of enemies like crazy.

Other:

Vanara: There really isn’t a word for just how happy I was to figure out that I could make my superior Skillmonkey an actual monkey. Vanara are particularly well-suited to the Rogueish roles of the Guru class, with perfect stat placement, a Climb speed, prehensile tail, and bonuses to Stealth and Acrobatics.

Tiefling: There really is a Tiefling for everything. In your case, taking Fiendish Heritage to be the spawn of an Asura, Demodand, Devil, Oni, or Qlippoth (especially Demodand or Qlippoth) is up your lane. Many of the random modifiers are awesome for you as well if they are in play.

Aasimar: Probably surprising nobody, Aasimars make powerful Gurus. In particular, Archon and Garuda-blooded have awesome stat arrangements. If your build is going to have open feat slots, then they have several exclusives that will save you Veil slots.

Feats:

Let’s face it, there is a LOT to go through here. Though I may eventually do something more comprehensive, the only section that I’m going to cover 100% is the new Akashic selection. Anything else, I’m only going to cover if it’ll be useful to a primary build, on request, or if it is a notable one to avoid.

Akashic Feats: These will be important for you, including a couple that are absolute must-takes (one of which is a must-take as many times as you can free space for).

Access <level> Chakra Slot: You miss a couple of Chakra Binds while levelling, unlike the Vizier. Two of these, the Wrists and Shoulders, can be accessed by the Middle version of this feat. You don’t have much natively to put there, but more can be acquired in various ways if you’re willing to invest in it (Wrist leads to the highest DPR build of the Guru)

Akasha-Imbued Body: You can pick up around 22 bonus HP with this if you’re determined, which isn’t bad, but there are better ways to do this (and we’ll see it later).

Akasha-Infused Spells: Gurus have no way to use this natively, and if a caster is dipping Akasha they are more likely to go with the Vizier

Chakra <Save> Series: These will never be your first choice, but they are excellent ways to fill empty slots. Each gives a point of Essence (always in demand), and can always be filled up fast if you see a particular save is going to be needed (upwards of +7 native capacity for you can turn a near-impossible save into a fighting chance)

Chakra Power: Adds a LOT of pain to what you can dish out. In most cases this will be a very early pick for you.

Chakra Targeting: You don’t have anywhere near enough ranged capability in most cases, but if that’s going to be your thing this goes sky-blue

Daevic Rage: If you multiclass something with Rage, then this is really good… especially combined with a Vicious weapon. Otherwise, obviously, you won’t be taking it

Divine Essence: Again only for multiclassers, but if you have access to a Channel pool this lets you bust the limits wide open for your abilities.

ENHANCED CAPACITY: I cannot make this any bluer, since ultraviolet isn’t on my list of colors. I’m serious, if you don’t have this feat at least once I’m sure there is a place you can get it in somewhere. Going to an 8-essence investment in combination with your class abilities is crazy good on most veils (not to mention your Gentle Touch).

Essence Expertise: A niche combat style, but if you take the base version, no reason to not upgrade with this. There will usually be better uses for your slots

Essence Focus: If you have Psionic flavor and the ability to use your Focus in more ways than one, this is a critical feat to take. Again, it’s going to be niche, but this one is easier to access and activate than most (and doesn’t actually require multiclassing in some cases). If you dip PsyWar, this is awesome

Essence of Movement: Beats the crap out of Mobility if you’re going that way anyway (for example, making a Spring Attacker), but you’ll want to avoid it otherwise. If your DM lets you use Veils to qualify for prereqs, then avoid this feat since it’s available from the Foot bind of Coward’s Boots.

Essence of Recovery: Most notable use is for a Paladin who splashes Akasha, this requires some building around but can put your recovery abilities distinctly above what many enemies are ready to compensate for.

Essence Rejection: Do I really need to comment here? I don’t have a darker Red shade to use.

Essence of the Immortal: Con 15 is very easy for you to get, given how much you like melee. Add in the absurd amount of health it can grant (+40 base, I’ve seen over 50 in standard builds and +80 on some Akasins) and you have one of the strongest feats in the game. NOTE: This is being reviewed for balance reasons, and may be changed in the future

Extra Essence: If you are an Akashic class and you do not take this, YOU ARE WRONG. Two extra points of essence is clutch frequently, especially in the early game. You WILL take this by level 7 at the latest.

Focused Critical: For the crit-fishers out there, this is a low-priority but nice investment

Life Bond: You have two native veils that do this better. Pass

Pistolero’s Bind: No Philosophy includes a gun-kata anywhere.

Shape Veil: This isn’t one you should have to take, but it can let you complete some builds or bridge gaps. Armory of the Conqueror and Storm Gauntlets can be good to have, for example.

Shared Veil: While strong, this is very difficult for you to properly utilize without heavy further investment. If you have a Druid splash, for example, or take Eldritch Heritage (Arcane). Same rules from the Vizier apply, otherwise, with the added bonus of being able to share things like the Gloves of the Master Thief for fun times.

Tainted Chakra: The Evil Veils include some big winners… none of which you have native access to. If you plan on taking Shape Veil for Heart of the Wight, for example, this can be good. Bit of a stretch, though

Treewalker: I’m probably rating this a hair high due to fondness for the effect (and some hilarious moments in playtest), but if trees and lichens are common in your typical environments then the movement options this can provide can be useful. Drops off once you have a decent fly speed, which is unfortunate.

Twin Veil: Can be good depending on how you want to build, but you don’t rely on your veils nearly as much as others and so you are less likely to feel the burning need to double up a slot

Veilbound Metamagic: Hugely powerful for those able to use it, but those are not likely to pick Guru as their path to success. Possibly a Caster Cleric who dips Guru.

Veilbound Resilience: Scales exponentially, up to a maximum of 240 points of healing per day (320 if you put an Expanded Capacity on it). The problem is that it locks essence for the day, which is something to avoid unless it’s oozing out of your pores with nowhere to go. If that is the case (and you already have Essence of the Immortal), then this starts becoming decent.

Veiled in Purity: There are a couple of Good veils which are nice to have, plus being able to hide your alignment if Evil can become critical in a generally-good society. Situational, and only useful if Evil, but something to consider.

Veil-Touched Nemesis: You have a hard time activating this without a significant dip, and even if you do you’ll have a hard time triggering it more than 1-2 times per turn.

Veil-Woven Channel: This is more for Clerics who like healing to use as a means to bolster allies, but if you gain a Channel pool this becomes a great way to spend a supporting action. Healing plus dropping a significant bonus to whatever your team happens to need is far better than healing alone.

View Energy: Oh, if only the Sight lasted longer. This is a decent way to break through most forms of sneaking, but requiring a Move action every round to keep using is going to murder your action economy.

Wildclaw Chakra: A solid investment for Druids splashing into Akasha, this is unfortunately not usable by you without a significant dip.

Teamwork Feats: You utilize these through the Collar of Skilled Instruction, not through a constant effect like the Vizier. This will usually limit you to ones that don’t have Teamwork prerequisites. You also have a MUCH wider variety of potential threats you can become due to the way the class works. I’ll put up anything the class can possibly qualify for, as long as it doesn’t have the aforementioned prereqs including other Teamwork feats. Also leaving off Betrayal feats again, spreading them doesn’t do anything.

Amplified Rage: If you have multiple ragers in the group, you might want to make a half-orc for access to this. Otherwise, don’t bother. You don’t want it.

Back to Back: Could be worse, this eliminates flanking advantage from opponents who get it. If you often find yourself or your buddies in this kind of trouble, it can be worthwhile.

Broken Wing Gambit (and Wounded Paw Gambit): Broken Wing requires striking in melee, gives enemies a bonus to hit you, and grants a melee AoO. Usually not going to be a good choice, but if you have a heavily melee party then sharing it might be viable. Upgrading to Wounded Paw, however, means EVERYBODY is sharing in on the action if one of your buddies triggers it. Wounded Paw is good enough that it may be worth taking Broken Wing to qualify for regardless.

Brutal Grappler: One team setup that came up in discussion included this plus the Spiked Pauldrons, teaming up with a Tetori Monk or Shapeshift Grapple Druid for massive damage. Then we all realized that the Pauldrons give no bonus to CMB, just CMD, and it fell through. Just skip this, Grappler is not a role that multiple teammates take often.

Cavalry Formation: Again, it’s team-dependent, but the benefits here are intense if your buddies like mounting up frequently.

Circling Offense: A solid set of benefits, but you usually don’t want to be deliberately provoking attacks of opportunity. Tons of ways to avoid them, rather than risking them actually connecting with a primary attack.

Combat Medic: I really wanted to like this one, but let’s face it. By level 5, SOMEONE in your group probably has a means of delivering small amounts of in-combat healing, which negates the need for most of it. If you are in a particularly magic-restrictive campaign and can’t get any such thing (which would explain why Heal is at 5 ranks), this gets a bit better.

Coordinated Defense: Nice to have, I suppose, but very dependent on having enemies that both like combat maneuvers and aren’t overwhelmingly good at them.

Coordinated Maneuvers: Slightly better than Defense, this will let you stand behind an ally and give him a leg up when he tries to trip or grapple an enemy.

Coordinated Reposition: Polearm Akasins, particularly Large characters, can threaten immense swaths of ground. This will let you take frequent advantage of allied steps.

Coordinated Shot: Long way of saying that you get nice bonuses by having an ally in melee. You’ll usually be that guy in melee, but there are a few ranged weapons on the Philosophy lists that will make this nice on both sides of the coin.

Covering Fire: Very long way of saying “Make a standard-action shot to give your allies +2 AC against the target, including up to one without this feat”. Not a bad thing to do if you move and shoot, actually.

Darting Retrieval: Very niche, though if your guy in the thick of things likes to Disarm this gets evil, quick.

Disarm Partner (and Improved): You are banking on someone who wants to make Disarm checks failing to do so, then you succeeding on multiple rolls. Big pass on this.

Distracting Charge: Generally good for any group, most Melee like to charge on the opener and the bonus stacks with everything else when the target survives it.

Duck and Cover: Reflex saves are the least dangerous kind (and yours will likely be sky-high), but there is no reason to take more damage than you need to. If you are grouped together and able to use the feat, it will very likely come in handy.

Enfilading Fire: The most common team arrangements tend to be two melee and two ranged. This lets those melee give their flanking bonuses to their ranged buddies, which can come as a nasty shock to the poor victim. Actually stacks with Coordinated Shot, above, and this can make things get a bit silly at times

Ensemble: Very, very niche. Even bards aren’t going to need this much assistance too often. Very flavorful if you’ve got a rock band going, but that’s about it (Barbarians wrecking face with a Mighty Axe gets much funnier, though).

Escape Route: Probably the best default you can get, this plus properly delayed actions and battlefield movement will deny tons of attacks from enemy teams.

Extend the Bulwark: A bit of an odd duck. Giving up your armor’s bonus to AC to give an ally half that for a round can be clutch, but it’s situational. If you have multiple heavy-armor types, they can alternate the person up front, and interestingly enough this Circumstance bonus applies to Touch.

Feint Partner (and Improved): Being able to give this to the entire group means that one guy making one skill check can take huge chunks off of an opponent’s AC for your entire team’s turn. If the group is heavily melee, then Improved will break the action economy in half. If you have multiple members rocking Sneak Attack dice, then this becomes effectively a SOD for your enemy. The latter is good enough to make taking the former on a regular feat an option.

Fighting Frenzy: Requires the Rage class feature to learn, which is a wasted level for you. Pass

Focusing Blow: Hobgoblin-only, has a prereq that’s only good in Hobgoblin-heavy parties, and requires allies to deal damage to each other... but breaking Enchantments can potentially turn a hideous Domination into a mere delay if you are willing to deal with it.

Friendly Fire Maneuvers: Basically says that you can ignore the fact that allies are anywhere near enemies, but this usually isn’t needed with smart play. Also good if you have a relatively indiscriminate blaster on the team, +4 against allied Reflex saves can be nice to have.

Harder They Fall: Good benefit if you can afford to burn a turn, assuming you have an ally who likes those combat maneuvers

Horde Charge: There are a LOT of potential bonuses to the Charge in this section, and they all stack up together quickly. If you are already on the right race (or Bloodline), this one can turn your group charging into battle that much more lethal.

Intercept Charge: This will be invaluable for allowing your tanks to do their job, but even the squishier members of your group can act to save the tail of one of your buddies if they are about to take a potentially-lethal hit. Note that you can use this to stop the opponent WAY short of the target, which can let them get some needed movement free.

Lookout: Acting in the surprise round is not to be underestimated for someone who can rearrange his bonuses to suit the situation as a Swift. If everyone has decent Perception, this actually goes Sky Blue for allowing full actions in the surprise round.

Outflank: Goes up noticeably in value when you have a bunch of guys on your team that like getting up close and personal.

Overwhelm: Good effect, but difficult to reliably trigger. Fact is, your Melee guys will often want to be upped in size, which will disable this.

Pack Attack: Might cost 5-ft steps, but this allows you to permeate enemy defensive lines without provoking and it is all in addition to regular movement.

Paired Opportunist: This is one of those feats that looks a lot better than it is. The problem is that you both have to threaten the target, and most opponents who are in that kind of situation aren’t going to be doing any provoking if they can possibly avoid it. barely squeaked into Yellow due to the possibility of builds meant to force attacks of opportunity.

Phalanx Fighter: Goes up a half-notch if you’re crusading against the forces of Evil, you can nearly always arrange for a +1 to +3… but requires you to bunch up.

Precise Strike: Much like Outflank, this feat goes up in value if your team likes to get up close and personal.

Seize the Moment: Crit-dependent, but if you are friendly with a crit-fisher or two this can get sick quickly.

Shake it Off: I wanted to rate this higher, I really did, but this is very dependent on your team all wanting to be in the same general area. If you regularly form up a square (and you aren’t afraid of AoE attacks from enemies), this is sick. Take this if your group clumps a lot.

Shield Wall: This would be a lot better if it didn’t require BOTH partners to be using a shield. You will not often be able to benefit from this, nor provide allies with benefits. If you do (and also partner regularly with another shield-user), it gets better

Shielded Caster: You don’t usually use shields.

Snapping Flank: Good feat for extra attacks, as long as the party has a bunch of Bites. Unfortunately situational.

Spirit of the Corps: If you have one buffer in the party (or really, anyone who can generate Morale bonuses), suddenly EVERYONE gets to benefit at least in part. The invocation is a nonaction, and not limited to your turn (so you can borrow a Barbarian’s Will bonus from rage, for example).

Stealth Synergy: Becomes bluer than the bluest blue that ever blued if you are in a stealth-heavy campaign, since if your team is all rolling checks and everyone takes the highest, there is virtually no chance of failure outside of overwhelming odds. Doubly true for you, given how high you can boost your Stealth to begin with.

Stone Dodger: Really niche applications, but if you fight Giants and/or deal with Sieges a lot, this will be a lifesaver. Feast or famine here, it all depends on the campaign.

Suppress Regeneration: Again, depends on how much it comes up. Turning off Regeneration for potentially 3-4 turns is a game-ender for most creatures who normally rely on it, the trick is triggering it when you can capitalize.

Swap Places: More methods to get you and your allies repositioned around the fight outside of standard moves, ignoring AoOs. Although most of these feats are good, you won’t need more than one or two if your group knows how to move normally.

Swing About: You get the point by now. Another good movement option, but don’t waste all your slots on that.

Tandem Evasion: Only useful against Dragons, and only against their Breath and Tail Sweep. While they tend to be serious threats, the big things to watch out for are not those unless you’re fighting a brood of them. You have better ways to resist energy, and the Tail Sweep is limited in damage.

Tandem Trip: The benefit here is crazy good if you like tripping and/or have a buddy who does.

Target of Opportunity: As long as you have another ally who doesn’t like to be in the thick of things, this busts the action economy wide open. When we are talking about Gentle Touch empowered attacks, these bonus shots HURT. Any optimized ranged attacker will tell you that the key to true damage is extra attacks… and this gives everyone an additional one per round, effectively.

Team Pickpocketing: Cool and flavorful, check. Effective? Not so check. This is highly niche  and can’t really get much of anything, so unless you are in a mission where this pops up a lot then avoid this.

Topple Foe: As long as the enemy is both bigger than you and ground-bound, and either you or someone else in the group likes to Trip, this can be decent.

Tribe Mentality: The bonus is massive, since your high-Will guys can share with their lower save buddies. The problem is that it’s a narrow subset of spells that have to ALSO hit multiple allies simultaneously. Those situations are very rare.

Underhanded Teamwork: Dirty Trick is an unusual specialty, but if your buddy manages to get a Blind off and you extend it by a bit, the game just ended in your favor.

Volley Fire: Doesn’t benefit the team as much, unless you are heavily ranged. In that case, allies will LOVE generating +3 and +4 to their ranged attacks.

Wall of Flesh: This is a very good feat with a sharp limit, that is that you have to be Small to take it, and most teams don’t have more than one of the Little Folk. You know what? It can be just YOU now, and your bigger teammates don’t need to meet the prereqs when you share it. Now, they will be BIGGER than they were, which has nice effects on things from your point of view.

Bloodforge Feats: These feats offer quite a few benefits that you can’t really pick up normally via feats. Several of these provide effects that Gurus are missing, or that may save you from needing to shape certain veils. I am only skipping four feats here. Bloodsong Adept and Blood Affinity you don’t qualify for, and Kinslayer and Resist Kindred are so campaign-dependent that I can’t effectively rate them here.

Mixed Blood: Starting this a bit out of alphabetical order, since this is the “additional feat” I was talking about. Several subtypes give very potent bonuses, such as Low Light or Darkvision, Scent, and resistances. Some give skill bonuses and movement modes, which can help. Others (such as the Alignment and Dwarf subtypes) should be avoided at all costs unless they are absolutely required in order to acquire something you need later. I will be factoring the value of your required Mixed Blood into the value of the feats that require them.

Vestigial/Aerial Wings: While there are other ways to get this instead of sinking 2-3 feats into it (most commonly Wings of Flying), Flight with good speed and Good maneuverability, plus Fly as a class skill, is not to be discounted. The subtypes accessing this include several good ones, so you aren’t wasting the Mixed Blood to get in

Altered Life/Unnatural Resistance: You need a good Con score to begin with, and none of your Mentals are really a higher priority than that. Pass

Ancestor’s Breath: You do not natively possess the Gorget of the Wyrm or any of the other myriad multitarget attack Veils, and have a relatively tough time doing the street-sweeper thing. Sonic or Negative Energy as damage types are usually going to be the best picks, due to being resisted less often.

Blood Frenzy: You are going to have a very hard time triggering this yourself, due to your preference for Subdual against most enemies. Perhaps if you are in a heavily Undead or Construct-themed campaign, since you deal Lethal to them once you acquire the right abilities.

Champion’s Presence: Very rough subtypes to enter with, and the benefit is restricted to those who are sticking to your particular alignment. Broad but minor bonus as well, in a common bonus type that will often be overwritten. Usually going to be a trap, may see some use if your group is both devoted to a given alignment and also lacking a Bard or other similar buffer.

Feral Fighter: Easy subtype entry, but remember that the natural weapons are not going to be a part of your Philosophy.

Sanguine Sorcery Line: There are mostly nifty tricks for you, just remember that Charisma is the save-stat for them and you normally dump that one. Stick to self-buffs and ones that don’t allow saves, like Magic Missile/Mage Armor, Haste/Invisibility, and Dimension Door

Spark of Divinity Line: Same save concern as Sanguine, but these have a lot more of the critical self-buffs you were missing. Divine Power is the big capstone here if you can get to it. Outsider type is VERY easy to get without needing feats, if you’re willing to look for races, but the skill bonus from Mixed Blood for it is decent.

Hold Prey/Instinctive Grapple: It is unclear whether the damage from Constrict would apply as being part of a Sineater’s Unarmed Philosophy, but if your DM lets that slide then these get a lot better. Without that, though, you have better things to do while grappling.

Mighty Frame: If raw damage or Combat Maneuvers are your game and you don’t already have Powerful Build, you have no reason whatsoever to pass this one up. Easy to get into the subtypes, and if you need a feat to do it then several also give good bonuses from Mixed Blood

Overflowing Life: Never rely on this in-combat, but having the ability to start things off at full health reliably throughout even the worst adventuring day is important (plus the ability to spread the love via Martyr’s Toga).

Scrambler’s Gift: Climb speed is generally not going to be worth it when you can just as easily acquire Fly with Good or better maneuverability. Avoid this even if you come by the prerequisites without having to go out of your way (and ranks beyond 1-2 in Climb are usually out of your way in most campaigns)

Small Stature: Decent if Stealthy is your goal, but being able to use downsized weapons without penalty is rarely going to be a big benefit (other than, again, being easier to hide). Flip side, Philosophy is Philosophy, so a Tiny dagger gets the same bonus dice as a Huge one.

Woeful Stench: VERY easy entry, and Sickened is a fight-ender… but this does not exclude allies and targets the save that is traditionally highest on your opponents.

Other Feats: There are hundreds of feats out there, but most conventional wisdom stays true. They don’t usually need to get reviewed one by one, you’ve probably seen it all by now. Some highlights:

Power Attack, Piranha Strike, and Deadly Aim: Same thing for different weapons, but very nice to have. If you expect to be able to use it, grab one and it’s Akashic sequel.

Point Blank Shot (series): If you’re into ranged combat, take these and don’t look back. Get, at the very least, PBS and Precise Shot. Rapid Shot is optimal for damage if that’s what you’re going for, but going for Shot on the Run can be fun.

Armor Proficiency (Medium or Heavy): Something to look at if you are a Dwarf, since it won’t slow you down any further. If you plan on going heavy into melee, this can be very good. Then again, if you’re going after Heavy Armor, might as well dip Fighter (especially AkWar) one level, get the proficiencies, and a bonus feat with it.

Improved Initiative: A classic standby, and still very nice to have. You getting a Swift action available before your opponents means that you can set yourself for the attack.

Combat Reflexes: Take a good hard look at this one. Your Dex is usually going to be high, and a few of your builds can threaten massive areas.

Two-Weapon Fighting (Series): Much of your damage comes as bonuses per-strike, and just like a rogue the more qualifying strikes you make the better. Akasins have the Two-Bladed Sword. Sineaters have the Bo Staff, Tonfa, and Dan Bong. Vayists have the 9-section whip in the main hand, and Nunchaku for either (though dual-wielding Nunchaku is an awesome image)

Eldritch Heritage: If you have the 13 Charisma and three spare feat slots (two for Half-Elves), grab this for Arcane to get a familiar of your choice and Share Veil. Standouts include the ones that can speak, along with the Dodo (+4 untyped bonus to Initiative) and the monkey (which has all Chakra slots to share).

Enforcer: If you trained up Intimidate, then the Gentle Touch style will ensure that your Shaken status will last for a good long while off of this. Worth a look.

Reckless Offense: For melee attacks, you usually don’t care about your AC due to your enemy going down very quickly. This can help offset Power Attack, TWF, and similar penalties

Equipment: I am not even going to remotely try to go through every possible enchantment, wondrous item, and other mystical gizmo that may or may not help you in your quest. That would quite frankly be futile, since every character is different and every campaign even more so. Instead, I am going to give some general guidelines and point out some major stand-outs to save you some searching. If you know of any item that is undeniably awesome for the class that I missed, please tell me and I’ll look it up for inclusion here.

Generic items: +stat for the ones you care about, +save items, plus movement and vision modes you don’t have, are always going to be important.

Specific Items: This will heavily depend on your chosen role. Warrior’s Girdle out of Bloodforge helps both your melee attack and Acrobatics, which is good for just about any Guru.

Armor Enchants: Fortification is going to be important, but you don’t have a shield to use for extra slots (unlike the Vizier).

Weapon Enchants: Merciful is effectively a free +1d6 at all times, unless you’re fighting Undead or Constructs. You like dealing Subdual anyway. Guided is crazy for Gurus as well, allowing you to dump Strength entirely without needing to dip levels.

Builds:

<WIP>

“I Can’t Believe It’s Not Paladin!”: Strength build. Akasins with polearms, particularly Gamla and their subraces, threaten a huge area and can deliver punishing amounts of damage. Power Attack and Chakra Power help scale this rapidly, and your Philosophy abilities will let you take more punishment than you’d think. Add on things like Immaculate Touch and Martyr’s Toga for healing as well. Consider Shape Veil for Armory of the Conqueror to greatly increase damage, and a feat used on armor proficiency may help as well. One level dipped in the Akashic Warrior Fighter archetype can also be awesome for this, granting Heavy armor and a bonus feat. You can also dip four there, but you lose some of your high-end abilities.

Unarmed and Dangerous (Sineater/Deadly Fist): This build is EXTREMELY heavily Wis-focused, dumping Strength completely by using class abilities to replace the mods with Wis (or, if your DM doesn’t Dispel or Sunder often, the Guided weapon property). Just make sure you have a convenient way to carry gear, Strength 7 hurts your capacity. Comes online at a minimum level of three (Sineater 1/Deadly Fist 2) in order to get the Focused Offense bladeskill. You will most likely want to go to level 4 on Deadly Fist in order to grab a second Skill and the fourth iterative attack at level 20

Akashic Rogue: The Dex build. This one makes specific use of the Dangerously Curious trait for UMD, then goes all-out on the sneaking and lockpicking. Weapon Finesse and Deadly Agility let you engage in combat, while having Stealth, Acrobatics, Disable Device, and Sleight of Hand breaking 15-17 total bonus by level 4 will nearly always get you where you need to go. Vayists often go Dervish Dance with this build as well. but can also TWF with Nunchaku (and given that your rogue side requires almost no feat investment, you can take that to its logical conclusion)

Two-Weapon Fighting: This can go by any Philosophy, and ends up as the highest potential Damage Per Round of any Guru build if you are going for it. Just pick which weapons you want, slap Guided on them if available, and take Shape Veil for Storm gauntlets and Open Wrist Chakra to bind them. Piranha Strike and Chakra Power, plus stuffing all applicable receptacles with Essence, makes each strike deal damage that sounds like an oncoming train when rolled. You then attack seven times per round once Haste is applied. Deliquescent Gloves and Fiery just add more dice. All remaining feats should be dedicated to accuracy (Reckless Offense is a good example) or to anything that gives more Essence, since when you go for the kill you’ll never have enough (Touch, Gauntlets, and Chakra Power alone add up to 21 essence, not counting assorted other Veils or accounting for Burn)
