The Spell Sage Cometh        

Introduction

In homage to Treatmonk’s Guide to the Pathfinder Wizard: Being a God do I honor the great legacy it started. How else should a wizard extend the craft but by acknowledging its legacy? I won’t go into as much mechanic detail as there are wizard guides out there that show you to control, how to blast, and everything in-between. Instead, this guide will offer roleplay tips and some synergies that work well with the archetype. To preface, I find this to be the singularly most potent archetype that you can play from level 1 through 20. Access to the bard, cleric, and druid spell lists on top of the wizard spell list literally gives you control over people, the divine, nature, and the underpinnings of reality from the beginning. It is your foundation instead of your goal. You have more freedom than mystic theurges in that you can devote 100% of your build resources being a wizardy wizard and practically still do what they do. Access to other spell lists is layers upon layers of icing on that cake. It does come at a cost, and I will show you how to mitigate that cost to great effect. I would almost advocate selecting this archetype by default for all Wizard characters and only keeping the core wizard schools if you really want their particular effects. It’s practically a lateral shift that puts you right next to being mythic except we got here purely by our own efforts. We have forged our destiny instead of it being inherited or given to us. Thus, if Treatmonk’s guide showed us how to be a god then this is the archetype that lets you ascend and do what the vanilla wizard would do anything to emulate it. It is good this archetype is tucked away for only the worthy should seize upon its promise. I also call this archetype Nethys’ Favored for one reason alone: how pleased must the All-Seeing Eye be when your mastery over the Gift is so great that you even temper other traditions. Let’s get started…

The Spell Sage Awakens[1]

A spell sage has mastered spells of all types, and is able to increase the effectiveness of his own spells and eventually even cast spells from other classes' spell lists. While most wizards focus on a school, the spell sage looks at the particular aspects of individual spells and is not afraid to modify them with interesting bits of insight to gain deeper understandings of the formulae.

Focused Spells (Su): At 1st level, once per day the spell sage's understanding of spells allows him to increase his caster level by 4 for a single spell cast. He can do this twice per day at 8th level, and three times per day at 16th level. This ability replaces arcane bond.

To be upfront, the sacrifice of the arcane bond can suck. However, this ability basically subsumes a trait, an ioun stone, and a couple feats all into one so you can focus elsewhere. This ability alone can save you a lot of juggling so you can focus on your true calling, apotheosis. It gives +4 CL and all the glory that entails. You need to be smart with it, though, as the tradeoff of those build resources is that, as you can see, it’s a limited per day use ability. That’s okay, though, as it lets you build out. With this archetype there is one overriding theme: mastery of all spells and by extension a mastery of magic itself. Trying to focus too much on one thing actually loses focus on the big picture of the archetype. It’s okay to spread your resources around a bit, and we can get away with it. We’re gods, and at this stage you’re nascent in that realization.

Spell Study (Su): At 2nd level, the sage's understanding of the spells of bards, clerics, and druids is so great that he can use his own magic in an inefficient, roundabout way to duplicate those classes' spells. Once per day, a spell sage can spontaneously cast any spell on the bard, cleric, or druid spell list as if it were a wizard spell he knew and had prepared. Casting the spell requires the spell sage to spend 1 full round per spell level of the desired spell (if the spell is on multiple spell lists indicated above, using the lowest level from among those lists) and requires expending two prepared spells of that spell level or higher; if the spell's casting time is normally 1 full round or longer, this is added to the spell sage's casting time. For example, if a spell sage wants to use spell study to cast cure light wounds (cleric spell level 1st), he must spend 2 full rounds casting and expend two prepared wizard spells of 1st level or higher.

At 6th level and every 5 levels thereafter, a spell sage can use this ability an additional time per day (to a maximum of four times per day at 16th level). This ability replaces arcane school.

This is the fulcrum around which we wield our power and that which ingratiates us to be a peer even to Nethys himself. It appears rather limited and on its surface it is. Once per day usage for 5 levels can seem kind of salty. This is where other avenues to explore our power become paramount to ensure your longevity no matter the situation. Do not despair for we need not resort to cheap trinkets such as pearls of power to do so. There will be more on that later. For now, revel in your power. You can cast any cleric spell without alignment restriction. You can intrude upon Gozreh’s domain and humble nature itself. The hearts and minds of friend and foe alike are at your beck and call. All this in addition to what your “normal” brethren settle for which amount to table scraps by comparison. This is the promise of your studies and where your long labor has brought you. Rest, brother, and steel yourself for what’s to come.

Beyond these two abilities I would highly suggest taking up a True Neutral path. Do not burden yourself with the moralistic cares of this world and instead be your own ally. Follow Nethys’ example for there is wisdom in it. I do not claim to know if the universe always gave way to the TN path or if Nethys’ condition pushed it in that direction. I simply know to maximize the fruits of our labor this path will serve us best. It is also a shield among our soon to be peers for when you truly touch the divine you will be judged by the gods themselves. Should any find offense in you before you have ascended to their ranks, they or their agents could easily snuff you out. The boasts in this document are not meant to inflate ego, dear sister, for they are simply statements of fact.

The Sage Takes Form

The shell for your power is an important one. I personally find favor in the samsaran[2] lifespan for its multiple lifetimes and carried memories are such that even if we fail in one life we can continue nearly seamlessly in the next. One of the ways to expand on our usage of our insights as I promised is also lodged within their cyclic existence for we may carry over spells which we have found useful in previous incarnations that we may cast those spells without using our spell study ability saving it for truly awesome displays of power. But, be careful. Should a heady upstart try to capitalize on your soul he might even find a way to siphon your power and steal your legacy. For this reason, next I would consider the elf[3]. Having a slightly shorter but similarly long life, they are very capable vessels. However, they only get one chance so be sure to make the most of it. Their natural resistance to sleep magics and ability to penetrate magic defenses will aid you greatly in keeping these dangers at bay. If you find even an elf unsuitable then a half-elf[4] will do in a pinch. These crossbred specimens are actually quite adept at what they do.  Lastly, if you must, the frail, short lifespan of the human[5] can be an option. Their one redeeming grace is they seem to have an innate expanded capacity that could, with the proper dedication, rival the other vessels at our disposal. There is but one exception to these I shall mention outright. The aasimar[6] come in many subraces of their own. There may be one that would particularly suit your goals. I would caution you that their relation to the divine as a race may offend some of our deific brethren. Tread lightly here.

Naturally, the keen craftsmanship that is our minds is the driving engine going forward. See to it that your form can accommodate as sharp a one as possible.

Let Enlightenment Commence

Early in your path you would do well to focus on abilities that showcase your worthiness of your noble pursuit. Again, as other guides have gone to great lengths to show feats of ability in general I will not do so here. Instead, I shall capitalize your time and show you the Arcane Discovery. Specially crafted for the wizard, these discoveries unlock our potential and push our horizons in fantastic directions. The spell sage in particular does will with this as its class features free us from the trappings that might entice us to less dynamic options. However, I will point out that the spell sage is both prepared and spontaneous caster. They can cast both arcane and divine spells. This opens up a few feat options for sages. You’ll need to be mindful of what individual feats actually do as they won’t mechanically work for a sage. For example, expanded arcana means nothing to us and we can’t use metamagic with spell study. As far as I can tell, divine interference, dreamed secrets, prophet, divine dignity, glorious heat, and blessed strike all technically work. However, you’ll need your GMs approval on these as most will consider the mere ability to cast enough benefit of spell study and may view these feats as too much.

As but a babe…

To start, let’s begin with the traits that define us and then move on to feats of the arcane.

Traits[7]

Combat

Careful Combatant (Champions of Purity pg. 11): You have a strong sense of self-preservation, believing it is more important to safely extract yourself from a fight that has turned hopeless than to stubbornly stand your ground and risk death—for when you’re dead, you can’t protect the innocent. When using the withdraw action, both the first and second squares of your movement are not considered threatened by any opponents you can see, rather than just the first square.

Early game you’re likely to simply not use this. However, mid to late levels this can see some pretty good returns when you start facing large and larger creatures more regularly. However, you’re a wizard and a spell sage at that. You have far better options.

Hard to Kill (Champions of Purity pg. 7): Your strong will to live and spread good combined with your pure physicality makes you a tough opponent to take down. You may have discovered this as a child after a tragic accident or during the course of your first battle. When you are attempting a Constitution check to stabilize when dying, the penalty on the check is only half your negative hit point total instead of your full negative hit point total.

That errant strike that gets through to you can be nasty. This makes recovery easier. However, I will show you a better trait than this later all thanks to our devotion to the god of magic.

Muscle of the Society (Pathfinder Society Primer pg. 15): Your intense training with the Swords has taught you how to infiltrate otherwise inaccessible ruins and extract their treasures without too much exertion. You gain a +2 trait bonus on Strength checks made to break doors and lift portcullises, and you treat your Strength score as 2 higher for the purpose of determining your carrying capacity.

This is useful in a couple ways. It’s orange because we’re wizard and aren’t often in the business of breaking things. However, coupled with the Knowledge Is Power discovery later in this guide, you can be pretty capable in that kind of thing. The other nifty tidbit is that you can actually tank strength slightly in order to put points on another ability and still be able to carry your gear. However, your melee touch attacks will still suffer.

Resilient (Ultimate Campaign pg. 53, Advanced Player's Guide pg. 1, Second Darkness Player's Guide pg. 12): Growing up in a poor neighborhood or in the unforgiving wilds often forced you to subsist on food and water from doubtful sources. You’ve built up your constitution as a result, and gain a +1 trait bonus on Fortitude saves.

Bonuses to saves are always nice. However, we’re wizards. See the pattern in the advice? We either entirely bypass these nasty effects or simply remove them. They’re barely a blip on our radar in the grand scheme of things.

Faith

Disdainful Defender (Ultimate Campaign pg. 54): You are resistant to the magic of other faiths. You gain a +2 trait bonus on all Will saves you attempt against divine spells. This bonus does not apply against divine spells cast by a caster who worships the same deity you do.

+2 vs basically half of all magic is pretty potent. Nethys being a neutral god doesn’t care how you use your magic and your brothers and sisters of the faith are likely of similar mind so it’s incredibly unlikely for the same deity provision to come in to play.

Fate's Favored (Ultimate Campaign pg. 55): The fates watch over you. Whenever you are under the effect of a luck bonus of any kind, that bonus increases by 1.

Luck bonuses are rare and anything to make them more powerful is pretty nifty. But, it requires an item or something else to do anything at all.

Indomitable Faith (Ultimate Campaign pg. 55, Advanced Player's Guide pg. 1, Second Darkness Player's Guide pg. 12): You were born in a region where your faith was not popular, but you still have never abandoned it. Your constant struggle to maintain your own faith has bolstered your drive. You gain a +1 trait bonus on Will saves.

A blanket +1 to will saves. Solid choice.

Inspired (Ultimate Campaign pg. 55): A positive force, philosophy, or divine presence fills you with hope, and is a guiding force of inspiration. Once per day as a free action, roll twice and take the better result on a skill check or ability check.

I’ve got a soft spot for “try again” abilities and this is no different. Reroll and take better is what makes this blue. Many reroll affects force you to take the second result so this stands apart. That it applies to all skill and ability checks makes it versatile.

Mystery Initiate (Quests and Campaigns pg. 19): You were initiated into a mystery cult in your youth, and learned secrets that turned your understanding of your faith and the world on its head. This experience has given you a knack for lateral thinking. Once per day, you may reroll any Knowledge skill check. You must decide to use this ability after the first roll is made but before the results are revealed. You must take the second result, even if it is worse.

Again, retry abilities provide nice safety nets. But, this is one of those take the second roll ones. It protects against those occasional low rolls. If you’re trying to solve a puzzle or identify a monster those simply can’t happen since you can’t retry knowledge checks.

Priest-Blessed (Faiths and Philosophies pg. 33): You were guided to maturity by a trusted priest of an ecclesiastical tradition, who inspired you and made you understand that the authority exerted by a religious hierarchy is a blessing rather than a restriction, allowing the faithful to always feel confident in their direction and the righteousness of the guided actions. Now, your faith wells up whenever you are aided by divine magic, and you find it easy to open up to the magic and succor of any faith, seeing the similarities between all such traditions. Three times per day when you are the target of a harmless divine spell, you may increase its caster level by 1 for purposes of its effect on you alone.

This gets included for almost no other reason than you can be your own priest and gels well with the spell sage. Increases to CL are almost always welcome.

Magic

A quick note on magic traits: I’m not going to cover even half of them and almost all are at least decent for various builds. These are simply what impress me off the list for spell sages specifically. That last line applies to this whole guide overall. However, it’s especially true for this category.

Ascendant Recollection (Ultimate Campaign pg. 56): Your first bloodline power is particularly strong. Your sorcerer level is considered to be 1 level higher when determining the effects of your 1st-level bloodline power.

This only has benefit if you take the Eldritch Heritage feats. Particularly, it’s useful for the Arcane bloodline if you choose the familiar aspect of the arcane bond. I only include it here for how useful that bloodline can be.

Balancer’s Banishing (Champions of Balance pg. 9): The interference of the Outer Planes in mortal affairs angers you to no end and invigorates the potency of your magic. As long as you remain neutral, whenever you cast a spell that would send an outsider with the chaotic, evil, good, or lawful subtype back to its home plane, the spell’s saving throw DC increases by 1.

Increasing DCs is always nice. That it’s agnostic to magic school is very nice. It doesn’t apply to just dismissal and banishment. It also applies to prismatic spray, for instance, if you roll the send to another plane result.

Dangerously Curious (Ultimate Campaign pg. 56, Second Darkness Player's Guide pg. 12, Advanced Player's Guide pg. 1): You have always been intrigued by magic, possibly because you were the child of a magician or priest. You often snuck into your parent’s laboratory or shrine to tinker with spell components and magic devices, and frequently caused quite a bit of damage and headaches for your parent as a result. You gain a +1 bonus on Use Magic Device checks, and Use Magic Device is always a class skill for you.

You’re a spell sage. The spells you have access to are the wizard, cleric, bard, druid, and alchemist (as I’ll show later) lists. UMD let’s you do everything else. This is ideal for our goals.

Desperate Focus (Cheliax, Empire of Devils pg. 19): You’ve often found yourself in situations where a lack of focus can lead to worse than a lost spell. You gain a +2 trait bonus on concentration checks.

A universal +2 is a solid choice if you’re going to take a trait for this.

Desperate Resolve (Ultimate Campaign pg. 56): You are adept at casting spells even in the most precarious situations. You gain a +1 trait bonus on concentration checks. This trait bonus increases to +4 when you are grappled, pinned, in violent weather, or entangled.

A passive +1 with an upgraded ability when it really matters is also very useful.

Fortunate (Quests and Campaigns pg. 20): You are blessed by fate. Once per day when you use a spell or magic item with a randomized effect (such as confusion, mirror image, prismatic spray, or a bag of tricks), before you roll to determine the result, you may choose to roll twice and pick either result.

A pretty good ability but you’re not likely to see use of its effect often.

Greater Adept of the Society (Shattered Star Player's Guide pg. 5, Faction Guide pg. 63): Requirement(s) Wizard, Pathfinder Society; Months of meditation and practice with the greatest spellcasters of the Society have given you an increased capacity for arcane knowledge. You gain one additional 0-level spell slot.

Additional spell slots are hard to come by. That’s worth consideration alone. That it’s a cantrip is rather limiting. Some ability to graduate it, even if that were just a 1st or 2nd level spell, would make it a green and higher or more than one slot would be blue but that’s likely well outside the bounds of traits, anyway.

Lunar Birth (People of the Stars pg. 11): Born during a lunar eclipse, you were forever marked by the moon’s mysterious and ancient magic. You gain a +1 trait bonus on saving throws against the spells and spell-like abilities cast by humans.

Humans being the most numerous of any race alone means they’re usually the most kind of caster you’ll come across. Particular campaign likelihood should be factored in.

Magical Lineage (Ultimate Campaign pg. 57, Advanced Player's Guide pg. 1, Second Darkness Player's Guide pg. 13): One of your parents was a gifted spellcaster who not only used metamagic often, but also developed many magical items and perhaps even a new spell or two—and you have inherited a fragment of this greatness. Pick one spell when you choose this trait. When you apply metamagic feats to this spell that add at least 1 level to the spell, treat its actual level as 1 lower for determining the spell’s final adjusted level.

This is good for regular wizards. The name of our game is diversity so it’s not as great for us.

Pragmatic Activator (Ultimate Campaign pg. 58): While some figure out how to use magical devices with stubborn resolve, your approach is more pragmatic. You may use your Intelligence modifier when making Use Magic Device checks instead of your Charisma modifier.

This is practically as good as Dangerously Curious. You don’t get the +3 class skill training bonus but you don’t have to invest in charisma and your Intelligence modifier will likely make up for that and your deficient Charisma score to really make this good. If you really want to capitalize on this take Skill Focus (UMD) and you’ll get +6 by level 10 letting you use UMD about as well as any charisma based caster.

Precise Treatment (Ultimate Campaign pg. 58): You treat others with a clear and calculating intellect. You gain a +1 trait bonus on all Heal checks, and you may use your Intelligence modifier when making Heal checks instead of your Wisdom modifier.

This makes Intelligence just that more useful. Most spells can negate the usage of the Heal skill altogether. However, if you want it then this is a way to make it good.

Resilient Caster (Ultimate Campaign pg. 58): Your great force of will makes your magic harder to dispel. Your caster level is treated as 1 level higher whenever someone attempts to dispel one of your spells.

A universal +1 to dispel DCs. Nifty. However, having your spells dispelled isn’t all that common throughout the course of a campaign and we have a floating +4 CL if we really don’t want something getting dispelled, anyway.

Social

Bruising Intellect (Ultimate Campaign pg. 59): Your sharp intellect and rapier-like wit bruise egos. Intimidate is always a class skill for you, and you may use your Intelligence modifier when making Intimidate checks instead of your Charisma modifier.

There is one aspect of intimidate that gets drastically overlooked, the ability to force someone to be friendly for a short time. This is great for getting information out of someone as long as you have a strength heavy character who can pin them.

Clever Wordplay (Pathfinder Society Primer pg. 11): Your cunning and logic are more than a match for another’s confidence and poise. Choose one Charisma-based skill. You attempt checks with that skill using your Intelligence modifier instead of your Charisma modifier.

The ability to choose the skill is what makes this blue. This lets you basically do Pragmatic Activator if you used your Magic trait for something else.

Student of Philosophy (Quests and Campaigns pg. 21): You were trained in a now defunct philosophical tradition—such as that of the now destroyed Galtan universities or Lirgeni astrologers—and learned to use logic and reason to persuade others. You can use your Intelligence modifier in place of your Charisma modifier on Diplomacy checks to persuade others and on Bluff checks to convince others that a lie is true. (This trait does not affect Diplomacy checks to gather information or Bluff checks to feint in combat.)

This single trait goes a long way to making us capable socially. If you don’t have a Charisma heavy character or are in a social heavy campaign this will get a lot of use.

Tireless Logic (Ultimate Campaign pg. 61): Your curious mind figures out even the most complex problems. Once per day when you make an Intelligence-based skill check or ability check, you can roll twice and take the better result.

This is extremely handy with Pragmatic Activator since the UMD skill doesn’t let you take 10 and is now an Intelligence-based skill. Otherwise, it’s another great way to make sure you never fail a knowledge due to poor rolls alone.

Others

Other trait categories that have something to offer include Faction, Region, and Religion. I won’t cover them all but will simply list the stand outs. Note that even though I present them under italicized headings, they are still separate categories so you can absolutely mix faction and religion traits, for example.

Faction

Attuned to the Ancestors (PFS Guide to Organized Play pg. 16): Requirement(s) Osirion; You were raised to believe that undead are nothing to fear—they are simply the unliving remnants of your honored ancestors. Once per day, you can surround yourself with an aura of unlife. Unintelligent undead ignore you unless you take action against them, per hide from undead. The protection lasts 1 round for every two character levels you possess (with a minimum of 1 round). If you take any offensive action against any undead, this effect immediately ends. This is a supernatural ability.

Effects that let you enemies wholly ineffectual and render puzzles quaint are what the wizard is known for. That alone makes it worth mention. What stops it from being green is its ultra-limited duration and that it only applies to unintelligent undead. See Corpse Dodge later on.

Eastern Mysteries (PFS Guide to Organized Play pg. 17): Requirement(s) Qadira; Ever since you were a young child, you studied the ancient arcane arts of several eastern societies, and those studies have made your spellcasting ability difficult to resist. Once per day, you may select one single spell that you are casting that allows for a saving throw. You may increase the DC of that spell by +2.

A floating +2 to make something stick when you really need it to. This is easy to take advantage of to really unfair proportions. I’m looking at you, Persistent Spell. God help anyone if you have a Misfortune slinging witch in your group.

Regional

Corpse Dodger (Undead Slayer's Handbook pg. 15): Requirement(s) Ustalav; You spent your formative years in the town of Ravengro, where your proximity to both a prominent graveyard and an infamous prison left you no stranger to the occasional zombie or skeleton. As such, you developed unique methods of dodging these pests. Once per day, you can cast hide from undead as a spell-like ability. The casting time is a swift action and the caster level for this effect is always 1st; this ability is otherwise identical to the spell.

This is a meatier version of Attuned to the Ancestors. You get the spell and not some aspect of it. That means it applies to intelligent undead as well with a save and lasts for 10 whole minutes? Green.

Cyphermage Backer (Pirates of the Inner Sea pg. 11): Requirement(s) Riddleport; You’ve sold relics to a Cyphermage in the past, and she’s agreed to assist you with future findings. When in Riddleport, you can use your contact to gain information about artifacts and history. Treat this ability as a single Knowledge (arcana) or Knowledge (history) check with a skill bonus of +15. You may make this check once per day that you spend in Riddleport. You can make a new check every day. Each time you do so, you can choose whether to use Knowledge (arcana) or Knowledge (history).

At low levels this can remove some dependence on needing the history skill as most likely you’ll be investing in arcana. However, it’s just a d20+15 check.

Demon Eradicator (Knights of the Inner Sea pg. 12): Requirement(s) Mendevian Crusader; Anytime an outsider with the demon subtype must make a Will save against one of your spells, increase the DC of that save by +1.

Demons are very common foes. This rating applies to that generalization. If you’re playing in Wrath of the Righteous (a PERFECT AP to play this archetype as if mythic PCs needed even more flexibility, hah!) it could easily be blue.

Forbidden Knowledge (Undead Slayer's Handbook pg. 11): Requirement(s) Geb; As a child, you somehow got your hands on a lurid, forbidden text written by a now-forgotten Blood Lord of ages past. You studied the details within intently, learning much about extraplanar undead from beyond this reality, and you developed a strange, almost macabre way of thinking because of this book’s influence. Anytime you would roll a Knowledge (planes) check, you may roll a Knowledge (history) or Knowledge (religion) check instead.

This is an effect that lets you roll up multiple skills into one making your ranks more valuable. An absolutely superb option. If it gave us choices then it’d have easily been a blue option.

Religion

Defensive Strategist (Inner Sea Gods pg. 219, Faiths of Purity pg. 17): Requirement(s) Torag; Your study of dwarven history has trained you in defensive strategy. You aren’t flat-footed during a surprise round that you don’t get to act in or before you get to act at the start of a battle.

You aren’t flat-footed, period. This lets you get off immediate action spells at the start of battle, initiative be damned. It kind of takes the quintessential aspect of the Diviner wizard and put it into an efficient little trait. The spell sage I advocate with this guide should have several options to make this useful. Against a cave-in or some upstart rogue, that’s windy escape; against traps for yourself or allies, that’s liberating command; against a sudden collapse that’s feather fall. All of these are low level spells and all of these are common perils of adventuring.

Denial of Fate (Inner Sea Gods pg. 219): Requirement(s) Urgathoa; The Pallid Princess has blessed you with the ability to avoid the final fate. Once per day while you are below 0 hit points, at the start of your turn you can choose to stabilize without needing to succeed at a Constitution check.

Being able to choose to not die is worthy to be on this list. I simply added it to contrast your options and to encourage you to ferret out better options.

Magic Is Life (Ultimate Campaign pg. 64): Requirement(s) Nethys; Your faith in magic allows you to reflexively use the energy of any spell effect currently on you to save you from death. As long as you are under the effects of a spell, you gain a +2 trait bonus on saving throws against death effects. If you are reduced to negative hit points while you are under the effects of any spell, you automatically confirm stabilization checks to stop bleeding.

Unlike Denial of Fate, you don’t choose. You simply are. The +2 against death effects is a nice side effect. For samsarans, that’s a +4 bonus.

Magic’s Might (Inner Sea Gods pg. 221): Requirement(s) Yuelral; Your natural affinity for magic allows you to affect even those who resist it. You gain a +1 trait bonus on caster level checks to overcome spell resistance.

SR can be a mage’s bane. That you can get bonuses specifically against it in a trait is very nice.

Underlying Principals (Faiths of Balance pg. 15): Requirement(s) Nethys; You’ve spent a large amount of time around magical items, and understand the similarities between many of them. You gain a +1 trait bonus on Use Magic Device checks, and it becomes a class skill for you.

Yet another way to gain UMD as a class skill. Otherwise, this is nothing special.

Arcane Discoveries

Arcane Builder (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): You have an exceptional understanding of the theory behind creating magical items. Select one type of magic item (potions, wondrous items, and so on). You create items of this type 25% faster than normal, and gain a +4 bonus on Spellcraft checks (or other checks, as appropriate) to craft items of this type. You may select this discovery multiple times; its effects do not stack. Each time you select this discovery, it applies to a different type of magic item.

This discovery pays back in spades in the late game but, yes, we can take it as early as level 1. It can apply to scrolls but this is ill advised unless you’re anticipating being out in the field trying to crank a lot of them out. Coupled with Craft Wondrous Item this becomes green. What stops it from being blue is that your craft time at the table is usually hand waved and it doesn’t save you any money.

Balanced Summoning (Champions of Balance pg. 20): You maintain balance by calling on opposing forces when summoning. Whenever you cast a summon monster spell, you can summon two creatures from a single list 1 or more levels lower than the level of the spell. The two creatures must have alignments that are opposite along at least one axis (chaotic and lawful or evil and good). For example, if you cast summon monster III, you could summon a celestial wolf and a fiendish hyena from the 2nd-level list.

It’s right there in the example text that you can get different creatures from a single casting. That’s unheard of. Coupled with Superior Summons it becomes blue hands down as the text implies you still get one more. This should play out in your getting two of one creature and one of another. That’s allowed combos such as two ranged support creatures that can double as a healer as well as a melee focused creature to keep the front line intact, provide ranged support, and bolster your rear lines all at once. This is the kind of flexibility that serves the spell sage well.

Creative Destruction (Su) (Champions of Balance pg. 21): You have learned how to use destructive energy to empower yourself. When you cast an evocation spell that deals damage, you gain a number of temporary hit points equal to the total number of dice used to determine the damage caused by the spell. Temporary hit points gained from this discovery do not stack and disappear after 1 hour.

Passive temporary hit points that keep coming as long as you have a couple blasts in your repertoire is only good and makes your character more resilient. I can see the argument for this being an orange ability in the early game. However, in later levels it can really shine. Also, a 1st level spell sage throwing out a CL 5 burning hands almost double his effects hit points for an hour while dealing damage. As early as level 3 you get scorching ray, which, on a crit doubles the number gained from that ray. It’s not bad by any means. Contagious flame can make it frustrating to actually do real damage to you. Polar ray is your friend at high levels.

Defensive Feedback (Su) (Champions of Balance pg. 21): Rather than dissipate damaging energy, you can redirect some of it back to its source. When an abjuration spell you cast prevents damage (with damage reduction or energy resistance), if the attacking creature is within 30 feet of the protected creature, the foe takes 1d6 points of damage for every 10 points of damage prevented.

This is another ability that passively gives gains with no effort on your part other than having abjuration spells that prevent damage. Hit your party with a communal spell at your earliest convenience. Since it’s 1d6 for every 10 points of damage prevented, that chain lightning for 33 average damage for a party of 4 when each party member has resist 30 lighting turns into 12d6 back at the caster for free and you only  take 3 damage. Since it also groups damage reduction in there, it’s yet another reason to use stoneskin.

Knowledge Is Power (Ex) (People of the River pg. 9): Your understanding of physical forces gives you power over them. You add your Intelligence modifier on combat maneuver checks and to your CMD. You also add your Intelligence modifier on Strength checks to break or lift objects.

In spite of me really liking the flavor of this ability, it really is only OK. Yes, it can make you surprisingly hard to grapple and gives you a decent chance at punching down a door. However, wizards aren’t often in those situations. It’s a fallback ability at best if a creature charges you in defiance of your party’s formation or knows to take out casters first. Which, given the other spell sage combinations makes it really unlikely unless your GM goes total war on your party in combat.

Off come the training wheels…

Alchemical Affinity (Magical Marketplace pg. 18): Having studied alongside alchemists, you’ve learned to use their methodologies to enhance your spellcraft. Whenever you cast a spell that appears on both the wizard and alchemist spell lists, you treat your caster level as 1 higher than normal and the save DC of such spells increases by 1. Additionally, you may copy spells from an alchemist’s formula book into your spellbook just as you could with another wizard’s spellbook. You must be at least a 5th-level wizard to select this discovery.

So, this ability gets a drastic reduction due to the following FAQ found here.

New Spells Known: If I gain the ability to add a spell that is not on my spell list to my list of spells known, without adding it to my spell list, can I cast it?

No. Adding a spell to your list of spells known does not add it to the spell list of that class unless they are added by a class feature of that same class. For example, sorcerers add their bloodline spells to their sorcerer spell list and oracles add their mystery spells to their oracle spell list. The spell slots of a class can only be used to cast spells that appear on the spell list of that class.

posted Jul 10, 2014

It still grants +1 CL and +1 DC to a lot of spells. However, most of those are transmutations for the spells that exist on the bard spell lists as I mention in my struck text below. I’m leaving that text so you can see my original thoughts on the ability.

This is a must have for the spell sage. It gives us alchemical formulae in our spellbook as regular spells. Go look at the alchemist’s formula list. That’s heal, spell resistance, cure wounds spells, and twin form on our repertoire among many others. In addition, it increases our CL and DC for duplicated entries. This let’s use bleed directly into divine casting without using our spell study ability. Perhaps the easiest sell for an iffy GM is that the alchemist list is extremely balanced. There aren’t any early access spells that I’ve seen so far. If you chose samsaran as your race then you have one list of cherry picked spells and two lists you can choose from almost at your leisure. That reduces reliance on spell study even further. Note, however, that this doesn’t make those spells part of your spell list so what you can do with spell completion and spell trigger items without UMD isn’t expanded.

Fast Study (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): Normally, a wizard spends 1 hour preparing all of his spells for the day, or proportionately less if he only prepares some spells, with a minimum of 15 minutes of preparation. Thanks to mental discipline and clever mnemonics, you can prepare all of your spells in only 15 minutes, and your minimum preparation time is only 1 minute. You must be at least a 5th-level wizard to select this discovery.

This is an iconic wizard discovery and perhaps is the best well known besides split slot. However, given our wide spread of abilities so far we have a very robust kit moreso than the wizard spell list as hard as that can be to imagine. There shouldn’t be a lot of pressure to pick this up. That said, it isn’t bad by any means. Get it if you want it.

Feral Speech (Su) (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): You gain the ability to speak with and understand the response of any animal as if using speak with animals, though each time you speak to animals, you must decide to communicate with either amphibians, birds, fish, mammals, or reptiles, and can only speak to and understand animals of that type. You can make yourself understood as far as your voice carries. This discovery does not predispose any animal addressed toward you in any way. When you reach 12th level, you can also use this ability to communicate with vermin. You must be at least a 5th-level wizard to select this discovery.

You can do better. Unless animals are a particular feature of your campaign or you just want a cool ability that’s not likely to see a lot of use, pick something else.

Multimorph (Su) (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): Your studies in transmogrification have increased your control over shapechanging spells. When you cast a spell of the polymorph subschool on yourself, you may expend 1 minute of the spell's duration as a standard action to assume another form allowed by the spell. You can do this as often as you like, subject to the duration of the spell. You must be at least a 5th-level wizard to select this discovery.

If you’re going heavy on polymorphing spells this can get a ton of mileage and is potentially even blue especially at higher levels where polymorphed spells start getting wrapped into single castings like the polymorph spell and eventually shapechange. Otherwise, it’s relatively mediocre but still gives a great advantage for the targeted spells such as elemental body.

Split Slot (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): Once per day when you prepare spells, you may treat any one of your open spell slots as if it were two spell slots that were two spell levels lower. For example, a 9th-level wizard can split a 5th-level slot into two 3rd-level slots, preparing fireball and lightning bolt in those 3rd-level slots. For all purposes, the two lower-level slots are treated as that lower level (so the split 5th-level slot used for a fireball has a DC as if it were in a normal 3rd-level slot). Splitting a 2nd-level slot lets you prepare two additional cantrips (which you can cast over and over, just like normally prepared cantrips). This discovery has no effect on cantrips or 1st–level spells. You may select this discovery multiple times; each time you select it, you may split another spell slot when you prepare spells. You cannot split a slot that you created by splitting a higher-level slot. You must be at least 5th level to select this discovery.

Used well, this is well worth a feat in that your spell study ability now only effectively consumes a single slot instead of two. Given that spell study explicitly lets you trade up in slots used, you can take this once, use spell study for a single slot expenditure for low to mid-level spells and reserve the two slot price for those more powerful spells.

Yuelral's Blessing (Champions of Purity pg. 27): You cast any spells that appear on both the wizard and druid spell lists at +1 caster level and with +1 to the save DC. In addition, you may replace the material component of any arcane spell with gems of the same value. You must be at least a 5th-level wizard to select this discovery.

I consider this a blue. The ability to carry regular gems and use them as material components is amazing especially if you’re having problems finding the more rare gems and diamond dust to use. You’re likely to avoid in-game suspicion from not carrying around large quantities of diamond dust and singular diamonds. Since gems are trade goods, too, then you can use them as everyday currency as well whereas not all merchants may want to give change for or take large diamonds. In a metagame sense it also makes you less reliant on GM permission to be able to cast expensive spells that isn’t blood money cheese. That you also gain +1 CL and DC is sweet, sweet icing. Combined with Alchemical Affinity the only spells that overlap with the wizard, druid, and alchemist lists tend to be transmutation spells so your +DC isn’t likely to get much action there but you’ll get bonus rounds/minutes/etc. of duration and they’ll be harder to dispel. However, many offensive spells overlap between the wizard and druid lists so you’ll be able to enjoy that DC increase.

Well on our way…

Resilient Illusions (Magical Marketplace pg. 18): You are able to conjure illusions so lifelike that they defy disbelief. Anytime a creature tries to disbelieve one of your illusion effects, make a caster level check. Treat the illusion’s save DC as its normal DC or the result of the caster level check, whichever is higher. You must be at least an 8th-level wizard to select this discovery.

This is a nifty ability. You get a chance for higher DCs and the normal DC is the fallback anyway. However, it’s restriction to illusion spells doesn’t make it a particularly strong choice, per se. Upgrade to green if you’re going for an illusion heavy build or wanting to capitalize on the Shadow Conjuration spells.

Beyond Morality (Su) (Champions of Balance pg. 21): As long as you are neutral, you may choose to be treated as the most favorable alignment when affected by spells whose effects vary based on alignment (such as holy word). If you are neutral in relation to evil and good, you may choose to be treated as good or evil. If you are neutral in relation to chaos and law, you may choose to be treated as lawful or chaotic. You may only choose to be treated as one alignment type along a single axis at a time (for instance, if you were within the area of both a magic circle against evil spell and an unholy blight spell, you would have to choose to be either evil, good, or neutral for the purpose of determining the spells’ effects). You must be at least an 9th-level wizard to select this discovery.

Unless you’re encountering a lot of outsiders or enemy casters who use alignment based spells, this isn’t a particularly great option. It’s great flavor and is always handy in one off situations, though.

Golem Constructor (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): You have learned the art and craft of creating a single type of golem (such as stone golems or iron golems). When creating a golem of this type, you count as having the Craft Wondrous Item, Craft Magic Arms and Armor, and Craft Construct feats. You must meet all other construction requirements for the golem as normal. You may select this discovery multiple times. Each time you select this discovery, it applies to a different kind of golem. You must be at least a 9th-level wizard to select this discovery.

If you’re creating golems, this is three feats in one. You can’t pass this up. The only downside is that it applies to a single type of golem. I don’t see why you couldn’t combine this with Arcane Builder, either.

Steward of the Great Beyond (Champions of Purity pg. 27): Whenever a creature attempts to use a teleportation effect or summon a creature within 30 feet of you, you may attempt to block the effect. Make an opposed caster level check (1d20 + caster level) as an immediate action. If the check succeeds, the spell or effect fails and is wasted; otherwise, it is unaffected. You can use this ability once per day plus one additional time for every 5 wizard levels you possess beyond 10th. You must be at least a 9th-level wizard to select this discovery.

This isn’t restricted by spell. It doesn’t even have to be a spell. If it’s a teleportation effect or any summon you can stop it. Plus, it scales with level for uses per day. It would be blue if the ability to prevent the effect was a bit more in our favor as Paizo APs tend to involve higher level casters than you vs your entire party. But, the mere fact you can, indeed, stop that glabrezu from being two glabrezus is plenty worth the chance. We can’t use our Focused Spells ability with it, though.

Idealize (Su) (Champions of Balance pg. 21): In your quest for self-perfection, you have discovered a way to further enhance yourself and others. When a transmutation spell you cast grants an enhancement bonus to an ability score, that bonus increases by 2. At 20th level, the bonus increases by 4. You must be at least a 10th-level wizard to select this discovery.

Another great flavor option with circumstantial benefit. In its defense there are many transmutation spells that give enhancement bonuses especially the mass ability spells. It’s almost enough to make it green. If the +4 kicked in before 20 or your campaign is definitely going to 20 then this is certainly a green, perhaps even blue.

Time Stutter (Sp) (People of the River pg. 9): You can briefly step out of time, pausing the world around you. This ability acts as the time stop spell, except that you gain only 1 round of apparent time. You can use this ability once per day plus one additional time for every 5 wizard levels you possess beyond 10th. You must be at least a 10th-level wizard to select this discovery.

Early access time stop. That’s single action summons and makes low level spell study usages more combat viable. It scales with level for times per day albeit that’s only 3/day at level 20. It removes the impetus to absolutely require the actual time stop spell the moment you get 9th level spells which afford for greater flexibility and gives extra swift and move actions in any case which are always tactically useful.

Infectious Charms (Magical Marketplace pg. 18): Your charms are so smooth that they’re contagious. Anytime you target and successfully affect a single creature with a charm or compulsion spell and that creature is within 30 feet of another opponent, your spell has a chance of affecting the second creature as well. As a swift action immediately after affecting a creature with a charm or compulsion spell, you can cause the spell to carry over to the nearest creature within 30 feet. The spell behaves in all ways as though its new target were the original target of the spell. You must be at least an 11th-level wizard to select this discovery.

It’s basically free bouncing spell for charms and compulsions. You do have to affect a target first, admittedly. However, the ability to pass around your spell effects is powerful. As you subdue enemies in combat you can shift your spells getting more bang for your buck.

Staff-Like Wand (Ultimate Magic pg. 87): Your research has unlocked a new power in conjunction with using a wand. Similar to using a magic staff, you use your own Intelligence score and relevant feats to set the DC for saves against spells you cast from a wand, and you can use your caster level when activating the power of a wand if it's higher than the caster level of the wand. You must be at least an 11th-level wizard and must have the Craft Staff feat to select this discovery.

This is essentially a class with two feats. Having 50 castings of anything at your disposal that you can use your own statistics for is certainly a best in class ability. For all intents and purposes, low level spells on wands become more powerful than staves with low level spells in your hands.

True Name (Sp) (Ultimate Magic pg. 87): Your researches into ancient tomes and your inquisitions of bound spirits have led you to one of the best-hidden secrets of the multiverse: the true name of an outsider—the name that defines the very essence of the creature and that gives the speaker control over the being. This outsider can have no more than 12 Hit Dice. Once per day, you can speak the common name by which the outsider is known, and the outsider travels to you as if you had cast planar binding upon it. It must obey you to the best of its ability, without pay or bargaining for its services, for its fear that you might release its true name to the wider world is enough to bring even the most recalcitrant of outsiders to bear.

If the creature is within 100 feet, as a move action, you may punish it by deliberately mispronouncing its name, wracking its very essence and giving it the sickened and staggered conditions for 1 round (even if the creature is normally immune to these conditions). You cannot use true name in an area of silence, but the creature does not have to be able to hear you for it to be harmed by the ability.

It is in your best interest to call this creature only sparingly, and occasionally reward it in some fashion to mollify its wrath. If you repeatedly fail to offer it a reward appropriate to its type and ethos, the creature may begin plotting ways to destroy the bond between you, whether by creating an accident that will destroy your memory of the name, by plaguing you with nuisances or dangers until you vow never to call on it again, or by actively seeking to destroy you through its own devices or those of an underling. If this creature is of a lawful type and you are violating its ethos, its superiors may even destroy it or you rather than allow you to contaminate their servant further. Worse, they may establish situations where it is necessary for you to summon this outsider, opening gateways to infernal or angelic interference, in order to gain a foothold on the Material Plane.

You may select this discovery multiple times. Each time you select this discovery, it applies to a different, specific outsider. You must be at least an 11th-level wizard to select this discovery. If you select this discovery at 15th level or higher, the creature may have up to 18 Hit Dice and the call acts as greater planar binding instead of planar binding.

This is a standard action summons and is awesome. However, its major weakness is that if your particular named outsider is killed then it’s gone forever. You should wait until 15th level to take it, though, to make sure you get the most out of the ability. Also, remember to not abuse it. It’s for emergencies only, but having your god’s herald on speed dial is all kinds of awesome.

Completing our journey…

Werewolf Shape (Blood of the Moon pg. 25): When you cast beast shape IV or shapechange, you can choose to take the shape of a werewolf in addition to the other shapes available. While in werewolf shape, you gain all the effects of the werewolf template instead of the spell’s normal benefits. You act in all respects as a natural lycanthrope for the duration of the spell, including the ability to inflict the curse of lycanthropy using the spell’s save DC. You must be at least a 15th-level wizard to select this arcane discovery.

As cool as being a natural lycanthrope can be, werewolves aren’t the best of them. On the plus side, your gear doesn’t meld with you per the natural lycanthrope rules so you can still cast spells in hybrid form. Get a transformation spell going with the Idealize discovery for +6 to all your physical scores as well. As neat of a combo as that can be it’s not that great.

Immortality (Ex) (Ultimate Magic pg. 86): You discover a cure for aging, and from this point forward you take no penalty to your physical ability scores from advanced age. If you are already taking such penalties, they are removed at this time. You must be at least a 20th-level wizard to select this discovery.

I’m kind of surprised to mark this one orange. Immortality should be a big deal, and it is. However, for the spell sage, if you haven’t gotten Nethys to nod in your general direction at least once by now then there’s no hope for you. For a normal wizard I can see the appeal. However, it’s too similar to the ravings of lich wannabes rather than being good enough for quasi-divine beings who can cast miracle and wish back to back.

As you can see, a lot of abilities are centered around neutrality. Given that spell sages are are in the middle of all kinds of magic leads me to advise similar characters follow suit. The class and many of the abilities that suit it well seem modeled around Nethys and his exalted example. Devote thyself, initiate, to magic and not the squabbles of mortality and materialism and you will flourish.

The Spell Sage Cometh

At the end of the day, you’re still a mortal wizard unless you’ve been a truly exceptional individual who has been invited by the All-Seeing Eye to join his court which is exceedingly unlikely. You still need to cast spells to express your power in most cases. Outside of spells you should have a robust set of tools via discoveries and traits earlier discussed. What makes a truly remarkable spell sage is how he selects his spells. Take for instance a build I recently completed as a samsaran picking bard spells for amplifying spells and to have a paralysis and enchantment breaker, utilizing alchemy formulae as spells for semi-divine native casting and the ability to run around perfectly safe in melee via twin form and SR 30 on command, and wizard spells for the more raw/powerful effects such as gaining access to 36d6 disintegrate all at level 14. Spell study in that build is for big things like resurrections, restorations, awaken, and so on. Ultimately, this is up to you to decide and so I leave it blank save for this sole paragraph. The other guides on their respective classes give great advice. Read them but don’t be so quick to trust their ratings. Slow down a little and consider what it’d be like with the other spell lists present and what kind of combinations work for your needs. The one bit of advice I will push in particular is to choose your spells more like how sorcerers do so. Stick to spells which have multiple uses, partial saves, affect groups and so on. Summon monster spells, minor/major creation, sculpt sound, invisibility, black tentacles, mass protection from energy, telekinesis, heal, limited wish, arcane concordance, investigative mind, apparent master, prismatic spray, etc. are all very strong choices to target. This is the test that shows you have talent with the Gift, my young pupil. Nethys is watching…
