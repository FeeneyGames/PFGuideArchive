WHAT IS SPHERES OF POWER?

Spheres of Power is a third party product made by Drop-Dead Studios for use with the Pathfinder RPG. It creates an alternative magic system to use alongside or replace the existing vancian slot and level system, giving at-will abilities and a resource pool for augmenting them, broken down into ‘spheres’ focused around a variety of themes, each sphere providing basic abilities that are expanded or altered by a talents that go with that sphere. For example, the warp sphere allows a character to teleport a limited distance, while talents that increases the range, allows you to target an enemy, or ignore line of sight for the teleport.

WHAT IS THE THAUMATURGE?

The thaumaturge is a base class, one of eleven created for the Spheres of Power system, though existing classes are adapted as well. The thaumaturge gains a limited selection of spheres, but can boost their caster level above that of any other class, for a price, not unlike the wilder for those familiar with psionics. This makes them an excellent specialist but a poor generalist.

GUIDE TO COLOR CODING:

Red options are terrible and should generally be avoided.

Orange options are niche or mediocre and should be avoided outside of particular builds.

Green options are decent but not top priorities.

Blue options are the strongest and/or most versatile and should be prioritized.

THE BASICS:

D8 hit dice - perfectly standard for a specialist caster type. Keeps you alive decently, you shouldn’t be tanking anyhow.

Medium BAB - does what it needs to. Most sphere effects target touch AC if an attack roll is needed, though melee build will need to hit full AC, for which this should be adequate.

Good Will save, poor Fort and Ref – Would have liked to see a good fort save here, but if you are only getting one it may as well be Will.

4 Skill Points/Level - You aren’t a skill monkey, but you can cover your bases. Intelligence-based characters will have plenty.

Simple Weapon, Light Armor – Not bad for caster-types. Melee builds will need to consider improving this though.

High-Caster - In the Spheres of Power system, caster level is like BAB, including the way it stacks between classes. You have full caster level, so at level 20 your CL is 20. As good as it gets.

Spellpool - this is the resource you use to boost all of your sphere abilities, equal to level + casting mod. It isn’t rated since it is the same for all sphere casters.

Magic Talents – 10 in twenty levels, the worst for any of the new classes, only beating out the paladin and ranger archetypes. You have to be VERY careful in selecting your talents and leveraging sphere-specific drawbacks, and even then plan on using feats to pick up more.

ABILITY SCORES:

Str – Str based melee builds obviously need this. Others mostly just need enough not to be encumbered by their gear. Items fix that issue for relatively low cost.

Dex - You second highest stat priority. Governs your ranged touch attacks (and melee if using weapon finesse), boosts AC (you have light armor), reflex saves, and initiative (if employing battlefield control, going first is vital. Good for everyone else too.). Also key for dex-based melee builds, of course. If you are getting heavier armor from somewhere and not taking spheres with ranged attacks, you can get by without much here, though a penalty would still hurt.

Con - Shoot for 14 here. D8 is decent, but no one ever died from too many hit points or too high of fort saves. Blood-mage types (draining casting drawback, fortified casting boon) max it.

Int – Skills, skills, skills. Also fuels kirin style, if you go that route. Spellcraft is a good skill to have, especially if crafting items or spellcrafting. If this is your casting attribute, obviously it is blue.

Wis - You have a good will save, but there’s no such thing as too much. Between saves and perception, I can’t recommend a penalty here unless you are getting a big save boost from somewhere. Leave it at 10 and boost it if you have spare points.My first choice for casting stat. If this is your casting attribute, obviously it is blue.

Cha – Face skills, not really vital for you. This would be my last choice for casting stat on its own merits, but some builds or concepts can use it. If this is your casting attribute, obviously it is blue.

Sample Stat Arrays:

Wis-Based Caster:                 WIS>DEX>CON>INT>>>STR=CHA

Int-Based Caster:                 INT>DEX>CON>WIS>>>STR=CHA

Cha-Based Caster:                 CHA>DEX>CON>WIS>INT>>>STR

Str Based Melee Magus dip:        STR>INT>DEX=CON>WIS>CHA

Dex Based Melee Magus dip:        DEX>INT>CON>WIS>>>STR=CHA

SKILLS:

Class Skills:

Craft (Int) – Don’t need this, if you want to craft magic items take spellcraft, mundane crafting isn’t generally worthwhile. Works with the skilled casting drawback.

Disguise (Cha) – some campaigns may find good use for it, many others won’t.

Diplomacy (Cha) - My preferred social skill. If Cha-based, you can handle face duties.

Fly (Dex) - You have the means to get varying degrees of flight and good dex, so tossing a few points in won’t hurt anything.

Intimidate (Cha) - The mean social skill. Great if you work in dazzling display or another means to either spread it over an area or without actions.

Heal (Wis) – Meh. Grab the life sphere if you want to do this.

Knowledge (All; Int) – Nice to drop a few ranks for the major monster identifiers (arcana, religion, planes). If Int-based you should grab more and play encyclopedia.

Linguistics (Int) – Probably not. Grab a rank if a particular language is needed or it fits your background

Perform (Cha) – only use is fluff or for the skilled casting drawback.

Profession (Wis) - Fluff skill only. Grab something thematic if using background skills. Works with the skilled casting drawback.

Sense Motive (Wis) – Wis-based builds can be good lie detectors. At least one person in the party NEEDS it, backup doesn’t hurt.

Spellcraft (Int) – It’s great, especially if crafting. Note that a spellcraft check is, shockingly, required for spellcrafting and magic item crafting as well. Spellcrafting can be all kinds of fun.

Non-Class Skills (incomplete):

Acrobatics (Dex) - nice if you get in a tight spot, but rolling verses CMD gets tricky.

Bluff (Cha) - a nice social skill, though I prefer diplomacy. Hard to get caught telling the truth. Works for feinting, but feinting is melee-only by default, so unlikely to be worth building around. Melee-based character probably have better things to do with their feats than boost this anyway.

Climb (Str) - you are two talents from flight, so not really a priority.

Easy to get in class with a trait.

Escape Artist (Dex) - Great to have when you need it, but you should be avoiding those situations. I like the warp sphere for that reason.

Perception (Wis) - Most rolled skill in the game. Max it if possible.

Stealth (Dex) - You should have decent dex and light armor, so this isn’t a bad investment. Depending on your focus, you could become an excellent scout. If you want it, grab a trait to get it in class.

Survival (Wis) - Not your job. Wis-based can cover this by taking 10 anyway.

Swim (Str) - Not more than a point here unless in an aquatic campaign. If in an aquatic campaign, get a swim speed from somewhere (alteration sphere, for example).

UMD (Cha) - Handy for filling in all kinds of affects you can’t get natively or don’t have room for. Remember the pragmatic activator trait if going for an Int-based build.

PARTY ROLES:

        

        Party roles are tricky to rate for this class, since you can build for almost anything. Instead, I will provide concepts for how you go about building for that role. 

Melee Striker – Two levels of magus, then take the devourer archetype. You should crit frequently, allowing you to use forbidden lore without risk frequently in combat. You could also go for natural attacks, picking them up from race and the alteration sphere. You can also cover this with summons.

Tank – You don’t have anything natively here, but the protection sphere combined with other abilities can make this work, boosting AC and reducing incoming damage. Think more about mitigating damage to allies than standing up front and drawing attention. Alteration is a good source of natural armor. War can help with tanking too. A horde of zombies (death), animated objects (enhancement), or an outsider meatshield (conjuration) work too.

Ranged Striker – Destruction is the go-to here, though telekinesis, weather, and other spheres present options as well.

Debuffer – Death carries a good number of debuffs, as does destruction with rider effects on destructive blast. Light, dark, time, many spheres have debuffs of varying potency. You can’t take all or even most of them though, so if this is your goal, pick a sphere heavy in it.

Buffer – alteration, enhancement, and time are golden here.

Utility – Again, scattered throughout the spheres. Hard for you to have the breadth to cover the wide variety of niche situations you rely on a utility caster for. This is an incanter’s job, but you can build for maximizing your utility or get real creative with the most versatile spheres.

Battlefield Control – (BFC) Creation, destruction, nature, telekinesis, warp, weather. A vital role for the party to have and you can do it well.

CLASS ABILITIES:

Forbidden Lore – starts off at +2 cl with a 15% chance of backlash. Backlash is nasty. Note that you can’t go below an effective CL of 1, handy at low levels. The loss of action is painful as well. This is really the backbone of the class, but using it is risky, especially prior to level 7, where you can pass off the effects with an incantation. Devourers don’t mind as long as they are getting crits.

Invocations – Limited uses per day with a variety of effects as you level.

Meditation – retry a knowledge check. Great if you are the party encyclopedia, not so much otherwise.

Lingering Pain – fort save or sickened as a rider to another effect. Sickened softens up their saves, but fort saves tend to be high in the bestiary.

Lingering Blessing – can’t complain about temporary hit points (THP), even in modest quantities. Mostly useful if you are buffing/healing in combat a lot.

Empowered Defense – a nice dodge bonus for yourself, great for the martially inclined, but be aware that it won’t stack with other deflection bonuses.

Empowered Attack – excellent for those reliant on attack rolls, useless if not. Untyped, so should always stack.

*Channel Punishment* – the reason to consider taking the extra invocations feat. Pass off your backlash to another creature. Unwilling targets get a save, so if you don’t feel lucky find a way to get a familiar, psicrystal, animal companion, hireling, etc to take the pain for you.

Defensive Invocation – save bonus, untyped. Helps with your weak saves, could be a life saver.

Soulfire – boost everything if you take backlash. It’s a solid bonus, but unless you know you are in the final battle of the day it’s risky. Usually you will prefer channel punishment over this.

Item Lore – add forbidden lore bonus to item CL. This is really excellent since with your limited talents you will be very invested in expanding your abilities with items. Combine with the wand wielder feat for maximum effect. Note that it does not say you use forbidden lore, just that you add the bonus, so no backlash!

Empowered Resistance – evasion and stalwart rolled into one, but only for one roll. Great if you know what you are getting hit with.

Rebuke Death – best not to need it, but handy if you do. Doesn’t help with death effects, but by 19 you should have those covered anyway.

Occult Knowledge – Scaling bonus to good skills. It’s nice, but don’t be afraid to trade it out. If you rely on forcing saves, ditch it to take unspeakable pact.

Bonus Feats – anything that requires casting. Mostly going to be extra talent, though there are some fun other feats if you can spare the room.

Master Invoker – unlimited use of two invocations? Sweet. Too bad it’s so late you won’t get to use it. Channel punishment is a top pick, defensive invocation or item lore next, depending on your build and magic item availability.

WHY PLAY A THAUMATURGE?

The first question in choosing a Spheres of Power class is ‘Why not play an incanter instead?’ This is a good question. The thaumaturge is capable of being a powerful caster in any sphere, but the selection of talents is so limited that you have to consider carefully what is required for your concept and if the class can deliver.

Vs Incanter

The thaumaturge has a stronger chassis, with better HD, skills, and armor proficiency, for what it’s worth. Aside from that, the comparison becomes difficult. The thaumaturge always has the highest potential caster level, and thus raw power, but you cannot count on this power at all times and the loss in sheer number of talents is staggering, allowing the incanter to go deeper into a given sphere faster than an equal level thaumaturge, which in some cases will, at least for a time, compensate or even surpass the difference in CL. Many spheres have talents that increase the maximum size or range of the effects available, essentially treating the caster as if they had a significantly higher CL for that purpose. These are also available to the thaumaturge, of course, but the opportunity cost is higher.

There are two areas that the thaumaturge is unsurpassed in, however. With the master of cosmos feat, the thaumaturge will have the most powerful conjuration companions in the game, though other classes will be able to have a greater number of companions. Flooding the field with a huge number of summons is bad form at most tables, so I don’t recommend building an incanter with every talent spent on extra companion. Recent errata has changed the master of cosmos feat from adding your forbidden lore bonus to CL for determining your companion’s strength to being a flat bonus to attack rolls and skill checks. Still nice, not nearly what it was.

With the master of Death feat, the thaumaturge will be able to animate more and more powerful undead as well, making it the choice class for minion-mancy. Since these feats don’t actually require using forbidden lore, they just add your forbidden lore bonus to your CL for the given purposes, you don’t risk backlash in their use either.

Ultimately, the thaumaturge is best played when you want to specialize, especially in spheres lacking specialist classes already, and don’t mind a bit of risk on your path to ultimate (narrow) power. Note that some spheres scale more heavily on number of talents rather than CL, such as the life sphere. These aren’t good for you.

Vs Elementalist

For a specialist in the destruction sphere, a comparison to the elementalist is in order as the blasting potential of the thaumaturge will seem obvious to many. The elementalist has two more good saves, evasion, dodge bonus, free movement modes, and resistances. The thaumaturge get full CL for all spheres, but gets less talents overall and requires special considerations in character design to mitigate the drawbacks of its feature ability, forbidden lore. Over the levels, the thaumaturge will be able to attain a caster level between 2 and 6 (8 if using the human favored class bonus) levels higher than the elementalist, which is between 1d6 and 3d6 damage on your basic blast or 2d6 – 6d6 if spending a spell point. This results in 3.5 to 10.5 extra damage base, 7 to 21 damage augmented. By the time the thaumaturge is getting +6 level from forbidden lore (17th level), the elementalist is getting 1.5*level damage on their 1st favored element, so +25 at level 17 and +30 damage at level 20, with +17 on their second favored element, and +8 on their third.

So the elementalist can do more pure damage, but only with one blast type. However, the thaumaturge risks backlash every time it boosts it’s CL. You lose the action even when using channel punishment, which is painful in combat, and incantations are limited. The dearth of talents granted the thaumaturge means that feats are tight, so the elementalist actually has more room to diversify both within destruction and into other spheres, especially since the combat feats you need can often be covered by your bonus feats.  

The damage comparison gets shifted a bit if you dip sorcerer, boosting damage by +1/CL, but the risk of using forbidden lore frequently remains.

For a specialist in the Nature Sphere, the geomancer archetype elementalist gets a constant+6/+4/+2 CL over the levels to three of the four geomancing packages, making it appealing for a specialist. The thaumaturge can boost all of the packages, but loses out in other areas. Both choices are legitimate, though don’t forget that obtaining all four geomancing packages will eat into your talents.

Vs Other Specialists

You cannot match the versatility of the fey adept with create reality and illusions require a fair number of talents to really get going. Create reality is so good that I can’t recommend playing an illusion specialist without going fey adept. The eliciter gets significant bonuses to mind sphere DCs, making the forbidden lore bonuses less appealing for enchantment specialists. Thaumaturges have a fair argument against symbiats (telekinetic warrior) in the telekinesis field, given the advantages of high CL in lifting objects and weapons, though a dip in symbiat can be beneficial for such a build. Hedge witch (triple goddess archetype) will be better with the life and fate spheres due to having more talents, though not able to match the HD of undead controlled from the death sphere.

RACES

First, a few notes about priorities here that will guide race choice regardless of sources available. Bonuses to dex and your casting stat are great, bonuses to con are good, and penalties to str if you aren’t str-based melee or cha if you don’t use it to cast are acceptable. Small size is a boon for casting-focused builds, since generally speaking combat maneuvers aren’t your thing and the bonus to AC and attack rolls is lovely. Small creatures also have an easier time bringing mounts inside without spending a feat on undersized mount. The stealth bonus is nice for some builds too. By the same token, large races are generally going to be bad unless looking for melee reach. Darkvision is a nice bonus, as are movement modes, though you get flight in-class at a reasonable level so climb speeds aren’t terribly important. Natural attack builds of course want free natural attacks. Magus dippers will appreciate the prehensile tail races for ease of juggling wands, rods, and staves, though they are handy for anyone once having a variety of items in combat becomes important.

Core Races

Dwarf – great choice for Wisdom-based builds, okay for Intelligence-based builds, terrible for Charisma-based builds. Grab the steel soul feat and glory of old trait to shore up your saves. Darkvision is nice to have and the skill bonus could work out for you if you are using the skilled casting drawback.

Elf – solid choice for Int-builds, though Con loss is never pleasant. Weapon proficiencies are decent if going single-class melee, and a bonus to beating spell resistance is nice. You can get darkvision with alternate racials.

Gnome – small size and great stat boosts for Cha-builds. Skill bonus can work for the skilled drawback. Fire talents could get a bonus from the pyromaniac alternate racial, ask your DM.

Halfling: - perfect stat spread for Cha-casters, small size. Grab the alternate racial to boost your speed up to 30’.

Half-Elf – choice of stat boosts is nice and some options are decent, but not a standout race.

Half-Orc – choice of stats and ferocity are good, as is getting darkvision. Can get a bite attack with alternate racials. Free proficiency with the falchion is excellent for devourers that don’t dip magus.

Human – free feat, extra skills, good at anything. What did you expect?

Other Notable Races

Aasimar – they come in a variety of stats boosts and the sidebar even allows small size, with resistances to boot! Good for anything.

Goblin – Paizo’s favorite miniature psychopaths, the big dex boost is great, just don’t do Cha-based. Make good mounted characters, too.

Orc – you’re joking, right? Useable for Str-based melee builds.

Merfolk – great for aquatic campaigns, not so much otherwise. Given the stat spread the strongtail option will likely be unwelcome in most campaigns.

Tiefling – like aasimar, but with a -2 to a stat. Plenty of options. The tail is nice for juggling staves, rods, and wands, which should be a concern once you have some money, so is highly recommended (fiendish sorcery wasn’t likely to help you anyhow). You can also get natural attacks if going that route. Meshes nicely with a magus dip. Possibly the best race available, due to the tail, resistances, and stat arrays. Don’t forget the sidebar allowing you to grab small size!

Skinwalker – another race with a variety of alternate options to suite your stat needs. Note that bloodmarked can get flight by turning into a bat for only one feat, while ragebred have unusual natural attack options if you want to go melee.

Dhampir – choice of stats, healing can get tricky. Most alternates offer strength bonuses, so there should be something for melee types.

Kobold – they stink, but do have some natural attack options if going dex-based melee. 

FAVORED CLASS BONUSES

Core Races

Dwarf – boost empowered defense’s duration. Casting mod duration should already get you through a combat, so not great.

Elf – boost occult knowledge slightly. Not great and you may be swapping it out with an archetype anyhow. +3 over 20 levels does not impress..

Gnome – reduce backlash chance. It is a small amount, but worthwhile.

Half-Elf – 3 talents over the levels is solid.

Half-Orc – duration boost for empowered offense. Since melee types won’t have as high a casting mod, this helps a bit, but won’t really be worthwhile at higher levels once you have boosted your stats.

Halfling: Extra uses of invocations are helpful, worth taking.

Human – the interesting option. Boosts your forbidden lore bonus, but also backlash. Since it doesn’t kick in until you already have channel punishment, I think it’s worthwhile, doubly so for devourers. Note that half-elves and half-orcs can take this as well.

Other Notable Races

Aasimar – extra THP from lingering blessing. Again, it’s okay for buffers, but requires using forbidden knowledge and an invocation.

Goblin – extra spell points are excellent.

Merfolk – Boost invocation DCs, great for channel punishment used in a hostile manner.

Orc – increase damage from any sphere? Trying to make up for a bad race. Worth taking if you want to hurt things, most likely with a strength-based devourer. Half-orcs can grab it as well.

Tiefling – increase linger pain’s duration. Makes lingering pain much better, doesn’t do anything about the save though.

ARCHETYPES

Presently, all thaumaturge archetypes are found in Spheres of Power: Expanded Options. There are currently two, though one provides three options.

Devourer

The round after you confirm a critical hit, you can use forbidden lore with no chance of backlash. The downside is that your base backlash percentage is 20% instead of 15%. Awesome for melee builds that can grab a keen weapon with high crit range. Long duration buffs can be cheesed by nonlethally punching your friends or random livestock until you get lucky.

Eldritch Cultist (Div) – Trade backlash for a chance to be confused that increases each time it's used and delays invocations to get the divination sphere. Nice for long-term buffing as long as you can deal with the damage you deal yourself. Melee builds will like it too, since confused characters can always attack the creature that attacked them last round, so you won’t feel the full effect of the drawback. Dangerous to use for an offensive caster that doesn’t want to get physical.

God-Blooded (SkyPG) – (aasimar only)  locks you into a tradition (verbal and emotional drawbacks aren’t too bad, though watch out for fear, easy focus boon is nice) and trades occult knowledge for a couple domains, the second coming online at 10 & 14, plus fluff abilities replacing master invoker. Locks you out of pact mage, which hurts a bit. It works if you are in the setting and want the fluff, but it's nothing revolutionary.

Pact Mage

Lose occult knowledge for new options for invocations. The three options will be rated individually. Abilities come at 2nd, 6th, and 14th levels.

Celestial Pact:

Lingering Guidance - minor buff with tiny duration that requires using forbidden lore on the targets first? Pass.

Celestial Blessing - lingering blessing duration boost, okay.

Empowered Recovery - increasing healing received by 50%. Nice.

Infernal Pact:

Lingering Wrath - trivial damage buff, meh.

Infernal Pain - increase to lingering pain duration, okay.

Dark Lore - AWESOME forbidden knowledge boost. Note that you can still pass off the penalties with channel punishment, so this really just cost two invocations per use.

Unspeakable Pact:

Curse of Entropy - big penalty to a save that can be applied as a free action and does not itself allow a save? Sweet. Makes this the best pact if you aren’t spending enough time at 14th and higher to really make use of Dark Lore from the infernal pact. This one starts useful and scales nicely, making this the strongest pact for most of the game.

Gift of Knowledge – makes meditation much better. Great for the party encyclopedia, not very helpful if you don’t take ranks in knowledge skills.

Lingering Madness – add a wisdom damage rider, will negates. The damage is small, though could soften up will saves for future abilities. Better off just using curse of entropy, it will be a bigger penalty by this level. You could stack them, but you’ll burn your invocations too fast.

Soulfire Master (Des) – burns CON instead of normal backlash. No failure on backlash but has an increased chance (50%). Bonuses using the soulfire feat. I can’t rate it since I wrote it.

Unseen Horror (TK) – Trade out bonus feats for an unseen servant and the ability to augment your servant in various ways. There is some real utility here, but the thaumaturge will feel the loss of the feats given their tiny talent pool. The more focused you are on a limited set of abilities, the more you can afford it, but it is going to be a tough call for the limited capabilities of the unseen horror at the levels you can start stacking on multiple. A grappling hook or the ability to slip through cracks are cool and flavorful, but you could have grabbed warp and unseeing teleport instead.

SPHERES

Every sphere has its uses and as a full caster you can use them to their fullest extent. The best spheres for a thaumaturge to focus on are ones that depend heavily on scaling CL effects and saves, since you have the means to push yours higher than anyone else..

Alteration - grabbing this and the right talents can get you movement modes early, enhanced senses, size alteration, etc. Versatility that you can use. For melee types, this is even more key, since you can pick up natural attacks this way and you will want as many as possible, plus you can get pounce to boot. Strength melee loves the size boosts, but everyone can enjoy fly speeds and natural armor.

Conjuration - a very strong school and even dipping your toes in can yield significant benefits, granting an ally not dissimilar to an eidolon. You are one feat away from the strongest companions possible, having yours meet or exceed your HD.The feat is nice, but no longer lets you boosts companion HD. You still get an extra body, which is nice.

Creation - Creation is limited by only your imagination and your spell points. Forbidden lore will get you bigger creations earlier, so that’s a plus. The sooner you can drop a wall around an enemy caster to break line of sight the better. The greatest drawback is that the at-will is weak, so anything interesting requires spell points.

Dark – Gives some fair debuffing and battlefield control which could be combined well with the right race (fetchlings for example can get see in darkness from feats).

Death – Ghost strike gives some powerful debuffs and you will have the save DCs to make them stick, especially with the unspeakable pact. You also can create the biggest, most powerful undead of any class, so this is a great choice. Ghost strike also works well with magus-dippers.

Destruction – You can make far use of this, especially on a melee build, but if you really want to specialize here, why not play an elementalist?

Divination – probably necessary if you are playing the role of primary arcanist. There are some good talents to boost sense, though nothing in the sphere really plays to your strengths. Necessary if you need to read magic writing for rituals and such. Basic divinations take way too long without spell points, but there are some good sense options. Better for an incanter than you, given that it expands its usefulness primarily based on how many talents you have to be able to detect and see through different things.

Enhancement – way too many enhancement bonuses here for my preference, since they won’t stack with common items. That said, cripple and steal senses are fun debuffs and with your CL boosts you can have GREAT fun with animated objects. Want a squadron of flying cannons? Animate object, bestow intelligence, and mass enhancement. You can easily animate an entire ship by level 10! Combine with conjuration and the mounted caster feat for another way to have fun, buffing your mount into a combat beast. A solid choice for building around.

Fate – All-purpose buffs, protection from alignment, and rerolls. The best uses are going to burn through spell points, but clever players can have fun with judgement. A useful sphere for playing support.

Illusion – another sphere limited only by your creativity. Worth picking up even if you just do it to get invisibility for shoring up your defenses. If you do, use the vanisher drawback so it only takes one talent. Not a prime choice for heavy focus, if you want to do that, play a fey adept.

Life – the problem with life is that you tend to get greater benefit from putting more talents into it than from higher CL. Grab it to be able to cover the role and use healing wands and such if your party needs you to, but don’t go deep.

Light – the base ability isn’t much to write home about, but a few of the talents are worth considering, especially repelling light to keep enemy melee off you and revealing light for dealing with invisibility. Bound light is a must have as well and makes for an excellent spellcraft combo with blinding light to completely hose a target (provided your party has ranged attacks so as not to blind themselves when taking advantage of the situation. Encompassing light is a great melee buff you both you and your companion as well. Remember that you can only have one source of actual size increase and one of virtual size increase at one time. This provides the virtual increase, alteration the actual, allowing you to act VERY large. No good direct damage here, but surprisingly strong otherwise.

Mind – will save or lose: the sphere. You have the oomph to make these stick, but so does the eliciter, who doesn’t risk tanking his abilities to do so. Grab a couple, charm and dominate are nice, but you shouldn’t make this the heart of your abilities. If you grab any, grab the expanded charm talent to make sure it's useable unless you are in a campaign that guarantees you don’t need it.

Nature – there are some gems here, though you’ll have to ask if you are better off playing a geomancer instead. If only taking one package, earth offers the excellent forge earth talent for minimal investment.

Protection – another good support sphere, allowing you to apply long-term buffs out of combat and soften enemy attacks during combat. Hr/level duration on aegis effects make them perfect bases for spellcrafting, if that isn’t too cheesy for you table (anchor haste on your AC buff, etc.).

Telekinesis – the art of lifting things with your mind and smacking them against other things. You can get better use out of hostile lift than anyone, which can shut-down an enemy without ranged abilities, and can get more items in the air faster as well. Note that Adam Meyers has said that dancing reach weapons should still threaten larger areas, so you can manage some impressive threatened area with combat reflexes. Powerful telekinesis is a must ASAP. If telekinetic maneuvers didn’t eat so many penalties I would recommend them as well, but that -5 really hurts. Still, forbidden lore makes you better at it then anyone else. I wouldn’t specialize in bullrushes though, an air warrior elementalist does that better. Tripping may be a good idea, since tripping feats also help you make use of a field of dancing weapons, though if you are tripping with dancing weapons anyway you are better of using them rather than giving them a will save AND having to beat CMD. Don’t forget quick draw to bring them out fast enough!

Time – Teammates and conjured companions will love an extra attack from level 1 (works best with the easy focus boon) and you have some very solid save-based abilities such as aging, slow, and steal time (which is just wonderful, daze enemy plus extra action, yes please!). This one loves good teamwork, so can be very powerful in the right group. If you have no martial characters to buff, you lose a bit, but there’s still something to be had here.

War – the mass buff/debuff sphere. Going to be more campaign dependent than most, since the buffs are relatively small to balance the fact that they can cover huge areas, making them amazing for games with large, martial heavy parties or when employing large forces (mass combat rules perhaps?). The rally talents are useful as well, being immediate actions that can, for example, grant a re-rolled save. You may want to look at them for supporting your party, taking the drawback to remove the ability to use totems and thus saving you the action of placing a totem to have ready for rallying. If you keep your totems, look at combining some using spellcrafting for vastly more efficient buffing.

Warp – tactical teleportation is awesome. So is having a panic button to get away. Offensive uses are limited though, keeping this from a higher rating. There aren’t that many saves or powerfully scaling abilities to warrant focusing a thaumaturge on, but even the basic ability is nice to have just in general. Teleportation beacon can save the party as well, so consider getting it via item if you can fit it in.

FEATS

A note on Metamagic: most metamagic feats work with the spheres system, increasing casting time and costing spell points equal to their level adjustment. This gets expensive quickly, but metamagic is powerful. I recommend metamagic rods given the limited spell points you will have, but grabbing still spell or silent spell could save your bacon if you took certain drawbacks, neatly getting you out of the occasional situation the drawback hurts you in.

Spheres Feats:

Advanced Magical Training - a multiclassing feat, letting you get ½ CL for non-casting class levels. Obviously the use of this feat is entirely build dependent. The more levels you spend elsewhere, the more you need it.

Arcane Empowerment - this is for traditional casters dipping their toes in spheres, not for you.

Artificery - it’s the master craftsman feat plus. You aren’t limited to what crafting feats you can apply it to and you get +2 to the skill. Not really for you, though needed as a prerequisite for dedicated crafters.

Artificery, Improved - you no longer need the base sphere to craft an item. Useful for a dedicated crafter. Probably want to be intelligence based to boost spellcraft, though if you choose a profession skill for artificery then wisdom based builds work just as well. Crafting is going to be group, DM, and campaign dependent, so take it if it works.

Basic Magic Training - you don’t qualify.

Cantrips - you get an array of minor magical abilities that mimic traditional cantrips. Note that detect magic is not on the list. Adam Meyers has stated that it was omitted deliberately, due to personal preference to not have at-will magic detection. Ask your DM. If you are playing with traditional casters, they have it anyway but there is no reason for you not to.

Circle Casting - you can aid another to increase CL, with a large enough group getting up to +5 CL max. Not that great in combat, but powerful for long duration out of combat spells. It would be blue, but it depends on other people's builds unless you can get leadership or another source of minions. Even then, it requires CL 10, so it isn’t happening early.

Contingency - combine with the right sphere and this is a life saver in a very literal way. Check out teleportation beacon, for example.

Counterspell - you can spend a spell point to dispel an ongoing effect or as a readied action to counterspell. Dispel is a thing casters are expected to have, so this feat is good if no one else in your group can cover it.

Counterspell, Improved - spend more spell points to hit more effects or an area. If you have counterspell, you probably want this too.

Counterspell, Greater - you can hit artifacts now and dispel in a 40’ radius. Less necessary than its antecedents.

Counterspell Mastery - counterspell as an immediate action. Shutting down an enemy caster’s big moment can make a tough encounter a cake-walk. If you are in the counterspelling field, get it.

Craft Rituals - rituals are a way to get traditional magic effects that are absent from the spheres system. Useful if traditional magic is absent from your campaign, not worth it otherwise.

Create Spell Book - if you use the spell crafting system heavily, this will be nice to have, but given the more focused nature of your specialization, you can probably cover what you need to without it.

Delay Magic - could be useful in the right situation, but requires planning and foresight.

Extra Invocations – good if you find yourself running out, since you really don’t get that many.

Extra Magic Talent - The feat you take whenever you don’t have something you really need. Every feat should be weighed against this one, since you can never have too many talents.

Extra Spell Pool - 2 more spell points each time. You can never have too many.

Fool Magic - a neat, unique feat. If you go Cha-based then you can make good use of this (or if you trait bluff over to Intelligence). Prerequisites are harsh though. Good for an intrigue type campaign.

Master Artisan – add forbidden lore bonus to the CL cap for one craft feat. Niche.

Master of Cosmos – do you have the conjuration sphere? Then you have this feat. Take it, take it NOW! This makes you the strongest conjuration user in the game, especially combined with boosts like the human FCB. Note that Adam Meyers has stated that you should extrapolate the chart for CLs above 20. Errata nerfed this to being a bonus to attack and skill checks equal to your forbidden lore bonus. Still a necessary feat if you are using conjuration, but only a shadow of its former self.

Master of Death – Boosting your undead control cap is handy.

Mounted Magician – Are you mounted and using the enhancement sphere heavily? Then it’s great. Otherwise stay far away.

Ritual Caster – you don’t need the base sphere to perform a ritual. Campaign dependent, as with all the ritual feats.

Ritual Master – Use your MDB instead of your CL for ritual CL. You have full-CL unless dipping, so not really worthwhile.

Spellcrafting – you can mix and match spheres when creating spells. Some effects will require DM judgement, but this is potentially amazing depending on your creativity in combining effects.

Sphere Focus – pick a sphere, +1 DC. Good for focused casters, which covers many thaumaturge builds. Small boost, but you should grab it when you can if you focus on a save-heavy sphere.

Wand Wielder – requires CL10 like a number of feats, lets you use your CL if higher than your wand’s CL. Wands are a great way to expand your options and low CL wands are cheap.

Blessing/Blight (Versatility, Mastery), Elicit strike, Extra: (Arsenal Trick, Bestial Trait, Emotion, Invocations, Mystic Combat, Nexus Powers, Psionics, Secrets, Shadowstuff), Greater (Hypnosis, Shadowmark), lingering link & greater, Mind Opener, Occult Savant, Perceptive Psionics, Shadowblast, Superior Rebuff,Trance - for class features you don’t have.


Combat Feats:

Blindfight – Not a prime pick, but it’s nice filler if it comes up in your campaign with some regularity.

Dazzling Display – Gives an area debuff to your intimidate skill, but you have other things to do with your actions. Requires weapon focus, which ain’t grand either.

Dervish Dance – You are taking this ASAP if going melee as a magus in a game without Path of War. Others can skip it.

Dimensional Agility/Dervish/Savant – ask your DM if you can use the Warp Sphere for this feat line, otherwise you can’t take it. Agility doesn’t do anything, but Dervish is fun and you may as well grab savant if you are this far in. Note that a magus dip lets you simulate part of this (teleporting and full attacking) with spell combat, otherwise it would be rated higher.

From Adam Meyers:

“Personally, I would require Quick Teleport as well, and require them to spend that spell point each time they use it, but otherwise I don't see why not.”

If your DM makes you spend a spellpoint, I wouldn’t bother, just use spell combat.

Escape Route (teamwork) – Nice if you are using conjuration, especially if mounted. Otherwise group dependent.

Improved/Greater Bull rush – Nice if you want to focus on destruction’s airblast or telekinetic maneuvers, probably not worth the trouble.

Improved/Greater Trip – for area control with telekinetic weapons. Nice early, loses its luster as the game progresses and more things fly and CMDs go up.

Improved Initiative - always good, especially for getting off BFC or a critical debuff.

Kirin Style/Kirin Strike – for Int-based builds, you want this, though no one else does. A swift action for double your casting mod on your attacks is fun. Doesn’t help your AOEs, but makes your other attacks more potent. Obviously only for those with a martial focus.

Mounted Combat – Good choice if using conjuration to get a mount or feats to get an animal companion, useless otherwise.

Point Blank Shot – It’s not that you want it, it’s that you have to take it for precise shot. Pay the ranged combat feat tax and move on.

Power Attack – strength based melee might want this, though I’m not overly eager for it on a medium BAB chassis. 

Precise Shot – The -4 for firing into melee kills at low levels. If starting at high levels, you may be able to get by without it, since you are targeting touch AC. Obviously not needed if not making ranged attacks.

Weapon Focus – Gives +1 to hit. Meh.

Martial Training (Path of War by Dreamscarred Press) – there are some interesting options here. Really, this requires a guide of its own.

Deadly Agility (Path of War by Dreamscarred Press) – adds dex mod to melee damage.  A magus dip or natural attacker with fire-wielder can be pretty wicked with this.

General Feats: Just a few notables, since there are far too many to review completely.

Animal Ally – If you want an animal companion, you can get one at full strength for the cost of three feats (nature soul, animal ally, and boon companion). The list is limited, but you can get a mount or defender that will scale decently.

Boon Companion – Only if you have an animal companion whose level needs boosting, such as from animal ally.

Combat Casting – You don’t want to be in melee range, but it happens and sometimes the 5’ step isn’t getting you out. This will save you from having to reduce your CL to get your next spell off, which may save your hide. Of course, you could just use a reduced CL teleport from the warp sphere to get away, so it isn’t required. Very good for melee types.

Craft (x) – Crafting is heavily campaign dependent. Crafting with Spheres of Power changes the relative value of the feats dramatically as well, making rings, wands, and staves much more important than before, while drastically reducing wondrous items. No ratings; if you are savvy enough to build a crafter you can figure out your priorities here.

 

Divine Protection – Errata destroyed this, forget about it.

Eldritch Heritage – This one is all over the map, depending on which heritage you pick. You can grab a familiar or some free metamagic, so might be worth a look. Only really an option for Cha-based builds though.

Familiar Bond – get a familiar without any of the benefits. Nice for having a place to dump your backlash penalties with channel punishment. Ask your DM if this qualifies you for improved familiar. If so, there are some good choices and you don’t have any bonuses to lose by taking it. Gets the full benefits if you take improved familiar bond or get a level in a familiar-granting class (like taking arcane bloodline on an incanter dip). A familiar for two feats, just like eldritch heritage, but you don’t need 13 CHA and the first feat is slightly better, but you don’t get the familiar bonuses.

Improved Familiar– plenty of interesting options here, like the faerie dragon that has its own casting, which is useful for having it use wands and such.  

Improved Familiar Bond – you get all the abilities for your familiar. Nice if you plan on using the familiar, not as great is just using it as a place to dump your backlash though. Still, with the right familiar this is as good as improved initiative with some side benefits. See if it’s required for improved familiar.

Iron Will – weak feat, prerequisite for familiar bond.

Nature Soul – Terrible, but required for Animal Ally.

Uncanny Concentration –Green if mounted or in a campaign where it comes up often, otherwise skip it.

Shape Veil (Akashic Mysteries by Dreamscarred Press) – you have a pile of options here granting varying degrees of power and utility. You certainly won’t have the means to push them to their limits, but properly chosen they can be most helpful. Really requires a guide of its own. Natural attack melee want to look even closer, since several veils give natural attacks. Look over here for more information.

Open (X) Chakra (Akashic Mysteries by Dreamscarred Press) – to go along with the above.

TRAITS

Too many to list. Basically, grab whatever you need to get the skill access you want and buff anything you feel lacking. You can get +1 to any save, +2 concentration, any skill as a class skill, etc. Interesting ones let you move a physical skill to Wisdom (Wisdom in Flesh) or prevent you from being flat footed (Defensive Strategist). If going intelligence based, you can move any charisma based skill to intelligence, some with more than one option so you don’t run afoul of the 1 trait/category rules. Some of the more interesting traits are listed here and a guide is started here.

DRAWBACKS

Spell points looking scarce? Trying to match a particular character from fiction or the standard magic system? Looking for limitations on your arcane power? Drawbacks are the answer! You may, with DM approval, take a number of drawbacks. These can be general drawbacks, taken at creation, which can either be traded in for boons or to increase the rate you accumulate spell points as you level (1/level maximum with five drawbacks), or sphere-specific drawbacks, taken when first gaining a given sphere, which grant a bonus talent in that sphere. Sphere specific drawbacks can also be removed by ‘paying’ for the bonus talent later on, making them a good option early on for expanding your breadth of abilities.

General Drawbacks

Addictive Casting - make a fort save every time you use a spell point, with an increasing DC or gain an addiction. This one counts as two drawbacks, but it is really nasty. Could be workable if you have lots of downtime and few encounters per day, but I count it mostly as NPC or word-building material.

Draining Casting - Take unhealable non-lethal damage every time to use a sphere ability. It works as the basis for a blood-mage type character combined with the Fortified Casting boon. Great if all you do is summon conjuration companions or raise a few undead each morning, but anyone that needs to cast almost every round (blasters, debuffers, magus-dippers) will hate it.

Extended Casting - All your sphere abilities take longer to use, increasing times by one step. Excellent for dedicated minion masters or builds that do all their casting out of combat. Taking a full round for anything in-combat hurts and it’s even worse if you already are lengthening casting times with spellcrafting.

Focus Casting - Casting requires a focus object, with penalties similar to the wizard’s arcane bond. Depends on your DM. If disarms and prisons feature heavily in your campaigns, avoid it. Note that you can make the DC for your casting lower by voluntarily reducing the CL of the ability you are using, which can help ensure you get the ability off in a pinch (best if the ability isn’t save-dependent). Grab the warp sphere and teleportation beacon to make sure you can call it back to you if lost.

Magical Signs - some tell-tale lets everyone in 30’ knows you are casting and what the nature of the spell is. Avoid it if you are trying to be stealthy, but most of the time the ball of death you are hurling will give you away anyhow.

Material Casting - every ability costs money based on CL. The exact nature of the material spent is dependent on your DM. Another one that is fine for builds that use a few long-lasting abilities.

Painful Magic - fort save or be sickened every time you cast, escalating to nauseated if already sickened. Duration is short. Almost a freebie if doing the blood-mage combo (draining casting + fortified casting) since you will have such high CON you won’t miss the save often. For everyone else, you can pull it off if you optimize your Fort save, but you risk bad rolls stealing actions at key times. As is often the case, it doesn’t hurt out of combat casting at all.

Prepared Caster - You have to assign spell points to spheres at the beginning of each day. Since you will likely focus heavily on only a few spheres, this can be a decent option. For example, set aside just enough SP to keep your conjuration companion going all day, then dump the rest in your other main sphere. More diversified builds should be cautious though.

Skilled Casting - you have to make a perform, craft, or profession skill check or suffer reduced caster level. This meshes well with many concepts and characters from fiction. It might be tough early on, but skill checks can be optimized, especially after you have a few levels under your belt. An interesting option. Note that humans and half-elves can get skill focus at level 1 and other races offer various skill boosting options. Also note that the chosen skill might carry drawbacks, like perform (sing) doesn’t work in a silence effect or (dance) if you are immobilized. One rank, 18 in the stat, favored class bonus, a trait, and skill focus get you to 12 at level 1, so a racial bonus or another skill feat pretty well gets you there.

Somatic Casting - you require a free hand to cast and now have to pay attention to arcane spell failure chances. Taken once allow light armor, twice allows no armor. Avoid it if your DM likes grappling enemies, but otherwise it is a strong option for taking once, provided you aren’t dipping cleric or another class and getting better armor. Note that only armor is mentioned, so bucklers are fine. If your DM decides otherwise, mithral bucklers have 0% ASF and are reasonably priced. Grab still spell sometime, just in case.

Verbal Casting - you now have verbal components. If you have skilled casting in a skill that requires vocalization, this is a freebie. Not so good for sneaks, good for others. Grab silent spell for emergencies.

Wild Magic - 10% chance to add a rod of wonder effect to your casting. Hilarious, but your party may hate you. If your DM uses the rod of wonder table as suggested, this is actually quite good so long as you make sure your effects happen AT enemies, since most of the options on the table impact the target. Not recommended if you do healing and buffing. If your DM uses a different table your mileage may vary. Unrated since it is so concept and DM dependent.

Sphere Specific Drawbacks

Only going to cover the notable ones here.

Alteration:

Beast Soul - can’t use the blank form. Decreases your versatility, but gets a form talent so may be worthwhile. If you only want to grab movement modes, use this to get elemental form, which is really nice for a single talent. Combine with lycanthropic to get the talent to increase duration and you can get most of what you really need from alteration unless you are a natural attacker.

Lycanthropic - you can only use alteration on yourself. Fine early on, keeping it will depend on your party composition. Sometimes it’s handy to be able to give everyone flight for a bit, but party buffing isn’t necessarily your job. If you can be selfish, take it.

Conjuration:

Elongated Summoning - summoning your companion takes a minute. If you grab the talents for an all-day companion, this becomes trivial. If you want to use your companion as a combat summons, then leave it alone.

Creation:

Limited Creation – choose either alter or create. Turning a door into candy to break through more easily is too much fun and you certainly don’t want to give up creation, so pass.

Material Focus – only one type of material? Workable. Stone or iron are likely the most versatile options, though you can go nuts with thematics here, since even shadowstuff is an option (for all your shadowcrafting needs). I’ve even seen this as part of a ‘candy-mage’ build, which is too awesome not to mention (if the candy-mage originator reads this, drop a note to get credit!).

Dark:

Meld into Darkness – You can’t create darkness, but can meld into any dim or darker light. In the right campaign, this could be useful, not really a fan in general.

Death:

Deathful Touch – ghost strike is melee only. Workable on magus builds, I hate giving up the range though.

Necromatic Limit – lose either ghost strike or reanimate. If you only want minions OR debuffs, this is great. Depends on your concept.

Destruction: The latter two are better for another class dabbling in destruction, not for a specialist.

Aligned Combatant - scales damage down based on how close the target is to your alignment. Campaign dependent, would be great in Wrath of the Righteous, but terrible in many others.

Energy Focus - can only use on blast type. This is for being dedicated to a theme. This guide is for practical optimization, not thematics. If you take force or crystal you can get by, I suppose.

Destructive Touch - you now have no range, destructive blast is touch only. Locks you out of several blast shapes and the remaining ones have to start adjacent to you. Blech. With a magus dip this can be less bad, but I still would be reluctant to lose your ranged option.

Divination:

Limited Divination – Lose sense or divine. If you just want a few helpful options, you can lose divine and its choice of ridiculous casting times or spell points cost coupled with immobility.

Hidden Magic – lose the detect and read magic base abilities. Detect magic is nice to have (though much better in cantrip form rather than 10-minute casting or daily resource form). Okay if someone else can cover it easily.

Enhancement:

Bodily Enhancement - can’t target equipment or objects. Maybe very early until you buy it back.

Personal Magics - can’t target others. Be greedy, let the full casters buff the party.

Fate:

Neutrality – can’t use hallow. Basically, you give up protection from (alignment) for a bonus talent. If you are getting the effect consistently from elsewhere then it’s a good idea.

Personal Fate – can only target self. Okay if only dipping your toes in for a couple buffs, but you lose any of the debuffing potential of the sphere.

Illusion

Disappearance - you have to take invisibility as your bonus for this drawback and can’t use illusions for anything else. Nice for being sneaky, but illusions can be very powerful. Worth a look, but might be better to get invisibility via wand and keep your versatility.

Personal Illusions - can only put illusions on yourself. Not worth it.

Life

Limited Restoration – lose restore or cure/invigorate. The great strength of the base life sphere is how well it covers your healing needs with only the base sphere. This gimps your effects terribly. If you have to take it, keep restore and get HP healing from items, since ability damage and status conditions are harder to heal anyway.

Regenerate – doable if you already have someone in the party that can cover healing, but if you are picking up the sphere you should probably be able to use it on others as well.

Light

Touch of Light – another one to knock range down to touch. Some effects, like blinding light, would be really annoying to have only at touch range. Others would be okay. Choose carefully.

Mind

Animal Shaman – affect only animals. Doesn’t grant a talents and doesn’t bar you from taking expanded mind, which you want right away if using the mind sphere, so this is really just for NPCs and dedicated druids types.

Empath – can’t plant suggestions. Suggestions aren’t bad, but if there is another charm you prefer to start with then go for it.

Nature

Nature Spirit – Can only use spirit powers, not geomancing. Terrible. You’ll be able to talk to anything, if you take the right talents, but lose all offensive abilities. Nope.

Protection

Limited Protection – Lose ward or aegis. If using protection primarily for long-term buffs, losing ward doesn’t hurt too much since you will be using other actions in combat. Not sure I would give up aegis though.

Protected Soul – aegis is self only. You don’t get many talents, buffing everyone else shouldn’t be your job.

Aligned Protection – the sphere only works against a certain alignment. Not very good generally, but fine in certain campaigns where 99% of enemies will be of a particular alignment (Wrath of the Righteous, for example).

Time

Personal Time – self only. Lose all the fun offensive uses? No thanks. If you only are grabbing time to haste yourself on a melee build it works though.

Altered Time – lose haste and slow options. You have other debuffs than slow and the usefulness of haste is dependent on how much of the party needs full attacks. Workable for caster-types.

Telekinesis

Limited Telekinesis - You can only use telekinesis on one type of material. Only if you are building a themed character. Hostile telekinesis is right out the window with this one.

War

Battlefield Manipulation – lose totems, rally effects work within a radius from you. Pretty nice, actually, since you no longer have to spend an action setting up a totem and rally effects are fairly action efficient. That said, most of the sphere options are totems, so you have a pretty limited selection.

Solo Combatant – can only rally yourself. Reroll saves, block critical hits, damage attackers, you have some decent options here. Combine with the above for some decent defenses on a martial build.

Warp

Limited Warp - can only warp to areas that meet certain conditions (light, dark, water, etc).  You have the potential to be able to start things on fire or create water, but I still can’t recommend it since warp suffers too much if you have to spend a turn setting it up.

Personal Warp - selfishness again. Nice way to get a talent to boost your mobility if you don’t care about playing chessmaster or using the unwilling version.

Bender – can’t teleport. Just buy a handy haversack already!

Weather

Focused Weather – only affect one weather category. Meh. Wind or rain are your best bets, but I wouldn’t go this route except for thematic reasons.

BOONS

Here is the payoff (other than spell points) for your drawbacks, each boon costing two drawbacks. Not many options, but worth looking at.

Deathful Magic - you get a CL boost when injured, +1 below 50% HP, +2 under 25%. I prefer to avoid being able to use this one, but things go south and that extra juice may make a difference.

Easy Focus - you can maintain concentration as a move action. Required if focusing on spheres that use concentration abilities heavily, allowing you to save precious SP. If casting out of combat or not using concentration abilities, skip it.

Empowered Abilities - Bonus CL if you get low on spell points. Interesting way to boost yourself through the end of the day and doesn't require being near death.

Fortified Casting - Con as your casting stat! Too bad you can’t take it without the draining casting drawback. If you qualify, take it.

Overcharge - you can add +2 CL at the cost of being fatigued, then exhausted, then collapsing. Neat way to boost your power for finishing that final fight or escaping in an emergency. Better if you have a way to quickly recover from the conditions inflicted. Great if focused on long-term buffs and other out of combat casting.

EQUIPMENT

The section so many handbooks omit or never complete. This won’t be comprehensive, just going to shoot for the highlights.

Mundane Gear:

Armor - chain shirt, upgrading to mithral chain shirt once you get over 18 DEX. You could go for a mithral breastplate, but you would need medium armor proficiency. If you get heavier armor, feel free to use it, though watch the max Dex, especially if you want to have good Dex for ranged attacks.

Shield - get a masterwork buckler to pile enchantments on. It’s cheap AC. Doesn’t work for magus dips or natural attackers though. If you get proficiency from elsewhere, grab a heavier shield so long as it doesn’t interfere with anything.

Weapons – Caster-types can grab a longspear to discourage enemies from charging you or just have a dagger to hold the defending enhancement to boost your AC. An elf might want a bow for long range. A magus dipper will want a scimitar (if Str-based or dervish-dancing) or a rapier (deadly agility) for the crit range, making it keen ASAP. Dipping symbat gets you rapiers, another good choice.

Misc - Rope, waterskin, mirror, marbles, nothing here but your usual adventurer kit.

Spheres of Power Magic Items: Spheres of Power has a re-worked magic item system to correspond with the casting system. Some games may use it exclusively, but many will use them alongside traditional items. Ultimately, you want a good staff.

Scrolls - you need to have the base sphere to use a scroll without UMD, so these are mainly good for niche talents that aren’t worth picking up to have all the time, but may save you some heartache that one time in the campaign it comes up.

Wondrous Items - great for constant effects or talents that you need once or twice a day or for at-will charged items (like normal wands, sphere wands work differently).

Rods - for at-will abilities. Great for shoring up your weak points. By the upper levels you should have a golf-bag full of these for various situations.

Rings - all continuous items fall here, boosting the utility of the craft rings feat. Mostly relevant if you are only using sphere items since normal wondrous items are not available for you stat boosts and other necessary effects.

Wands - another way to grab talents you don’t have for base spheres you do. Note that the DC of wand abilities is set by the wands level and your casting mod. Wands come with a small pool of spell points that the caster has to recharge, max 1/day recharge rate.

Staves - staves work differently with spheres. Basically, they carry an enhancement bonus, like a weapon, that applies to a certain sphere. They can also be made to carry additional talents or apply to other spheres, with costs similar to weapon enchantments. You want the biggest bonus to destruction you can afford (+5 is the max, +10 equivalent with other abilities). Expanding it to cover your other favorite combat spheres is a good investment. Carry separate staves for out of combat spheres since combining them gets expensive.

Traditional Magic Items: Mostly, you want the standard array of stat and save boosters. Your casting should primarily be augmented with Spheres items, with the exception of metamagic rods. Note that you can use traditional scrolls and wands that correspond to your spheres and talents, though some adjudication is required from your DM.

Metamagic Rods - these are handy. Because your equivalent spell level will go up as your CL does, unless you lower it willingly reducing your effects and save DCs, you will generally want to highest grade rod available for your desired effect (why extend a power if you have to cut its duration in half to use the rod?). Dazing is of course wonderful, empower and maximize are nice, rime is great if you grab the cold abilities. If it’s good for a normal caster, it’s probably good for you.

Handy Haversack - you don’t likely have that much strength.

Muleback Cords - the other solution to carrying capacity.

Stat boosters - keep your casting stat maxed, obviously, and other stats depending on build.

Gauntlets of Skilled Maneuver - nice if you focus on a maneuver (dancing weapons for tripping, air blast for bullrushing, etc).


Pauldrons of the Bull - only if you bullrush. If you do, these are amazing.

Amulet of Mighty Fists – only for the natural attackers, but essential for them since you can enhance all of your attacks at once.

MULTICLASSING

As has been frequently observed in this guide, judicious multiclassing can be a significant boon. Due to the importance of caster level in the Spheres of Power system, it is strongly advised that you do not lose any caster levels unnecessarily. This severely limits the wise options for multiclassing. That said, there are options that can make significant contributions to your build. Note that losing more than three levels will cost you the final upgrade to forbidden lore, making three the most you want in a level 20 build (not that most games go to 20 or spend much time there if they do).

Spheres of Power Classes:

Incanter drops a die size and two skill points, but gets 3 talents per 2 levels and has either bonus feats or specialization options. Specialization gets you an extra caster level and talent, so is great for boosting your favorite sphere. Using your other points to pick a domain that grants good base bonuses, like darkness (blindfight), liberation (freedom of movement), or travel (+10 movement speed), can be beneficial as well. Pay attention to which feats are lost by specializing; if you are going to lose all the feats from the levels you plan on taking, then you might as well spend all the specialization points. A one level dip here is required for getting the highest possible CL in a given sphere, if that is your goal.

Fey Adept and Soul Weaver are great classes, but offer little for a thaumaturge dip since their scaling abilities would be largely wasted.

Archetypes (SoP):

Paladin Only there for a two level dip to get divine grace, then grab advanced magic training so you only lose one CL. Getting your casting stat to saves is awesome, though the other benefits are limited.

Sphere Bard A few rounds of performance aren’t worth it. Losing caster levels is bad! Cha-based.

Sphere Cleric medium armor and shields don’t hurt, plus you can get a useful weapon with the right deity. Domains give an extra talent and has two good saves, so it’s not a bad dip. Archetype diving might turn something up, like the crusader getting a bonus feat from a limited list (good if you want heavy armor, for example).

Sphere Druid medium armor (non-metal only) and shields can help, plus you get some weapon proficiencies, scimitar being the most interesting. Animal companion won’t scale, but a domain would grant a talent. Go cleric instead unless dying for skill points.

Sphere Wizard Upon further review, actually a very solid dip, since they get two talents at level one and a free specialization, which grants a talent also, giving three talents for one level. You like having a familiar as well to use with channel punishment as well. Great dip as long as you don’t get saddled with the recommended tradition, potentially worthwhile even then.

Sphere-casting Archetypes (Expanded Options):

Sphere arcanist gives a floating talent, which makes them rather unique, but they suffer from the dearth of talents just as much as the incanter. The floating talents would require two levels, so stay away unless you really want the arcana you get at first level.

Sphere Bloodrager No! Bad player! No! (Could perhaps be usable on a melee build. Still wouldn’t do it.)

Sphere Inquisitor has nothing for a short dip that you wouldn’t be better off getting somewhere else. Losing caster levels is bad!

Sphere Magus is excellent for a non-natural attack melee build. Grab two levels for spell strike and spell combat. Spell combat also neatly reduces the need to the easy focus boon. Not great for others though, since you do lose a caster level. See the question and answer to confirm that spellstrike works with all known applicable talents.

Combine this with the devourer archetype for scary CL spikes as you full-attack.

Sphere Oracle There are some interesting things you can pick up with a short dip here, like Cha to AC, or martial weapons. You can probably find something worthwhile. Curses do scale at half-rate with non-oracle levels, so they aren’t as bad as they could be. Cha-based.

Sphere Summoner doesn’t give you anything you need. Master of cosmos makes gives you a more powerful companion, while the summoner gets some neat tricks, putting them together will just detract from both.

Sphere Witch is in the same boat as the shaman, minus armor proficiency, HD, and skills. Int-based.

SPELLCRAFTING

A neat little option in the advanced magic section to allow you to combine abilities at the cost of longer casting times and spell point costs. You can only have a number made up to your casting mod, so make them count, though you can grab the spellbook feat to keep more handy. Without the spellcrafting feat, you can’t combine different spheres, but even with the limitations you should make a few, updating them as needed as you gain talents.

For destruction, I would recommend a simple combo of your favorite blasts, like force blast as the base (best damage type barring vulnerabilities) with crystal blast (to entangle, which helps offset the AC increase from prone). Combining the two is only complexity 1, so you can use it as a full-round action at no cost. Add your favorite shape in for the base ability for a second spell, say the orb shape, and you have a wide AOE blast that will do good almost never resisted damage, even to incorporeals, while knocking them prone and entangling them, at the modest cost of losing your move action that round.

Note that with the spellcrafting feat, you can combine different spheres and most importantly, the base sphere sets the CL and saves of the finished spell. Charming crystal trap? Teleport an enemy and make them blink out of time for a bit? Go nuts. Just keep in mind the complexity, you don’t want to push your casting time past a full-round action for anything to be used in combat and the spell point cost for higher complexities gets painful fast. Also note that duration is set by the base ability, so look at abilities like protection (aegis) for anchoring shorter duration buffs to get them for 1 hr/level. Even without doing this, it’s great for doubling up buffs to save time in combat.

Note that all spells are subject to DM approval. This is especially relevant for getting tricky by extending short term buffs by stacking them on protection or other spheres with hour long options.
