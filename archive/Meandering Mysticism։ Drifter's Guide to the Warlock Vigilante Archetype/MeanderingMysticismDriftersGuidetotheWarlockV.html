1. Introduction

Hello, and welcome to this first foray into making a guide for Pathfinder material! I greatly enjoy reading guides other players have made, as I have a terrible habit of making far more characters than I will ever get to actually play. As an enormous sucker for the Vigilante, seeing the Warlock got my hopes soaring high for an “all-day” blaster. I was let down rather abruptly upon realizing that Mystic Bolts are just flat out BAD if you treat them as your main focus. This, combined with sharing the same name as a class from various D&D editions (despite sharing almost no similarities) put the Vigilante’s Warlock archetype in a rough spot, teetering on the edge of “worthless archetypes” like so many others. The archetype isn’t unsalvageable (like that other class we dare not speak the name of practically is), though! Well, it’s still mediocre if you’re sticking solely to first-party material, but even then it’s at least doable! You have spells! I make no claims to be any kind of expert, I can’t cover everything, and you’re going to find that I’m writing partially off my own opinions, but hopefully I can provide an ample overview and help you squeeze out as much as the class has to offer you!

Just a warning, if you want advice for the organized play of Pathfinder Society, I’m sad to say I’m not at all a fan of PFS or the majority of its rulings and won’t be putting any effort towards keeping this guide PFS-friendly. Take that as you will.

Rating System

Note: Due to just the sheer volume of options for every section, I can’t reasonably cover everything, but I will do my best to cover everything that I feel would be worth keeping note of. If you feel like something is missing, feel free to tell me!

Black (?): This option, for one reason or another, cannot be rated. What a mystery!

Red (*): This option is just really bad, or even intentionally hurts. Just don’t!

Orange (**): A sub-par option, you might find use in some specific cases, or for flavor.

Green (***): Good, solid options, you can’t go wrong here!

Blue (****): Amazing options! It’s not always perfect, but it’s always good!

Purple (*****): A class defining option. You want this. You NEED this.

2. Class features

HD and BaB: You get a d8 and 3/4th Bab progression, which is pretty average.

Saves: Your only bad save is Fortitude, which is unFORTunate, but not the end of the world.

Skills: Holy moly, even losing perception as a class skill, you’ve just got SO MANY class skills. You’re also an Int-based class with 4+Int skill points per level. You’re no skill monkey, but you’ll still be swimming in them!

Proficiencies: You get to cast in light armor, drop medium armor proficiency, and have the normal vigilante weapon proficiencies. I’m not gonna complain, but it feels odd that you kept martial weapon proficiency.

Spells: 6th level prepared spellcasting with a spellbook, using all 6th level and lower spells off the Wizard/Sorcerer list. You’re trading half of your talents for this, and it’s a good trade. I might be overrating 6th level casting, but this alone keeps a warlock treading water. It’s unfortunate you don’t get any spell discounts.

Dual Identity and Seamless Guise: It’s okay, and can give you some fun dynamics if your campaign allows it, but some GMs get huffy about it. For most cases, you could almost totally ignore it and the class would work just fine. If your GM tries to pull something dumb, like “oh, it’s not an intrigue campaign so you ignoring dual identity means you don’t get social talents”, just slap him with a book. Yes, someone’s GM actually tried to pull that.

Social Talents: These vary from “amazing boosts” to “only good if your GM is on board”. Even ignoring dual identity you’ll have plenty of options.

Vigilante Talents: Oof, as a Warlock, you get punched in the gut here, as you lose half of your talents. You have so many good talents to pick from, but you get so few! You get some really nice unique talents, as well.

Mystic Bolts (with 3pp): You get to pick from fire, cold, electricity, and acid initially, and get to use another energy type every six levels. It’s 1d6 damage plus 1/4th your level, you can use it in melee and at range with a hardlocked 30 ft range, and you’re always considered armed. You are EXTREMELY limited in what ways you can boost the power of this ability, and despite ignoring DR, are completely screwed over by elemental resistances. It’s a handy to have if you’re worried about losing your weapons, and a fantastic weapon for early on, but it quickly falls behind once enhanced composite longbows and such start coming into play.

Piercing Bolts: This is a REALLY good ability, even if it doesn’t totally save Mystic Bolts. At 3rd level, a single bolt per turn can be designated as hitting touch AC. At 5th level, every attack you make with Mystic Bolts is a touch attack. While your damage won’t be anything to write home about, it will be consistent! Your touch attack Mystic Bolts won’t work with Power Attack and Deadly Aim, but the wording sort of implied that didn’t work even before they became touch attacks.

Startling, Frightening, Stunning Appearance: I don’t like these three features very much, even on a regular vigilante. I’m being a bit unfair, since you can trigger them quite easily with spells, but they don’t feel like they fit the warlock. The DC to save against them being based on your dump stat really doesn’t help their case.

Vengeance Strike: It’s a boring but functional capstone, at least for the Warlock. Each of the three bonuses can be really nice, and if you want to pop out of invisibility with a bang, get your party to wait so you can study a target. Opening a fight with a guaranteed minimum dice roll of 3-5 likely means never missing that shot, and a +9d6-12d6 to a single hit is cool opening move.

Roles

As a warlock, you’re… Well, you’re in a really weird spot. Nothing you can do is something another class couldn’t normally do better.

Ranged Attacker (with 3pp): Due to the 30 ft range restriction on Mystic Bolts, you’re about on the level of someone using throwing weapons, and need the same feats. Thrown weapons is probably the most obnoxiously overtaxed combat style, but at least you don’t have to worry about quickdraw! Your pitiful damage scaling and inability to get through resistances with Mystic Bolts means you’re also going to be putting out less damage than even a basic archer. At least at later levels you can try to vary up your energy types if you know an enemy has no resistances to a particular element, but it’s really easy for you to get screwed over.

Melee skirmisher (with 3pp): Mystic Bolts don’t add your Str Modifier to damage when used as a melee attack, and you don’t have many ways to make Mystic Bolts worthwhile as anything but a short-ranged cantrip. If you use a real weapon, you might be able to skate by, but you’re going to be bad at it.

Utility caster: You’re not going to be as good at it as a full caster, but with the sorcerer/wizard spell list you’re still able to keep plenty of useful spells handy.

Item crafter: As a 6th level caster, you’re not going to be the best, but being Int-based means you can certainly do it if you somehow have feats to spare.

Skill monkey: You have a massive list of class skills. That said, you’re not going to be particularly good at skills in general compared to other classes, or even a base vigilante.

Skills

As mentioned in the class features section, you have a LOT of class skills. You’re down to 4+Int from 6+Int and drop some skills (losing perception? Ouch!), but you’re intelligence based, so you’re going to end up with more skill points than a base vigilante. Due to just HOW MANY skills there are to talk about, I’m going to consolidate some of them together. A fair warning that YOUR MILEAGE MAY VARY. Skills depend on your GM to make them relevant! As such, while for the most part something like Appraise or Heal are absolutely awful in most groups, maybe your GM has made them invaluable skills to have and altered it so that magic doesn’t make them totally irrelevant.

Class Skills

Spellcraft: As a spellcaster, shame on you if you don’t put points into this. You’ll need it to manage your spellbook and identify magical objects.

Knowledge (Arcana): This covers so many monsters it’s actually kind of ridiculous. Considering you’re an arcane caster, it’s probably also a good idea to know about what you’re doing too, don’tcha think?

Knowledge (Dungeoneering): The main target here is identifying Aberrations, though if your DM was a fan of Dragonquest you might get some mileage out of the ability to identify oozes. Unless you’ve got a lot of aberrations or spend a lot of time in dungeons (hello, Shattered Star) you’re not gonna get much from this.

Knowledge (Local): Adventurers don’t tend to need to know a whole hell of a lot about villages besides “where’s the tavern” and “who has the dangerous jobs”. There is, however, great value in being able to identify humanoids! The possible flavor text is always fun.

Knowledge (Nobility): An even more narrow version of Local, unless you’re in an intrigue heavy game that deals with nobility, just pass. Note: Even though the SRD doesn’t list this as a class skill, an FAQ specified that it SHOULD be one.

Appraise: Knock knock. Who’s that? Oh shit, it’s detect magic, here to take all of the appraise skill’s stuff. I suppose depending on your GM you could bump this up a little,

Swim and Climb: These are both Strength based, and often highly situational. A point in climb will probably at least cover early levels, but it isn’t a mandatory thing.

Acrobatics, Escape Artist, Stealth and Ride: Haha, cute, skills for stuff you can either do/cover with, spell slots. They’re all Dex-based, so if you do have the spare skill points and don’t want to rely entirely on your spellbook, you’re not going to be wasting points for the most part. The DC’s for Escape Artist are all pretty high, so that one in particular will require plenty of investment.

Bluff, Disguise, Diplomacy, and Intimidate: The whole social suite for the Vigilante, these all have potential uses to keep your identity or maneuver social situations… But since you’re likely going to have mediocre to bad Cha, and only ever have one social trait open for swaps, you might be better off passing.

Use Magic Device: One of the most powerful skills in pathfinder, and by now you probably know the drill. You can take a trait to make it key off your Int mod, but you also probably have most of the spells you’d want to use on your spell list.

Off-Class Skills

Perception: You lost this as a class skill, but it is the most-rolled skill in the game. At least, that’s how it’s been for all the games I’ve seen. You’re not going to be the best at it, but it’s worth taking a trait to get it back, and worth putting ranks into it regardless.

Fly: There aren’t a lot of ways to get this as a class skill without dipping into another class, which is really unfortunate. That said, since you want high Dex anyway, you can still work it in, and probably won’t be needing a particularly high bonus. Note: There’s a rule tucked away that getting a permanent fly speed will make Fly a class skill automatically.

Heal: I don’t want to rate it red, since it’s… I think it can do SOMETHING, but it’s not a class skill, you’re not going to have good wisdom, and… Well, everything it can do can just be done via magic.

Knowledge (Nature): Animals are usually pretty common enemies at low levels, and if you’re fighting fey a lot you’ll want to know about them! Don’t feel bad about leaving it to an alchemist or ranger buddy though.

3. Ability Scores

Strength: Like so many other casters, you don’t really have much use for strength. If you’re going to try to focus on your mystic bolts and spellcasting, feel free to dump away. If you want to use a real weapon (cough, longbow, cough) at all, you shouldn’t dump this though.

Dexterity: There’s no way around it, you are effectively a dexterity based class. Your Mystic Bolts are ranged attacks, and you can only wear light armor, so you’ll want this pretty high.

Constitution: There is NO PC who doesn’t want a good CON score. Unless you somehow don’t even have a CON score, you want at LEAST a 12 post-racials.

Intelligence: This is your casting stat. You don’t really need to pump this to maximum since you’re a 6th level caster, but you’ll want to shoot for a 16 base at least.

Wisdom: Everyone benefits from Wisdom, but you don’t particularly need to invest in it. 10-12 should probably be good enough in most cases.

Charisma: You really don’t need this and can safely dump away. Some of your class features, however, are based on your Charisma, so if you want any mileage out of those you’ll have to think twice.

Races

If I missed a race, rule of thumb is that anything that gives a penalty to Int (or Dex) is probably Red, anything that gives a bonus to one of those is usually Yellow-Green, and anything with a +Dex/Int bonus is usually Blue.

Core

Human: As with almost every class, humans are excellent at being warlocks. The Bonus Feat is good, because you’re going to want as many as you can get, as early as possible.

Elf: As someone who clings tightly to giving every character 14 Con, the penalty to it really hurts my soul. That said, Warlocks LOVE both Dex and Int, so you’re still coming out ahead. There’s a few good alternate racial traits as well.

Half-Elf: Good ol’ halfbreeds. They’re nothing too exciting, but you have a lot of cool racial options. Dual-minded is always an awesome choice, but popping a Skill Focus into Linguistics could mean an easy Orator.

Half-Orc: As with half-elves, nothing exciting, but you can’t really go wrong with them. The alternative racial trait for a luck bonus to saves, coupled with fate’s favored, nudges them slightly above half-elves, since it nets a +2 to all saves.

Halfling: And here comes the first of many +Dex/Cha races. Halflings are an interesting lot, and there’s a lot going for them. With an ART for a 30 ft land speed and a +1 to all saves, they’re at least decent.

Gnome: Small size with a penalty to strength, but +Con/Cha and nothing in particular to make them stand out. They’re not the worst choice since they’re a small race, but they’re also not a very good one.

Dwarf: I really don’t know what to think here. Con and Wis are good for pretty much everyone, and the penalty to Cha is negligible…  But there just isn’t anything to draw dwarves to being warlocks, even with the ARTs.

Other Races

Android: +Dex/Int with a Cha penalty, as well as a couple alright resistances and immunities. Not being able to benefit from morale bonuses sucks, but you’re still in a really good spot.

Tiefling: As is appropriate, tieflings make AMAZING warlocks. With a good array of alternate traits (prehensile tail is always useful, no matter what class you are) and even the ability to be small-sized, you can only go wrong in the character fluff department here. You can be a tiefling warlock, but don’t be “that guy with the tiefling warlock” character. Nobody likes him. You know what you did, Jim.

Aasimar: On the other side of the proverbial coin to tieflings on outsider heritages, the celestial Aasimar are… Okay, I guess? You don’t have a penalty stat, and if you pick up one of the alternate heritages (Garuda or Peri, namely) you could get a bonus to Dex or Int.

Orc: A penalty to all mental scores is bad, enough said. Nothing’s stopping you from trying it anyway, but Greg the Orc is gonna have a bad time when he reaches for his spellbook.

Kitsune: I’m not a huge fan of Paizo’s interpretation of kitsune, as an aside. I have been warming up bit by bit. Mechanically, the +Dex/Cha is passable as usual, and they have some really cool racial feats, which likely won’t matter until very late game. Note: As of Blood of the Beast, Kitsune can swap out the +Cha for +Int, can get either the Fox Shape feat or additional skill point per level in place of Kitsune Magic. That’s pretty darn nice. They’ve got some nice 3pp support as well.

Tengu: Oh boy, another weeb race, surely they must also have a bunch of cool things like kitsune? Hah, no. It’s +Dex/Wis with a Con penalty and a few passable traits, and they have awful racial feats compared to their vulpine friends. Note: DSP’s Bloodforge Infusions introduces a small-sized, -Str, +Dex/Int variation of Tengu. The book is still merely in playtesting, though.

Goblin: I HATE, no, LOATHE Golarian goblins, in both lore and design. Unfortunately, these annoying little things have a hefty +4 to Dexterity to go with small size and 30 ft speed, a decent selection of ARTs, and no intelligence penalty for some reason. If you have some way to justify your goblin toting around a spellbook, go ahead.

Kobold: Kobolds don’t deserve to be worse than goblins, but at least these little lizards are canonically allowed to read and write. Goblins skate by with a big old boost to Dexterity, but Kobolds get punished harder and compensated less. Honestly, if I were a GM, I’d just let them use Ratfolk or Halfling ability score modifiers instead, but that falls in the realm of houserules.

Catfolk: A penalty to Wis ALWAYS hurts, for any class barring maybe a Paladin. Other than that, another barely passable Dex/Cha race. One of the ART’s is a racial climb speed that could come in handy early on, but aside from that they’ve just got nothing worth writing home about.

Changeling: I’m not a fan of pathfinder’s changelings, but these hag-born don’t look too hot with with that Con penalty and bonuses going to Wis/Cha. With the Witchborn alternate trait, you get Int instead of Wis, possibly bumping them up to a low Green. It’s a shame Mystic Bolts isn’t considered a spell with the fire descriptor.

Dhampir: Oh look, a +Dex/Cha race with a Con penalty, wonderful. Alternate heritages could net you better scores, but negative energy affinity is irritating to deal with. Vatela-born are +Dex/Int with a Wis penalty, but they have a nasty weakness that you might not be able to trade away depending on how your GM rules the ARTs.

Merfolk: Jinkies merfolk, why did papa paizo let you have a +2 in THREE stats? They don’t have an Int bonus, but they also don’t have any penalties. Getting stuck with an abysmal land speed by default sucks, but taking the Strongtail can patch that until you get flight. Have fun trying to justify being away from water, though!

Sylph: Falling under the “humans with a funny hat” umbrella, Sylphs are actually pretty nice. They have the same ability score modifiers as elves, but are outsiders with darkvision, and a nice selection of alternate racial traits.

Ratfolk: Small-sized? Check. +Dex/Int? Check. Penalty to a stat we don’t need? Check! Built-in Darkvision and a racial bonus to Perception and UMD? Be still my beating heart. Only having a 20 ft land speed is lame, plus you’re playing a big bipedal rat, but you can’t be perfect in every way.

Wyrwood: Beep boop you are a small-sized construct that is easily refluffed, with 30 ft land speed, a penalty to charisma, and a boost to Dex/Int. Goodbye Constitution score, goodbye standard healing, hello immunity to damn near everything. If your group starts games at level 3-4 or so, there’s really no mechanical downsides here (Make Whole is on your spell list!), but I really doubt very many GMs will just let you play the race without any nerfs. (The Emotional ART should really be considered a mandatory power down.)

Skinwalker: Wow, look at those colors. The base skinwalker’s Int penalty is absolutely crippling, but with variant heritages you can bump up, with werebat and wererat as standouts. The bestial features can be good, with stuff like scent, darkvision, or a climb speed. Werebats can even feat into a (kind of terrible) flight speed!

4. 3PP Content, The Warlock, and You

Feel free to skip this section if you only want first-party content! just know to skip over things labeled (3pp), and that most ratings will assume you are not using that content! Also worth noting is that I am not a writer for any of the 3pp publishers that appear in this guide. If it appears in the guide, that just means I feel like it’s something of high enough quality to use!

Now that that’s out of the way, here’s where I address the elephant in the room… Again. See, at the time I started writing, Paizo hasn’t added anything to make Mystic Bolts more attractive to use, and knowing their track record for archetypes, they probably won’t in the future. Mystic Bolts, using only 1pp, SUCK the further the game progresses. At the time of writing this, Legendary Games, a 3pp publisher, had recently finished a playtest for their Legendary Vigilantes and Legendary Villains: Vigilantes books that both contained quite a few things that give the Warlock a leg (or in some cases, a crutch) to stand on, and even excel, with his bolts. To keep things easy to read, I’m going to make sure all 3pp options are separated from Paizo content.

The main things I want to bring up, though, are the following feats, and how they changes ratings around! The first two MUST BE TAKEN AT FIRST LEVEL, but you can also exchange a social talent for them to keep your first level feat open. If you can do that, I HIGHLY recommend it. You don’t choke as hard on feats when using 3pp, so you’re actually slightly free to take some of the more fun options.

Note!: Neither of the books have been released at this time! As such, consider the reviewing of all content from it as an early look. Once it has been released, I’ll remove this note and add a link to the book(s) in the extras section!

Legendary Villains: Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Genius Vigilante: This is a bandaid feat, as it makes all of your class features now key off of Intelligence instead of Charisma, to match your spellcasting. Depending on how far your GM takes this, it winds up with a few dopey interactions (Intelligence-based companion to the lonely?) with stuff, but it’s pretty much a feat/talent tax to get the warlock running.

Charismatic Caster: This feat totally changes the warlock’s focus. You turn into a Cha-based Spontaneous caster, using the same spell list as normal but gaining the spells-known of a bard. Charisma becomes a blue ability score, features based on charisma go up in rating a little, and most +Cha races jump from orange or green to Blue. I dare say halflings go all the way to purple. Charisma is a weaker ability than Intelligence, as is spontaneous casting, but it’s a fun, different way to play.

Mystic Accuracy: And here we are, the last major patch for the Warlock’s Mystic Bolts. You can now benefit from Power Attack (or Piranha Strike) and Deadly Aim with Mystic Bolts, even while making touch attacks. Clustered Shots also becomes essential, letting you add your bolts together before reducing the damage from resistances. You FINALLY get proper damage scaling now! With just the feat, it’s half of your Int modifier to damage, but it jumps to the full modifier with Genius Vigilante. With Charismatic Caster, it is instead your full Charisma modifier!

Archetype Stacking

There are things that stack with the Warlock? Why yes, yes there are! These are, for the most part, 3pp unfortunately. I’ll try to stay on top of paizo’s releases to update this if we get any others, though!

Paizo:

Wildsoul: No no no, no, and no. Just don’t. You end up losing ALL of your vigilante talents, in exchange for things you could already do with your spells, or things that you can’t actually make use of.

3PP Archetypes

Legendary Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Exposed Vigilante: You lose dual identity and are locked out of some social talents, and always count as in both identities for the sake of class features. At first level, you also get a bonus social talent, and +1 skill points per level. You do lose out on possibly getting an extra vigilante talent via the Masked Identity Feat chain, but personally it’s well worth it for what you get. I can already hear the torches and pitchforks. “But Drifter, what’s the point of a vigilante without dual identity!?”, well good reader, the point is that it’s a gimmick not everyone wants, and this unshackles the Warlock, and the vigilante’s mechanics as a whole, from their dual identity. Stacks with: Inquisitive Detective, Focused Hunter

Focused Hunter: If you tend to have a lot of the same kinds of terrain in your campaign, and you don’t like trying to set up ambushes to use the appearance features, then here’s the archetype for you. At 5th level you gain favored terrain like a ranger; 11th level gets you the stalker vigilante specialization’s “hide in plain” talent; and 17th level gets you get half of your favored terrain bonus to your CMB and attack and damage rolls. It’s still a niche set of abilities, but it’s about as good as the appearance . Stacks with: Consumed Vigilante OR Exposed Vigilante, and Inquisitive Detective

Legendary Villains: Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Consumed Vigilante: On the flipside from Exposed just dropping dual identity entirely, this is instead becoming consumed by your vigilante identity! You lost ALL social talents and may NEVER get any through any means. Instead of having two identities, you get the nameless one feat and +1 hp per level. There’s also a ton of other cool boosts, such as improved will saves, automatic renown, and more, but losing that first social talent is a real bummer for the warlock, since you could have gotten Genius Vigilante or Charismatic Caster with your first one, but being Human can soften the blow. Stacks with: Focused Hunter

Intrigue Archetypes from Legendary Games

Inquisitive Detective: This is a neat little archetype. You trade a few social talents and gain the inspiration pool of an investigator, and can your remaining social talents for investigator talents. You use your Cha mod instead of Int for all things related to inspiration, but Genius Vigilante winds up flipping that right back to Int, letting you be the scholarly mage you’ve always wanted to be! The old playtest version had you losing your first level social talent, but the current version instead starts at 3rd level.  Stacks with: Exposed Vigilante, Focused Hunter

5. Traits

Oh BOY are there a lot of traits to try and cover. Seriously. I tried to keep it to only the relevant ones and there’s still just so many of them. I suggest going to check out a guide on nothing but traits if you want to know about more of them. I won’t touch campaign traits, since those will likely only come up if you happen to be playing that campaign.

Combat

Reactionary: Probably the most common, stock standard trait for every single class out there. A PC getting beaten up as a child is pretty common for whatever reason. It’s a +2 trait bonus to initiative, no strings attached. Does ANYONE need to have an explanation for why this is good?

Resilient: A +1 trait bonus on Fortitude saves. It’s not fantastic, but if you’re worried about it it’s a nice patch. It competes with reactionary, though, which would let you possibly avoid even needing to take the save at all.

Social

Adopted: Congratulations, you were adopted! Jokes aside, how good this is depends on what race you were adopted by, as this COULD let you get some excellent traits. At the same time, a lot of racial traits are the same as other social traits, so you might just wind up spending two trait slots for what could have been handled by one.

Bruising Intellect: Int to intimidate, a skill you likely won’t make the best use of, but might be worth it if you’re creative.

Student of Philosophy: For most purposes this is just Int instead of Cha for both Diplomacy and Bluff, so if you need to be the party face and dumped charisma, here’s your trait!

Seeker: Perception as a class skill and a +1 trait bonus to it, covering the fact that you lost it by going Warlock!

Hunter’s Blood: +1 bonus to Diplomacy, Knowledge (Religion), and Survival when related to undead. Choose one of these skills as a class skill. You already have Diplomacy, Survival is kind of helpful, but Knowledge (Religion) is a good skill to have. If your campaign features lots of undead, this is an excellent trait, otherwise… Well, it’s still a class skill.

Natural-Born Leader: All cohorts, followers, or summoned creatures under your leadership gain a +1 morale bonus on Will saves to avoid mind-affecting effects. If you take Leadership, you gain a +1 bonus to your Leadership score. If for some reason you are allowed to take leadership, or are allowed to give the bonus to allied PCs, it’s a pretty cool bonus… But it’s really situational, and assumes that Leadership is available for you.

Wicked Leader: It’s similar to the above trait, except exclusively for evil characters, and it means your Cohort will only be one level behind, rather than two. If you’re playing an evil character with leadership, it’s your trait.

Princess (female only): +1 bonus to Diplomacy and Intimidate, and one of these (your choice) becomes a class skill. You already have both of these as a class skill, but the trait bonus is nice, and you can say you’re a princess.

Racial

Carefully Hidden (Human): Oh baby, it’s a +1 trait bonus to Will saves, which is nice to have, but it ALSO has a +2 trait bonus to saving throws versus divination effects. While that may not come up that often, it’s in addition to a bonus to something that WILL come up often. Auspicious Tattoo is also a +1 to will saves, without the divination effect, so it’s not as good.

Balloon Headed (Goblin): +1 bonus on Perception, and Perception becomes a class skill. Any Escape Artist check that requires you to squeeze your head through a tight space take a –8 penalty. The downsides are actually very big, mainly because you have to play a Goblin to even take this trait, but perception IS the best skill.

Elven Reflexes (Half-Elf) OR Warrior of Old (Elf): If you want a +2 initiative but don’t want to get the stigma of taking reactionary, this is your trait.

Magic

Pragmatic Activator: Use your Int mod for UMD checks. You’re probably going to want Tattoo Chamber, and UMD is one of the best skills in the game anyway, so it’s a good idea to grab this.

Magical Knack: What? Multiclassing? Ew, no, don’t do that. You’re going to do it anyway? Well fine, grab this and at least keep your caster level up.

Magical Lineage: A metamagic trait that is a staple for any blaster build. Unfortunately, you’re not one of those blasters, but if you want to focus on a certain spell enough to get a metamagic feat, this will be a great help.

Spark of Creation: You gain a +1 trait bonus on Craft checks, and the cost of creating magic items is reduced by 5%. Yea, you saw that right. There’s also Hedge Magician, which only gives the reduction to magic item cost. The usefulness varies depending on just how much you intend to craft, or if your GM lets you craft at all.

Faith

Fate’s Favored: Here’s a quick test for this. Do you have a constant luck bonus somehow? Then take this trait. If you don’t? It can still be useful, but you probably want to pass.

Birthmark: A +2 to saves vs charms and compulsions, and your body becomes a divine focus. You don’t benefit from the latter half, but if you’re worried about those pesky enchantment spells messing with your brain, this could give you a hand.

Child of the Temple:  +1 trait bonus to Knowledge Nobility and Religion, pick one as a class skill. Barring a intrigue campaign with lots of nobles, you’ll take Religion, unless your GM ignores the FAQ for nobility.

Devotee of the Green: +1 to Knowledge Geography and Nature, pick one as a class skill. Nature is usually going to be used far more, so that’s a safe bet.

Indomitable Faith: Oh boy, another +1 to a saving throw, this time it’s just straight will for anyone! If you’re a human or half-human race, Carefully Hidden is better.

Scholar of the Great Beyond: A +1 trait bonus on Knowledge History and Planes, and pick one as a class skill. Planes is the real winner here, even though my favorite mages tend to be history nuts.

Unnatural Presence: Do you like intimidating things? Do you want to intimidate animals and vermin? Then here you go. Unfortunately you are not an archetype that excels at intimidation, that’s more the stalker specialization’s thing.

Regional

Dim Seer: If you want to get Perception, keep your social talent open, and get a mechanical bonus for being the edgelord sitting in the dark corner of a tavern, does Paizo have the trait for you!

Feats

There are an absolutely overwhelming BOATLOAD of feats in pathfinder. A lot of them are also not very good, and a Warlock is going to choke if he’s not careful with his feats.[d]

General

Great Fortitude: While not exactly something to get early on, your weak fortitude save is going to hurt down the line.

Iron Will: Will saves are a pain, and a higher will save is never bad. You have a good will save, so this is a luxury feat, unless you’re one of those people who really dig low wisdom characters, like JJ.

Leadership: It’s busted. If it’s legal and someone with higher charisma hasn’t taken it, you should at least consider it. Its usefulness varies massively depending on your GM, though!

Squire: See leadership, but you can take it four levels earlier, albeit with a few restrictions.

Spell Focus: As a 6th level caster, your spell DC’s aren’t going to be all that impressive, and adding a +1 to them isn’t going to really fix that.

Improved Initiative: As with every class, going first is good, be it to get buffs out or getting the first hits in. If you have a feat open somehow, go for it.

Additional Traits: Traits are fun, and sometimes they can make or break builds. They likely won’t be doing that on the warlock, but more traits are always fun.

Improved Familiar: Improved familiars are nice. Their inability to take the majority of familiar archetypes sucks, but the gaining a fairy or pseudodragon as a familiar is pretty sweet. Most are able to do UMD and sometimes they have some sweet SLAs, which is a plus![e][f]

Cosmopolitan: I can hear you now “What the heck even is that? That’s a feat?” Well, it is! Why would you take it? Well, it’s functionally a much simpler version of Extra Traits for people who want more class skills, without spending trait slots. Is it worth taking? Not really, but I think it’s at least worth mentioning.

Eldritch Heritage: No. This requires Skill Focus and 13 Cha, and eats up feats you want to spend elsewhere. I love bloodlines, but a vanilla Warlock is actively hurting himself by taking this. It’s not totally worthless, but it’s going to require bending over backwards to justify it.[g]

Possessed Hand: You’ve got a ghost in your hand that distracts you from spellcasting, but helps in other ways. That -2 on concentration checks sucks, but you shouldn’t be casting spells if you’re going to need to make those checks. On the flipside, a +1 insight bonus with attack and damage rolls made using that hand, as well as a few other minor bonuses, is pretty nifty. You’ll likely only look at this late in your career. (Haunted Heroes Handbook, p25)

Hand’s Autonomy: This is… I don’t really know how to actually rate this, so I’m going to settle on green. It reduces TWF penalties (down to a minimum of -1) and lets your hand do a host of actions when you’re unconscious, or otherwise unable to act. (Haunted Heroes Handbook, p25)

Combat

Weapon Finesse: It’s an alright feat to have if you want to use the melee part of Mystic Bolts, especially for AoO’s. There’s little benefit to focusing on melee over ranged though, so there’s nothing wrong with skipping this. It benefits all melee touch attacks though, if you happen to be making a lot of those via spells.

Point-Blank Shot: Ah, Point-Blank Shot, the beginning of the mandatory feat tree for ranged combatants. As a Warlock this is excellent, as enemies will ALWAYS be within 30 ft of you, meaning it’s effectively a permanent +1 to attack and damage rolls so long as you’re attacking “at range”.

Precise Shot: The second mandatory feat for ranged attackers, removing the penalty to hitting targets in a melee is always nice, but it’s not very exciting.

Combat Expertise: Considering you’re attacking touch AC, this isn’t the worst feat to take, and it’s a prerequisite for a load of other feats… But you’re already pretty feat-starved as-is.

Moonlight Stalker: The prerequisites are pretty hefty for a situational boost, but a +2 to attack and damage rolls is at least passable if you absolutely had to scrape out more bonuses.

Moonlight Stalker Feint: Feinting isn’t very good, and this is a large investment for something you’re going to get little benefit from.

Moonlight Stalker Master: Do I really have to explain why this is a bad idea?

Improved Precise Shot: I’m disappointed by this, honestly. If your GM really likes partial cover and concealment below full, it might be a nice pickup for late levels, but there are way better things to spend those feats on.

Clustered Shots: It’s very unfortunate for you that this feat doesn’t say anything about totaling damage before applying energy resistance, because you really could benefit from the feat if it did. It you decide to grab a bow, you’ll get some mileage out of this still. If you can convince your GM to let your Mystic Bolts use this against energy resistances, though, it immediately becomes Mandatory for (I hope) very obvious reasons.

Manyshot: If you’re using a bow, you can feel free to grab it, otherwise ignore it entirely. Anyone with bow proficiency can just grab archery feats and do well, that doesn’t make it good for you.

Rapid Shot: As is typical of ranged builds, an extra attack is always welcome. The -2 to hit is negligible considering you’ll be hitting touch, and are more hurt by the fact that you can’t get through energy resistances.

Rapid Reload, Crossbow Mastery: This feat is worthless for you, and you’re the wrong class for doing anything with guns or crossbows. Drop them both off at some other sap’s doorstep.

Two-Weapon Fighting, Improved, Greater: Since it works with both melee and ranged mystic bolts, the TWF feat chain is an absolutely amazing investment for a warlock. Anything without ridiculous touch AC or energy resistance will eat a barrage, which can give you a good bump in the damage department.

Combat Reflexes: Oh hey, more attacks of opportunity, and you’re always armed, what’s not to love? Well… Unless you take weapon finesse, you’re not going to get much out of it, since all your good Dex score means is that you’ll get more chances to miss your opponent with melee attacks of opportunity.

Snap shot: Oh look, a feat you can’t get until really late, to let you do something you could just do with weapon finesse! If it let you attack with reach, it might be worth it, but...

Improved Snap Shot: Oh boy, TWO WHOLE FEATS (and combat reflexes too) in order to… Have 10 ft range on attacks of opportunity? Pass.

Power Attack: It’s a good feat, but nothing you do actually lets you get the benefits of it without DM fiat. Pass.

Piranha Strike: Power Attack’s Dex-based little brother, who has all the same problems and more. Pass again.

Deadly Aim: As power attack, your Mystic Bolts won’t benefit from this without GM fiat, but it’s one of the feats in the stock “guy with a bow” build. I’ve seen people argue that “if you don’t make your bolts touch attacks, it counts!”, which could true… But then you’re losing out on making touch attacks, which is a big thing Mystic Bolts have going for them[h].

Crafting

Craft Wondrous Items: Really, if you’re any Int-based caster and you have the opportunity, there’s no real reason not to take this at SOME point. The sheer variety of wondrous items is frankly ridiculous, as is being able to have them for half price.

Craft Magical Arms and Armor: Simply for the fact that this saves you and your party money, this is an okay feat. You’re probably not the right person for it, though. It’s lower than Craft Wondrous Items, but still excellent.

Inscribe Magical Tattoo: “Aren’t spell tattoos exorbitantly expensive and way worse than scrolls? There are only like, three wondrous tattoos, right?” you ask? Well yes. It’s also REALLY COOL. Depending on how far you read into it, you can even convert some wondrous items into tattoos. Unfortunately, it’s one of the more confusing feats, which means its usefulness is reliant on how your group interprets it.

Scribe Scroll, Brew Potion, Craft Wand: These are all VERY situational, and would be better on a class that’s less dependant on needing a ton of feats to function properly. I would pass on all of them as a general rule, unless you have something highly specific in mind.

Racial

Racial Heritage (Human): You count as a member of another race for the sake of prerequisites. There are far better uses of your bonus feat, but it’s at least a fun option.

Angelic Blood (Aasimar): A wholly mediocre feat to eat up your limited supply, but it leads into...

Angel Wings (Aasimar): For a regular old vigilante, this would be amazing, even with the prerequisites, simply because an anytime 30 ft fly speed is nice. For you it’s decent but a bit mediocre, as the main benefit is getting Fly as a class skill and not needing to use a spell slot on Fly all the time.

Airy Step (Sylph): Much like Angelic blood, this feat is situational and just serves as a tax for the next one. If you’re worried about falling a lot, you can also pretend to be a cat.

Wings of Air (Sylph): You increase the bonus from the prerequisite feat, and gain… Anytime supernatural flight equal to your base speed, with good maneuverability. It doesn’t say you get Fly as a class skill, but per rules, having a Fly speed should net the class skill.

Bloodmarked Flight (Skinwalker, Werebat): You gain an additional bestial feature for your werebat form, and it’s anytime flight! It’s also poor maneuverability at best. On the bright side, you can take it very early (level 5, compared to level 11 or 9) and without needing any prereqs beyond race, which makes it at least acceptable. Like Wings of Air, despite not saying it gives you Fly as a class skill, having a Fly Speed should give it to you anyway.

Swift Kitsune Shapeshifter (Kitsune): “Wait, don’t you hate pathfinder’s kitsune? Why cover their feats?” you might ask? Hate is a strong word, I just highly recommend refluffing. Why am I covering this in particular? Well if you take a look at what it leads too...

Vulpine Pounce (Kitsune): If you’re playing a melee warlock, you’re often shit out of luck for ways to stay mobile and still attack. This feat lets you expend a swift action during a charge... To pounce. For you 1pp-only warlocks, this is awful for you, since trying to melee with mystic bolts is a joke at best. If you’ve got 3pp handy? Kitsune wind up a good choice if you were looking to slap people with energy. Constantly needing to shapeshift is kind of annoying, and melee mystic bolts will always be worse than ranged, though.

Fox Shape (Kitsune): I will admit I am very biased with this, especially since you can get it at level 1 as a racial feature. I’d advise against Taking it normally, but if your GM says you’re still able to use your Mystic Bolts normally in fox form, then this can just hop all the way to blue, since being tiny all the time is amazing. It would be purple if you could also still use spells.

Kobold Confidence (Kobold): This is red solely because you have to jump through spiked, flaming hoops to get to it it, nevermind that it’s a feat that’s Cha-based. Cha 13 AND being a kobold (or being Human and blowing a feat on Racial Heritage) is just… Seriously? ONLY consider this if you houseruled kobolds up to snuff and happen to be running with 3pp.

3PP Feats

Legendary Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Just to reiterate, if you have access to these books, YOU ARE TAKING Mystic Accuracy at some point, and either Charismatic Caster or Genius Vigilante at first level. Go scroll back up to the section on 3pp content if you forgot why.

Masked Identity: This is redundant. If you somehow have an archetype that trades out dual identity, and you still want it, you could theoretically get some use out of it... But you’re a vigilante for crying out loud!

Masked Identity, Improved: If you have Masked Identity or the Dual Identity feature, this is… Well, it’s basically Skill Focus: Disguise by another name. On it’s own, it’s really lame, but what it leads up to...

Masked Identity, Greater: This is the one of two ways to get an extra vigilante talent. Considering the power of talents, two feats to get another is totally a worthwhile trade. You only count half your level for qualifying, and must choose a general talent, but get your whole level for effect. Personally, I’d rate this blue for ALL vigilantes, since you could easily make up for the two feats in the chain, depending on the talent. You lose out on this if you take either the Exposed or Consumed archetypes, however.

Cross Specialization: You can qualify as a Vigilante with the Stalker or Avenger specialization, using your Vigilante level -4 for the sake of qualifying for talents of that specialization. It’s cool if you’re dying for specific talents (stuff like Signature Weapon, Unkillable, and Mad Rush come to mind), but it falls a little flat. You already have a wide variety of base talents from this book alone, plus your warlock talents, and tons of better ways to spend your feats.

Signature Talent: This is a bit of a wonky feat, since it reduces the level requirement of a specific talent, as well as any talents it requires, by 2. You also get a bonus effect depending on the talent. An extra +1 to the DC, or +1 to attack rolls while using the talent, is a pretty nice boost, but there’s not a whole lot of talents you’re going to be itching to get early.

Legendary Villains: Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Extra Social Talent: Social talents range from being as good as the average feat, to “very underwhelming”. That said, if you wanted one of those niche talents and don’t have anything better, it’s not a completely unworthy trade.

Sniper Bolts: A boost to, that even scales up with levels. It starts by adding 15 ft, and increases by an additional 15 ft at 6th, 12th, and 18th levels. It does screw up your relationship with Point Blank Shot, and you’ll never catch up to a longbow, but you’ve got more range than most close-range spells.

Explosive Bolts: As the name might imply, this makes your bolts detonate when they hit their target, dealing damage to anything nearby with a reflex for splashed targets to halve the damage. It only procs off of ranged bolts, but the save DC scales up with level. If you’re fighting crowds of enemies that get clustered together, this could be absolutely devastating.

Vigilante Casting Savant: Do you hate having so many talents to choose from, but so few chances to grab them? Well here you go, you get to grab an extra talent, just as though you had gotten one normally! It requires level 5, but doesn’t say you can take it multiple times. That’s a bummer, but another talent at all is excellent!

Vigilante Savant: You can’t take this AND Vigilante Casting Savant. It opens up at level 7, and treats your level as half for the sake of qualifying for talents, so it’s a direct downgrade for you.

Wise Vigilante: Where Genius Vigilante made all your features Int-based to match spellcasting, and Charismatic Caster shifted you to a spontaneous Cha-caster to match your class features, this shifts all your vigilante features to Wisdom. But doesn’t touch your spellcasting, which kinda kills its appeal.

Forbidden Vigilante Knowledge: You’ll have to scroll down to see just WHY this is so good, but this actually gives you the spells that Magical Limit Break gives you slots for. As-written it doesn’t work with spontaneous casting, but word is there’s an errata on that coming “Soon™”.

6. Vigilante Talents

Hoho, oh wow there are a lot of talents that are terrible for the Warlock. As is typical of pathfinder, there’s always some stinkers in any list of abilities, but almost all of the base talents are terrible choices, since they either don’t work at all for you, or take up talents that would be better in other things.

Base Talents

Lethal Grace: If you intend to use a normal finesse weapon without Dex-to-Damage it’s great! But you don’t get the scaling damage bonus with Mystic Bolts, so you miss out on the only real benefit of the talent.

Inspired Vigilante: Gimped investigator inspiration, with no way to get free uses. Definitely not worth one of your talents.

Perfect Vulnerability: This ability is nigh-useless for you, because you’re going to be targeting Touch AC anyway, and hitting flat-footed isn’t all that helpful.

Shadow’s Sight: Low-Light and Darkvision are both really good to have, but not an amazing use of your limited talents. If you want this, why not get it from your race choice?

Shadow’s Speed: I like going fast, and extra base speed is cool, but again, limited number of talents.

Shield of Blades: This ability just doesn’t work for you at all. It would be yellow if only for warlocks who decide to use melee weapons, EXCEPT you MUST be using your STR mod to damage, and you must still meet all the feat prerequisites.

Strike the Unseen: For the most part this is kind of useless for a warlock, but getting all these bonus feats for one talent is pretty sweet. The defensive benefits are pretty nice if that kind of thing comes up a lot, too. It lets you get at Moonlight Stalker, so there’s that.

Sure-Footed: Ahahahaaa, for a normal vigilante this might be amazing, but you’re a 6th level spellcaster. You’re going to be flying around and using invisibility.

Vital Punishment: If your Mystic Bolts had ANY damage dice scaling at all, this might actually be a cool talent to invest in. Alas, it was not meant to be.

Warlock Talents

Arcane Striker: Simply put, Arcane Strike as a bonus feat is the biggest slap in the face to the terrible scaling of Mystic Bolts possible... But you’re probably going to want it anyway. You have to wait until level 12 before you get any additional effect to make this talent better than just getting the feat, and you still have to spend a swift action to activate it, meaning if you ever have to spend an immediate action on anything you’re going to cut your flat damage in (about) HALF. If your campaign is ever going to get to level 12 or higher, you’re going to want to pick it up anyway, but it’s a very lackluster talent before then.

Elemental Armor: A scaling resistance to one energy type, as well as dealing damage to anything that attacks you. It’s not a good choice early on, but at 16th level you become immune to one energy type. It’s functional, and getting an immunity is always nice, though you have tons of ways to avoid the damage altogether.

Familiar: I do not have to explain why familiars are amazing. That you can get one with multiple identities is just gravy, though I don’t know how that interacts with Improved Familiar.

Social Simulacrum: [Explanation Soon™, I swear!]

Tattoo Chamber: Now THIS is a talent. If I had the time, I could probably write a whole section on this ability alone. You can store one item, plus an additional item per 3 warlock levels, in a tattoo on your wrist, and can remove it with a swift action. If it’s a spell-trigger item, you can use it while it’s still in your tattoo, letting you keep your hand free! Slap your favorite wands into your wrist and go nuts!

3PP Talents

Legendary Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Deft Shot: Dex to damage capped by your level, for either firearms, crossbows... Or your Mystic Bolts. This damage doesn’t stack with Mystic Accuracy, so just save your talents. You really don’t need to bother with firearms or crossbows.

Diamond Determination: Toughness and Great Fortitude mashed into a talent. An okay bandaid for the Vigilante’s poor Fort save and low HD, but not exactly the best choice for a talent.

Furious Flurry: Trade one talent for Two-Weapon Fighting, and automatic progression of the Two-Weapon Fighting feat chain at 8th and 15th level. For a single talent, this is a solid investment, but it doesn’t really start to pay off until 8th level, which might be the tail end of some games.

Spell Master: Early on, this doesn’t do a whole lot for you. Not needing to make UMD rolls for any spell on a chosen spell list is nice, but not needing UMD is only a marginal advantage. At 6th level, you can add your Charisma modifier (or your Intelligence modifier with Genius Vigilante) to DCs, which is actually very nice if you use some of the cheaper utility wands. At 12th level, when combined with Tattoo Chamber, this talent could get downright ridiculous with the right wands, since you’re now using your vigilante level in place of the caster level of the wand. If your familiar can benefit from this somehow, I’d need a new color.

Swift Defence: You get the AC bonus of a Brawler. It’s… Not great. You’re likely going to have High Dex and plenty of defensive spells. Funnily, Jolly actually mentioned that he thought the Brawler got the same kind of AC bonus as a Monk, but decided against changing this talent to reflect that.

*Steel Body: Have you ever looked at a Barbarian and said “man, I sure wish I had his DR/-”? Well, if for some bizarre reason you have, here it is, and it even acts like it for purposes of determining what stacks. It’s DR 1/-, increasing by 1 at 8th and every 4 levels, capping at 5/- at 20th level.

Legendary Villains: Vigilantes by Legendary Games

Beast Brethren and Beastmaster: Anyone familiar with animal companions probably knows about Animal Ally. This is Animal Ally and its prerequisite rolled into one. Beastmaster requires level 6, hands you Boon Companion, then lets you pick up a companion off the druid list if you so desire. Two talents, and only picking from the full list at 6 kind of kills it for me, even though I really love my animal companions.

Bloody Wrath: This is probably the edgiest thing you could possibly take, but it’s almost comically effective if you want to have a “blood mage” theme. If you suffer from a bleed effect, you gain a bonus to attack and damage rolls that scales up. In addition, so long as you have the means handy, you can cut yourself as a swift action to cause 1 point of bleed damage a round, taking more damage as the benefits go up. If you feel like living dangerously, it could be a good alternative to arcane strike. Or you can stack it with arcane strike for even more boosts.

Critical Violence: Yo dawg, I heard you liked crits. Since your Mystic Bolts are considered a light weapon for the sake of feats that call for a specific weapon, they do in fact qualify here, as confirmed by a writer. This increases your crit range by 1, and by 2 at 12th level, meaning you net an 18-20 crit range by 12th level. If you take Improved Critical, this would only increase to a 17-20, since the talent’s increase is applied after that feat, but it’s still a pretty good deal for mid levels.

Critical Virtuoso: On the flipside to the above is this little number, which is Critical Focus and Improved Critical bundled together, with the added bonus of treating your class level as your BaB for qualifying for critical feats. Honestly, you’re not much of a crit-fisher, and while the benefits are nice, you’re really better off looking elsewhere with your talents.

Ranged Adept: This Point-Blank and Precise Shot rolled into one, with the added benefit of the (slightly superfluous) Point-Blank Master at level 8. This lets you have Genius Vigilante, Mystic Accuracy, and either Rapid Shot or Deadly Aim by level 3! It’s not too exciting, but anything to ease the feat tax of ranged combat is helpful.

Improved Familiar: This is, to be perfectly honest, a bit of a waste. It’s Improved Familiar, with a clause that lets you give the familiar an archetype it might not otherwise qualify for. If you want your Lyrakien Azata to have the Valet archetype, now you can! But if you wanted her to have Sage, I’m pretty sure she could already do that. It really should have let your familiar swap between two forms to be worthwhile.

Lethal Locks: I’m giggling at this, partly because this is an amazing talent, partly because the mental image is hilarious. It’s an all-day Prehensile Hair hex, which is pretty darn useful if you want to keep your hands free, or deliver a touch spell from a distance... But it then gets scaling damage dice, and can count as a manufactured weapon for iteratives. Meaning you can full attack with your hair. If it wasn’t for the fact that there’s so many things to boost Mystic Bolts, I would tell you that you should pick this up first and just use it instead, but those Mystic Bolts barely scrape ahead of it. It’s still totally worth nabbing, since an extra limb and increased touch attack range is pretty sweet. Even though this is a guide for the Warlock, it’s worth chiming in to say that for other Vigilantes this is totally worth being considered a Blue talent. At least in my opinion.

Magical Limit Break: Knock knock, 7th level spell slots called. You want this and Forbidden Vigilante Knowledge as soon as you can get them so that you can be a 7th level caster, which means levels 17-18 at LEAST, but if you somehow make it to 20th level you can take it a second time to hit 8th level spells. You have fewer per-day slots than a real fullcaster, but who cares? Unfortunately, by the time it actually gets going, most games will likely be over.

Social Talents

Two sets of talents? TWO sets of talents? This part always gets me about the Vigilante, because it was how a martial should be designed in the first place. The Warlock archetype is a Gish though, and you STILL get all those talents by default, which is pretty sweet.

Base Talents

Any Guise: If this came earlier, it would be a really good ability… But it’s just so LATE. Many Guises comes online way sooner, and has easier utility. If you want to be a specific person, just use some magic and hope your GM doesn’t have scry-and-die wizards looking for you.

Renown: While this leads into some pretty nice stuff, renown on it’s own is really bad. A small village of 200 people who know about your vigilante identity, and an intimidate bonus? And you need to spend 4 hours every day for a whole week to transfer it? Unfortunately, without renown your list of social talents is practically slashed in half. Note: As noted in another guide, this actually affects a mile radius around your area of renown, making it much better.

Obscurity: The Flipside of Renown, rather than making you more well known, it lets you become nobody important and not need to worry about someone seeing through you. It functions otherwise the same, and lets you take most of the same talents, meaning it suffers about as much as Renown does. (Blood of the Beast, p.15)

Great Renown: Okay, now we’re picking up to something more reasonable, increasing the maximum to a town of 5,000 people, or two smaller towns of 2,000 each. That’s a BIG jump, and almost makes up for the base talent being so mediocre. It’s still kind of finicky, though.

Incredible Renown: You multiply the number of people that know about your vigilante identity from Great renown by 5, and have an even bigger intimidate bonus. 25,000 people in a largy city, or 10,000 in two smaller cities is nothing to sneeze at.

Instant Recognition: If you’re going to take renown, and you need to move around a lot, spending a whole week to transfer your “place of renown” is a pain. Cutting that down from 4 hours every day for a whole week to just 4 hours for a single day? If you’re even using renown at all, just pick this up and save your party the headache.

Feign Innocence: Within his area of renown, this provides a non-magical effect identical to the Innocence spell for a Vigilante’s Social identity. If you don’t tend to stick around your area of renown, or have a small area to work with, this isn’t going to do much… But if you do, and also happen to be doing a lot of lying in an intrigue game, it’s very handy!

Subjective Truth: This is slightly better than Feign Innocence, mostly because it seems to work outside your area of renown. Unfortunately, it still requires you to take that talent, and it’s still VERY situational. For heavy intrigue games it’s a nice thing to have in your pocket.

Safe House: This is kind of cool. You get a safe house that gets progressively larger and harder to find. If you have renown, you can also move this from place to place, which is pretty good if you like having a hidden home base[i].

Social Graces: You get to pick any one Int-, Wis-, or Cha-based skill other than Perception or UMD, and get a +4 Circumstance bonus in your social identity. At 5th level, and every 4 levels, you add this bonus to another skill with the same parameters. This is a nice bonus, even if there’s lots of ways to get better circumstance bonuses, and is still infinitely better than something like Skill Focus.

Seamless Shapechanger: Do you just REALLY want to pretend to be something else when your shapechanger polymorphs? Well here you go, this lets you add that Seamless Guise bonus on those disguise checks. I have no idea how to actually rate this, because its even more niche than the Vigilante’s normal Dual Identity[j].

Transformation Sequence: Sorry Magical Child, but your one gimmick is now available to any vigilante capable of using magic for the low, low cost of a single social talent. It’s mostly a talent all about the flavor, since it’s not really much of an upgrade from just taking Quick and Immediate change. Though it does let you swap identities with a mere swift action with Immediate Change. (Blood of the Beast, p.15)

Quick Change, Immediate Change: Two talents that should really just be part of the base class. Quick Change means that you only need two full-round actions to totally change identities, and Immediate change drops this to just a move action to completely change guises.

Many Guises: This ability is silly. Instead of a Social or Vigilante identity, you get the third option of just being a “mundane” member of your race, receiving a big ol’ +20 circumstance bonus on disguise checks to appear as a boring old joe shmoe. There’s all kinds of uses for this, especially if you need to hide from the big bad diviners.

Mockingbird: You get Ghost Sound, Ventriloquism, and Vocal Alteration as at-will, Extraordinary abilities, rather than as SLAs. You get to skip needing to prepare them, can do them at-will, AND you don’t need to worry about things that screw over spells? Well dang, sign me up!

Double Time: Do you happen to want to do a lot of mundane crafting? Yes? Take this, then take Social Graces. Otherwise… Well, it’s not bad, but it’s still mundane crafting.

Intrigue Feats: You don’t skip feat requirements, meaning the main benefit of gaining odd niche feats is already kneecapped out of the gate. Orator can be great since you’re an Int class, but you need Skill Focus (Linguistics) to qualify. Ironclad Logic is cool, but you might not be able to get your Int high enough to actually get the full benefit. Nerve-Racking Negotiator would be AMAZING for warlocks who want to intimidate, but it requires you to have Persuasive. Ungh.

Companion to the Lonely: The fluff for this is extremely awkward for everybody and on fluff alone I suggest you avoid it just to be safe. That said, the actual mechanical benefits are amazing, since Will Save rerolls are fantastic. Fair warning, unless your warlock has good Cha for whatever reason, or a high-cha party member/cohort willing to get down and dirty (or whatever your GM lets you refluff [k][l]it as) with them, you’re not going to get many uses/day of this[m].(Inner Sea Intrigue, p.33[n])

9. Multiclassing, Prestige Classes, and Gestalt

Multiclassing

For the most part, multiclassing in pathfinder just isn’t worth it. There’s a few things that you could justify for a small dip, though...

Alchemist: Only mildly useful, but if your GM rules mystic bolts as splash weapons and limits you to Paizo content, a dip for Throw Anything would net you Int to damage! Consider adding vivesectionist so you can squeeze in a sneak attack die. Two levels for a discovery couldn’t hurt either.

Fighter: A two-level dip for bonus feats is as far as you’ll want to go here, and even that’s stretching it a bit.

[More to come!]

VMC

O Variant Multiclassing, you poor system. Most VMCs are really, really bad, and you’re kind of struggling to keep up with feats as-is, so the short answer is that VMC as a whole is Red with 1pp. If you’re using 3pp, or houserules that alleviate the feat strain? Well...

Wizard: Is very solid with certain schools. A familiar at 3rd level (skip using a talent? nice!), 1st level school powers (Teleportation, hands down), and 15th level sees you getting a Wizard Discovery. The 11th level cantrip is really lame, as is how long it takes to get the 8th level school power.

Sorcerer: With bloodline familiars, this is another way to pick up a familiar at 3rd level. The quality of powers all depend on what bloodline you take, and this becomes a much better choice if you’re allowed 3pp. If you’re doing a high level campaign and don’t mind the hoops, Eldritch Heritage gets you the 15th level power sooner (17 instead of 19).

Magus: Eldritch Pool by RAW doesn’t work on your Mystic Bolts (as it enhances a single specific weapon), so that’s already a potential strike. Some of the arcana are pretty nice, and spellstrike might let you add damage to your bolts, but otherwise it’s very underwhelming.

Prestige Classes

The short answer on prestige classes: don’t even bother. Maybe something will come up in the future, but at this point in time just don’t.

Gestalt[v][w][x]

Gestalt games are something I’ve always been interested in, but have only ever done in depth via character building and theorycrafting. I’m not going to even try to list every possible combination, just highlight a few interesting ones that would synergize well. If you don’t know what gestalting is, it’s an optional rule from 3.5 that allows you to have the features from two classes, as well as the better HD, BaB, and Saves between the two! You can find more on the 3.5 SRD Here!

Wizard and Witch: Both blue, simply because they’re Int-based prepared casters, though it’s redundant and lazy what with your spellcasting. Both get familiars, too! The witch doesn’t have as much potential to totally break things, but hexes are really fun to play around with, and you get a few spells you wouldn’t otherwise have access to (namely the cure and inflict line).

Sorcerer and Oracle: For normal Warlocks, these two are terrible choices. For a warlock using the Charismatic Caster feat from Legendary Vigilantes, they become very, very good choices! Oracle pulls slightly ahead, since it gives you access to a wider variety of spells. Note: How I forgot it the first time escapes me, but the Sage variant of the Arcane bloodline switches Sorcerer to being Int-based. You don’t have the sheer variety of wizard, but early-access spells and boatloads of slots are nice to have.

Alchemist: Ah the alchemist, one of my all-time favorite, ultraversatile pathfinder classes. Passively, you get a better fort save and the alchemy and extracts, as well as discoveries. The sheer variety of ways to build an alchemist means there’s bound to be SOMETHING to your liking. Archetypes of note: Vivesectionist, Preservationist, Promethean Alchemist (A shame), and Homunculist

Rogue (Unchained): Rogues get a lot of justified flac relating to all their weaknesses. Personally, even Unchained Rogue is super niche at best compared to its “replacement” classes. With Warlock, you can actually make very good use of the tools the Rogue does have, with Debilitating Injury likely guaranteeing a full attack will vaporize your target. There are a LOT of archetypes, all of which work with Unchained, but the majority of them are pretty bad. Archetypes of note: Phantom Thief, Scout, Kitsune Trickster (Racial), Hidden Blade (Path of War) [Special shoutout to Underground Chemist for those of you whose GMs say that an Alchemist’s Throw Anything applies its damage to Mystic Bolts.]

Bard: by default, the bard is a poor choice overall. With 3pp* it actually swaps up to green, though. You don’t solve your poor Fort, d8 HD, or 3/4th BaB, but it’s a fun combo for Charismatic Casters. Archetypes of note*: Dervish Dancer, Archaeologist, and Magician

Investigator: Unsurprisingly solid. Like a bard, you don’t patch up your poor fort save, or get a better HD or BaB, but studied combat is pretty swell for melee, and you wind up with ALL the skills. Empiricist makes you a master of skills, too! If you want to be slinging bolts you have to take Ranged Study, limiting you to your native 30 ft range.

Fighter: Fort Save, d10 Hd, Full BaB, bonus feats out the ears, and plenty of decent archetypes. Unfortunately an FAQ decided that Mystic Bolts don’t qualify for weapon training due to not belonging to an actual weapon group, so you’ll have to hope your GM lets you ignore that or else trade it out. The fighter doesn’t have much in the way of versatility, but it’s a great passive class all around. Archetypes of note: Eldritch Guardian, Lore Warden, Mutation Master, and Sensate.

Magus:[y][z] The good Fort save, magus arcana, and more spells are the main things to take away. Eldritch Pool by RAW doesn’t work on your Mystic Bolts (as it enhances a single specific weapon), and Spell Combat as-written doesn’t let you TWF due to how pathfinder handles “free hands”. *Depending on how your GM interprets some archetypes or rules (and what books you use) it could actually be REALLY GOOD, but you can’t rely on DM fiat to decide on if something is good or not. Archetypes of note: Card Caster*, Eldritch Archer?*, Hexcrafter[aa][ab][ac]
