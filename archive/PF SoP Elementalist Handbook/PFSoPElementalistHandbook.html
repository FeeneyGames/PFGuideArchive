WHAT IS SPHERES OF POWER?

Spheres of Power is a third party product made by Drop-Dead Studios for use with the Pathfinder RPG. It creates an alternative magic system to use alongside or replace the existing vancian slot and level system, giving at-will abilities and a resource pool for augmenting them, broken down into ‘spheres’ focused around a variety of themes, each sphere providing basic abilities that are expanded or altered by a talents that go with that sphere. For example, the warp sphere allows a character to teleport a limited distance, while a talent increases the range, allows you to target an enemy, or ignore line of sight for the teleport.

WHAT IS THE ELEMENTALIST?

The elementalist is a base class, one of eleven created for the Spheres of Power system, though existing classes are adapted as well. The elementalist is a specialist in the destruction sphere, though as a spheres caster is capable of branching out into other areas as desired.

A GUIDE TO COLOR CODING:

Red options are terrible and should generally be avoided.

Orange options are niche or mediocre and should be avoided outside of particular builds.

Green options are decent but not top priorities.

Blue options are the strongest and/or most versatile and should be prioritized.

THE BASICS:

D8 hit dice - perfectly standard for a specialist caster type. Keeps you alive decently, you shouldn’t be tanking anyhow.

Medium BAB - does what it needs to. You are either targeting touch AC or saves pretty much all the time, so you don’t need higher.

All good saves - Yup, can’t ask for anything more here. The right options can boost saves even higher, putting you in a pretty good place for non-AC defenses.

4 Skill Points/Level - You aren’t a skill monkey, but you can cover your bases.

Simple Weapon, Light Armor - You don’t really care about the weapons anyway and light armor is pretty decent.

Midcaster - In the Spheres of Power system, caster level is like BAB, including the way it stacks between classes. You have medium caster level, so at level 20 your CL is 15. Note that for the destruction sphere, you are a full caster.

Spellpool - this is the resource you use to boost all of your sphere abilities, equal to level + casting mod. It isn’t rated since it is the same for all sphere casters.

Magic Talents - 1/caster level. You can’t compete with the incanter, but it gets you what you need.

ABILITY SCORES:

Str - This is usually going to be your dump stat. If you want to optimize air blast, then you may want 13 here for getting power attack to meet the prerequisites for improved bullrush. With the changes to force blast though I’m not sure I would bother. Make sure you can carry your gear without being encumbered (mules are cheap, muleback cords and handy haversacks are your friends). Melee builds can use this, especially if deadly agility from Path of War is off the table. If so, it becomes your primary stat, reducing Dex to whatever you need for initiative and AC.

Dex - You second highest stat priority. Governs your ranged touch attacks (and melee if using weapon finesse), boosts AC (you have light armor), reflex saves (evasion!), and initiative (you have some great battlefield control, you want to go first!). Also key for dex-based melee builds, of course.

Con - Shoot for 14 here. D8 is decent, but no one ever died from too many hit points or too high of fort saves.

Int - You aren’t going to be the party encyclopedia and you should be able to cover your needed skills from your base points, so don’t worry about this much. You can take a penalty here if necessary, especially if you are human or taking SP for your favored class bonus. If dipping to change your casting attribute to Int, obviously it goes to blue.

Wis - You have a good will save, but there’s no such thing as too much. Between saves and perception, I can’t recommend a penalty here unless you are getting a big save boost from somewhere. Leave it at 10 and boost it if you have spare points. If dipping to change your casting attribute to Wis, obviously it goes to blue.

Cha - Your default casting stat, so vital for your save DCs. You can be party face with your skills, especially if helped along by traits. Drops to red if you move your casting stat.

SKILLS:

Class Skills:

Acrobatics (Dex) - nice if you get in a tight spot, but rolling verses CMD gets tricky.

Bluff (Cha) - a nice social skill, though I prefer diplomacy. Hard to get caught telling the truth. Works for feinting, but feinting is melee-only by default, so unlikely to be worth building around.

Climb (Str) - you are two talents from flight, so not really a priority.

Craft (Int) - You don’t have the intelligence or the need to take this.

Escape Artist (Dex) - Great to have when you need it, but you should be avoiding those situations.

Fly (Dex) - You have the means to get varying degrees of flight and good dex, so tossing a few points in won’t hurt anything.

Intimidate (Cha) - The mean social skill. Great if you work in dazzling display or another means to either spread it over an area or without actions.

Knowledge (Arcane; Int) - It’s a good identifying skill, but that isn’t really your forte.

Perception (Wis) - Most rolled skill in the game. Max it.

Profession (Wis) - Fluff skill only.

Spellcraft (Int) - If switching to an INT build its great, especially if crafting. Otherwise it’s at least nice to have a shot at knowing what is coming your way. Note that a spellcraft check is, shockingly, required for spellcrafting as well. Spellcrafting can be all kinds of fun.

Stealth (Dex) - You should have decent dex and light armor, so this isn’t a bad investment. With the right talents you could even make a decent scout, though that is outside the purview of the destruction sphere you specialize in.

Survival (Wis) - Not your job.

Swim (Str) - Not more than a point here unless in an aquatic campaign. If in an aquatic campaign, get a swim speed from somewhere (alteration sphere, for example).

Non-Class Skills (notable only):

Diplomacy (Cha) - My preferred social skill. Easy to get in class with a trait.

UMD (Cha) - Handy for filling in all kinds of affects you can’t get natively or don’t have room for. Remember the pragmatic activator trait if going for an Int-based build.

PARTY ROLES:

Melee Striker - You don’t do this well, normally. You might think you can with your medium BAB and D8 HD, but you won’t have the feats to make it worthwhile. There is a talent to allow you to piggyback a destructive blast onto your melee weapon attack, but this requires hitting normal AC and a standard action, forgoing iteratives, bonus attacks, haste, etc. Basically it’s a warlock’s hideous blow, which should tell you enough. You could build to deliver your blasts from touch range exclusively if you have a concept that requires it, but it isn’t recommended. However, if you dip a sphere-archetyped magus for two levels and spellstrike OR grab the fire wielder talent from the nature sphere and stack up natural attacks from race, dips, or alteration, you can actually be good at this. The latter is best done with an archetype, though your damage drops precipitously against fire resistant enemies, which are common enough.

Tank - Your dex, armor, saves, and AC are good enough to cover you, but you don’t have the tools to prevent or take hits for others in a significant way. If you are the tank, the party is in a bad spot.

Ranged Striker - At first glance, this seems like your primary role. Destruction gives unlimited blasting that can come in every elemental flavor and then some. The right talents can give you good areas of effect (AOE) and repeatability to conserve your precious spell pool. Ultimately though, it’s only damage and dedicated martial builds will hit harder. Think of 3.5’s warlock for your output here, though without eldritch glaive or claws.

Debuffer - You can do this as you blast and BFC! Many of your blast type options carry rider effects (prone, bullrush, entangle, staggered, etc).You aren’t on the level of a witch, but it still has an impact.

Buffer - Technically, there is one talent in destruction that grants a buff. If you want to do this though, you can pick up some other spheres to help out. If this is your main focus, you are in the wrong class.

Utility - Only from other spheres, unless blowing things away counts. As a mid-caster you can grab a few key abilities that make a big difference (alteration, warp, etc.) and have the caster level to make them useful.

Battlefield Control - (BFC) In my mind the most important role you can fill. Your ability to toss up walls of debuffs to shape the battlefield is awesome. It may not seem like much at first glance, but consider layers: a crystal wall to lock your enemy in place with an air wall to knock enemies back into the first wall, or stacking a force wall to make them immobile and prone, while requiring that many more saves to leave the space.

Ultimately, destruction is best for holding the enemy in place while slowly damaging them and applying debuffs, for partially covering three roles in one sphere.

CLASS ABILITIES:

Weave Energy - you get the destruction sphere and full CL when you use it! Otherwise you would be worse at your shtick than a soul weaver, fey adept, or incanter.

Evasion - Defend yourself from others like you. You have all good saves and a need for dex, so you should be able to put this to good use. Only works in light armor, but you only get light armor.

Combat Feat - Five free combat feats over the levels. Feats are always good, but the feats you want most aren’t going to be combat, at least once you cover the basic ranged essentials.

Favored Element - you get to boost the damage of three of your blast types over the levels. Choose wisely, since each time you get a new one you also increase the bonus damage of the others, meaning you get class level x 1.5 on your first choice eventually. Makes you better at blasting than the default 1d6/2 levels would indicate. This no longer ties in with elemental defense, so just pick your favorite blast type.

Dodge Bonus - Additional AC that applies to touch AC, eventually hitting +5? Not amazing, but a nice addition. With this and evasion, elementalists are well suited to defend themselves from other elementalists.

Elemental Defense – flat resistance to the four elemental energy types. Never goes amiss.

Elemental Movement - Swim, fly or burrow. You get all three eventually, and the speeds increase as you level. You are taking fly first. Just do it. Burrow would be my second pick, generally.

Improved Evasion - Reflex saves really don’t scare you now.

Energy Body - immunity to crits is nice, DR10/magic may as well not exist at this level. Seriously, what’s the point? If you follow my advice on multiclassing you won’t be seeing this anyhow. Capstones are overrated generally and this one isn’t worth the wait.

Please note that there are no class abilities that actually require charisma, making it very appealing to change your casting stat by simply dipping another class. More on this in the multiclassing section. Adam Meyers has stated, when asked about why some classes have fixed casting stats and others do not:

“As for casting stats, we wanted to give people the freedom to pick their own stats, while not bogging down people who didn't care for extreme customization with too many choices (hence, why some classes can pick and some classes can't.) I see no problem in letting people change it up, though…”

So talk to your DM if you want a different stat and don’t want to dip.

WHY PLAY AN ELEMENTALIST?

Given the way the Spheres of Power system works, any class can grab the destruction sphere and blast away, while an elementalist can grab any sphere and do other things too. So, why take this class over others that get full caster level or flashier class features? Good question. Let’s look at a few relevant direct comparisons, focusing on the destruction sphere:

 

Vs Thaumaturge

Compared to the thaumaturge, the elementalist has two more good saves, evasion, dodge bonus, free movement modes, and resistances. The thaumaturge get full CL for all spheres, but gets less talents overall and requires special considerations in character design to mitigate the drawbacks of its feature ability, forbidden lore. Over the levels, the thaumaturge will be able to attain a caster level between 2 and 6 (8 if using the human favored class bonus) levels higher than the elementalist, which is between 1d6 and 3d6 damage on your basic blast or 2 – 6d6 if spending a spell point. This results in 3.5 to 11.5 extra damage base, 7 to 21 damage augmented. By the time the thaumaturge is getting +6 level from forbidden lore (17th level), the elementalist is getting 1.5*level damage on their 1st favored element, so +25 at level 17 and +30 damage at level 20, with +17 on their second favored element, and +8 on their third.

So the elementalist can do more pure damage, but only with one blast type. However, the thaumaturge risks backlash every time it boosts it’s CL, though at level 7 an incantation can be used to pass the penalty off, making it a good idea for thaumaturges to invest in a familiar or other willing target to dump the penalties on. You still lose the action, which is painful in combat, and incantations are limited. The dearth of talents granted the thaumaturge means that feats are tight, so the elementalist actually has more room to diversify both within destruction and into other spheres, especially since the combat feats you need can often be covered by your bonus feats.  The thaumaturge is useful for many things and can make a respectable destruction-wielder, but by no means overshadows the elementalist.

Vs Incanter

The elementalist chassis brings armor (obtainable through dips, but nice to have), saves, BAB (matter even for touch attack early on), and skills (not much of an issue if going Int-based on the incanter). Incanters get an advantage in feats, talents, and specialization options.

No one beats the incanter in sheer breadth of options. They get 15 more talents, base, than the elementalist and eleven bonus feats that can be used to grab even more. Specialization gives them a big damage boost and higher CL on destruction as well. So, why bother with the elementalist?

The CL boost from specialization is easy to pick up from a 1-level dip. An incanter that specializes also loses all but three bonus feats. The damage from specialization falls behind favored element as well, though applies equally to all blast types. Rolling twice vs spell resistance is nice, the other specialization abilities are unimpressive.

As far as focused destruction-wielders go, the incanter doesn’t top the elementalist, but wins hands-down when measuring overall casting ability. The elementalist in turn brings defenses (hit points, evasion, dodge bonus, resistances), accuracy (medium BAB, not huge when making touch attacks) and a small skill boost. Trading the three bonus feats left after specializing for evasion, dodge bonuses, combat feats, etc is definitely worth it, it's just the difference in talents that makes this tough. If you want to wield destruction, be an elementalist, if you want to be a generalist wizard, be an incanter.

Note that the incanter can’t compete with the geomancer in terms of nature sphere CL, though getting an animal companion is pretty nice. Natural attack melee geomancers greatly benefit from the higher HD and BAB, keeping the geomancer superior in that regard.

Vs Magus

Similar classes in many respects, HD, BAB, base CL, etc. The magus is the measuring stick for gishes (martial/caster hybrids). The two classes may seem to be competing in the destruction sphere, but in base builds there is very little overlap, since a base elementalist does not want to be in melee, which is where the magus lives. The magus will get less damage out of destructive blasts with their lower destruction CL and lack of flat bonuses like favored element, but will get damage spikes from criticals.

The real comparison is between straight magus and an elementalist with a 2-level magus dip. The magus gets some very nice arcana, on-demand enchantments that reduce any need for the enhancement sphere, and big boosts to spell pool, which is hard to obtain elsewhere, then eventually gets heavier armor (great for strength-based builds). The elementalist/magus will get more pure damage, resistances, evasion, reflex save, movement modes, etc. Free movement modes and higher base damage are pretty nice and most arcana are trash, though the ones that aren’t can be real gems. Both work, neither is absolutely superior.

RACES

First, a few notes about priorities here that will guide race choice regardless of sources available. Bonuses to dex and your casting stat are great, bonuses to con are good, and penalties to str or cha if you don’t use it to cast are acceptable. Small size is a boon, since generally speaking combat maneuvers aren’t your thing and the bonus to AC and attack rolls is lovely.  The stealth bonus is nice for some builds too. By the same token, large races are generally going to be bad. Darkvision is a nice bonus, as are movement modes, though you get flight in-class at a reasonable level so climb speeds aren’t terribly important.

Core Races

Dwarf – great choice for Wisdom-based builds, okay for Intelligence-based builds, terrible for Charisma-based builds. Grab the steel soul feat and glory of old trait to make your saves amazing. Darkvision is nice to have and the skill bonus could work out for you if you are using the skilled casting drawback.

Elf – solid choice for Int-builds, though Con loss is never pleasant. Weapon proficiencies won’t matter, but a bonus to beating spell resistance is nice. You can get darkvision with alternate racials.

Gnome – small size and great stat boosts for Cha-builds. Skill bonus can work for the skilled drawback. Fire could get a bonus from the pyromaniac alternate racial, ask your DM.

Halfling: - perfect stat spread, small size. Best choice for a straight-class elementalist. Grab the alternate racial to boost your speed up to 30’.

Half-Elf – choice of stat boosts is nice and some options are decent, but not a standout race.

Half-Orc – choice of stats and ferocity are good, as is getting darkvision.

Human – free feat, extra skills, good at anything. What did you expect?

Other Notable Races

Aasimar – they come in a variety of stats boosts and the sidebar even allows small size, with resistances to boot! Good for anything.

Goblin – Paizo’s favorite miniature psychopaths, the big dex boost is great, just don’t do Cha-based. Make good mounted characters, too.

Orc – you’re joking, right?

Merfolk – great for aquatic campaigns, not so much otherwise. Given the stat spread the strongtail option will likely be unwelcome in most campaigns.

Tiefling – like aasimar, but with a -2 to a stat. Plenty of options. The tail is nice for juggling staves, rods, and wands, so is highly recommended. You can also get natural attacks if going that route.

Skinwalker – another race with a variety of alternate options to suite your stat needs. Note that bloodmarked can get flight by turning into a bat for only one feat, while ragebred have unusual natural attack options if you want to go melee.

Dhampir – choice of stats, healing can get tricky. Most alternates offer strength bonuses, so it’s not as good, generally, as the other diverse races.

Kobold – they stink, but do have some natural attack options if going dex-based melee with fire wielder. Pick red scales and grab the trait to see through mundane smoke, it will come in handy since you will likely be using the fire warrior or geomancer archetypes.

FAVORED CLASS BONUSES

Core Races

Dwarf – a point of resistance to an element that thoughtfully stacks with elemental defense. Nice for pumping one resistance or shoring up others, especially if losing your other elemental defense options to an archetype.

Elf – add to your movement. Only matters in increments of 5 and doesn’t help your elemental movement until you get it unless you retrain.

Gnome – 3 talents over 18 levels isn’t bad.

Halfling: boost your dodge bonus to AC. It’s decent, boosting touch AC never hurt anyone and yours should be decent to start with..

Half-Elf – trivial bonus to unarmed strike damage. Really? Don’t do this.

Half-Orc – boost damage for non-favored elements. Solid choice, even better for the archetypes that get a less diverse selection of favored elements.

Human – 3 talents over 18 levels still isn’t bad.

Other Notable Races

Aasimar – bonus damage against evil outsiders and undead. Great for some campaigns, terrible for others. If you can use it, it will stack with favored element so you can get some nice blasting going.

Goblin – damage against flanked or denied dex enemies. Goblins are good at stealth, so grab invisibility and have fun. Flanking requires melee though, so avoid that. Invisibility fades in usefulness as you level though.

Merfolk – 3 DR/piercing over the levels? Blech, worthless. Doubly so since piercing weapons will be common in games where merfolk aren’t terrible.

Orc – damage against object? Meh.

Tiefling – smaller damage bonus than the other races, but its negative energy, which should help punch through resistances to land your rider effects. You should have plenty of options for avoiding resistances though, so that will really only kick-in when you don’t know the target is resistance. Still, it will stack with favored element, so it's extra damage. Unique.

SPHERES

So, the class looks pretty sparse, right? Like any caster, your strength lays in your spells, or spheres and talents in this case. We’ll start off with your bread and butter, which you get free just for taking the class, Destruction.

Destruction

Base ability is a blast that does bludgeoning damage, 1d6 per odd caster level, with the option to spend spell pool to boost it to 1d6/CL (min 2d6). Close range or melee touch by default. Nothing to write home about, but you always have a way to hurt things. Your blast gets modified by talents, but can only get 1 blast type talent and 1 blast shape talent on it at a time.

Blast Type Talents:

Acid Blast - blast does acid damage and hits again the next round for 1/dice of the blast. Not a bad element to have for straight damage. Less commonly resisted than fire and cold.

 

Air Blast - blast is now non-lethal and you get a free bullrush on the target using your CL + casting mod verses CMD. This is fun, given the way it interacts with some of the shapes. If you boost your strength and grab the required feats this could get really fun. Even without that, you should still be able to separate the mooks from the threats with this.

 

Crystal Blast - reduces your damage to d4’s and deals piercing damage. Why blue? Your target needs a reflex save or is entangled and unable to move until breaking free. Also, the target’s square or the blast’s area of effect are difficult terrain. A reminder about entangled:

“The character is ensnared. Being entangled impedes movement, but does not entirely prevent it unless the bonds are anchored to an immobile object or tethered by an opposing force. An entangled creature moves at half speed, cannot run or charge, and takes a –2 penalty on all attack rolls and a –4 penalty to Dexterity. An entangled character who attempts to cast a spell must make a concentration check (DC 15 + spell level) or lose the spell.”

You want this. Best BFC blast and debuff blast at the same time.

 

Electrical Blast - bonus against metal targets and a damage type that isn’t resisted often unless fighting celestials. You shouldn’t be missing anyway.

 

Force Blast - force damage (awesome) + save vs prone (good). Second favorite blast type. Note that prone targets get +4 to AC vs ranged attacks, but take a penalty versus melee, so this is great for targets that your buddies are beating on, but not for sniping. Remember that it costs a spell point!

 

Fire blast - fire damage and sets the target on fire. On fire is weak after the earliest levels, so if you aren’t staying at real low levels, don’t bother unless you expect to face fire-vulnerable critters. Frequently used and frequently resisted. Has some use for shutting down the more common regenerations, but the latter can often be covered by acid anyhow.

 

Frost Blast - cold damage, fort or stagger. Staggered is a nice condition to inflict, though the enemies you want it on the most have good fort saves. Still, beats fire. Commonly resisted, so have other options.

 

Nether Blast - negative energy and shaken, or no damage and frighten undead. Shaken lowers future saves, if they aren’t immune.

 

Stone Blast - all three physical damage types, plus bypasses all kinds of magical defenses (SR, globe of invulnerability, etc). Necessary at higher levels, though you could try to boost your spell penetration and just assume some blasts will get resisted.

 

Thunder Blast - sonic is rarely resisted. Deafened isn’t great, but it does mess with verbal casters.

Blast Shape Talents

Blast Trap - leave a blast for someone to step on. Has some possibilities, but it's likely a filler choice for later on. Has some potential with spellcrafting.

Energy Sphere - costs a spell point, makes a sphere for rounds/level that you can direct as a move action. Directing it as a move action means you can make it hit twice per round, helping your spell point economy by stretching other points you spend on it. This is why it’s not efficient to spend points on your regular blast, spend an additional point and use it twice per round all fight instead! Great for single targets and small groups.

Energy Wall - makes a good length wall or a modest diameter circle. This is the key to your BFC. Costs a spell point, sadly.

Energy Weapon - standard action to channel destructive blasts through your weapon. Might see use in an E6 type game where haste and iteratives won’t be as prevalent for weapon-users, otherwise it isn’t enough to be worth having to hit full AC just to add weapon damage. Basically, it’s the 3.5 warlock’s hideous blow (which blows hideously, as the saying goes). If you want to do this, take two levels of magus instead, it's vastly superior.

Explosive Orb - basically, you spend a spell point to blast a wide area, ala fireball. It’s a mook killer, though combined with crystal blast it becomes battlefield control, turning a large area into difficult terrain.

Guided Strike - spell point to add +20 to your attack with destructive blast. Uhm...if you really need to hit a target with great touch AC and evasion? Doesn’t help with miss chance or mirror images. Used to be an auto-hit, but got nerfed.

Rebuff Blast - 1 round minor AC and reflex buff, spend a point to make it last two rounds or use as an immediate. Not worth the standard action when you could be hurting things, the boost to AC as an immediate is nice if you know it's a close roll. Gets better if you can cheese the duration with spellcrafting.

Sculpt Blast - cone or line, cost a spell point. No thanks, better options are available.

Other Talents

Crafted Blast - good for getting around physical resistances and boosting damage on your base blast, but you can do the first with an element and the second is not a huge difference.

Extended Range - close goes to medium, medium goes to long. Not top priorities, but you want it at least once. Less important if you are only doing dungeon crawls with small rooms, more important if in the open frequently.

Advanced Talents

Calamity – gives some different area options. Expensive and doesn’t come online until late. Meh.

Nature Sphere

For the (elemental) warrior and geomancer archetypes the nature sphere is also significant. Your base abilities here are divided into four geomancing packages, plantlife, water, earth, and fire, each with sub-options, and the spirit option, which does nothing by itself but describes how some talents are used. Fire, water, and plantlife benefit from being able to supply their own prerequisites, with water and plantlife doing so elegantly.

Plantlife

Entangle – concentration duration, entangle anything that fails a save and make difficult terrain. Similar to the druid spell but without the ludicrous area at level 1. Amazing BFC when you have plants around, useless otherwise. Fortunately you have ways to fix that.

Growth – spend a spell point to make plants produce food. Not really helping in combat outside of some unusual situations, but helpful in a survival type campaign. Also thematic.

Pummel – concentration, makes a tree attack nearby enemies. First of all, the image is beautiful and I recommend plantlife as a package just so you can have trees beat people up. The size of the tree or branch is limited by your CL and of course the presence of trees.

Water

        Vortex – If your enemies are in a large body of water, this is great. If not, move on.

Fog – fog is great, but you need water to start with. You can cover that with the create water talent or with the weather sphere, though if you have weather you can make your own fog anyway.

Freeze – cross lakes in style! Freezing enemies is excellent, you just have to get them wet first, meaning it isn’t often going to be a power you can lead with. The create water talent solves this issue, so spread the frostbite!

Earth

Bury – another terrain dependent control option, requiring sand. If you can choose or prepare the terrain you will be awesome. Earth would make for a nasty desert encounter NPC. If only you had a way to knock enemies prone…

Tremor – Oh, wait, you do! Costs a spell point, but gives you an AOE trip attempt. If you go intelligence based you can grab combat expertise and improved/greater trip to augment this check. Prone is good, prone, immobile, and suffocating is better (see bury). Too bad suffocating takes so long. Still, holds them in place and out of your hair nicely.

Dust Storm – area concealment, requires sand or loose dirt. Loose dirt shouldn’t be too hard to come by outdoors, could be harder inside the king’s palace.

Fire

Manipulate Lava – remember how all these geomancing abilities are somewhat limited in utility by the terrain available? Yeah, this one takes the cake. Most enemies that you could catch in a lava vortex probably are not going to be terribly inconvenienced by swimming in lava, though it still does offer control over a VERY specific battlefield. Making obsidian is probably more useful, but you still need material to work with.

Create Fire – you can make modest sized fires without fuel. Unleash your inner pyro.

Affect fire – make fires larger or smaller, limited by CL. Could make the ‘on fire’ rider of fire blast more useful.

Talents

Animal Friend – spend a spell point to improve an animal’s attitude for a time. You can also call an animal to you and you know if none of that type are in the area. Nice if you face animal encounters often as you can just shut them down. Creative use of knowing what animal types are nearby could come up (the enemy is known to have a pet crocodile, which are not native to the region, which he takes everywhere!).

Create Water – now you can get things wet. The action automatically combines with water geomancing effects, so your action economy doesn’t suffer. Costs a spell point.

Expanded Geomancing – Get a package you don’t already have. The warrior archetypes may not care too much, but the Geomancer is taking this at least twice.

Feed on Fire – gain fire resistance and heal a bit from the fire damage you resist. Works as a roundabout self heal. Costs a spell point, so no free heals. Not convinced it’s worth it.

Forge Earth – You can reshape the terrain. Tactically useful, strategically wonderful. Given enough time you can build a labyrinth.

Fire Wielder – Add fire damage to your unarmed strikes. I can’t recommend building around flaming punches, but if you must then this is a fair way to do it. However, if you grab a large number of natural attacks, you can actually become potent against anything not fire resistant. Note that the current text specifies unarmed strikes only, but per Adam Meyer:

“Fire Wielder should apply to natural attacks. We borrowed language from similar abilities from Core Pathfinder to make sure our language was congruent, and they only mentioned unarmed strikes, but I'd say it should apply to natural attacks as well.”

Greater Range – exactly what it says. Close to medium, medium to long. You will likely want it at least once.

Grow Plants – the plantlife equivalent of create water. Need a tree to slap someone? Now you have it. Has the same action-economy built-in, so you can call up thorns and have them entangle someone all in one action. No fancy plants, so just stop those thoughts of black lotus farming.

Move Fire – take a fire and move it, plus you can sustain it with a spell point. Not enough damage potential for primary offense, it has its uses though.

Speak with Animals – nice to have, combos well with animal friend to get a helper in a pinch. It shouldn’t be hard to find an animal that has seen something useful, though they likely won’t understand what they saw. If you are taking the alteration sphere, get speak with animals there instead, it doesn’t cost a spell point.

Speak with Plants – more intelligence gathering capability. Plants are everywhere and you can talk to them. Limited by your DMs interpretation of just what a plant can perceive.

Speak with Stone – one of these speak talents should be able to get you information of just about anything. Again, limited by your DM.

Thorns – the damage is weak, so it’s not worth it if you don’t have the means to keep enemies in them. Stack it with entangle (or better yet, spellcraft it to combine them into one action).

Towering Growth – Once flying enemies become common, you need this. Remember to laugh manically as a bunch of weeds pull a dragon to the ground.

Waterwalk – walk on water and get a swim speed for a spell point. Water Warriors get a swim speed anyway and small bodies can be walked over with freeze. Meh.

Wave – spell point for an area bullrush. It’s okay, but most of the time you will have to create water for it to work, which gets pricey. Better if in an aquatic campaign.

Whirlwind – vortex for earth-users. Needs sand.

Other notable spheres: Every sphere has its uses and as a medium caster you can apply them reasonably well. I’m only going to look at ones that are either particularly powerful with only limited talents spent on them or that grant a high degree of utility to diversify your casting ability.

Alteration - grabbing this and the right talents can get you movement modes early, enhanced senses, size alteration, etc. Versatility that you can use. For melee types, this is even more key, since you can pick up natural attacks this way and you will want as many as possible, and you can get pounce to boot.

Conjuration - a very strong school and even dipping your toes in can yield significant benefits, granting an ally not dissimilar to an eidolon. Unusual in that it has no uses that don’t require spell points and that it has a talent to boost your CL for the sphere, making you one talent away from matching a full caster on this one.

Creation - Creation is limited by only your imagination and your spell points. You probably don’t have the talents to go deep here, but as a tool-box it can be very useful. The greatest drawback is that the at-will is weak, so anything interesting requires spell points.

Dark – Gives some fair debuffing and battlefield control which could be combined well with the right race (fetchlings for example can get see in darkness from feats). Nothing that you need to grab unless it fits a concept.

Death – Ghost strike would give different options, but probably not worth the actions given the choices you already have on-hand. Reanimating shambling bodies to act as shields has some value though.

Divine – probably necessary if you are playing the role of primary arcanist. There are some good talents to boost sense, though nothing in the sphere really plays to your strengths. Necessary if you need to read magic writing for rituals and such.

Illusion – another sphere limited only by your creativity. Worth picking up even if you just do it to get invisibility for shoring up your defenses. If you do, use the vanisher drawback so it only takes one talent.

Life – healing and status removal are always welcome. Could be worth a talent just to be able to use items to heal, unless you have good UMD.

Light – the base ability isn’t much to write home about, but a few of the talents are worth considering, especially repelling light to keep enemy melee off you and revealing light for dealing with invisibility.

Time– steal time and shift time are what you are looking for here. Steal time spellcrafted onto an energy sphere or wall gets you extra actions, which is awesome. Shift time can get expensive, but you can keep using it so you don’t pay the penalty until after the fight. The ability to hit ‘redo’ on a turn is sweet as well. Haste doesn’t benefit you but could help the party, while slow has spellcrafting potential.

Warp – tactical teleportation is awesome. So is having a panic button to get away.

FEATS

A note on Metamagic: most metamagic feats work with the spheres system, increasing casting time and costing spell points equal to their level adjustment. This gets expensive quickly, but metamagic is powerful. I recommend metamagic rods given the limited spell points you will have, but grabbing still spell or silent spell could save your bacon if you took certain drawbacks, neatly getting you out of the occasional situation the drawback hurts you in.

Spheres Feats:

Advanced Magical Training - a multiclassing feat, letting you get ½ CL for non-casting class levels. Obviously the use of this feat is entirely build dependent. The more levels you spend elsewhere, the more you need it.

Arcane Empowerment - this is for traditional casters dipping their toes in spheres, not for you.

Artificery - it’s the master craftsman feat plus. You aren’t limited to what crafting feats you can apply it to and you get +2 to the skill. Not really for you, though needed as a prerequisite for dedicated crafters. Note that your skill ranks will be higher than your non-destruction CL, if your DM doesn’t allow you to use your destruction CL to qualify.

Artificery, Improved - you now longer need the base sphere to craft an item. Useful for a dedicated crafter. Probably want to be intelligence based to boost spellcraft, though if you choose a profession skill for artificery then wisdom based builds work just as well. Crafting is going to be group, DM, and campaign dependent, so take it if it works.

Basic Magic Training - you don’t qualify.

Cantrips - you get an array of minor magical abilities that mimic traditional cantrips. Note that detect magic is not on the list. Adam Meyers has stated that it was omitted deliberately, due to personal preference to not have at-will magic detection. Ask your DM. If you are playing with traditional casters, they have it anyway but there is no reason for you not to.

Circle Casting - you can aid another to increase CL, with a large enough group getting up to +5 CL max. Not that great in combat, but powerful for long duration out of combat spells. See conjuration sphere, for example. It would be blue, but it depends on other people's builds unless you can get leadership or another source of minions. Even then, it requires CL 10, so it isn’t happening early.

Contingency - combine with the right sphere and this is a life saver in a very literal way. Check out teleportation beacon, for example.

Counterspell - you can spend a spell point to dispel an ongoing effect or as a readied action to counterspell. Dispel is a thing casters are expected to have, so this feat is good if no one else in your group can cover it.

Counterspell, Improved - spend more spell points to hit more effects or an area. If you have counterspell, you probably want this too.

Counterspell, Greater - you can hit artifacts now and dispel in a 40’ radius. Less necessary than its antecedents.

Counterspell Mastery - counterspell as an immediate action. Shutting down an enemy caster’s big moment can make a tough encounter a cake-walk. If you are in the counterspelling field, get it.

Craft Rituals - rituals are a way to get traditional magic effects that are absent from the spheres system. Useful if traditional magic is absent from your campaign, not worth it otherwise.

Create Spell Book - if you use the spell crafting system heavily, this will be nice to have, but given the more focused nature of your specialization, you can probably cover what you need to without it.

Delay Magic - could be useful in the right situation, but requires planning and foresight.

Extra Magic Talent - The feat you take whenever you don’t have something you really need. Every feat should be weighed against this one, since you can never have too many talents.

Extra Spell Pool - 2 more spell points each time. You can never have too many.

Fool Magic - a neat, unique feat. If you go Cha-based then you can make good use of this (or if you trait bluff over to Intelligence). Good for an intrigue type campaign.

Mounted Magician – Are you mounted and using the enhancement sphere heavily? Then it’s great. Seems unlikely though.

Ritual Caster – you don’t need the base sphere to perform a ritual. Campaign dependent, as with all the ritual feats.

Ritual Master – Use your MDB instead of your CL for ritual CL.

Superior Rebuff – Grant improved evasion when using rebuff to give cover. Giving improved evasion as an immediate action is nice, but it would get awfully expensive and comes online late.

Spellcrafting – you can mix and match spheres when creating spells. Some effects will require DM judgement, but this is potentially amazing depending on your creativity in combining effects.

Sphere Focus – pick a sphere, +1 DC. Good for focused casters, such as… elementalists. Small boost, but you should grab it when you can.

Wand Wielder – requires CL10 like a number of feats, lets you use your CL if higher than your wand’s CL. Wands are a great way to expand your options and low CL wands are cheap. You already use your stat for the DC, so this is great.

Blessing/Blight (Versatility, Mastery), Elicit strike, Extra: (Arsenal Trick, Bestial Trait, Emotion, Invocations, Mystic Combat, Nexus Powers, Psionics, Secrets, Shadowstuff), Greater (Hypnosis, Shadowmark), lingering link & greater,  Master (artisan, of Cosmos, of Death), Mind Opener, Occult Savant, Perceptive Psionics, Shadowblast, Trance - for class features you don’t have.


Combat Feats: You get bonus combat feats. Not sure why you get limited to combat, but feats are feats.

Blindfight – Not a prime pick, but it’s nice filler if it comes up in your campaign with some regularity.

Bullseye Shot – Not really worth the feat, so shouldn’t be missing much as it is. That said, you can cover the prerequisites easily enough and often can afford the move action.

Dazzling Display – Gives an area debuff to your intimidate skill, but you have other things to do with your actions. Requires weapon focus, which ain’t grand either.

Dervish Dance – You are taking this ASAP if going melee as a magus in a game without Path of War. Others can skip it.

Escape Route (teamwork) – Nice if you are using conjuration, especially if mounted. Otherwise group dependant.

Improved/Greater Bullrush – Nice if you want to focus on airblast, probably not worth the trouble.

Improved Critical (Ranged Touch) – Not terrible for a later pick. Crits are fun. Really only filler. Opens up other critical feats, which can be interesting if you have a way to capitalize on them. Most subsequent feats have significant BAB requirements though, so I’m not rating them presently.

Improved Initiative - always good, especially for getting off BFC or getting an extra round out of your energy sphere blast shape. A nice, easy way to fill a bonus feat slot.

Kirin Style/Kirin Strike – for Int-based builds, you want this, though no one else does. A swift action for double your casting mod on your attacks is fun. Doesn’t help your AOEs, but makes your free attacks more potent.

Mounted Combat – Good choice if using conjuration to get a mount or feats to get an animal companion, useless otherwise.

Point Blank Shot – It’s not that you want it, it’s that you have to take it for precise shot. Pay the ranged combat feat tax and move on.

Power Attack – Only ever take if you want to buff your airblast and need it as a prerequisite for improved bullrush.

Precise Shot – The -4 for firing into melee kills at low levels. If starting at high levels, you may be able to get by without it, since you are targeting touch AC.

Weapon Focus (ranged touch) – Gives +1 to hit. Meh.

Martial Study (Path of War by Dreamscarred Press) – there are some interesting options here. Boosts that work with ranged attacks should work with your destructive blasts, while Riven Hourglass has some goodies that non-initiators can make use of. Really, this requires a guide of its own.

Deadly Agility (Path of War by Dreamscarred Press) – adds dex mod to melee damage.  Useless for most builds, but a magus dip or natural attacker with fire-wielder can be pretty wicked with this.

General Feats: Just a few notables, since there are far too many to review completely.

Animal Ally – If you want an animal companion, you can get one at full strength for the cost of three feats (nature soul, animal ally, and boon companion). The list is limited, but you can get a mount or defender that will scale decently.

Boon Companion – Only if you have an animal companion whose level needs boosting, such as from animal ally.

Cartwheel Dodge – You qualify with class abilities, it’s kind of neat for extra movement.

Combat Casting – You don’t want to be in melee range, but it happens and sometimes the 5’ step isn’t getting you out. This will save you from having to reduce your CL to get your next spell off, which may save your hide. Of course, you could just use a reduced CL teleport from the warp sphere to get away, so it isn’t required.

Craft (x) – Crafting is heavily campaign dependent. Crafting with Spheres of Power changes the relative value of the feats dramatically as well, making rings, wands, and staves much more important than before, while drastically reducing wondrous items. No ratings; if you are savvy enough to build a crafter you can figure out your priorities here.

Divine Protection – Domains are easy to get with a dip, but you don’t meet the 2nd level divine spells requirement unless you can talk your DM into letting a sphere apply somehow. If so, it’s a good reason to stay charisma based in and of itself. Errata made it 1/day, so forget it was ever printed.

Eldritch Heritage – This one is all over the map, depending on which heritage you pick. You can grab a familiar or some free metamagic, so might be worth a look.

Nature Soul – Terrible, but required for Animal Ally.

Uncanny Concentration –Green if mounted or in a campaign where it comes up often, otherwise skip it.

Shape Veil (Akashic Mysteries by Dreamscarred Press) – you have a pile of options here granting varying degrees of power and utility. You certainly won’t have the means to push them to their limits, but properly chosen they can be most helpful. Really requires a guide of its own. Fire-wielding melee want to look even closer, since several veils give natural attacks. Look over here for more information.

Open (X) Chakra (Akashic Mysteries by Dreamscarred Press) – to go along with the above.

TRAITS

Too many to list. Basically, grab whatever you need to get the skill access you want and buff anything you feel lacking. You can get +1 to any save, +2 concentration, any skill as a class skill, etc. Interesting ones let you move a physical skill to Wisdom (Wisdom in Flesh) or prevent you from being flat footed (Defensive Strategist). If going intelligence based, you can move any charisma based skill to intelligence, some with more than one option so you don’t run afoul of the 1 trait/category rules. Some of the more interesting traits are listed here.

DRAWBACKS

Spell points looking scarce? Trying to match a particular character from fiction or the standard magic system? Looking for limitations on your arcane power? Drawbacks are the answer! You may, with DM approval, take a number of drawbacks. These can be general drawbacks, taken at creation, which can either be traded in for boons or to increase the rate you accumulate spell points as you level (1/level maximum with five drawbacks), or sphere-specific drawbacks, taken when first gaining a given sphere, which grant a bonus talent in that sphere. Sphere specific drawbacks can also be removed by ‘paying’ for the bonus talent later on, making them a good option early on for expanding your breadth of abilities.

General Drawbacks

Addictive Casting - make a fort save every time you use a spell point, with an increasing DC or gain an addiction. This one counts as two drawbacks, but it is really nasty. Could be workable if you have lots of downtime and few encounters per day, but I count it mostly as NPC or word-building material.

Draining Casting - Take unhealable non-lethal damage every time to use a sphere ability. Not for you. It works as the basis for a blood-mage type character combined with the Fortified Casting boon, but you want to be using your abilities basically every round of combat, plus other uses outside, so ought to be avoided unless you have 15 minutes adventuring days.

Extended Casting - All your sphere abilities take longer to use, increasing times by one step. Fine for dedicated minion masters, which you are not (see the Thaumaturge). Taking a full round for you blast hurts, don’t do it.

Focus Casting - Casting requires a focus object, with penalties similar to the wizard’s arcane bond. Depends on your DM. If disarms and prisons feature heavily in your campaigns, avoid it. Note that you can make the DC for your casting lower by voluntarily reducing the CL of the ability you are using, which can help ensure you get the ability off in a pinch (best if the ability isn’t save-dependent).

Magical Signs - some tell-tale lets everyone in 30’ know you are casting and what the nature of the spell is. Avoid it if you are trying to be stealthy, but most of the time the ball of death you are hurling will give you away anyhow.

Material Casting - every ability costs money based on CL. The exact nature of the material spent is dependent on your DM. Another one that is fine for builds that use a few long-lasting abilities, not for you.

Painful Magic - fort save or be sickened every time you cast, escalating to nauseated if already sickened. Duration is short. Almost a freebie if doing the blood-mage combo (draining casting + fortified casting) since you will have such high CON you won’t miss the save often. For everyone else, you can pull it off if you optimize your Fort save, but you risk bad rolls stealing actions at key times.

Prepared Caster - You have to assign spell points to spheres at the beginning of each day. Since you focus so heavily on destruction, this can be a decent option, depending on how much you branch out. For example, set aside just enough SP to keep your conjuration companion going all day, then dump the rest in destruction. More diversified builds should be cautious though.

Skilled Casting - you have to make a perform, craft, or profession skill check or suffer reduced caster level. This meshes well with many concepts and characters from fiction. It might be tough early on, but skill checks can be optimized, especially after you have a few levels under your belt. An interesting option. Note that humans and half-elves can get skill focus at level 1 and other races offer various skill boosting options. Also note that the chosen skill might carry drawbacks, like perform (sing) doesn’t work in a silence effect or (dance) if you are immobilized.

Somatic Casting - you require a free hand to cast and now have to pay attention to arcane spell failure chances. Taken once allow light armor, twice allows no armor. Avoid it if your DM likes grappling enemies, but otherwise it is a strong option for taking once, since you aren’t wearing heavier than light armor anyway. Note that only armor is mentioned, so bucklers are fine. If your DM decides otherwise, mithral bucklers have 0% ASF and are reasonably priced.

Verbal Casting - you now have verbal components. If you have skilled casting in a skill that requires vocalization, this is a freebie. Not so good for sneaks, good for others.

Wild Magic - 10% chance to add a rod of wonder effect to your casting. Hilarious, but your party may hate you. If your DM uses the rod of wonder table as suggested, this is actually quite good so long as you make sure your effects happen AT enemies, since most of the options on the table impact the target. If your DM uses a different table your mileage may vary. Unrated since it is so concept and DM dependent.

Sphere Specific Drawbacks

Only going to cover the notable ones here.

Alteration:

Beast Soul - can’t use the blank form. Decreases your versatility, but gets a form talent so may be worthwhile.

Lycanthropic - you can only use alteration on yourself. Fine early on, keeping it will depend on your party composition. Sometimes it’s handy to be able to give everyone flight for a bit, but party buffing isn’t necessarily your job. If you can be selfish, take it.

Conjuration:

Elongated Summoning - summoning your companion takes a minute. If you grab the talents for an all-day companion, this becomes trivial. If you want to use your companion as a combat summons, then leave it alone.

Destruction: The latter two are better for another class dabbling in destruction, not for a specialist.

Aligned Combatant - scales damage down based on how close the target is to your alignment. Campaign dependent, would be great in Wrath of the Righteous, likely excluding most of your party from your AOEs and hitting almost all enemies for full damage, but terrible in many others.

Energy Focus - can only use on blast type. This is for being dedicated to a theme. This guide is for practical optimization, not thematics. If you take force or crystal you can get by, I suppose.

Destructive Touch - you now have no range, destructive blast is touch only. Locks you out of several blast shapes and the remaining ones have to start adjacent to you. Blech. With a magus dip this can be less bad, but I still would be reluctant to lose your ranged option.

Enhancement:

Bodily Enhancement - can’t target equipment or objects. Maybe very early until you buy it back.

Personal Magics - can’t target others. Be greedy, let the full casters buff the party.

Illusion

Disappearance - you have to take invisibility as your bonus for this drawback and can’t use illusions for anything else. Nice for being sneaky, but illusions can be very powerful. Worth a look, but might be better to get invisibility via wand and keep your versatility.

Personal Illusions - can only put illusions on yourself. Not worth it.

Telekinesis

Limited Telekinesis - You can only use telekinesis on one type of material. Only if you are building a themed character.

Warp

Limited Warp - can only warp to areas that meet certain conditions (light, dark, water, etc).  You have the potential to be able to start things on fire, but I still can’t recommend it.

Personal Warp - selfishness again. Nice way to get a talent to boost your mobility, since you are likely only dipping your toes in this sphere.

BOONS

Here is the payoff (other than spell points) for your drawbacks, each boon costing two drawbacks. Not many options, but worth looking at.

Deathful Magic - you get a CL boost when injured, +1 below 50% HP, +2 under 25%. I prefer to avoid being able to use this one, but things go south and that extra juice may make a difference.

Easy Focus - you can maintain concentration as a move action. Normally I have a hard time NOT taking this one, but destruction has all of 0 talents that require concentration. You can already move the sphere blast shape as a move. Therefore, the use of this boon is entirely dependent on your other spheres, so I am declining to rate it. That said, MANY other spheres benefit from it and it is all but required for any of the archetypes.

Empowered Abilities - Bonus CL if you get low on spell points. Interesting way to boost yourself through the end of the day and doesn't require being near death.

Fortified Casting - Con as your casting stat! Too bad you can’t take it without the draining casting drawback. If you qualify, take it.

Overcharge - you can add +2 CL at the cost of being fatigued, then exhausted, then collapsing. Neat way to boost your power for finishing that final fight or escaping in an emergency. Better if you have a way to quickly recover from the conditions inflicted.

EQUIPMENT

The section so many handbooks omit or never complete. This won’t be comprehensive, just going to shoot for the highlights.

Mundane Gear:

Armor - chain shirt, upgrading to mithral chain shirt once you get over 18 DEX. You could go for a mithral breastplate, but you would need medium armor proficiency. No thanks.

Shield - get a masterwork buckler to pile enchantments on. It’s cheap AC.

Weapons - Who cares? You have a suite of at-will damaging abilities that can cover every damage type. Grab a longspear to discourage enemies from charging you or just have a dagger to hold the defending enhancement to boost your AC. An elf might want a bow for long range early on. A magus dipper will want a scimitar (if Str-based or dervish-dancing) or a rapier (deadly agility) for the crit range, making it keen ASAP.

Misc - Rope, waterskin, mirror, marbles, nothing here but your usual adventurer kit.

Spheres of Power Magic Items: Spheres of Power has a re-worked magic item system to correspond with the casting system. Some games may use it exclusively, but many will use them alongside traditional items. Ultimately, you want a good staff.

Scrolls - you need to have the base sphere to use a scroll without UMD, so these are mainly good for niche talents that aren’t worth picking up to have all the time, but may save you some heartache that one time in the campaign it comes up.

Wondrous Items - great for constant effects or talents that you need once or twice a day or for at-will charged items (like normal wands, sphere wands work differently).

Rods - for at-will abilities. Great for shoring up your weak points. By the upper levels you should have a golf-bag full of these for various situations.

Rings - all continuous items fall here, boosting the utility of the craft rings feat. Mostly relevant if you are only using sphere items so that normal wondrous items are not available for your stat boosts and other necessary effects.

Wands - another way to grab talents you don’t have for base spheres you do. Note that the DC of wand abilities is set by the wands level and your casting mod. Wands come with a small pool of spell points that the caster has to recharge, max 1/day recharge rate.

Staves - staves work differently with spheres. Basically, they carry an enhancement bonus, like a weapon, that applies to a certain sphere. They can also be made to carry additional talents or apply to other spheres, with costs similar to weapon enchantments. You want the biggest bonus to destruction you can afford (+5 is the max, +10 equivalent with other abilities). Expanding it to cover your other favorite combat spheres is a good investment. Carry separate staves for out of combat spheres since combining them gets expensive.

Traditional Magic Items: Mostly, you want the standard array of stat and save boosters. Your casting should primarily be augmented with Spheres items, with the exception of metamagic rods. Note that you can use traditional scrolls and wands that correspond to your spheres and talents, though some adjudication is required from your DM.

Metamagic Rods - these are handy. Because your equivalent spell level will go up as your CL does, unless you lower it willingly reducing your effects and save DCs, you will generally want the highest grade rod available for your desired effect (why extend a power if you have to cut its duration in half to use the rod?). Dazing is of course wonderful, empower and maximize are nice, rime is great if you grab the cold talent. If it’s good for a normal blaster, it’s probably good for you.

Handy Haversack - you don’t likely have that much strength.

Muleback Cords - the other solution to carrying capacity.

Stat boosters - keep your casting stat maxed, obviously. Dex, then Con after that.

Gauntlets of Skilled Maneuver - nice if you focus on air blast bullrushing.


Pauldrons of the Bull - only if you like airblast. If you do, these are amazing.

Amulet of Mighty Fists – only for the natural attackers, but essential for them since you can enhance all of your attacks at once.

MULTICLASSING

As has been frequently observed in this guide, judicious multiclassing can be a significant boon. Due to the importance of caster level in the Spheres of Power system, it is strongly advised that you do not lose any caster levels unnecessarily. This severely limits the wise options for multiclassing. That said, the elementalist capstone is not worth worrying about. In fact, losing two levels costs you little beyond your elemental movement and the scaling increase to elemental offense and defense.

Spheres of Power Classes:

There are four classes that give a choice of casting stat, the Incanter, the Hedge Witch, the Mageknight, and the Thaumaturge. Of these, only the Incanter and the Thaumaturge are full casters; any other option would cost a caster level. The Fey Adept and Soul Weaver are also full casters, but since they both have Charisma as a casting stat, they are not terribly helpful for this purpose. So, Incanter or Thaumaturge?

Thaumaturge has better HD and skills points than the Incanter, but their class features are lacking for a short dip. The penalties for using their signature ability are too high for you to bother with since you won’t be going deep enough for the ability to dodge it. Also, they don’t get a talent at level 1.

Incanter drops a die size and two skill points, but gets 3 talents per 2 levels and has either bonus feats or specialization options. Specialization gets you an extra caster level and talent, so is great for boosting your destruction sphere. Using your other points to pick a domain that grants good base bonuses, like darkness (blindfight), liberation (freedom of movement), or travel (+10 movement speed), can be beneficial as well. Pay attention to which feats are lost by specializing; if you are going to lose all the feats from the levels you plan on taking, then you might as well spend all the specialization points.

Fey Adept and Soul Weaver are great classes, but offer little for an elementalist dip since their scaling abilities would be largely wasted.

Sphere-casting Archetypes (SoP):

Paladin Only there for a two level dip to get divine grace, then grab advanced magic training so you only lose one CL. Getting your casting stat to saves is awesome, though the other benefits are limited. You don’t actually need to sphere casting archetype here since it doesn’t kick in until level 4.

Sphere Bard A few rounds of performance aren’t worth it. Losing caster levels is bad! Cha-based.

Sphere Cleric medium armor doesn’t help unless it's mithral since it would shut off your evasion. Domains give an extra talent and has two good saves, so it's not a bad dip to go Wis-based. Archetype diving might turn something up.

Sphere Druid medium armor doesn’t help at all since you can’t wear metal. Animal companion won’t scale, but a domain would grant a talent. Go cleric instead unless dying for skill points. Thematically appropriate with a geomancer. Wis-based.

Sphere Wizard Basically an incanter with pre-selected options. A familiar can be cool, bonded items less so, though it’s mostly harmless if you take the bonded casting drawback anyhow. Probably just take incanter, honestly. Int-based, obviously.

Sphere-casting Archetypes (Expanded Options):

Sphere arcanist gives a floating talent, which makes them rather unique and thus a fair option if you want to be Int-based. Pick up a level early if you are going this route, maybe more later if you have everything you want from elementalist.

Sphere Bloodrager No! Bad player! No! (Could perhaps be usable on a melee build. Still wouldn’t do it.) Cha-based.

Sphere Inquisitor has nothing for a short dip that you wouldn’t be better off getting somewhere else. Losing caster levels is bad! Wis-based.

Sphere Magus is necessary for a non-natural attack melee build. Grab two levels for spell strike and spell combat. Spell combat also neatly reduces the need to the easy focus boon, though you weren’t too concerned about that for the destruction sphere. Nature uses concentration heavily though, for you archetyped elementalists. Not great for others though, since you do lose a caster level. Adding a dip into incanter could let you still go wisdom based as well, since nothing in sphere magus requires intelligence for a short dip. See the question and answer to confirm that spellstrike works with all known applicable talents.

Sphere Oracle There are some interesting things you can pick up with a short dip here, like Cha to AC, though that one is less help since you want good Dex anyhow, or martial weapons, which you won’t use. Still, there are many options, so you can probably find something worthwhile. Curses do scale at half-rate with non-oracle levels, so they aren’t as bad as they could be. Cha-based.

Sphere Sorcerer with the orc bloodline (or draconic or primal wildblooded elemental, if focusing on only one elemental damage type and not wanting light blindness) can grant an extra +1 damage per damage die, a nice boon for your damage totals. Multiclassing sorcerer can also grant +1 CL to a sphere and a bonus talent if you prefer to trade out the arcana.

Sphere Summoner doesn’t give you anything you need. You lose CL for your primary spheres. If you want to dabble in summoning, just take some conjuration talents. If you want to focus, don’t be an elementalist!

Sphere Witch is in the same boat as the shaman, minus armor proficiency, HD, and skills. Int-based.

SPELLCRAFTING

A neat little option in the advanced magic section to allow you to combine abilities at the cost of longer casting times and spell point costs. You can only have a number made up to your casting mod, so make them count, though you can grab the spellbook feat to keep more handy. Without the spellcrafting feat, you can’t combine different spheres, but even with the limitations you should make a few, updating them as needed as you gain talents.

For destruction, I would recommend a simple combo of your favorite blasts, like force blast as the base (best damage type barring vulnerabilities) with crystal blast (to entangle, which helps offset the AC increase from prone). Combining the two is only complexity 1, so you can use it as a full-round action at no cost. Add your favorite shape in for the base ability for a second spell, say the orb shape, and you have a wide AOE blast that will do good almost never resisted damage, even to incorporeals, while knocking them prone and entangling them, at the modest cost of losing your move action that round.

Note that with the spellcrafting feat, you can combine different spheres and most importantly, the base sphere sets the CL and saves of the finished spell. This provides a sneaky way to circumvent your reduced CL for non-Destruction spheres. Charming crystal trap? Force blast of silence? Rebuff that grants a protective wall? Go nuts. Just keep in mind the complexity, you don’t want to push your casting time past a full-round action for anything to be used in combat and the spell point cost for higher complexities gets painful fast. Also note that duration is set by the base ability, so use conjuration as your base and make that elemental rebuff last 24 hrs.

Note that all spells are subject to DM approval. This is especially relevant for getting tricky by extending short term buffs by stacking them on conjuration or other spheres with hour long options.

ARCHETYPES

Presently, all elementalist archetypes are found in Spheres of Power: Expanded Options. We get five, more than any other class, though really it is four variants of one archetype and then another rather dramatic one. Because I like you all so much, I’ve even included a non-existent archetype for your reading pleasure.

You get four (element) warrior archetypes, fire, water, wind, and earth. Each limits your favored element to a single, pre-selected type, limits you elemental defense to a single element and your elemental movement to one type with increasing speeds, and modifies your capstone. Interestingly, each also grants a bonus to CL for the relevant nature sphere package, excepting air which could have granted a bonus for weather but instead boosts your CMB for bullrushing. Note that none of the archetypes restrict you from using other blast types, you just can’t take favored element with them.

Fire Warrior

Fire, frequently resisted and poorly rated blast type above. That said, resistance to fire is nice to have. You get a huge bonus to your land speed, so that is interesting, though ask the monk just how awesome outrunning a cheetah is (Answer: not very). Immunity to fire is a nice addition to the capstone, but DR10/magic is still a joke. Getting your full level to the fire package of the nature sphere is nice, though doesn’t happen until level 9. If you want to make a fire-bender, this is a good way to go, but I’m not going to recommend it highly on its merits. It does what it sets out to do nicely, but loses out compared to other options based on the weakness of fire as an element and as a blast type. Nature does allow you to be adept at moving fires around, but the action cost versus the utility seems questionable. Grab the shape veil feat from akashic mysteries for the circlet of brass to boost your fire damage further. This archetype is actually rather strong for melee builds utilizing natural attacks via the fire wielder talent.

Water Warrior

Cold, also frequently resisted, but at least you get a decent rider effect. You get the water package from nature and a swim speed. Swim speeds are only situationally useful in most campaigns and can be easily negated with the alteration sphere most of the time. Nature gives the strong freeze and fog abilities, though supplying the water will get pricey in terms of spell points. Stronger in an aquatic heavy campaign.

Wind Warrior

No nature package for you, instead you get +2, then +4 on bullrush checks. The bonus is nice to keep your checks relevant and combined with improved/greater bulrush, pauldrons of the bull, and gauntlets of the skilled maneuver you should be able to apply some significant BFC via pushing people around (and triggering AOOs from your allies due to greater bulrush). High flight speeds with improving maneuverability is nice, so that is an improvement as well. Immunity to non-lethal at your capstone may open up shenanigans if you get tricky with a source of regeneration. I’m probably overrating this one, but I’ve been looking for a reason to pump air blast. <shrug>

Earth Warrior

Stone blast, earth package, DR/adamantine. By the time the DR matters a decent number of enemies will bypass it one way or another. DR3/adamantine is very weak for level 17. Bumping to 15 as the capstone is better, but still not a big deal. Stone blast is always applicable, a burrow speed opens up fun tactics, and tremorsense is nice, giving this archetype a nice boost over other choices. The forge earth talent is your strongest nature ability unless you happen to have a rather large pile of sand handy, but it’s a doozy both in and out of combat.

Geomancer

I suppose this is where I should make an avatar joke, but I never watched it. Hah! That said, this is the archetype that dramatically changes the class, swapping full levels in destruction for full levels in nature, with CL boosts to specific packages in place of favored element. One package ends up with CL +6, another +4, a third +2, which means you are getting some serious boosts. Wish they had the same thing for the weather sphere (What, I like tossing tornados at my enemies!). See the Spheres section for more info on the nature sphere.

As a geomancer, I’m probably taking plantlife first, followed by earth and water. The geomancer should always be able to use one of the geomancing options regardless of location, though picking up weather to bring the rain when you want to save spell points may not hurt. Compared to the base elementalist, the geomancer should have lower damage, higher utility, and similar BFC.

Geomancers should be gaining knowledge (nature) and losing escape artist in future releases/errata.

Stormcaller

This is the archetype that isn’t. I noticed that it would be easy enough to make the Geomancer work for the weather sphere and was told:

“We'd already done 5 elementalist archetypes so we had to draw the line somewhere, but yeah that would be completely doable and was slated to be the 6th.”

I slapped a name on it of my own devising and decided to include it as a bonus in this guide since no one can stop me.

Weave Weather (Su): A Stormcaller gains the Weather sphere as a bonus sphere at 1st level, and uses his elementalist level as his caster level with that sphere. This stacks normally with caster levels gained from other sources.

This replaces Weave Energy.

Favored Category (Su): At 3rd level, a stormcaller chooses one of the weather category from the Weather sphere and gains a +2 bo­nus to their caster level with that category. At 9th level, this bo­nus improves by an additional +2, and they may choose another category to gain a +2 bonus with. At 15th level, all previously chosen categories increase their caster level by another +2, and the stormcaller may choose a third category to gain a +2 bonus.

This replaces favored element.

 

Impervious (Ex): At 5th level, stormcaller may treat all weather effects as being 1 step lesser in severity. This increases to two steps at 11th level, and three steps at 17th level.

This replaces Elemental Defense.

 I would recommend taking wind first, then rain, then cold or heat. The temperatures I find a bit lackluster on their own, though they all have uses. Note that this sphere takes some time to get running since when you move, the new area has to have time to build up to strength, which is not entirely clear from the document but was clarified here:

“As the weather sphere effect moves, each new 5-ft square would need to grow into the full effect a round at a time, and the 5-ft squares left behind would go back to normal weather a round at a time.”

Taking wind lord, rain lord, and storm lord x 2 gives you 1/round lightening for respectable damage at no additional action cost and can affect a large area (incredible with the advanced talent) while also debuffing ranged attacks and hindering movement. The ramp time is really the only thing holding it back, so if you spend all your time indoors, don’t bother.
