Intro

Occult Adventures is a magnificent book. Six new classes and so many new archetypes, among other occult stuff. Guides for the new classes have begun popping one at a time, and for a long time the spiritualist had to manage without a guide.

At first glance spiritualist is just a summoner… without summon monster SLA. Or pounce beast eidolon. Or evolutions to customise the companion. So, what is a spiritualist, and how is a player supposed to build and play one? Well, you’ve come to the right place.

This guide uses the basic, color-coded rating system:

Red: A very poor option. Either it doesn’t do anything worthwhile or doesn’t work as promised. Other options are alway better. Should be avoided.

Orange: A mediocre option. May be useful in some ways, but is generally subpar to the other choices.

Green: A good option. Often useful, but not necessarily always. A solid pick overall.

Blue: An excellent option. So good that you might regret not picking this one.

As a side note, I recommend checking out Phantom of the OP-era by CockroackTeaParty and Nova Wurmson, which is far more abundant in content. I began writing Eternal Servitude on Oct 21st 2015 but they got theirs done first. Our opinions differ but that’s a good thing: now the readers always have access to a second opinion.

Roles

Spiritualist, like all classes in the game, can fulfill several different roles depending on how it’s built.

Fighting style

Spiritualists cast spells, but being 6th-level spellcasters as they are they can’t do spellcasting all day every day. Don’t get me wrong, spiritualists need to be competent in spellcasting, but they should also have something to do when they can’t spend their actions buffing the party, throwing save-or-sucks at the enemy or whatever. Look at it this way: why be just a competent spellcaster when you can just as easily also be a competent melee combatant or an archer?

This is why I recommend for all spiritualists to choose a fighting style and have the build accommodate that style. How much you want to focus on the martial side at the expense of spellcasting is up to you, but I recommend a healthy balance of being competent in both.

2h-Melee (aka Reaper)

A melee-oriented spiritualist may sound odd. After all, the class does not even have proficiency with martial weapons. But they do have proficiency in scythes (martial 2h weapon). Obviously there are better martial 2h weapons out there, but a scythe does the job nicely (and is thematic and badass as duck!). If the choice of weapons, or lack thereof, is bothering you there’s always half-orc and dwarf.

What really makes spiritualist a good melee character is that it is essentially TWO melee characters: the Reaper and his Phantom. The Reaper focuses on being a standard 2h martial character, not that hard thing to do in Pathfinder. A good Strength score and a Power Attack goes a long way, with spellcasting as an added bonus but we’ll get to that later. The Phantom, while weaker than any true martial character, can still provide a lot: battlefield control by taking up a space, ability to transfer touch attacks (inflict serious wounds at 7th level!) to deal with high-AC enemies, provide flanks, and even act as a meat shield should a need for one arise.

But this is nothing new. Melee druids, rangers, and tag-team summoners have done this for a long time. And do not mistake spiritualist as a good melee class on its own. d8 HD and light armor proficiency is all the melee defense it gets, aside from having a phantom meat shield. I criticize the ranged role for being feat-heavy, but truthfully Reapers have to spend at least Power Attack, probably Toughness and maybe even Medium Armor Proficiency. And that’s just for basics!

Melee builds may be criticized for being a one-trick pony that struggles to do anything if the problem solving does not involve moving next to an opponent and hitting it. That is not the case with Reapers. All Reapers most likely learn fly at 7th level, and a phantom can fly indefinitely starting at 9th level (albeit only in incorporeal form), and can pass through objects… in fact, just read the other roles: you’ll see that a spiritualist can do a lot. Don’t be afraid to be a melee guy.

Archer (aka Master)

A staple choice for a pet class. The Reaper fights alongside his Phantom, whereas the Master stays in the rear while his Servant fights in close-combat. This is perfectly acceptable, as long as the Master also provides something for the team. Archery is a good way to stay away from harm while being an effective combatant.

This role is noticeably the feat-heaviest, or at least a lot more feat intensive than being a Reaper. Archery takes dedication, and with no bonus feats to back this up the Master will always be a subpar archer compared to the true martials. Even choosing a weapon is a compromise: should you take Martial Weapon Proficiency (composite longbow), be an elf or settle for the crossbow?

But when done right, once you start having the right feats, there is little your duo of Master and Servant cannot do: one in melee, one in ranged. Every and any party will be glad have your duo. And the best part is, you don’t have to risk your life and limb to accomplish that.

Switch-hitter

Don’t get greedy. You have barely enough feats to afford either Melee and Ranged. Going switch-hitter (which, if you didn’t know, refers to someone who uses both ranged and melee weapons) would make you bad at both. Look, if you want a character who’s good at melee and ranged… that’s Master. One part of the duo is good at melee and the other is good at ranged. If you, the spiritualist, attempt to do both as a switch-hitter… that’s rather pointless and ineffective. Don’t get greedy, choose either Reaper or Master.

Two-Weapon Fighter

No real abilities to back this up. As feat extensive as Archer, if not even more extensive, but the damage is harder to come by than the archer or 2h fighting. If I see any hope in this role it’s that spiritualists are proficient in kukris and having a flank buddy helps offset the TWF penalties. But even then those negated penalties would have been bonuses if only you were a Reaper.

Feats aren’t the only problem with TWF spiritualist. You’d also become very MAD (multiple-ability dependent). Sufficient TWF would require investments in Dex, meaning that other ability scores would have to drop. Best case scenario you’ll drop Int and deal with less skill ranks, worst case scenario your Str, Con or Wis will take a hit.

Loafer

Look, whatever it is that you decide to specialise in, do NOT be this guy. Do NOT spend your actions in combat doing nothing. Pure spellcasting-focused spiritualists are in danger of falling to this fighting “role”. Make sure you can fight. If you don’t, your duo risks falling back to being only the phantom. And even a fighter, god have mercy, can do better than that.

Spellcasting

Spiritualist is a spontaneous 6th-level caster, so it is extremely vital to know what spells they should cast.

Buffer

You have really solid buff spells on your spell list (including haste and fly, and taking good spells from both wizard and cleric lists), so this is definitely something that every spiritualist should do on the side to some degree: all spellcasting roles can pick haste and fly because buffs do not have DCs to worry about. Unfortunately as a 6th-level caster spiritualist spells per day don’t warrant you to do this as a full-time job, except maybe at higher levels.

Debuffer

I feel a common misconception about the spiritualist that it is a class that revolves solely around bad touch. Yes, spiritualists also have solid debuff spells on their list, and even better they can deliver bad touch spells from afar thanks to the phantom’s deliver touch spells ability, starting at 3rd level. Spiritualists make good debuffers.

However, spiritualists have relatively low spells per day (as any 6th-level caster does) so if you go down this path as your main role, you’ll only be competent at debuffing during a handful of rounds per day, and then spend the rest of the adventuring day hiding behind your phantom and throwing lower-level spells in hopes a mook fails his save and takes some minor penalties. Buffs on the other hand last multiple rounds, have no DCs and also complement the two fighting roles (Master and Reaper).

You need to invest in Wisdom (or Charisma for Fractured Mind) if you want your spell DCs to actually mean something. In any case, not a bad role, and a great side-job for Masters.


Focused Reapers should stay away from this role. Rarely will a debuff spell work better than a well-placed buff or a full-attack.

Battlefield Control

OK, you have black tentacles on your spell list. You can summon monsters, too. That’s about as far as it goes. Besides, you are kinda already doing this without any spells needed. Your phantom acts as a meat shield that provides flank opportunities.

Blaster

Show me a single blast spell from the spell list. Then show a blast spell that would deal more damage than a full-attack action. Then tell me why spiritualists would make better blasters than, say, sorcerer, wizard or oracle. That’s what I thought.

Healbot

Just because you have cure spells on the spell list does not mean you should make your spiritualist a dedicated healer. Grab a wand of CLW and you’re dandy for a good number of levels.

Other/Out of combat

Scout

One thing that sets spiritualists apart from summoners is their unmatched ability to scout through walls. Or rather, the phantoms’ ability to do so. Many times will the GM sigh as your phantom goes through a wall, sees what is supposed to be a big surprise encounter and comes back to tell about it. Fortunately you don’t have to build around this: ectoplasmic phantoms’ Phase Lurch and incorporeal manifestation are complete freebies. This role is preinstalled. Good to go. Remember to have the phantom invest skill ranks in Perception, and maybe Stealth.

Party Face

Bluff, Intimidate and Sense Motive as class skills. With Fractured Mind you can switch your casting stat from Wisdom to Charisma. Diplomacy as a class skill is one trait away. Did I mention several of phantom emotions benefit from an Intimidating spiritualist?

...But of course if you are not a Fractured Mind, Charisma is a dump stat, so yeah...

Textbook

The bad news is that spiritualist is a Wisdom-based caster (or Charisma if you take Fractured Mind archetype), so Intelligence may end up being a dump stat. The good news is 4+Int skill ranks and very good class skills: all Knowledge skills, Spellcraft and UMD among others. Textbook is not really a role to specialise in, but having a good amount of skill ranks could make the class more wanted for parties.

Ability Scores

Here are some example ability score arrays. These numbers do not include racial modifiers. Wisdom is the default spellcasting ability, but Fractured Soul changes it to Charisma. If your Soul is a bit Fractured, you’ll have to make some difficult compromises, as you lose your safe dump stat. Even as a Fractured Mind, Wisdom is more important than Intelligence.

Reapers

Str > Wis > Con > Dex > Int > Cha

Strength makes your weapon hurt more, and damage is exactly what spiritualists struggle to find otherwise. Constitution keeps you alive, which is a big deal for a d8 class who dabbles into melee. Wisdom is equally important, as it not only is your casting stat but also affects Will saves and Perception. Dexterity is for AC and Init, both important, and for the occasional if undesired need to shoot things from afar. Intelligence earns you skills, which isn’t a main focus, but is nice to have undumped. Charisma is something you don’t need, dump it.

Example Arrays

20-point buy: Str 16, Dex 12, Con 14, Int 10, Wis 15, Cha 7

15-point buy: Str 16, Dex 12, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 14, Cha 7

Masters

Dex > Wis > Str(?) >= Con > Int > Cha

Archers need Dexterity to hit things, and it affects Initiative and AC, so Dexterity should obviously be pumped as high as possible. Wisdom is for casting spells, not failing Will saves and noticing things. Strength is worth investing only if you use a composite longbow (that is, you are an elf or half-elf). Constitution keeps you alive, which is always on demand, albeit not as much when you stay in the rear. Intelligence earns you skills, which isn’t a main focus, but is nice to have undumped. Charisma adds nothing to your table: dump it.

Example Arrays

20-point buy (composite): Str 14, Dex 16, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 15, Cha 7

15-point buy (composite): Str 12, Dex 16, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 14, Cha 7

20-point buy (no composite): Str 10, Dex 17, Con 12, Int 12, Wis 15, Cha 7

15-point buy (no composite): Str 10, Dex 16, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 15, Cha 7

Casting-focused

Wis > Str > Con > Dex > Int > Cha (for semi-Reaper)

Wis > Dex > Con >= Str > Int > Cha (for semi-Master)

And of course, investing more in Wisdom is perfectly acceptable. More extra spells and higher DCs in favor of being less focused in fighting can be a wise (pun intended) decision, so long as you do not altogether ignore the martial side of the build.

Example Arrays

20-point buy (semi-Reaper): Str 15, Dex 12, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 17, Cha 7

15-point buy (semi-Reaper): Str 14, Dex 12, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 16, Cha 7

20-point buy (semi-Master): Str 12, Dex 16, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 16, Cha 7

15-point buy (semi-Master): Str 10, Dex 15, Con 12, Int 10, Wis 16, Cha 7

What about Fractured Mind?

Oh no, Charisma is no longer a dump stat, and Wisdom can’t be ignored either: Will saves are a big deal in this game. Which means that either you compromise physical scores or dump Intelligence. Looks like it’s goodbye skill ranks.

20-point buy (Fractured Reaper): Str 16, Dex 12, Con 14, Int 7, Wis 10, Cha 15

20-point buy (Fractured Master): Str 12, Dex 16, Con 13, Int 7, Wis 12, Cha 15

20-point buy (Fractured Casting-Reaper): Str 15, Dex 12, Con 12, Int 7, Wis 10, Cha 17

20-point buy (Fractured Casting-Master): Str 12, Dex 16, Con 12, Int 7, Wis 10, Cha 16

Races

If you’ve read any other class guides, you should know the drill. Humans have bonus feat and are always a good pick. Anything with a bonus to Str (for Reapers), Dex (for Masters) or Wis (or Cha if you take Fractured Mind archetype) is a good choice. Penalty to Wisdom is not crippling as you can take Fractured Mind and replace it with Charisma.

Core Races

Dwarf (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Bonus to Wisdom and Constitution are highly, highly appreciated. No bonus to Str or Dex is a shame but not overly so. Dwarves make better Reapers than Masters due to Con boost and improved saves (Masters typically like to avoid having to roll a save in the first place). Darkvision is excellent. They also get proficiency in many weapons spiritualists wouldn’t normally have. Overall a fantastic choice for a Reaper. As casting-focused spiritualists, dwarves are on the top of the list, with a bonus to Wisdom and excellent defensive racial traits.

Elf (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Bonus to Dex and proficiency with composite longbows make elves a serious competition for humans for the title of best Master race (pun not intended). Int bonus is a waste, but maybe that means you can dump it a bit further. No Wis (or Cha) bonus means that elves should give up on trying to be caster-focused. Penalty to Constitution, and the lack of Str bonus means elves should leave Reaping for others. Don’t bother making a casting-based elf spiritualist, though, as elves have no bonus for Wisdom nor Charisma.

Gnome (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): As a small-sized race with Strength penalty, Reaping is a really tough thing to do for these guys. And as a race for Masters... no bonus to Dex or anything to help with archery, it’s hard to compete against other race choices. On the other hand, their bonus to Charisma can make them really solid spellcasters.

Half-elf (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): A floating bonus can go to Str, Dex, or casting stat. With Ancestral Arms (replaces Skill Focus) half-elves gain Martial or Exotic Weapon Proficiency as a bonus feat: this allows Masters to use composite longbows and Reaper half-elves to use something other than a scythe. Everything beyond that is just icing on the cake. As far as spellcasting goes, well, flexible +2 can go anywhere.

Halfling (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Small size won’t hurt Masters, and a bonus to Dex makes them good archers. Bonus to saves is good. As Reapers though, well, don’t even think about it. Small size and Charisma bonus are the things to look for when making a halfling caster spiritualist. Just don’t try using bad touch spells yourself, save those for the phantom.

Half-orc (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Proficiency with falchions and greataxes! Floating bonus to Str! Darkvision! Orc Ferocity/Sacred Tattoo! Time to reap some souls! Nothing overly great for Master half-orcs, but at least there’s the floating bonus to Dex, Sacred Tattoo, and Darkvision to help with shooting in dark areas. The same goes for spellcasting-focused half-orcs.

Human (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Hands up if you were surprised to see human as blue. Master is really hungry for feats, and human gets one extra, it’s a no-brainer. As for Reapers, they aren’t as feat-heavy, but still appreciate the options. And of course: floating bonus to either Str or Dex. Also extra skill ranks helps fill the Textbook and/or Party Face role(s), and offset the harms of low Int.

Other Races

There’s too many for me to bother to go over one by one. It’s simple, really. Bonus to Str makes a good Reaper, bonus to Dex makes a good Master. Also look for bonuses to Wis or Cha. Penalty to Con hurts Reapers but not Masters. Got a penalty to Wis? No problem, just take Fractured Mind. Look for anything that helps with archery, or with carrying weapons or surviving in melee.

Some races worth mentioning are:

Oread (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Think half-orcs but with more perfect ability modifiers but without Orc Ferocity/Sacred Tattoo.

Tengu (Reaper / Master / Spellcaster): Tengus have pretty much perfect ability scores for Masters: +2 Dex and Wis, and -2 Con doesn’t hurt archers too much. They would be the perfect race for Masters if they had something to help wield bows. They come close though, and that’s the reason why tengus make pretty OK Reapers as well: they are proficient with most swords (including eg. greatswords and falchions). Reapers benefit from Wisdom, a little boost to Dex never hurt anyone either, but it’s that -2 Con that sucks for Reapers.

Tiefling: Standard tieflings are okay: some resistances and Darkvision, good abilities for a Master, meh abilities for a Reaper. If your GM allows variant heritages, however…

Oni-Spawn (Reaper): Ooh, perfect abilities for a Reaper! I’d take this over half-orc any day.

Asura-Spawn (Master): Almost perfect abilities for a Master. Darkvision helps archers, but, as they have no bow proficiencies, Faultspawns not quite rival half-elves, elves and humans.

Phantom Emotions

Possibly the biggest decision each spiritualist has to make, after the role and the race, is the phantom’s Emotional Focus. Emotional Focus (or just Emotion for short) dictates much of the phantom’s abilities, so the decision has a huge impact on the duo.

tl;dr Version

If you want a general picture first and specifics later (or just can’t be bothered by specifics at all), read the following.

Anger: A straightforward emotion that deals excellent damage.

Dedication: Provides a hefty boost to saves. Damage is nothing to cheer for.

Despair: Becomes stronger against afraid enemies.

Fear: Amplifies the fear of enemies.

Hatred: Dex-based mini-slayer that focuses on dealing damage. Has the worst aura.

Jealousy: Helpful effects, both in slam and aura. A bit on the weaker side of green, but OK.

Zeal: Gets some real good stuff at late levels. Before that, below average.

Emotionless (Geist archetype): Weak bad touch, weak aura, some spell-like abilities.

Remorse (Occult Origins): Focuses on bodyguarding and debuffing, some lackluster abilities.

Greed (Occult Realms): Probably the worst emotion.

Lust (Occult Realms): Bodyguard phantom that has more hp and a bit annoying aura.

Pride (Occult Realms): Nothing worth mentioning.

In summary, every emotion (sans Greed, Pride and Emotionless) is valid and different emotions do different things. Anger and Hatred focus on damage, Dedication, Jealousy, Zeal and Remorse are more skewed towards protecting the spiritualist. Despair and Fear are fairly similar, focusing on intimidating and abusing fear conditions of afraid enemies. These two require a bit different focus on the spiritualist’s part, and appreciate debuffing more than any other emotion.

Detailed Version

Saves: I rate saves by their usefulness for the phantom. My thought process is this: If a phantom fails a Fortitude save it becomes less useful and can be ignored; if a phantom fails a Reflex save it takes a bit more damage; if a phantom fails a Will save it may become a hindrance and must be dismissed.

Obviously the most damaging failure is failing a Will save. Fort and Reflex are about even on their usefulness, but ultimately I rate Will > Fort > Ref.

Anger

Skills: Intimidate and Survival. Survival is useless, but Intimidate has some merit, or it would have if Anger relied on fear. It doesn’t.

Saves: Fort and Will. Excellent.

Strength Focus: Essentially this makes Anger phantoms Strength-based instead of Dex-based. Which means higher damage, lower AC and initiative. For spiritualists looking for a damage-dealing phantom this is a really good deal.

Powerful Strike: Slam deals more damage, Power Attack as a bonus feat. This, accompanied by Strength Focus, solidifies Anger’s position as the go-to emotion for damage-dealing phantoms.

Aura of Fury: All creatures (allies and enemies alike) within 20 feet gain a +2 bonus on melee attack rolls but take -2 penalty to AC. Depending on your party composition this may be either really good or even disruptively bad. Luckily it can be turned off as a free action.

Ferocious Mien: 1/day as a swift action the phantom can grow one category larger and be affected by rage spell. More damage. Very limited usage though but hey, more damage! And that’s what you’re choosing Anger for, right?

Furious Wail: 1/day as a standard action can use wail of banshee. A nice late game ability.

Conclusion: Anger is undoubtedly one of the best emotions. Raw damage is the real selling point, and that’s what spiritualists usually look for in a phantom.

Dedication

Skills: Diplomacy and Sense Motive. Diplomacy is a nice thing, especially for spiritualists who dump Cha. Just don’t be surprised when people don’t react well to phantoms doing the talking.

Saves: Reflex and Will. Good.

Iron Will: Iron Will as a bonus feat for phantom, and to the spiritualist whenever the phantom is within his consciousness. Will saves are a big deal in this game, and this helps both members of the duo.

Dutiful Strike: Extra damage against enemies who hurt the spiritualist. Situational, but works for Reaper who endanger themselves in melee. For Masters, not so much.

Defending Aura: Allies within 10 feet gain a +2 deflection bonus to AC and a +2 untyped bonus on CMD and all saves. The best aura any emotion gets, hands down. Your party will love you for this.

Devoted Servant: Very situational ability to briefly manifest the phantom. Does nothing for most part. But it can be a life-saver, maybe.

Steadfast Devotion: Phantom becomes immune to detrimental mind-affecting effects and all forms of possession and to banishment, dismissal etc. A solid buff to an already tanky phantom.

Conclusion: While the damage potential isn’t particularly high, Defending Aura is so good this emotion should be considered by Reapers and Masters alike.

Despair

Skills: Intimidate and Stealth. Despair phantoms appreciate Intimidate, and phantoms tend to get stealthy when scouting. Pretty amazing.

Saves: Fort and Will. Excellent.

Power from Despair: +2 bonus on attack and damage rolls vs creatures that are shaken, frightened, panicked, etc. If your party has an “Intimimancer” or a fear-specialised enchanter, this is pure gold. If not, well, then the spiritualist has to build around it and be a fear-based debuffer. Has some good synergy with Miserable Strike (see below), so maybe you can work something out.

As a side note, spiritualists have Intimidate as a class skill. Pick Fractured Mind archetype for higher Charisma and suddenly you have a working combination. Spiritualists intimidates and his phantom gains bonuses: sounds like a deal. If you do this, this emotion is green with shades of blue. If not, this emotion is yellow.

Miserable Strike: Each of the phantom’s slam attacks can cause the target to take a -2 penalty on attack and damage rolls for 1 round. Not really extra damage, and the DC is kinda low (phantoms do have a decent Cha score, but not overly high), and many enemies are immune to it, but has good synergy with Power from Despair. If it weren’t for Power from Despair this ability would be red.

Aura of Despair: All enemies within 10 feet take a -2 penalty on all saves. Hoo-boy, the casters in the party are going to love you and your phantom for this. To be honest, this is the primary reason to pick Despair, the rest is just some opportunities to work around with.

Despairing Shout: 3/day as a standard action can use crushing despair. Again, low DC. And it only mimics a 4th level spell, which is kinda late for a 12th level character. Also a bad use of a standard action in most cases.

Inescapable Despair: No save against miserable strikes. Other than the badass name, this ability is a lousy capstone.

Conclusion: Clearly a fear-based emotion. Has a nice niche which it works around. This emotion definitely benefits from Intimidating spiritualists, so if it’s Despair you want, take the Fractured Mind archetype and invest in Intimidate.

Fear

Skills: Intimidate and Stealth. Same as Despair: Intimidate should come in handy (especially with Increase Fear) and phantoms tend to get stealthy.

Saves: Reflex and Will. Good.

Stealthy: +2 (or +4 if you have more than 10 ranks) bonus on Stealth and Escape Artist. Who needs Power Attack, Iron Will or Weapon Finesse when you can have this bonus feat?

Horrifying Strike: Each of the phantom’s slam attacks can cause the target to become shaken for 1d4 rounds. DC is relatively low (phantoms do have a decent Cha score, but not overly high) and many enemies are going to be immune. At least the duration is decent.

Increase Fear: All enemies within 20 feet have their fear amplified by one step (shaken -> frightened -> panicked -> cower), Will save negates. This is the primary reason to choose Fear, and makes Intimidate a powerful skill. Too bad it doesn’t come online until 7th level.

Frightful Attack: Phantom’s slams now frighten instead of cause shaken. Certainly useful, as it makes Horrifying Strikes more effective.

Shelter Allies: Allies within Increase Fear aura (see above) are immune to fear. Spiritualist is always immune to fear (provided the phantom isn’t banished). A nice capstone ability, even providing the spiritualist with something very concrete.

Conclusion: Nothing too strong, nothing too weak, except for the bonus feat. This phantom’s job isn’t to deal damage, but demoralize the enemies and amplify their fear. Too bad the main schtick comes online as late as 7th level.

Hatred

Skills: Acrobatics and Perception. Perception was already a class skill, but having automatic ranks on it is great. And hey, Skill Focus (Perception) when the phantom is in inside your head. Neat!

Saves: Fort and Reflex. No Will, orange you’ll be.

Weapon Finesse: Wow, this is actually a good bonus feat, considering that phantoms’ Dex score scales automatically. Now you can spend your 1st-level feat on something useful!

Hated Target: As a move action, designate a creature as a hated target. Phantom gains +2 bonus on attack rolls and +½ per HD bonus on damage rolls against the hated target, but takes a -2 penalty on attack rolls against all other creatures. Your phantom is now practically a mini-slayer. Awesome! There are certainly situations where you don’t want to use this, but when you do want to use this, it’s pretty great.

Hateful Aura: Enemies within 10 feet who deal damage to the spiritualist or the phantom take damage equal to Charisma bonus. ...That’s all? Pathetic damage in a very situational circumstance? Worst part is, this is a mind-affecting effect (also pain), so some creatures are immune. At 7th level, your phantom’s Charisma score is something like 15 or 16. Wowzers.

Sneak Attack: Phantom gains sneak attack +3d6, but only against the hated enemy. A good, if a bit unreliable, boost to damage. Not bad.

Shared Hatred: Phantom’s allies also gain bonuses against the hated enemy (damage bonus is only +4 though). Phantom can  use hated enemy even in incorporeal form, and can make slam attacks in this form against hated enemies. Good support ability at best. Everyone likes damage.

Conclusion: A solid choice. Good damage potential, almost rivaling that of Anger’s. Shame about the horrible, horrible aura.

Jealousy

Skills: Appraise and Bluff. Hah... “Don’t worry, my phantom can bluff the guards”... Appraise might come in handy, if it wasn’t for the 7 Int...

Saves: Reflex and Will. Good.

Deceitful: Oh my god, Paizo actually made this the “bluffer” emotion?! I mean, I joked earlier about phantoms bluffing, but… look, very rarely do you need Bluff (or Disguise, for that matter) so badly that a phantom would make the situation any better. Take a look at the other emotions: they get stuff like Power Attack, Iron Will, Weapon Finesse. But Deceitful?

Jealous Combatant: Each time the phantom hits a creature with a melee attack, for 1 round that creature takes a –2 penalty on all attack rolls made against anyone other than the phantom. Pretty nice. It’s either a no-save debuff or it forces the enemy to target your phantom which means you and your party don’t get targeted. Pretty excellent.

Resentful Aura: Enemies within 20 feet who make an attack against or cast a spell that does not include the phantom in its effect become staggered until the end of the their next turn. Will negates. DC is kinda low (phantoms do have a decent Cha score, but not overly high), but the effect can be useful: staggered is devastating as it prevents enemies from full-attacking. This aura is certainly a helpful boon, which can not be said of all auras.

Retribution: If an enemy attacks senpai-spiritualist (whether the attack hit or missed), yandere-phantom’s attacks against that enemy deal +2d8 precision damage. A much-needed boost to damage, but Jealousy was never intended to be a damage-dealer type anyway. Anyway, Reapers who spend more time in melee than Masters appreciate this more.

Mine to Take: 1/day as an immediate action, just as the spiritualist is about to attempt a saving throw or be targeted by an attack roll, the phantom and the spiritualist teleport, swapping places. This is as ridiculously funny and awesome as it sounds. A hilarious and moderately useful capstone ability.

Conclusion: Despite its… less than optimal skills and bonus feat, this is a valid choice if you’re looking for a bodyguard phantom thanks to Jealous Combatant. Even so, Dedication might serve you better.

Zeal

Skills: Acrobatics and Survival. Pathetic skills that, somehow, may see use thanks to Tracking...

Saves: Fort and Reflex. No Will, orange you’ll be.

Tracking: The phantom gains a bonus on Survival checks made to follow tracks, equal to 1/2 its HD. Get out.

Ruthless Combatant: The phantom’s slams have critical range of 19-20 instead of 20. Nice boost to damage potential. At 11th level, the critical modifier becomes x3 instead of x2.

Determination Aura: Allies within 20 feet gain a +2 competence bonus on attack rolls and saving throws. I was getting worries this emotion had no salvation. Second best aura if you ask me, only surpassed by Dedication.

Steadfast Servant: Phantom can stay manifested even when the spiritualist is unconscious or asleep. Usually, if a spiritualist is knocked out there’s nothing the phantom can do about it. With this, your phantom always acts as some sort of insurance.

Zeal’s Resolve: 3/day as a free action, when the phantom misses an attack or fails a saving throw, it can reroll. Amazing capstone.

Conclusion: A pity that all the good stuff comes at late levels. Zeal has terrible, terrible statistics (skills, saves and bonus “feat”) but looking past that, it is a handy emotion at later levels.

Geist/Emotionless

(Only for geist channeler spiritualists)

Skills: Knowledge (arcana) and (religion). With 7 Int the phantom is not going to be a textbook, but oh well, there are worse skills out there… *cough*Survival*cough*

Saves: Reflex and Will. Good.

Iron Will: Awesome. Not as awesome as Dedication, because Dedication also gives Iron Will to the spiritualist whenever the phantom is within his mind. So Dedication wins this round.

Unnerving Touch: Touched target becomes shaken for 1d4+1 rounds, Will negates. Blah, this is a bad use of action economy, except maybe 1st level.

Poltergeist’s Knack: Telekinesis as a spell-like ability 3/day + 1 per 3 levels thereafter. Pretty decent stuff, telekinesis is a good spell.

Spectral Protection: The phantom can protect the spiritualist from incorporeal enemies by projecting an aura that repels them. Incorporeal enemies are pretty rare, and this can only be used while the phantom is within the spiritualist’s mind, so the usability is very limited, especially compared to the other aura abilities.

Malevolent Possession: Wow, the phantom can now possess other creatures. A nice late-game ability, limited by relatively low DC and being only 1/day.

Conclusion: Mediocre. See the archetype for details on how you’ll be able to take this (and why you shouldn’t).

Remorse Occult Origins

Skills: Perception and Sense Motive. Brilliant, even though the spiritualist may get a bigger Sense Motive bonus. Intimidate may have been more needed than Sense Motive because of Antagonistic, but Perception makes up for it.

Saves: Fort and Will. Excellent.

Antagonistic: Antagonize is a neat little feat that may be helpful if the phantom can tank some hits. In that case, Reapers and Master alike can enjoy this.

Long-Suffering Strike: Enemies hit by the phantom’s slam must make a Will save or any attacker gains a +1 untyped bonus on attack rolls against it. Short duration, allows a save, only a +1 bonus… not great.

Aura of Regret: Enemies within 10 feet take a -2 penalty on skill checks, caster level checks, combat maneuver checks and to CMD. Hm, in and of itself this is a nice aura, but when compared to the likes of Dedication’s or Despair’s it’s not certainly not the best. Situational, but that won’t change the fact that it’s useful.

Keening: 1/day as a standard action can affect enemies in a 30-foot cone with terrible remorse spell. At 12th level you’d expect something better than a 3rd/4th level spell 1/day.

Utter Misery: Long-Suffering Strike now also stuns. A lousy capstone if you ask me.

Conclusion: A mixed bag. Has some excellent perks, but also some real lackluster abilities. There are certainly better emotions out there, but overall this is not a bad choice at all.

Greed

Skills: Appraise and Sleight of Hand. With 7 Int don’t expect the phantom to be very good at Appraise, and it’s not a very thrilling skill anyway. Sleight of Hand is just not for phantoms. If you see a ghost walking past you you WILL know something was up.

Unless you’re thinking of being a literal Phantom Thief. Hm, now there’s a fun idea...

Saves: Fort and Reflex. No Will, orange you’ll be.

Eyes of Avarice: The phantom gains a bonus on Appraise check equal to 1/2 its HD (minimum 1). I’ll go get my red marker…. But wait, there’s more! While confined inside the spiritualist’s mind, the phantom grants this bonus to the spiritualist who’s using detect magic and Spellcraft to identify a magic item. OK, this is acceptable. As Wisdom/Charisma-based casters, spiritualists likely won’t have the best Spellcraft in the party, and identifying magic items is important. A small bonus never hurts. It’s still way too situational and minor benefit to call it any good (remember, this is the “bonus feat” ability of Greed phantoms!)

Ruthless Combatant: The same as Zeal’s: the phantom’s slams have critical range of 19-20 instead of 20. Nice boost to damage potential. At 11th level, the critical modifier becomes x3 instead of x2.

Assume Effect: When the spiritualist casts a spell on herself, the spell also affects the phantom. Only once per day (twice at 12th and thrice at 19th), so at most this’ll save you a spell slot. Buffers benefit from this more than debuffers, but then again even debuffers ought to have two buff spells by 7th level (namely, haste and fly). Again, this saves one spell slot per day and does very little else.

Covetous Aura: First of all I don’t like the rules wording of this ability. There’s a duration but no mention on how often it can be used. All other auras last until dismissed. In any case, this is a powerful aura. Or would be, if the phantom got it at 7th level, like every other aura. Basically the phantom can leech buffs cast by creatures within 20 feet. The bad thing is, these buffs must be 2nd-level spells or lower, and the original recipient still benefits from them. How many buffing spellcasters do you expect to face at 12th level that cast 2nd-level spells?

Stealing spell effects is the intended use. But if you think about it, this can also be used on allies. Basically no one ever needs to cast a buff spell (of 2nd-level or 1st-level) on your phantom any longer, because it automatically gains the benefit anyway. Sounds good? On the other hand, at 12th level your party full casters are already rocking 6th-level spells and just can’t care about low-level spells anyway. If only it wasn’t for the limit of spell levels, this would be useful.

Take It with You: If a phantom is brought to 0 hp or less, some buff spells are transferred onto the spiritualist for the remainder of their duration. Eh, a fun ability, but a mild capstone.

Conclusion: There’s absolutely nothing that stands out. The low-level stuff is almost completely copy-pasted from Zeal, and the high-level stuff is unamusing and underwhelming. Competes for the title of worst emotion, which is a shame.

Lust

For whatever reason this emotion is not PFS-legal. Just thought I’d say it right in the beginning.

Skills: Bluff and Diplomacy. Sigh. I know I’ve said this before in Jealousy’s entry, but there’s a good chance that people with any common sense won’t listen to a word that your phantom has to say. Yet, overlooking the non-mechanical limitations, these are two welcomed skills for phantoms as they have good Charisma scores. Diplomacy especially comes in handy for Lust phantoms because of Alluring Presence.

Saves: Fort and Will. Excellent.

Constitution Focus: The phantom has +2 Con and -2 Dex, and gains increased Constitution instead of Dexterity as it levels. Lust phantoms won’t be dealing damage but luckily their other abilities help them fill the role of a bodyguard.

Alluring Presence: Whenever an enemy is about to attack the spiritualist, by making a Diplomacy check you can force an enemy to target the phantom instead of the spiritualist. Kinda like Antagonize but with lower DC, Diplomacy instead of Intimidate and immediate action instead of standard action. Great if you want your phantom be a bodyguard. Make sure to invest in Diplomacy, and I do mean invest: Skill Focus (Diplomacy) levels of investment.

Mine Alone: The phantom and the spiritualist can roll their saves against charm and compulsion effects twice (using the phantom’s and the spiritualist’s modifiers) and take the better result. Situational, and pretty lame. This affects both members of the duo, so I rate this green.

Aura of Ecstasy: All creatures adjacent to the phantom are shaken and staggered. Will negates for 24 hours. Extremely short range and duration offset the extremely helpful condition. Unfortunately as the rules go, this aura also affects allies and even the spiritualist. Most of the time this won’t be damaging to the enemy but moderately annoying for the party. When it hits the enemy however, ooh boy is this powerful.

Sinful Command: 1/day dominate monster as a spell-like ability. Useful capstone ability.

Conclusion: A twist take on the bodyguarding phantom type. Most of the goodness can probably be outperformed by a Dedication phantom with Antagonize feat, but Lust is viable as well.

Pride

Skills: Intimidate and Perception. Both of these are very handy, and Perception especially would have gotten ranks every level anyway, so this frees some skill ranks for free use.

Saves: Reflex and Will. Good.

Resolve: Immune to fear effects which it loses for 1 hour if it fails a skill check or an attack roll. Fear is a moderately common effect, and a total immunity from 1st level is appreciated. Most of the time losing the immunity is not a big deal. Except when you are fighting a scary thing and really need it. So don’t worry about failing a skill check or an attack roll, unless you are about to fight a dragon or a banshee or something.

Flagrant Disregard: As a swift action, the phantom can take a -1 penalty to AC to gain a +2 bonus on attack rolls, scales with level. More accuracy is always welcome for phantoms, who have practically 3/4 BAB. This also reduces the likelihood that Resolve sucks. Pride phantoms still struggle to gain significant damage, unlike Anger and Hatred. So far they haven’t gotten anything that would determine their role.

Vainglorious Oration: As a swift action (bah, cannot be used on the same round as flagrant disregard!) can emit an aura. Enemies within 30 feet become shaken, provided they can hear the phantom and are not immune to mind-affecting or fear. No save: good! Lame effect: meh.

Overwhelming Confidence: As long as Resolve is active, the phantom doubles its morale bonuses. Cast heroism (2nd-level spell), boom, suddenly +4 bonus on attack rolls, saves and skill checks. I have to take a closer look at the spell list, because this could be blue if there are other, high-level spells that give morale bonuses.

Illusion of Perfection: Bonus on melee damage rolls equal to its Charisma modifier. Finally some much-needed boost to damage, at capstone level. And the target gains a Will save to halve the bonus damage. And it’s limited in minutes per day. At some point the devs needed to stop limiting the ability, but they just kept going. Compared to the stuff other phantoms get at this level, this is terribly weak.

Conclusion: Though Pride has nothing glaringly weak, it neither has anything that’d be worth selecting it for. Anything this can do some other emotion can do much better, especially on damage department and that’s apparently meant to be Pride’s main focus.

Feats

(If I’ve missed something awesome please, please let me know!)

A word on teamwork feats: I say you should avoid most of them. Why? Because it’s double the feats with only once the benefit. Take a look at Outflank for example. Outflank increases flank bonuses on attack rolls from +2 to +4. In other words, this is a +2 bonus on attack rolls when flanking. You are spending two feats to do this. With the same two feats you could have taken one feat for the spiritualist and another for the phantom. Let’s say Weapon Focus (scythe) and (slam) respectively. That would’ve given you two +1 on all attack rolls, instead of +2 on some attack rolls.

Another reason is because they are often problematic to arrange. Many teamwork feats have BAB prerequisites. Let’s take Outflank again as an example. The spiritualist can take outflank at 7th level at earliest (or technically 6th if you retrain an earlier feat). The phantom can take the feat at 6th or 9th level. If the phantom takes the feat at 6th level, the feat does nothing for a level. If the phantom takes the feat at 9th level, the spiritualist has a useless feat for two levels: 7th and 8th.

And then there’s the issue with teamwork feats in general: they are very situational. Many call for situations like “whenever you two are adjacent” or something like that. Do you want to take two feats that restrict your actions in combat to provide a minor benefit?

Bottom line is very rarely will a teamwork feat on both be better than individual feats.

For Reapers

Reapers should select feats that give them one of these two things: (a) more damage or (b) more survivability. As previously stated, Reapers are really putting their lives on the line, with no class features to back up melee combat, apart from having a phantom companion as a flank buddy and whatnot..

Combat Expertise: Yuck, a useless feat tax. I hope you know what you are doing if you need this for another feat’s prerequisite...

Combat Reflexes: Now, you might need to adjust your Dex a little to meet the requirements. If you do, this is a solid pick for any melee character. Would be more solid if you had a reach weapon. ...hope you like using the longspear?

Cornugon Smash: For Reapers with Despair or Fear phantoms and a good Intimidate, this really helps the phantom. Requires Power Attack and comes online at 7th level at earliest.

Dodge: If it’s AC you want, just take more armor proficiencies.

Fleet: If you don’t take armor proficiencies, extra movement might sound tempting. Unfortunately you are starved for feats, and this doesn’t really increase your damage output or survivability. Most of the time you’ll be full attacking anyway.

Furious Focus: Makes Power Attack miss less. Great boost.

Improved Critical: Scythes have 20/x4 critical, so it’s only natural you’d be willing to spend some resources on getting more crits. This feat does not stack with keen enchantment. So, ask yourself: would you rather spend a few thousand gold on keen, or a feat on this?

Improved Initiative: Going first is important, otherwise you might not get to go at all.

Improved Trip: Scythes have trip quality, so this is at least worth considering. Requires Int 13 and Combat Expertise, neither of which you want to invest in. Spiritualists are 3/4 BAB anyway, so good luck with that CMB. This feat won’t earn my recommendation.

Improved <other combat maneuver>: Is there a point? Is it worth it? Would other feats serve you better? (Correct answers: Maybe not, maybe not, yes.)

Iron Will: What, good Will saves and Wisdom-casting isn’t enough for you?

Medium Armor Proficiency: I recommend this feat for Reapers. Without this your best armor is chain shirt. With this, you can wield breastplates, effectively increasing your AC by 2. Shame about the movement and armor check penalties, though. When you can afford, invest in mithral breastplate. Has no armor check penalty, and does not limit movement. Around that time you start to learn fly, so getting rid of armor check penalty will be vital.

Heavy Armor Proficiency: I feel this might be an overkill. Sure, full plate is a big improvement over breastplate, but spending another feat for more AC feels redundant. After all, AC is not your only line of defense. It is a good line of defense in melee, though, so do as you will. If your campaign takes you to high levels (thus allowing you to pick feats more carefreely once all the truly good options are already taken) or you expect lots of enemies that do nothing but attack, treat this as green. If you care about flying, Mithral Full Plate has some armor check penalties which is definitely not optimal.

Power Attack: Did someone say two-handed combat? This is a given.

Cleave: Situational use (requires two enemies to be within your reach), and gives you a penalty to AC. I do not recommend. And don’t even mention the greater version.

Phantom Fighter OA: Basically this gives your phantom ghost touch properties, and allows you to use Ectoplasmic Spell metamagic feat when using deliver touch attacks. Feels redundant, when you could accomplish nearly the same effect by spending a full-round action to switch phantom from ectoplasmic to incorporeal. Still, full-round actions like that are pretty bad use of action economy, so you might appreciate having this feat when you and your party are struggling against incorporeals. You could also purchase ghost touch amulet of mighty fists +0 and give that to your phantom. Almost the same effect.

Skill Focus: No. Unless this is a prerequisite for something awesome.

Toughness: d8 Hit Die means you’ll always be more frail than true martials. This evens the odds.

Weapon Focus: 3/4 BAB is always a bit behind true martials. Without this you might struggle to hit enemies.

For Masters

Masters need those archery feats badly.

Deadly Aim: The Power Attack for an archer. But consider PBS for your 1st-level feat.

Cluttered Shots: An absolute necessity if you want to overcome DR. You qualify for this as late as at 9th level, unfortunately.

Point-Blank Shot: Not that thrilling on its own, but this is where most archers start. Because it’s required for...

  • Precise Shot: Basically required if you want to be an archer. Most of the time you’ll be shooting at things that are in melee combat (especially because your phantom will do just that! Except if it’s a bodyguard phantom.)
  • Rapid Shot: An extra attack. This is kind of like Power Attack for ranged builds: sacrifice accuracy for more damage.

Manyshot: A good damage boost.

Martial Weapon Proficiency (composite longbow): Poor unfortunate races that don’t have that elf blood will have to spend one feat on this. Or be stuck with a light crossbow.

Once you have all those feats, then you can start figuring out the rest:

Improved Initiative: As always.

Weapon Focus (longbow / light crossbow): With Deadly Aim and Rapid Shot, you’ll be sacrificing a lot of accuracy for damage. As you should! But this evens it out, greatly increasing your damage output.

For Casters

Spellcasting does not require many feats at all.

Metamagic feats: 6th-level casters in general tend to shy away from these feats. And, as a spontaneous caster, you’d have to spend a full-round action to cast a metamagic-ed spell.

Spell Focus: +1 DC is, for most part, not that thrilling. Debuffers can treat this as blue, others can safely ignore this feat altogether.

Spell Specialization: 13 Int? Caster levels? Who cares?

For Phantoms

Additional Traits: Hey, no one said phantoms couldn’t have traits. Some nice ones to pick are: Adopted (Helpful) if you have Bodyguard feat; something that makes important skills like Stealth, Perception or UMD class skills; Dirty Fighter if you have Outflank; etc.

Antagonize: A possible freebie (Remorse). If your phantom’s job is to stand guard, invest in Intimidate and get this feat.

Bludgeoner: Allows the phantom to deal nonlethal damage without penalties. Not all that thrilling on its own, but is practically a requirement for...

  • Enforcer: For Despair and Fear phantoms. We are talking about a real long duration of shaken if the phantom succeeds in Intimidate.

Combat Reflexes: Well, most phantoms definitely have the Dex to pull this off. Good for Hatred, I think.

  • Bodyguard: This feat’s name is exactly what some spiritualists want their phantoms to be. Without Additional Traits and Adopted (Helpful) the bonus is too small to make up for the situational use. If you do plan on making use of this feat and the aforementioned trait, then oh boy this can work pretty OK.

Dazzling Display: Pretty awesome for Despair and Fear. Requires Weapon Focus, but you’d get that at some point anyway. Unlike Enforcer, it affects multiple enemies, but also takes a full-round action instead of free action.

Improved Initiative: Going first wins the game.

Improved Natural Attack: Not legal for PFS (I think?). But if PFS doesn’t concern you, this is daaaamn great. Take as early as you can.

Iron Will: A possible freebie (Dedication). Some phantoms don’t have good Will save progression. Failing a Will save can be totally devastating for both the spiritualist and the phantom. Even phantoms that do have a good Will save progression might be tempted to take this.

Piranha Strike: Requires Weapon Finesse, but all phantoms sans Anger want to take that anyway. This is your Power Attack for Dex characters.

Power Attack: A possible freebie (Anger). The only Str-based phantom already has this.

Toughness: It sucks when the phantom is down. With this, it won’t go down as fast.

Weapon Finesse: A possible freebie (Hatred). All phantoms except Anger want this.

Weapon Focus (slam): +1 on practically all attack rolls. Sweet boost.

Spells (WIP)

When picking spells as a spontaneous caster, remember the mantra: “Will I find use for this every day? If not, it’s better as a scroll.”

Aside from Knacks which are relatively few in number, I won’t go over every spell in Spiritualist’s spell list, only showcasing the best spells to pick, and the trap options to avoid.

Recurring (no point mentioning these every spell level…)

Cure X Wounds: Grab a wand of CLW, that should take care of the healing for a dozen or so levels. So in general: no. Reapers may wish to take CMW or CSW, if only for the reason that sometimes casting it mid-battle may save their life in a pinch.

Inflict X Wounds: A notch above cure spells, at least for casting-focused spiritualists once the phantom becomes able to deliver touch attacks. Casting a touch attack from afar is neat, and it only takes the action of the spiritualist. The phantom who delivered the bad touch still gets to slam the poor enemy fella. Maybe Inflict Light Wounds is not worth investing a spell known, but Moderate or Serious may be worth to have for tough enemies with high AC.

Summon Monster I-VI: As always, this is a boost to action economy and provides even more flank buddies for the party. At some point the summoned creatures start having spells and SLAs, thus exponentially increasing the things you can use your spells for.

Knacks

Bleed: Most useless 0-level spell in the game, hands down. At no point will you ever cast this.

Daze: Useful maybe for 1st level casting-focused Masters. And even then you should probably just nock another arrow and deal damage from afar.

Detect Magic: Any surprise here?

Detect Psychic Significance: A newcomer from Occult Adventures. I don’t think it has much use outside of super occult-heavy campaigns. Take for flavor reasons?

Grave Words: This is becoming one of my favorite 0-level spells. A corpse starts blabbering nonsense, GM determines a 1-in-10 chance that the nonsense is actually meaningful. It’s silly, and definitely useless, but as a murderh- I mean, a Pathfinder you should expect to see a lot of corpses that make you ask “what was up with this guy?”. If you have an extra slot for a knack and don’t give a damn about it, pick this one for flavor and giggles.

Guidance: A free +1 competence bonus that applies on a lot of rolls. Remember to poke it every 1 minute.

Light: For races without darkvision, this is a free torch that has no weight. There are better sources of illumination, of course, but this has no cost nor weight. Worth considering, hm?

Mage Hand: It’s handy. ...Of course, your phantom itself acts as a servant...

Mending: An useful utility spell, but you can safely ignore this: very rarely will you think “darn, I wish I had mending now”.

Message: Cell phones haven’t been invented in Golarion yet, use this as the substitute. It’s more like a walkie-talkie than a cell, though, and the communication between you and your eidolon is always active anyway.

Open/close: Handy in a same way as mage hand. Open a door from distance and see if it was trapped or not (or locked).

Read Magic: Use scrolls? Take this.

Resistance: Like guidance, only that it gives a +1 resistance bonus on a save instead of any roll. Becomes useless once you buy your Cloak of Resistance, so if thinking ahead sounds smart, don’t take it at all.

Stabilize: Saves your from awkward situations like this: “Guys, we may have hurt that guy too badly… Hey spiritualist, you’re a spellcaster, right? Stabilize him before we lose a valuable witness!” “Uhh…” Also, you know, if a party member goes down this may save their life.

Telekinetic Projectile: Damage is too small to justify using this instead of a crossbow. And that’s just for Reapers: Masters would laugh at the mention of this knack.

Virtue: +1 temporary hit point for 1 minute. How very thrilling.

Top Picks: Detect magic, read magic, stabilize and maybe guidance are the ones everyone should pick. Races without Darkvision take light at some point, and the rest doesn’t matter too much.

1st

Burst of Adrenaline: A new spell from OA. Whenever you are about to make a roll based on Str, Dex or Con, cast this (it has casting time of immediate action) to gain a +8 enhancement bonus to the ability. Essentially this spell is a +4 bonus to Fort and Ref saves. And, with a range of personal, you can even cast this on your phantom, should you be within touch range. A downside is that you’ll be fatigued for 1 round afterwards, but you can manage that. Imagine: just picking this spell as a spell known means you can sacrifice a 1st level spell to get yourself a big bonus to a saving throw.

Burst of Insight: This spell is just like Adrenaline above, except it affects Int, Wis or Cha and leaves you dazed for 1 round afterward. So basically a +4 bonus to Will saves or any mental skill check. Just like Adrenaline, this is an amazing spell for situations when you really do not want to fail that one Will save.

Cause Fear: This is the standard 1st-level debuff that every debuffing-focused spiritualists will want to take. Made better if your phantom emotion benefits from scared enemies.

Expeditious Retreat: Now this is a fun little buff. Since it has range of personal, you can only cast it on yourself and the phantom.

Mage Armor: While this is useless for the spiritualist, phantoms can’t wear armor so this is a free +4 AC for them. Longer duration than shield. You may appreciate this more as a wand, however.

Magic Fang: I’d recommend taking this at low levels and then swap it out once the phantom gets magic attacks. And you never know when you might encounter DR/magic or a shadow or whatever. This side of the spell does not fit in the mantra, but hey, better safe than sorry.

Obscuring Mist: Pop this to protect you and your party from ranged attacks. No-save time out.

Protection From Evil/etc.: Stacks with mage armor and shield, and has some nice bonuses against evil creatures, which are very common. Other alignments, not so much.

Remove Fear: As a psychic spellcaster, you are vulnerable against fear. If you expect to fight something really scary, cast this on yourself: it gives a +4 morale bonus for 10 minutes. You might also want a few of these as potions, in case you are already afraid and can’t cast it.

Remove Sickness: As a scroll or potion, please.

Shield: A buff to defense that even heavy armor Reapers can enjoy. Buffing spiritualists want to take this at 1st level.

Top Picks: Buffing spiritualists take shield, magic fang, protection from evil, maybe mage armor. Debuffers take cause fear and some buff spells if they feel like it. I highly recommend every spiritualist to take burst of insight and/or burst of adrenaline for their last two spells known.

2nd

Aid: A small buff to attack rolls, saves vs fear and some temporary hp for 1 min per level. The buff to attack rolls and saves is so small you should take false life instead (it gives more temporary hp and lasts longer). Reapers may treat this as green, as nothing is stopping them from taking both. Masters would benefit more from false life.

Animate Dead (Lesser): Can’t make variant undead (eg. bloody skeletons), so whatever you animate with this will always be a weak combatant. But of course if necromancy is your thing you won’t listen to me anyway.

Death Knell: Literally reap the life out of a dying enemy to become stronger. Situational, and not a buff that is easy to get, but damn if it isn’t thematic for a freaking Reaper.

Disfiguring Touch: A bad touch spell for debuffing needs. Very long duration means this will last the entirety of battle, but the effects are moderate for a 2nd-level spell (-2 on attack rolls and saves, not overly severe). Consider ghoul touch for a more severe save-or-suck touch spell, or inflict pain for a more severe penalties.

False Life: Long duration and lots of temporary hit points.  Both Reapers and Masters appreciate this for different reasons: this keeps Reapers alive but wears off quickly in combat whereas Masters pop this and enjoy it for its duration in its entirety. This spell will stay relevant your entire adventuring career.

Ghoul Touch: An effective bad touch spell, if that’s your thing. Save-or-die, practically.

Inflict Pain: A new spell from OA, and a damn fine debuff spell if that’s your thing. Short duration (1 rd/level) but the real goodie is that the target takes a -4 on attack rolls and skill checks, while a successful save the duration is 1 round. So basically it always gives a severe penalty. Except against creatures that are immune to mind-affecting or pain, and it won’t do much against spellcaster enemies.

Invisibility: A no-brainer.

Levitate: Makes ranks in Climb totally useless. Useful, but as a scroll, please. Or just wait until you get 3rd level spells and can fly.

Psychic Leech: A new spell from Occult Origins. OK, this is both a small debuff and a small buff. The thing is, it’s a buff only if the target fails its save. You want better 2nd-level spells.

Remove Paralysis: Scroll or potion.

Resist Energy: Extremely helpful, but it may not see use every day. Buffers, select with consideration. Others, take it as a scroll.

Restoration (Lesser): Even if casting it would help mid-combat, the casting time is too long. Scroll or potion.

See Invisibility: Scroll. Most spiritualists get this as a spell-like ability at 9th-level, although only 1/day and way shorter duration.

Spectral Hand: Debuffers only. For them, this is very helpful.

Touch of Idiocy: A no-save touch spell that gives 1d6 penalty to all mental scores for 10 min/level. I repeat: no saving throw. Even non-debuffer spiritualists may want this to really give a middle finger to enemy spellcasters.

Top Picks: Invisibility is superb, and that’s about it for must-know buff spells. False life and/or aid for buffers. Debuffers enjoy a large assortment of good debuff spells at this level: ghoul touch, inflict pain, and touch of idiocy work a long way, as does spectral hand.

3rd

Animate Dead: Costly, but when animating the right critter you could get a massive increase in power. Even then, a scroll might be better. So don’t worry, if undead aren’t your thing you can safely ignore this; and if they are, you can pick this spell and see if you can reanimate something powerful.

Bestow Curse: A really good debuff spell.

Blindness/deafness: Hey! Wizards, witches, bards and bloodragers get this as a 2nd-level spell! What gives? Bah, take other debuffs.

Dispel Magic: A staple choice. Whether it is counterspelling (which other, full casting classes may do a bit better) or ending magical effects on enemies this is something that spellcasters just do.

Fly: All my yes. Take it take it take it.

Gaseous Form: Bah, a situational buff. Reapers: avoid. Masters: consider.

Haste: Shut up and take it. Are you taking it yet?

Heroism: +2 morale bonus (won’t stake with spells like aid) on attack rolls, saves and skill checks. Pretty long duration as well. Sweet deal.

Invisibility Purge: Uhm, scroll? Maybe? Situational enough?

Magic Fang (Greater): Ah, a solid buff on the phantom’s damage that lasts long.

Protection From Energy: Scroll? The main reason to take this as a spell known is the duration, and that’s already 10 minutes: plenty for a battle against a dragon or whatever.

Remove Blindness/deafness / Curse / Disease: OK, these may be expensive to take as potions and ineffective as scrolls. Choose wisely: your gp or your spells known?

Ray of Exhaustion: Ooh, another save-or-suck. In a ray form, hm, that could be problematic.

Sealed Life: A newcomer from OA. I just wanted to point out how weak this spell is. It stops the target from sharing life force or transferring damage (like a life oracle’s Life Link, or shield other spell). For 1 round per level. Ha!

Slow: And another debuff spell.

Spirit-Bound Blade: A newcomer from OA. You give a weapon ghost touch and 1 other property depending on which emotion focus you choose (does not have to be the same as your phantom). All the choices of weapon property are useful, especially Zeal (keen) and Despair (cruel, for fear builds). On the other hand, at this level anyone who wants one already has a +1 keen weapon, so all this spell does is add ghost touch and some minor benefit. Seems underwhelming for a 3rd-level spell.

Tongues: Scroll of comprehend languages doesn’t do the trick for you?

Top Picks: Haste and fly, obviously. Dispel magic for everyone at some point. For debuffers there’s slow and bestow curse. Greater magic fang and heroism are good buffs.

4th

Black Tentacles: Wizards get this at 7th level whereas spiritualists have to wait at 10th, but that’s to be expected as for a 6th-level caster. In any case, this is a brutal spell to cast on enemy spellcasters, and because it has no saving throw even non-debuff focused spiritualists can cast it with ease.

Archetypes

Ectoplasmatist

This archetype does not even feel like a spiritualist: it replaces phantom and everything related to the phantom. In fact, this archetype replaces every aspect of spiritualist sans the spell-like abilities. Instead, this archetype grants “ectoplasmic lashes” that are basically just free weapons that eventually gain increased reach.

This is going to be a full rainbow of class feature analysis...

Ectoplasmic Lash (Su): At 1st level, as a full-round action you manifest one or two lashes as weapons. You get to choose between two “machetes”, a single “longsword” or a single “earthbreaker” (with x2 crit modifier instead of x3). Of these, the “longsword” is probably the best (well, after you get Spiritual Combat, at least. Before that, use the earthbreaker).

These weapons gain scaling bonuses with your level, which isn’t anything special (saves you a few grand gp, but so does taking crafting feats). The real treat (and I suspect the only real reason to ever, EVER pick this archetype) is that the lashes gain +5 feet of reach at 4th and 8th level while still being able to attack anywhere where they reach, including adjacent enemies. This comes at a cost, however, as using this increased reach provokes attacks of opportunity (they are treated as ranged attacks for that purpose) (At 8th level they no longer do).

Honestly speaking, I can’t rate this feature fairly. This is the thing that replaces your phantom, and thus the primary reason to play a spiritualist. I see some merit in this class feature, but in the end I’d rather just have a phantom companion and wield actual weapons. Wielding actual weapons does not require sacrificing primary class features.

So on its own merits this is a very bad trade if you ask me, but because of Spiritual Combat (below) the ectoplasmatist is a playable archetype.

Spiritual Combat (Su): Long story short, this is the spell combat and spellstrike of a magus as a full-round action when using ectoplasmic lashes. There are some penalties, but in the end this ability is what makes this archetype playable. Comes at 3rd level, so 1st-2nd levels are going to suck for the ectoplasmatist.

So far you’ve lost the phantom and gained the ability to spellstrike as a magus. Seems fair? The only problem is your spell list. You’ll still have the spiritualist spell list, which is based around buffing and debuffing. You will not have the magus spell list which is made to fit maguses (magi?) and their needs: namely offensive, blasty touch spells. Granted, the spiritualist spell list does include some fun bad touch save-or-suck spells which are fun to spellstrike with, but spells like shocking grasp are still missing. And of course you won’t have magus’s arcane pool. Ech.

Ectoplasmic Armor (Su): At 4th level, ectoplasmic lashes provide a +4 armor bonus. Scales a bit at later levels. Well that certainly saves you the Medium Armor Proficiency feat… at 12th level. Before that you can wear chain shirts (+4 AC), so… what is this supposed to be providing at 4th level? Just armor without armor check penalty? Bravo?

Conclusion: This archetype lets you quit being a spiritualist and start being a magus without magus spell list or arcane pool. To be fair, you do get a cool ectoplasm sword and the ability to give touch spells from afar… Hey, where have I heard that before…?

Fractured Mind

I won’t rate this. Long story short, you WILL take this archetype if your race provides a Charisma bonus or a Wisdom penalty, and/or if you want a fear-based phantom and want to make an Intimidate build.

Caveat 1: You may find Wisdom to be a more rewarding casting ability score than Charisma, due to the simple fact that having higher Will saves and Perception is just more practical compared to having higher Diplomacy and Intimidate.

Caveat 2: If your race provides no bonuses or penalties to neither Wisdom or Charisma (such as elves, humans, half-orcs etc), this archetype is still be worth considering, depending on your planned phantom emotion. The standard spell-like abilities that spiritualist gets are not very thrilling, and this archetype gives ones that range from terrible to terrific: I advise referring to Phantom of the OP-era about these emotion-based spell-like-abilities.

Geist Channeler

So, you noticed the Emotionless/Geist emotion and got curious whether it’s worth considering. Oh boy, do I have bad news for you...

Geistform Phantom (Su): No beating around the bush, this sucks. Phantom can’t manifest in ectoplasmic form = good luck doing anything to non-incorporeal enemies. The tradeoff is that the phantom has starting Wisdom of 12 instead of 10. Thanks for the boost to Will saves, but I think the phantom might appreciate more being able to do physical stuff. At 5th level the phantom can use a swift action to become a hybrid of incorporeal and ectoplasmic, so that it can deal half damage to corporeal enemies. Well sure, x0.5 is better than x0, but this is still a horrible deal.

Emotionless (Su): The phantom must have Emotionless (aka Geist) emotion. See Emotions (above) for details. This means that, even if you found the Geistform Phantom an acceptable tradeoff (?!?!) you’d be stuck with a mediocre (or below mediocre) emotion. Or vice versa: if you thought the Emotionless was a pretty cool emotion you’d have to deal with Geistform Phantom.

Spiritual Manifestation (Su): And naturally you can only use bonded manifestation in incorporeal form. So much for that class feature granting options.

Conclusion: This archetype hinders the spiritualist.

Haunted

A well-designed archetype, I must say, in the sense that it feels balanced. You’ll trade some important options in battle and gain different kinds of important options in battle.

Only for spiritualists who have Anger phantom.

Usurp Manifestation (Su): This is sort of like a reversed Bonded Manifestation. Instead of Bonded Manifestation which basically buffs the spiritualist in exchange for having the phantom un-manifested, Usurp Manifestation allows the phantom to buff itself at the cost of the spiritualist becoming momentarily weaker. In practise, the phantom (1) grows larger as if affected by enlarge person and (2) increases its natural armor bonus by +2. While the phantom is usurping, the spiritualist becomes nauseated, effectively making him unable to fight. Because enlarge person increases Str and lowers Dex, this is only a good effect for Anger phantoms.

How useful or useless this ability is to you depends greatly on how you plan to use your phantom. If your duo puts more focus on the spiritualist, this is a bad trade since you lose a good way to buff the spiritualist while gaining an opportunity to weaken him. If so, skip the rest, you won’t like this archetype. On the other hand, if your duo is more balanced on the idea of teamwork, this might be worth considering. For example, say that a Reaper has taken heavy damage and wants to retreat to safety. Her phantom can usurp to gain a power boost while the spiritualist’s allies patch her. Or a Master that is being mind-controlled into attacking her own allies: the phantom could instantly put a stop on that.

The rest of the rating of this archetype assumes the latter.

Usurp Spell: At 8th level, as a swift action, the usurping phantom can “leech” spell slots from the haunted, casting a buff spell on itself. This is sweet, and provides a much-needed boost on action economy when usurping. Shame that it comes online as late as 8th level.

Teleplasmic Surge: At 13th level the usurping phantom gains a +2 morale bonus on attack rolls, saving throws and skill checks. Oh baby!

Ferocious Phantom: At 18th level the usurping phantom gains DR 10/slashing and some spell resistance. At this point the spiritualist might as well step back, start feeling nauseated and let the phantom wreck everything. I’m kidding, at levels as high as 18th spiritualists are throwing spells and attacks so good they probably won’t use Usurp Manifestation unless absolutely necessary.

Usurped Bond (Su): Replaces dual bond (which gives you more rounds of Bonded Manifestation), this gives you more rounds of Usurp Manifestation instead. This is basically obvious.

Conclusion: Choose wisely, tread carefully. Being Haunted always takes away a viable option in battle. Whether the option it gives back is less, more, or equally viable depends on the way you plan to play the duo. Oh, and this archetype is basically only for Anger emotion phantoms: other emotions do not benefit from this at all.

Onmyoji

A pretty neat archetype for spellcasting-focused spiritualists. You lose some kinda important things and gain more spells known from one of the best spell lists in the game.

Divine Spellcasting: Casts divine spells instead of psychic spells. Psychic spellcasting has its own perks and flaws (being grappled or silenced is not a problem, but being under emotion effect is), but so does divine spellcasting (other way around). This trade is probably neither a hindrance nor a boon on its own.

Spiritual Resistance (Su): A small bonus on saves against spell-like and supernatural abilities of fey, outsiders and incorporeal undead. This replaces shared consciousness (the two Skill Focus feats and +4 vs mind-affecting) and fused consciousness (always bonded senses and shared consciousness). Outsiders are usually common, but fey and ghosts can be much more rare. In any case, you lose some kinda important stuff and don’t get anywhere near as useful.

Divine Teachings (Su): In exchange for spiritual interference (+2 shield bonus) you get the ability to cast some cleric spells while the phantom is within the spiritualist’s consciousness. Okay, the latter part sucks: phantoms can do only do work when manifested and thanks to Spiritual Resistance they don’t even grant Skill Focuses or the +4 bonus against mind-affecting effects.

But adding cleric spells to your spells known can be a huge thing, especially at higher levels where the extra spells can really do some work. Because the phantoms has to stay at home base while casting these, you want to choose cleric spells that are effective out of combat. By doing so you can cast them out of combat and then manifest the phantom for battle.

Some suggested spells to take:

  • 4th level (2nd-level spell): align weapon or silence
  • 7th level (3rd-level spell): prayer or resist energy (communal) or stone shape
  • 10th level (4th-level spell): blessing of fervor
  • 13th level (5th-level spell): wall of stone
  • 16th level (6th-level spell): animate object or antilife shell or create undead or planar adaptation (mass) or word of recall
  • 19th level (6th-level spell): (see above)

(*Also keep in mind that nothing is preventing you from taking spells that appear both on the cleric and spiritualist list, like summon monster or heal.)

Conclusion: Diminishes the phantom’s role and benefits and strengthens the spellcasting aspect. If that’s what you want, go ahead.

Fated Guide

Are you the religious sort? Willing to worship one of select few deities and deal with Remorse phantom? If so, fated guide might be for you.

Shepherd to the Dead: Adds two very minor spells to the spell list. Not a deal-maker.

Phantom: Must have remorse as emotion focus. See Emotions. Being locked into a single emotion is no fun, but Remorse isn’t too terrible.

Divine Purpose: The remorse phantom has Deific Obedience as a bonus feat instead of Antagonize (the spiritualist has to do the obedience and the phantom gets the benefit, if I understand correctly?).

First and foremost, check out Walter’s Guide to Deific Obedience. Deific Obedience has a lot of room to be powerful, especially at higher levels. Basically it starts with a minor boon that starts to give a lot of great boons at 12th, 16th and 20th level. Yep, pretty high level stuff. Unless your campaign goes that high levels, I recommend picking a deity that gives a good boon right off the bat. Some suggestions from me for top picks:

  • Desna: +1 luck bonus on initiative and +4 sacred bonus on Perception checks made to determine whether you are aware of opponents just before a surprise round. (A good boost to the phantom, for sure, but think twice about taking this because of Thanatopic Bonded Manifestation, read below)
  • Iomedae: +4 sacred bonus on Diplomacy and Knowledge (nobility).
  • Irori: +4 sacred/profane bonus on ALL Knowledge skills.
  • Rovagug: +4 untyped bonus on attack and damage rolls against objects
  • Sarenrae: +2 sacred bonus on Perception
  • Zon-Kuthon: +2 sacred (?!?!) bonus on saves against spells that deal hit point damage.

Not bad for a feat at 1st level.

Thanatopic Bonded Manifestation: You can use bonded manifestation to get the benefits of the phantom’s Deific Obedience and can speak with dead, as per spell some rounds per day. Hell yeah! Hope you chose a good deity, cause this could mean you get +4 on all Knowledge skills or something as awesome as that.

At 8th level, the spiritualist can act normally even after being reduced to 0 hp or less (with some penalties) and at 13th level can cast antilife shell (6th-level spell!) as a spell-like ability (although its duration drains bonded manifestation rounds). And some anti-undead stuff at 18th level.

This replaces either ectoplasmic bonded manifestation or incorporeal bonded manifestation: choose to have it replace incorporeal version. Sounds like a pretty good deal to me, if only for the Deific Obedience part.

Iron Bond: Instead of see invisibility spell-like ability, the phantom gains a +4 bonus on saves vs banishment and dismissal. See invisibility was pretty lame, and this is very helpful when needed and pretty situational otherwise. An OK trade.

Undeniable Bond: At 16th level the bonus increases to +8. OK, this is a bit redundant, and replaces a pretty neat spell-like ability.

Conclusion: Being locked into Remorse phantom sucks a little, but if you are willing to deal with that and worshipping a certain deity you can snatch some cheesy powerful benefits from this, both for the spiritualist and the phantom.

Combinable Archetypes

Save for Fractured Mind which just swaps out casting ability and the spell-like abilities, spiritualist archetypes match quite poorly with one another, both because of overlapping modifications and for the fact that none of the archetypes are super excellent must-gets and most spiritualist builds only ever need one or none archetypes. Still, if anyone was wondering which archetypes can be used together, here you go.

Ectoplasmatist stacks with Fractured Mind.

Fractured Mind stacks with Ectoplasmatist, Geist Channeler and Haunted.

Geist Channeler stacks with Fractured Mind and Onmyoji.

Haunted stacks with Fractured Mind and Onmyoji.

Onmyoji stacks with Geist Channeler, Haunted and Fated Guide.

Fated Guide stacks with Onmyoji.

Occult Skill Unlocks

Occult Adventures introduced Occult Skill Unlocks: new uses for existing skills that only characters who can cast psychic spells (and anyone with the Psychic Sensitivity feat; what a waste of a feat!) can use. Spiritualists, of course, fall to this group. There is not much to “optimise” about these skill unlocks, but they are worth mentioning, I suppose.

Fractured Minds, being Charisma-based casters, are good at Hypnotism (Diplomacy). Other spiritualists are good at Faith Healing (Heal), Prognostication (Sense Motive) and Read Aura (Perception).
