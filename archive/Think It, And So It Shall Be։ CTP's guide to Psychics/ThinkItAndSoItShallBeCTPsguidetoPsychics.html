Think It, And So It Shall Be

CockroachTeaParty’s guide to the Pathfinder Psychic

Introduction

Of all the new classes introduced in Occult Adventures, the Psychic is perhaps the simplest to understand.  It is essentially an Int-based sorcerer with a slightly different spell list.  If you know how to make a solid sorcerer, you can make similar choices with a psychic and emerge a perfectly functional Tier 2 caster.

However, there are enough differences and nuances to psychic magic that a more in-depth look will certainly be beneficial.

A Psychic’s Role

“Know Thyself”

~ancient aphorism

A Psychic can fill in for a primary arcane caster; it’s closest cousins are the wizard, sorcerer, and arcanist.  That said, there are certain roles its class features and spell list make it gravitate towards.

Information Retrieval:  Much like the wizard, a psychic is a huge nerd.  With Int-based casting, all Knowledge skills, and access to several unique divinations and occult skill unlocks, the psychic is very strong at ferreting out clues, gathering intel, and uncovering secrets.

Party Face: Perhaps a bit surprisingly, a psychic can excel in social situations.  With access to all major social skills, their divination and enchantment spells can help influence, interrogate, and beguile, particularly if your discipline favors Charisma.  Best of all, psychic magic is subtle.  No wagging of fingers and speaking gibberish; your focused stare can turn a foe into a friend, or reveal just what the duchess is thinking…

Utility Caster: With limited spells known, you won’t have the versatility of the wizard or arcanist, but you get several key problem-solving spells on your list.  Teleport, fly, time stop, telekinesis; the list goes on.  Even with your limited spell selection, these are usually good spells to know regardless, and always help grease the gears.

Battlefield Control:  A psychic can certainly throw down when it comes to battlefield control.  They lack most of the fog spells, but make up for it with summons, some good wall spells, and things like black tentacles and etheric shards.

Buffer: A Psychic has respectable access to several useful defensive and offensive buffs.  Everybody loves Haste!  Psychics even sport a selection of spells capable of removing or alleviating annoying negative statuses.

Debuffer: The Psychic’s spell list seems to specialize in spells that cripple an opponent’s defenses, physically and mentally.  Typically targeting one or a small handful of targets, many of these are also mind-affecting, but the psychic has ways of working around these limitations.  A psychic’s best combat role is often locking one or two foes down with save-or-lose or save-or-suck effects.

Blaster:  Arcane casters definitely have the leg-up when it comes to blasting.  Most direct-damage spells on the psychic list target only a single foe, and they lack access to most elements other than force and sonic.  Even if you want to focus on damage, the psychic is lacking in big, flashy crowd-control spells.

Class Features & Chassis

“Think of the brain like a muscle.  Make it strong.”

~Melchia, psychic

d6 HD, +½ BAB, good Will save:  Your standard ‘squishy caster’ chassis.  You can’t take many hits, and you have no business getting in the middle of a fight.  Your mind is strong, but you’ll need to be wary of physical threats.

2 Skill points/level: You’re an INT-based caster, so you’ll have no shortage of skill ranks to throw around.  Your list of class skills is no slouch:

Bluff, Diplomacy, Intimidate, Sense Motive: You get all of the social skills.  These are particularly useful if your discipline favors CHA as a secondary score.

All Knowledges, Linguistics, Spellcraft:  You’ve got the INT to know things.  As a primary caster, it’s your duty to max Spellcraft.

Perception:  You get the god skill!  Max it and never look back.

Craft, Fly, Profession: They’re here if you need them.

Simple Weapon Proficiency, no armor:  Pretty standard.  Interestingly, it still is in your best interest to wear armor; armor with no check penalty does nothing to hinder you.

Psychic Spellcasting, 1-9:  The reason you came to this party.  You are the undisputed master of psychic spells, and gain sole access to the upper echelon of esoteric power.  Int-based spontaneous casting is pretty sweet.  You have the same slots per day and spells known as a sorcerer, so you’ve got great endurance.  I’ll talk about the quirks of psychic spells later on.

Psychic Discipline: Similar to a sorcerer’s bloodline or a wizard’s favored school, the various disciplines give you access to unique abilities and bonus spells.  These run the gamut from pretty sweet to terrible.  I’ll break them down below.

Phrenic Amplifications & Phrenic Pool:  You get a pool of points you can use to tweak your spells in various ways.  Most of them aren’t earth-shattering, but certainly nice to have.  Starting at 11th level, you get access to Major Amplifications, which are even more sweet.  We’ll be looking at all our options below.

Detect Thoughts, Telepathic Bond, Telepathy:  These essentially amount to bonus spells known.  Handy!

Remade Self:  You’ve made it to your capstone, congratulations!  It’s pretty cool: a constant buff, and a free 1 point phrenic amp for all of your spells.  Of the choices available, I’d probably pick arcane sight, fly, or haste.  Heroism and nondetection already have great duration, and you probably won’t need tongues on all the time.

Ability Score Considerations

“You call yourself a warrior?  You are puny and small!”

~Last words of Rark Scar-eye, orc warlord

STR:  You have no business being in melee combat; you just don’t have the HP or BAB to swing it.  This is your safest dump stat.  Mind your carrying capacity at low levels; if you’ve got a handy haversack or someone willing to carry your stuff, you can dump this into the dirt.  A 7-10 here isn’t hurting anything.

DEX:  It never hurts to have a decent DEX, especially for Initiative, but many of your offensive spells don’t require attack rolls, so your reliance on DEX is pretty mild.  A 12-14 or so is fine.

CON:  Everybody wants a decent CON for survivability, and to shore up your weak Fort. saves.  Hopefully you won’t be getting into combat too often to justify anything higher than a 12-14.

INT:  This is your one-in-all and all-in-one.  Embrace the SADness: your spell DCs, bonus spells, skill ranks, and many key skills are all based off of your INT.  Pump it as high as you can without sacrificing too much in your other scores, and always prioritize increasing it as you gain levels and wealth.  Aim for at least an 18 here at level 1.  

WIS:  Most of the best disciplines favor WIS as a secondary score, usually for Phrenic Pool recovery purposes.  A good WIS is useful regardless for Perception and Will saves.  You can get away with a 10 here, but a WIS-discipline psychic should shoot for a 12-14, and invest in improving it later in your career.

CHA:  If your discipline favors CHA, or you just want to be a decent party face, a 12-14 in CHA will serve you well.  If you don’t care about being a social butterfly, this is another pretty safe dump stat.

Races Gifted in the Inner Mysteries

With our ability score requirements in mind, certain races possess minds and dispositions better suited to unlocking psychic power.  In general, you want to avoid races with an INT penalty (which are pretty rare), while races with an INT bonus make natural choices.

Core Races:

Dwarf:  The only thing going against dwarves is their lack of an INT boost.  Otherwise they make great psychics, particularly those with a WIS-linked discipline.  A solid defensive choice, especially for games with heavy dungeon crawling.

Elf:  Elves make excellent psychics.  Their Elven Magic trait is always useful for a primary caster, and their racial favored class bonus allows them more use of their phrenic amplifications.  The CON hit is the only downside.

Gnome:  Much like dwarves, gnomes have great defenses but little else to truly synergize with the psychic’s abilities.  

Half-Elf:  The half-elf racial favored class bonus is okay if you like phrenic amps.  However, the human favored class bonus is amazing, and puts the Half-Elf in the top tier.

Halfling:  Again, no INT bonus makes the Halfling less attractive than other choices.  Solid defenses, but you’re better served elsewhere.

Half-Orc: Just like the half-elf, the half-orc’s access to the human favored class bonus makes them incredible.  Half-orcs make great scary psychics with their Intimidate bonus.

Human:  The bonus feat and skills alone make humans excellent psychics, but it’s their favored class bonus that puts them at the top of the pile.  Expanding your spells known is always worth it, and pushes the psychic to the upper limits of Tier 2.

Other Races:

Android:  On the surface, the android makes an amazing psychic, with an INT bonus and everything.  The problem is their emotionless quality prevents them from using psychic spells with emotion components (read: most of them).  There’s a feat that removes this ability, but you’re left with an otherwise decent racial chassis minus a more useful feat.  You could heavily rely on the Logical Spell metamagic feat, but again you’re limiting yourself a great deal.  Immunity to fear and emotion magic is a potent defense, but if you already have to axe emotion components anyway you haven’t gained much of anything.

Ratfolk:  Our first example of a small-sized race with an INT bonus, ratfolk make solid psychics.  Thematically, they have good synergy with the psychedelia discipline.

Sylph:  You’ll want to swap out Air Affinity for something more useful; otherwise, you’ve got an INT bonus and some other solid racial abilities.

Tiefling:  Hey, an INT bonus!  And some useful defenses.  Swap out Fiendish Sorcerery and you’re good to go.

Samsaran:  INT bonus aside, the real draw here is the Mystic Past Life alternate trait.  While Psychics have access to most psychic spells, a few other psychic classes have things you can cherry pick, like Cure spells from the Spiritualist, or Fireball from the Occultist.  Things like Animate Dead are particularly game-changing; lots of possibilities here.

Wayang:  These little guys make pretty good sneaky, creepy psychics.

Lashunta:  If you want to play alien women from Venus (er, Castrovel), they make perfectly serviceable psychics with built-in telepathy from the get-go.

Wyrwood:  No sane GM should let you play one of these.  Their size, ability scores, and construct immunities make them perfect psychics, but you are nevertheless extremely fragile with no CON score.  Barring high-powered games, they’re basically broken.

Phrenic Amplifications

Phrenic Amplifications (or phrenic amps) are similar to metamagic feats in that they alter or add rider effects to your spells.  You can only ‘attach’ one phrenic amp to a spell at a time (until level 20).  Each phrenic amp has a cost you must pay from your phrenic pool, so your uses will be limited.  Several psychic disciplines include a mechanic to replenish some of your phrenic pool, allowing you to further utilize them.

Basic Phrenic Amplifications

You get three of these before access to major amps, barring feats and racial favored class bonuses.  This might not seem like a lot, but there aren’t that many must-have amps.  Spend feats on the niche ones if you want, otherwise just grab the best ones.

Biokinetic Healing (OO):  While a form of self-healing is nice, this isn’t really worth the cost in points.  Several disciplines come with a form of healing if you’re interested, but I wouldn’t suggest spending a phrenic amp on it.

Complex Countermeasure:  In a caster-heavy game, I might consider this for a late pick before major amps come online.  While having your spells dispelled is a pain, you’ll probably want to reserve your pool points for offensive amps.  This is handy in a mage-duel situation, but a more traditionally varied game will prevent this from seeing much use.

Conjured Armor:  Given the disposable nature of summons, using limited resources to boost their AC isn’t really worth it.

Defensive Prognostication:  Most divination spells aren’t really for in-combat use.  If you like the Mind Thrust line, though, this could be a handy way to increase your defenses if you’re caught unprepared.

Dragon’s Breath Focus (OO):  Even if this only cost 1 point, it still wouldn’t really be worth it.  Typically a burst is superior to a 30 ft. cone in most situations regardless.

Focused Force:  There aren’t a whole lot of damaging force spells on your spell list, and this amp isn’t terribly stellar regardless.  A 5d4+5 magic missile deals an average of 17.5 damage.  This amp would boost that to 22.5; most spells are going to see an increase of 1 or 2 damage per die.  Damage isn’t your strong suit; I’d look elsewhere.

Intense Focus:  This basically saves you a feat on Combat Casting.  It’s certainly handy in clutch situations.

Mindshield:  This a decent amp for party buffers, but it quickly gets expensive for popular spells like haste.  If you’ve got a bard in your party, you can easily skip this.

Ongoing Defense:  The various ‘psychic defense’ spells are an odd lot, better if you’re routinely caught with your pants down.  If you’re prepping correctly and making informed decisions, you probably won’t need them, and thus can skip this amp.  If you find yourself using one a lot, this isn’t a bad amp, but it’s probably a late pick before major amps are available in that case.

Overpowering Mind:  It’s expensive, and it only effects mind-affecting spells, but this is probably the best offensive amp in this tier.  This is likely to hog 90% of your amp pool for the first half of your career.  Pick it up 1st or 3rd level.

Phrenic Strike (OO):  Only of interest to monk/psychic multiclass builds, this gives you something similar to a magus’ spellstrike.  If you’re not multiclassing or doing some sort of gestalt build, most psychics should avoid it.

Psychofeedback (OO):  First off, this ability is too expensive in terms of phrenic points.  You have to burn a 4th level spell to match the power of a 2nd level spell with a fraction of the duration…  It’s a bogus deal.

Relentless Casting:  Eventually you’ll start running into SR, and when you do, you’ll be glad you picked this amp up.  A very solid 7th level choice.

Telempathic Restoration (OO):  Burn a point and a spell slot / SLA use to remove certain conditions from your allies… and apply them to yourself.  Note that most of them disrupt emotion components.  Pass!

Transfer Fear (OO):  This is a better deal than Telempathic Restoration, certainly, but you’ll still need a way to cast a fear spell without emotion components to get it to work on yourself.  Doable, but hopefully a rare enough circumstance that you won’t need this.

Undercast Surge:  If you really like the undercast spells, this is very comparable to Overpowering Mind, and it will help stretch out your higher level spell slots.  It’s nice that it’s both offensive and defensive depending on your spell selection.

Will of the Dead:  Nothing shuts down a caster with lots of mind-affecting spells like the all-too-common undead.  This amp is thus rock-solid, a great 1st or 3rd level choice.  In undead-heavy campaigns such as Carrion Crown, this sucker jumps up to blue.

Major Phrenic Amplifications

You only get three of these, so choose wisely; Extra Amplification can’t get you more.

Deflection Field (OO):  This is an interesting little ability.  There aren’t a whole lot of psychic spells that grant a deflection bonus; as best I could tell, your only choices are Shield Other and the Stunning Barrier line.  The wording is a bit vague; it seems to me that you only burn points if you want to, and it doesn’t even take an action.  It also fails to differentiate between magical and non-magical attacks, so you could use it to send ray spells back at their caster.  As long as you’ve got phrenic points to burn, and boast a good AC or a solid miss chance, this can serve as a sort of all-day spell turning as long as you’ve got Shield Other up and running, which isn’t too bad a spell on its own.

Dispelling Pulse:  For all the limitations and restrictions, this amp is a bit too expensive for what it does.  Still, 3 points is cheaper for what amounts to an almost-free Quickened Dispel Magic compared to similar amps, as you’ll read below.

Dual Amplification:  This is a good 15th or 19th level choice; it eats through your points pretty quickly, but it has wonderful combo potential, primarily through Synaptic Shock abuse.  At 20th level, you can sling 3 amps in a round!  In an undead-heavy campaign, this jumps up a color, allowing rider effects with Will of the Dead.

Mimic Metamagic:  It’s got ‘metamagic’ in the name, so you know it’s gonna be good.  Pick two metamagic feats (even ones you don’t know), and you gain the ability to use them on the fly for double their level increase in phrenic pool points.  Why isn’t this blue?  Well, over-reliance on this will drain your pool points fast.  It’s best used on +1 or +2 level feats, but the best ones you should probably either actually know or have ready in rod form.  Thus, this is great for picking up niche feats you only need on occasion, or picking hard-hitting metamagic and tanking the cost for nova situations.  Good picks include Persistent Spell, Logical Spell, Enlarge Spell, or Bouncing Spell.  Remember: you’re trading pool points for higher level spell slots and better action economy, which is a pretty good deal.

Space-rending Spell:  This is a fun little ability that’s a nice panic button, combining offense with defense.  It’s too costly for utility; I wouldn’t spend more than 3 points unless the chips are down.

Subordinate Spell:  If you’re interested in Quickening 1st-2nd level spells without sacrificing higher level spell slots, this amp is cheaper than Mimic Metamagic with Quicken Spell.  Otherwise, Mimic Metamagic is the better deal; I prefer Mimic Metamagic for double the choices, but this is great for getting up quick buffs if you’re routinely caught unawares.  

Synaptic Shock:  Yes, it’s mind-affecting only, but it triggers on a successful save!  Being confused for even a single round can be a major pain, basically giving you a 75% chance of ‘I win’ for the round.  What really gives this the blue rank is how cheap this is, costing you a measly 1 pool point.  Compare to Mimic Metamagic Dazing spell; this amp is a steal, and highly recommended for your 11th level choice.  

Telepathic Targeting (OO):  This ability is a tad expensive, but it opens up some cool options for buffing and offense.  The mind-affecting limitation is the primary concern; on the offense you’re only getting use out of this with touch spells, such as Irresistable Dance, but you can provide spell support from any distance if you’ve got a telepathic bond up with an ally.  

Turning Spell:  This ability is far too expensive if you want to turn spells of higher levels (read: the ones capable of doing you the most harm).

Psychic Disciplines

Half flavor, half mechanics, your choice of discipline colors the sort of psychic you become, nudging you toward certain playstyles.  Discipline spells marked with an asterisk are not on the normal psychic spell list.

Note that certain discipline bonus spells have a higher or lower ranking than when they appear in the larger spell selection section.  This is because I am ranking their value as a bonus spell, rather than one of your actual choices.  For instance, dream voyage should be avoided by most people, but the dream discipline gets early access as a bonus spell, in which case it’s not terrible.  Also, this guide took a while to make, so my opinions on certain spells might have fluctuated!

Abomination (CHA)

Discipline Spells: ray of enfeeblement*, alter self, excruciating deformation, black tentacles, explode head, repulsion, insanity, orb of the void, telekinetic storm 

Dark Half:  A sort of ‘psychic rage,’ for a limited number of rounds per day you get some really nice benefits.  The main attraction is the save DC increase and the immunity to fear, providing an excellent defense from Intimidate and similar tactics right out of the gate.  The biggest downside is the potential to waste rounds after a fight as you attempt to regain control of yourself, with about a 50% chance of doing so.

Morphic Form:  Free DR while in your Dark Half is nice, but hopefully you’re avoiding getting attacked in the first place.  Still, it’s there if you need it, although rolling to see what type of DR you get each time is a bit annoying.

Psychic Safeguard:  SR is certainly handy, and it becomes truly legit SR while your Dark Half is up.  

Pros:  Front-loaded with a nice offensive and defensive ability, this is a good choice for those afraid of Intimidate tactics, particularly at low levels.  Early access to repulsion, interestingly.

Cons:  When you’re out of Dark Half rounds, this discipline basically does nothing for you until level 13.  The discipline spells aren’t especially great either.  Lastly, there’s no phrenic pool recovery mechanic.

For those of you looking for a darker sort of psychic, Abomination is a pretty solid choice.  Dark Half is a nice ace up your sleeve.

Dream (CHA)

Discipline Spells: sleep, oneiric horror, deep slumber, sleepwalk, nightmare, cloak of dreams, ethereal jaunt, dream voyage, microcosm

Dream Leech:  This is a source of phrenic pool replenishment, which is probably the primary use of it for most characters.  It works on unconscious creatures, so dying enemies are fair game.  It’s only 1/hour, and they get a save, so it’s not the most reliable thing in the world.

Oneiromancy:  This has largely RP/social implications, and until you gain access to dream and similar spells, it requires you to creep on sleeping people directly to use.  Possibly of use for interrogating enemies you put to sleep, this is a pretty niche ability.  

Mind Heist:  Another information gathering ability, usable only on sleeping targets.

Waking Dream:  You get this ability just after you learn cloak of dreams, so access to sleeping targets won’t be quite so difficult to come by.  This is an interesting ability for infiltration and scouting, as well as a unique and disturbing tool in a social or intrigue-laden setting.  You need to have a buddy watching your back to use it safely, though.

Pros:  Lots of good, creepy flavor, especially for an NPC villain psychic.  Make the sandman!

Cons:  This discipline is very reliant on a singular status: sleep.  None of your abilities will ever work on constructs, plants, vermin, oozes, elves… the list goes on.  Deep slumber remains a decent spell until level 8 or so, and then you have a nasty dry spell until level 12 when you get cloak of dreams, which requires you to get dangerously close to your targets.  Otherwise you have to do a lot of waiting around for people to hit the sack.

While you can make it work with some effort, this discipline does not lend itself to a typical adventurer’s lifestyle.  You get early access to dream voyage, but the rest of your spell list is pretty unremarkable.  This is largely a villain discipline for devious GMs.

Enlightenment (WIS; OO)

Discipline Spells:  acute senses, identify, clairaudience/clairvoyance, thoughtsense, atonement*, greater arcane sight, circle of clarity, protection from spells, overwhelming presence.

Expanded Awareness:  This is a nifty utility ability, also handy in clutch situations where your regular senses just aren’t cutting it.  

Patient Insight:  This is likely an out-of-combat or downtime power; your phrenic points are better spent on your spells on a proper adventure.

Focused Trance:  This is something the Lore discipline wishes it had.  This almost guarantees you an answer to a number of questions each day equal to your WIS modifier.

Empty Mind:  It costs phrenic points, but this allows you to get rid of some of the nastiest negative statuses in the game.

Pros:  Early access to greater arcane sight and acute senses, and a relatively solid bonus spell selection for information gathering.

Cons:  No phrenic pool recovery mechanic, and two abilities that spend phrenic points means the Enlightened psychic will be thirsty for phrenic points.  Your 2nd level bonus spell is wasted with a late-access identify for some reason.

The Enlightenment discipline is in many ways a slightly more active Lore discipline.  It doesn’t give you much of an edge in a fight, but you’ll be hard to sneak up on.  Overall, this discipline lacks punch.  It’s very middle of the road.

Faith (WIS)

Discipline Spells: bless*, spiritual weapon*, magic vestment*, guardian of faith*, commune*, psychic surgery, greater scrying, greater planar ally, miracle*

Deity:  You’ve got to pick a deity to follow, which comes with alignment restrictions.  Be careful not to shift alignment too radically, or you could lose access to your faith powers.

Divine Energy:  Everybody likes healing.  You won’t be as good as an actual divine caster, but the fact that you recover phrenic pool points with this ability is even better.  It’s almost like a bonus spell known each level.

Resilience of the Faithful:  Your shoulder item slot is now officially free!

Prayer Aura:  Prayer is a decent cleric spell, and this ability is like a slightly more potent version of it.  The fact that it’s a free action to activate makes it that much better.  When it matters, you can tweak the numbers of a fight in the favor of your party.

Pros:  Your bonus spell list is mostly made of non-psychic spells, most of them decent cleric spells.  You’ve got the easiest phrenic pool recovery mechanic, and the rest of your abilities are useful and not too complicated.

Cons:  Basically nothing, except for the alignment restrictions.

A faith-based psychic might not be the first idea that comes into your head when you think ‘psychic,’ but this is a really strong discipline with essentially no downsides.  Most published settings include lots of interesting deities, so there’s lots of fun potential for flavor and RP hooks.  You’re not necessarily affiliated with a church or tied to dogma; you could be a heretic, a lone voice in the wilderness, or a chosen herald.

Ferocity (WIS; UW)

Discipline Spells: anticipate peril, bull’s strength, heroism, freedom of movement, stoneskin, transformation, greater arcane sight, mind blank, foresight

Enhanced Senses: While this ability is sort of like a bonus spell known initially, it becomes downright excellent at the 11th level boost where it becomes permanently active.  This basically frees up some spells known for detection abilities like see invisibility or echolocation.  You’ll be hard to sneak up on, especially after the upgrade.

Survival Instinct: A handy boost to survivability.

Ferocity: By the numbers, this ability is great for your overall toughness and ability to withstand injury, but the fact that you won’t go down until you are dead makes you potentially more of a target.  Going unconscious at negative HP is certainly bad, but it often means enemy attention is drawn elsewhere, giving your allies perhaps a chance to wrap things up and heal you once the danger is through.  If nothing else, this allows you to hopefully escape once you hit negative health, just be careful; this isn’t an excuse to abandon caution.  

Primal Fury: In some ways this is worse than transformation, which is already a terrible spell for you.  Why would you want to turn off your ability to cast spells?  

Pros:  You’ll be hard to kill, and hard to sneak up on.

Cons:  The bonus spell list lacks zazz, and it seems to want to direct you towards melee combat, which is not something you should be doing outside of extremely specialized polymorph builds or somesuch.

For low to mid-level games, this discipline is okay.  It lacks focus, but you’ll have a bit of extra survivability.  The 13th level ability is a waste, and your bonus spell list is oddly lackluster; aside from transformation, none of your bonus spells are bad, but many of them are delayed-access, such as stoneskin and mind blank.  Foresight is also pretty tame for your bonus 9th.  No phrenic pool recovery mechanic is the final insult here.  There are many better options.

Haunted (CHA; HHH)

Discipline Spells: detect undead, calm spirit*, halt undead*, speak with haunt, disrupting weapon*, undeath to death*, ethereal jaunt, greater possession, etherealness

Lingering Spirits:  Effectively more cantrips known, limited use per day but a swift action to use.  I’ve certainly seen worse, but this is no Dark Half or even Divine Energy.

Spiritual Bulwark:  If you’re in a spooky game, this is a decent defensive perk, but otherwise it’s very limited.

Phantasmal Assault:  Assuming you know the Will of the Dead phrenic amp, the only thing this does for you is save you pool points.  The main reason I don’t like this ability is that it dictates how you allocate your spells known; you HAVE to have at least one spell like mind thrust or psychic crush to benefit from it, and this discipline doesn’t have the decency to give you at least one as a discipline spell.  

Pros:  You get a decent number of non-psychic spells on your discipline list, although most of them are pretty niche and underwhelming.  It’s thematically appropriate for a spooksome game.

Cons:  Late access to ethereal jaunt and etherealness on your discipline list, not to mention the redundancy.  Your 13th level ability is a more limited version of something every other psychic could do from level 1.  No phrenic pool recovery mechanic.

Like most minor splat disciplines, this thing just pales in comparison to the ‘core’ disciplines.  There are LOTS of ways to deal with ghosts and ghouls as a psychic; you certainly don’t need this discipline to do so.

Lore (WIS)

Discipline Spells: comprehend languages, hypercognition, dispel magic, mind probe, retrocognition, legend lore, greater arcane sight, moment of prescience, divide mind

Illuminating Answers:  Your phrenic pool recovery mechanic requires that you know at least one question-answering divination at all times throughout your career.  This is pretty manageable, and goes along with your chosen role nicely.  

Mnemonic Cache:  This is a pretty cool ability that’s open-ended and has lots of potential in a game heavy with puzzles, intrigue, and conspiracy.  The key is to remember that you have this ability, and make sure you use it!  The ability to disable written magical traps is interesting, but is probably only going to be relevant a handful of times over the course of a campaign.

Superior Automatic Writing:  There’s nothing wrong with the automatic writing skill unlock, and this makes you better at it.  The reason this gets a red rating is that you can still only do this once per week.  All other disciplines have a 5th level power that you can use every day, or even all the time.  The chance that you could still fail your skill check and be left with nothing makes this even less attractive.

Memory Palace:  Your final Lore ability is admittedly pretty cool.  It doubles as a safe spot to sleep or hide and a helpful tool for your information gathering.  

Pros:  Your bonus spells, while not especially stellar, are nice to have for a lore-master, and free your spell selection for other choices.

Cons:  There’s very little combat support here, and you’re really shoehorned into the diviner role.

This discipline has some great flavor, and if you really want to specialize in uncovering secrets this is a serviceable choice.  However, most other psychics will have an edge over you in a fight.

Mindtech (WIS; OR)

Discipline Spells:  technomancy*, protection from technology*, irradiate*, malfunction*, lightning arc*, destroy robot*, memory of function*, prismatic wall, time stop

Synergized Energy:  A phrenic pool recovery mechanic is always nice, but this isn’t really reliable until 7th level when you can pick up the Psychic Battery psi-tech discovery.  Hope you can find some batteries or generators!  

More insultingly, this is all you get from this discipline until 5th level.  Most other disciplines at least give you something else to do, some little perk, but this only does phrenic pool recovery.  

Attune Implants:  Hey, a way to boost your spell DCs!  That’s nice!  Get ready to shoehorn a decent chunk of your WBL at level 5, or you get nothing.

Once you can afford or acquire an implant, this gives you a nice little tool, but compare this to the Abomination discipline, which has been doing this, for free, with other benefits, since level 1.  And don’t forget, you can only do this once per day!  Joy!

Dominate Technology:  1/day control construct that’s actually more difficult to maintain than the regular spell.  Thanks?

Woof.  Even in a game involving technological stuff, this is a painful, painful discipline.  It’s skimpy on perks, extremely limited, and the benefits it gives you are worse than most of your regular spells.  The bonus spell list is… there.  Most of those spells are pretty terrible, even in a tech-focused game.  Leave this stuff to the wizard.

Pageantry (CHA; HotHC)

Discipline Spells: bless*, calm emotions, slow, overwhelming presence, seeming, heroes’ feast*, mage’s magnificent mansion, divine vessel*, heroic invocation

Ritual Unity:  A bonus to occult rituals, and you’re potentially better at Aid Another.  This ability actually gets more valuable as your ability to hit the DC 20 Aid Another mark improves, allowing you to more reliably recover phrenic pool points.  Aid Another can actually be optimized pretty far, but it’s a tactic better suited for other classes (and halflings in particular, which aren’t the best race for a psychic).  Still, it’s a phrenic pool recovery mechanic, and not a bad one to boot.  Note that there’s no cap on how many points you can recover in a day!  That’s a potential oversight, but until there’s errata I guess you can just go to town.

Power from Pageantry:  While this is less advised to do in combat, this is a great ability for buffing or out of combat utility casting.

Force of Habit:  While this ability starts okay, there aren’t a lot of concentration duration spells that are especially great, and this ability becomes less useful later in your career when swift actions are much more precious.  Eventually, this will be a obsolete ability, and the phrenic pool cost is a bit of a nuisance.  There’s a brief sweet spot when you first get it and spells like id insinuation are still good, but once you get confusion I’d probably never touch this ability again.

Unrivaled Focus:  The phrenic cost is a bit annoying, but nothing sucks more than failing a clutch concentration check.  Be judicious with its use.  It’s also handy for saving a move action when a thought component is involved.  

Pros:  You get some decent bonus spells from the cleric list.  Some other nice little perks rounds out the package, especially if you like pre-combat or pre-exploration buffing (which is a wise thing to do!).

Cons:  While decently front-loaded, your 5th and 13th level upgrades range from subpar to underwhelming.  This discipline is also suffering from poor editing.  Early access to overwhelming presence has been errata’d in the past, and the lack of a cap on Ritual Unity is probably an oversight as well.  You should talk to your GM about an appropriate 4th level spell replacement.

You’re here mostly for Power from Pageantry and the bonus spell list.  In situations where you don’t have time to prepare, you’re at a marked disadvantage.  

While gaining psychic power from tea ceremonies or pomp and circumstance is a bit odd, this is a decent-to-passable discipline for party cheerleader types.  The bonus list gives a lot of fun spells that are difficult to justify taking normally on a spontaneous caster.  As an added bonus, you could put ‘M.C.’ in front of your character’s name, and you could call all other psychics ‘suckas.’  

Pain (CHA)

Discipline Spells: persuasive goad*, pain strike, vampiric touch, mass pain strike, synapse overload, mass inflict pain, waves of exhaustion, horrid wilting*, mass suffocation

Painful Reminder:  You’ll learn to hate this ability.  It’s almost a waste of time, if it wasn’t tied to your phrenic pool recovery mechanic.  This also shoehorns you into a damage dealing build, and psychics aren’t the best at damage delivery.  The final insult is that it eats up your swift action, which will be especially annoying at higher levels.

Power from Pain:  Not all disciplines have a phrenic pool recovery mechanic, but this proves that it’s not all it’s cracked up to be.  This only triggers with your Painful Reminder ability, and only if you deal at least 5 points of damage.  Before 8th level it’s almost useless.

Live On:  And from out of left field, something worth having!  Lay on hands, and mercies, as a paladin 3 levels lower.  You can’t share this with your friends, but it’s great for your own selfish survival.  

Agonizing Wound:  If you’ve made it this far, this ability takes some of the sting off the damage-dealing role you’ve been forced into.  Adding some lovely debuffs to your damaging spells is a welcome addition to your arsenal.  

Pros:  You’ll be surprisingly tanky from 5th level onward, and if you like the idea of direct damage as a psychic, this gives you some nice tools later in your career.

Cons:  This discipline is front-loaded with crap, and like a cut in your mouth you can’t stop tonguing, the annoyance refuses to go away.  If you want to recover phrenic pool points, you have to sacrifice your swift actions on occasion to the crappiness that is Painful Reminder.  Your bonus spells are mostly ‘Fort. save or suck,’ which lacks variety.

The Pain discipline is… a bit of a pain.  It seems to favor ‘bad touch’ builds, and the Mind Thrust and Psychic Crush line will probably see ample use.  If you want a creepy masochist psychic, you’ll find plenty of flavor here, but mechanically there are less frustrating options.  Note that you get a lot of spells with the [Evil] descriptor, so they don’t get along with paladins.

Psychedelia (WIS)

Discipline Spells:  polypurpose panacea, mad hallucination, synesthesia, confusion, mirage arcana, joyful rapture, waves of ecstasy, euphoric tranquility, astral projection

Drug Resistance:  Few games ever feature drugs with any regularity.  Perhaps a bit ironically, you’ll mostly be benefiting from this ability by the drugs you craft or buy yourself.  Most drugs aren’t worth the effort, but at the very least this is flavorful.

Cognatogen:  It doesn’t last as long as an alchemist’s, but this ability remains useful throughout your career, essentially a 1/day buff to your save DCs and defenses at the cost of some ability damage.  Fortunately, the INT boost damages STR, which is the score you care the least about.

Warped Brain:  This is a great defensive ability; remember that you have it!  There’s a lot of mind-affecting stuff out there, so be sure to try and punish anyone stupid enough to examine your drug-addled mind.

Hallucinogenic Aura:  This is an extremely potent defensive and offensive tool.  Anything not immune to mind-affecting effects is at risk of becoming useless as soon as they get close to you.  In a hostile dungeon, this is amazing.  In a social setting, it’s a liability, because there’s no way to turn it off.  At high levels, you’ll pretty much have to act by proxy in towns and cities, because you risk causing a riot everywhere you go.

Pros:  Probably the best flavor of all the disciplines, you get some nice abilities out of it as well.

Cons:  The bonus spells don’t start getting good until the mid-levels.  The biggest downside is no phrenic pool recovery mechanic.  Also, in a game featuring lots of social interaction, I would highly suggest avoiding it if you plan on playing post level 13.  Depending on the game or the people you’re playing with, this discipline could be disruptive.

Have you ever, like, looked at a d20, man?  All those little triangles, but they become something more.  Whether you want to be comic relief, or just delve into the weird side of the occult, this discipline oozes flavor.  Be sure to take Craft (alchemy), and get ready for wackiness if you reach level 13.  This is also a good NPC discipline for the enlightened mystic hermit.

Rapport (CHA)

Discipline Spells:  charm person, enthrall, coordinated effort*, lesser geas, telepathy, battlemind link, mass hold person, mass charm monster, overwhelming presence

Emotional Bond:  This ability doesn’t get really good until 4th level, where it basically becomes a bonus spell known.  It’s handy for party scouts, as you can monitor them from a distance and quickly learn if they’re in danger.  

Emotional Push:  This is a handy panic button, and the fact that you can recover phrenic pool points with it is a nice perk.

Share Memory:  Hey, an at-will bonus spell.  I’ll take it.

Team Player:  Teamwork feats aren’t the greatest thing in the world, but you get some freebies, and the coordinated effort spell helps increase their usefulness.  This ability is stronger if you’ve got an inquisitor, cavalier, or hunter in your group.

Share Skill:  This is pretty tame for a 13th level ability, but it has its uses.  

Pros:  This does what is says on the tin, making you an effective team-player.  This is the quintessential buffer/cheerleader psychic discipline.  Note that when you reach 17th level, you can swap out telepathy for any 5th level spell you want.

Cons:  None, really, although your phrenic pool recovery mechanic isn’t the most dependable thing in the world, and you won’t have as much offensive power as other psychics.

Rapport makes for a great social butterfly and party support psychic.  Their bonus spell list is no slouch, and you’ll soon become the private hotline for your party’s thoughts and tactical communication.  

Rebirth (CHA; OO)

Discipline Spells:  burst of insight, false life, contact other plane, ancestral memory*, reincarnate*, transformation, ethereal jaunt, bilocation, akashic form

Past-Life Memories:  Essentially bardic knowledge on an INT-based class, this is quite choice.

Mnemonic Esoterica:  Sweet Dagon!  A floating spell known from any spellcasting class!  Even if it’s one spell level lower than your highest, this is still an amazing ability for a spontaneous caster.  Incredible for downtime, magic item creation, or tailoring your spells towards an encounter you know is coming up.  Sweet mercy, this is the best CHA-based discipline by a wide margin, for this ability alone.  Entangle, fireball, animate dead, heal, the possibilities are endless…

Resurgence:  This ability is quite expensive in terms of phrenic points, and isn’t even guaranteed to save your life.  Hopefully you’re not in situations where you can use this very often.  

Physical Regression:  Essentially a bonus spell known in the form of polymorph (which is basically three spells in one itself).  It costs phrenic points to use, but it has ten times the normal duration, which is cool.  Relatively open ended, it has more utility use than in-combat application.

Pros:  Front-loaded with savory goodness, Mnemonic Esoterica provides the ultimate resource for a spontaneous caster: versatility.  You also get crazy-early access to contact other plane.

Cons:  No phrenic pool recovery mechanic, and your discipline spell list has some mid-to-late career doldrums.  However, Mnemonic Esoterica practically makes it a moot point.

Psychics got a really nice toy out of Occult Origins with this discipline.  CHA finally gets to challenge WIS as the superior secondary mental stat for psychics with this beauty.  You’re here for Mnemonic Esoterica; everything else is just gravy.  This discipline begins to blur the border between Tier 2 and Tier 1, it’s that good.

Self-Perfection (WIS)

Discipline Spells: expeditious retreat, bear’s endurance, haste, freedom of movement, echolocation, transformation, ethereal jaunt*, iron body, akashic form

AC Bonus:  This is a handy ability, similar to the monk’s AC bonus.  Ironically, you’ll still probably be better off tromping around in a suit of full plate, but this is nevertheless handy.

Physical Push:  This is your phrenic pool recovery mechanic, and it’s stupidly easy.  A DC 5 acrobatics check gets you a point back.  Rolling initiative is an ability check; do you automatically succeed at it?  It wouldn’t be unreasonable for a GM to rule that you only get a point back if you’re actually challenged by the task at hand, in which case this drops to black.

Bodily Purge:  Similar to Pain’s Live On ability, this is a great, if selfish, source of healing and negative status removal.  It’s almost like 3-4 bonus spells known.

Pure Body:  Immunity to poison and disease?  On a Fort. weak class?  Gimme!

Pros:  Easy phrenic pool recovery, an extremely strong bonus spell list, and rock-solid defensive abilities put this in the top tier.  

Cons:  Your bonus 6th level spell is a bit of a waste, but I guess it’s the price you pay.  Your GM might get salty if you abuse the Physical Push recovery mechanic.

The path to self-perfection leads to true power.  The monk-ish psychic’s got it good, as the WIS disciplines continue to crush it.  This is just a solid, highly defensive option.  Your bonus spells cover most essential buffs and self-buffs, freeing up your choices for utility and offense.

Shadow (WIS; BoS)

Discipline Spells: blurred movement, fear the sun, deeper darkness, shadow step, shadow evocation*, shadow walk*, lunar veil, umbral strike, polar midnight*

Twilight Influence:  Essentially an extra bonus spell list, limited to 1/day per spell level.  There’s some good spells on the Darkness domain list, even if it’s redundant with a few of your discipline spells.  It’s an easy phrenic pool recovery mechanic as well, which is nice.

Dark Defense:  A minor AC buff that doesn’t stack with common spells and items, and it’s conditional.  You’ll probably be concealed most of the time if you’re taking this, but it’s just okay.

Adumbration:  Hide in plain sight is a nice ability… but you’ve been casting invisibility since level 4.  

Pros:  It’s almost double the bonus spells known.

Cons:  Sadly, they’re not the greatest spells, particularly your discipline list.  Your other abilities range from ‘okay’ to ‘easily forgettable.’

This is a perfectly functional discipline, but it’s on the lower end of the black rating, borderline orange.  There are much stronger choices out there.

Spirit Channeler (CHA; AG)

Discipline Spells: heightened awareness, bear’s endurance, aura sight*, persistent vigor, true seeing, thought shield V, mind blank, iron body, akashic form

Agitating Cognizance:  You’ll only get any use out of this in the early levels, before level 5, when the benefits of Metamorphosis are at their weakest.  An extra phrenic point is okay, but you’ll only have one or two phrenic amps to play with anyway.  After level 5, you’ll probably never benefit from this ability again.

Metamorphosis:  This is an odd ability, but it serves as the foundation for your later abilities as well.  Essentially a stranger version of alter self, you benefit from a physical ability score boost.  There’s no mention of a bonus to Disguise checks, which there probably should be.  If you spend a phrenic pool point, it more closely matches alter self, although the duration drops to hours per level, rather than permanent.  Typically you won’t want to do this, since the benefits of your later spirit channeling abilities outweigh the mild benefits of alter self.  
Basically, just look at this as a bonus to a physical ability of your choice for the most part.  A nice perk, but nothing earth-shattering.

Spirit Channeling:  Similar to the shaman’s wandering spirit ability, you can now pick up a 1st level spirit ability from any of the shaman spirits as long as your Metamorphosis ability is active.  Most of these are not very useful to a psychic, especially by the time you’re level 5.  Of the options available to you, the channel ability from the Life spirit is probably the most useful, giving you some helpful healing abilities.  The Slums spirit Doors to Everywhere ability is also pretty nice, especially if you’re in an urban environment or dungeon.  Those options I’d rate as green, but most everything else is red to orange, with the melee touch attack energy abilities being the worst, and the other fiddly abilities being slightly better.

Greater Spirit Channeling:  At 13th level you can pick up greater spirit abilities.  Most of these remain pretty crappy, but there are a few standouts.  The Heavens spirit Void Adaptation opens up some interesting strategies with the ability to see through supernatural darkness.  If someone else in your party can cast deeper darkness, you’ll have a potent tactical advantage, and the other defensive benefits are nice, including not needing to breathe.  Lore’s automatic writing ability is handy for downtime.  The Slums spirit remains useful: Doors to Everywhere continues to be handy, and the defensive benefits of City’s Shroud are nice.  If you’re going to be adventuring underwater, the Waves spirit can net you a swim speed and water breathing.  Lastly, Wood’s Bloody Roots ability is an interesting battlefield control option, saving you a spell on black tentacles, although the shelf life on that spell is starting to show by level 13.

One note for both spirit channeling abilities that should be mentioned: there is no language stating that you should count your Psychic level as Shaman levels for purposes of scaling, etc.  This is a safe assumption, but it’s worth bringing up.

Pros:  A nice bonus spell list, and decent passive defensive abilities through spirit channeling.

Cons:  No phrenic point recovery mechanic, and most of your discipline abilities are comparatively tame.

I like this discipline more on paper than actual execution.  This is a fine middle-of-the-road discipline, mostly defensive with a nice bonus spell list.  The real problem is the simple disappointing usefulness of shaman spirit abilities; the flexibility of your wandering spirit powers is hampered by the powers themselves being so lackluster, or meant to benefit someone with a better BAB.

Superiority (CHA; PFCC: DD)

Discpline Spells: moment of greatness, deflect blame, unflappable mein, majestic image*, mage’s private sanctum, transformation, project image, clone*, overwhelming presence

Self-Assurance:  This combos well with your first discipline spell, is a handy boost for all sorts of rolls, and replenishes your phrenic pool.  Nice!  The only downside is it doesn’t stack with heroism and its greater version, and might overlap with bardic buffs if you have one on your party.

At Arm’s Length:  Free metamagic with a built-in cost reducer.  Particularly handy if you’re interested in bad-touch spells.

Magical Hoarder:  This is a potentially neat trick, but risky; you have to be disconcertingly close.  Still, if you can make the Spellcraft check (and you should be able to!), you can piggy-back buffs or healing spells.  While it sucks that it doesn’t stop the BBEG from casting heal, at least you’ll get patched up too.  You could also use this to teleport with a bad guy to wherever they’re going, but you’ll be by yourself, so be careful.  It’s a bit thirsty for phrenic points too, so use your best judgement.

Pros:  Easy and useful phrenic pool recovery mechanic, few frills and fiddly bits.

Cons:  The spell list is lackluster.  Lots of niche-bordering-on-useless, trap, and long-casting time spells that are less useful for on-demand adventuring needs.

If this discipline had a better spell list, it would be an easy green.  As it stands, it has perfectly serviceable and easy-to-use abilities, but there are a lot of stinkers in the spells.  Not all of them are really that bad, but they’re not spells that your typical spontaneous caster wants or needs on-tap (clone, mage’s private sanctum).  

Symbiosis (CHA; UW)

Discipline Spells: hide from animals*, hold animal, dominate animal, command plants*, awaken*, liveoak*, greater scrying, control plants*, dominate monster

Animal Mastery: Sort of like a bonus spell known, this is a handy tool for scouting or spying, especially when combined with your various bonus spells.  Animals are pretty common if you know where to look; even the humble rat could help you map out a dungeon level, for instance.  The 7th level upgrade saves you spell slots and logistical hurdles.

One with Nature:  Two bonus spells known, at-will, with some extra benefits.  This will help you find those animals, and eventually help you communicate with them, saving you spells known and spell slots.

Bionetwork:  Effectively another bonus spell known, although the various limitations and ‘casting’ time hamper its usefulness.

Animate Tree:  A source of disposable minions, although they are slow, and their offensive usefulness is already starting to lag by the time you get this.  Handy for destroying objects or buildings, but you’re SOL if you’re someplace without trees.

Pros: Opens up the plant creature type for enchanters and controllers.  Good scouting and spying potential via animals.

Cons:  Bonus spell list is quite specialized; outside of a forest, your tree-based abilities are wasted, and treants aren’t the greatest minions either.  

While the flavor of this discipline leans towards a ‘druid psychic,’ it actually is a fine choice for a controller, telepath, or psychic that wants to play at minionmancy.  Your initial abilities are the most handy, especially if you make an effort to seek out animal life.  Befriend them, charm them, control them; they are your eyes and ears, and a source of useful information.  There will be few creature types you won’t be able to charm or control, and you get early access to dominate animal.  Without access to trees, many of your bonus spells and abilities are wasted, but you’ll still be able to pull your weight.  

Tranquility (WIS)

Discipline Spells: telempathic projection, silence, mantle of calm, mass daze, serenity, psychic surgery, mind blank, euphoric tranquility, time stop

Mental Placidity:  A nice defensive ability with phrenic pool replenishment wrapped up with it.  Certainly handy.

Calming Presence:  This is basically a bonus spell known, which is nice.  Calm emotions is not the most dependable spell, but it’s handy if you’re trying to play diplomat.

Purge Disquiet:  Immunity to some of the worst statuses for you, and the ability to help out your friends.  This is a great defensive benefit.

Pros:  A relatively strong bonus spell list at the early and late levels, and solid general defensive abilities with the power to regrain phrenic pool points.

Cons:  The mid levels will be a bit of a dry stretch for you, with poor bonus spells (late access to mass daze?  Thanks?) and a so-so 5th level ability.  Once you reach level 13, though, things get much nicer.

This is a very solid discipline.  The mid-level doldrums are what keeps it from hitting blue, especially in comparison to Faith and Self-Perfection.  Best for a diplomat, party face, or peace-loving psychic.

Feat Selection

Unlike sorcerers and wizards, psychics don’t have a source of bonus feats, so you’re going to have to make them count.

You can take Psi-Tech Discoveries in place of feats.

General Feats

Additional Traits (APG):  Some traits are really amazing.  Handy for expanding your class skills, shoring up saves, or boosting initiative as well.

Augment Summoning:  Spell Focus (Conjuration) is always something of a feat tax, but it’s even more painful for a psychic, since they lack grease and glitterdust.  If you pick up some summoning spells (and you should!), I suggest focusing more on utility or just simple cannon fodder.  However, this is a prereq for Superior Summoning, which is a great feat.

Careful Reader (OO):  Flavorful, but unlikely to be of use very often.

Combat Casting:  Many psychic spells are close range, so you may find yourself in sticky situations.  Certainly handy if you can fit it in.

Cranial Adjustment (OO):  The first (alphabetically) of many feats that concern occult skill unlocks.  These aren’t really worth your feat selection.

Cranial Implantation (OO):  See the above feat (even more so!  Avoid!).

Disciplinary Devotee (OA):  These bonuses are too niche and too mild; don’t waste a feat.

Eldritch Heritage (UM):  For the cost of a Skill Focus feat, you can use this to nab a familiar.  Other bloodline powers are of questionable use, but they’re there if you want them.  This is better for a CHA-based discipline psychic.

Elongated Cranium (OA):  Creepy weirdness aside, I can only recommend this for fronto-occipital elongation for a party face, social psychic.  The 1/day free Extend Spell is the best part; none of the other ones are worth it.

Empath (OA):  I would only consider taking this in a higher level game that featured a lot of RP or social situations.  It’s too risky to use in a dangerous area.

Expanded Arcana (APG):  Humans and half-humans probably won’t need this, but other races can always benefit from more spells known.

Expanded Phrenic Pool (OA):  For most of the better phrenic amps, this feat gives you basically one more use per day.  It just doesn’t feel too worth it to me.

Extra Amplification (OA):  If you could pick major amps, this would be great.  As it stands, there are only a few must-have amps, and you can grab them naturally.  If you like the sound of one of the more niche amps, though, this is certainly an option.

Familiar Bond, Improved Familiar Bond (FF):  This is a bit more feat intensive than the Eldritch Heritage route, but Iron Will is arguably more useful than Skill Focus as a prereq.  WIS-based discipline psychics might find these feats of interest, as a familiar is a very helpful servant and tool.

Improved Familiar:  If you took the trouble to get a familiar, one more feat can make your familiar into something truly special.  Look for SLAs not on your class list!

Favored Enemy Spellcasting (ACG):  If you’re playing a campaign that features a specific enemy type often, this is better than Spell Focus.

Fortune Teller (ISWG):  If you’re into divinations, this is an easy +1 CL.

Great Fortitude:  Failing a Fort. save is bad news.  This helps your squishy body.  If you’re in a particularly nasty game (lots of undead or poison), consider taking the improved version as well.

Haruspicy (OO):  Someone worked really hard on occult skill unlocks, and is very proud of them.

Hidden Presence, Manipulative Presence, Intrusive Presence (OA):  These feats are all useful if you plan on using the possession line of spells and generally being a creep.  If it weren’t for the intensive feat investment, I’d rank them higher; you either commit to this or you don’t.  That said, Intrusive Presence can have nightmarish repercussions.  Definitely a mid to late game consideration.

Iron Will:  Even though Will is your strong save, and you’ll have lots of spells and class features to improve it even further, nothing messes with your casting ability like a failed Will save.  Every little bit helps.  You’ll be happy to have the improved version when you need it as well; consider taking it in a very caster-heavy or occult-y game.

Keif Addict + others (GTK):  If you’d like to make more use of the drug rules as a psychedelia discipline psychic, these give you some flavorful options.

Lightning Reflexes:  Although Reflex is likely to be your worst save, I wouldn’t bother investing too much into it unless you’re in a dragon-heavy campaign or something.

Lucid Dreamer (OA):  This feat is almost entirely fluff and flavor, barring extremely niche circumstances.  If you’re playing in a game that heavily features dream-dimension stuff, and your GM rules you can gain XP from dream adventures, this becomes obviously better.

Lunging Spell Touch (ACG):  You’re probably better off with spectral hand if you want to use touch spells, but this is an interesting option at higher levels.

Mage’s Tattoo (ISWG):  This is an easy +1 to CL if you’re already taking Spell Focus.

Psychic Combatant (OA):  This makes the first thought-form creature you make free (or almost-free), a strong opening move.  It’s nice that it’s got some out-of-psychic-duel utility, but in general, psychic duels are an inefficient use of your resources.  See the Appendix for more on psychic duels.

Psychic Defender (OA):  Your own hp pool is less important than making a tanky thought-form creature.  Skip this.

Psychic Healer (OA):  Even if you pick up Heal as a class skill somehow, the faith healing unlock isn’t really that great, mainly due to the amount of time it takes.  During downtime it’s fine, but you probably shouldn’t blow a feat on a downtime activity unless it’s item creation.

Psychic Maestro (OA):  Investing resources into the occult skill unlocks is unadvised.  They’re flavorful and fun, but there are stronger options.  A Lore discipline psychic might consider this just to get some more use out of their 5th level ability.

Psychic Virtuoso (OA):  See above.

Rhabdomancy (OO):  This is here if you want to be better at dowsing…

Sacred Geometry (OM):  Please don’t use this feat.  I’ve never seen anything with the potential to grind a game to a screeching halt like this monstrosity.  

Shadow Gambit (ISM):  It isn’t the greatest, but this is a pretty cool feat, particularly if you like illusions.  It can even give the psychic some almost-fake area of effect damaging spells.

Shatter Mental Mask (OA):  Of all the psychic duel feats, this one is the most esoteric, and thus probably least worth your time.  It has a chance to reveal mystery opponents to you, but that’s likely only going to matter in a very intrigue-laden game.

Skeleton Summoner (UM):  This helps you become a fake necromancer.  Oddly enough, there’s a lot of decent necromancy spells on your class list; try charming undead with your phrenic amps to really confuse people.

Skill Focus:  Take it as a prereq for things like Eldritch Heritage if need be.  If you grab UMD as a class skill through a trait, this is also a decent option to expand your versatility.

Spectrum Sight (OO):  The read aura occult skill unlock might be the least useful of them all.

Spell Focus, Greater Spell Focus:  If you wind up favoring a certain school, these feats are a great help, particularly for enchantment and illusion.  

Spell Penetration, Greater Spell Penetration:  You’ve got phrenic amps to help you with this, but SR is a real pain, and every boost helps.  Of particular value in an outsider-heavy campaign.

Spell Perfection (APG):  It’s a long wait, but this is a high-level gem.  Pick your favorite spell and go to town.

Spell Specialization (UM):  This is a nice feat if you find yourself relying on a specific spell a lot, especially in combat.  It becomes less useful towards the late mid levels; I might consider retraining it by level 10 or so.

Spirit Sense (OA):  If you find yourself in a particularly haunted game, this feat could be of some use, but otherwise I suggest having contingencies, i.e. know a force spell or two.

Spontaneous Metafocus (UM):  If you’ve got a decent CHA, and have a favored combat spell, this combos well with the metamagic feat of your choice.

Steadfast Personality (ACG):  If you’re a CHA discipline psychic, this feat helps alleviate MADness by allowing you to ignore WIS entirely and still have a great Will save.

Steel Soul (APG):  If you’re playing a dwarf, this is a solid choice.

Superior Summoning (UM):  If you’re interested in summoning, this is the single best way to flood the battlefield with disposable meat sacks.  It only gets better with age.

Third Eye (OA):  This is garbage.  Just hot garbage.  SKIP.

Toughness:  Essentially +2 to CON for hp purposes, this is always useful on your squishy chassis.  Better for humans and half-humans who are using their favored class bonus for more useful things.

Trepanation (OO):  Gross.

Truth in Wine (OO):  Hey, at least you can get your drank on.

Xenoglossy (OA):  I like this feat, I really do.  But you get telepathy and all sorts of other magical options to communicate.  This feat isn’t for you.

Combat Feats

Armor Proficiency (light, medium, heavy):  psychic magic has no somatic components, so armor doesn’t inhibit your spells in any way.  Many of your offensive spells don’t require attack rolls, so if you want, you could theoretically stomp around in full plate awkwardly and tank the attack penalty.  Most psychics will do just fine with a mithril chain shirt and a MW buckler / darkwood heavy shield if they’re feeling especially fancy.

Defensive Combat Training:  This feat benefits you more than ¾ BAB types.  

Improved Initiative:  Is there a guide out there where this isn’t blue?  Going first means getting your spells off before something bad happens.  Always worth picking up if you can.

Item Creation Feats

If you’re in a campaign that has plenty of downtime, you might consider item crafting feats; you’re basically trading feats for more magic gear, which can be worth it.  You have a harder time meeting prereqs than wizards or arcanists, but it’s not an insurmountable obstacle.  

Craft Rod:  This is likely a late-game pick.  The more powerful metamagic rods are extremely expensive, but they’re arguably more useful than knowing the feats themselves.  A worthy investment, certainly.

Craft Wand:  This is nice if you’re especially paranoid about fear effects, and don’t get access to blanket fear immunity.  A wand of Remove Fear is nice and cheap, and can help get you back in the fight.  There’s all sorts of spells that are handy to have in wand-form.

Craft Wondrous Item:  Probably the best item creation feat for the sheer volume of wondrous items out there; the possibilities are almost endless.  

Metamagic Feats

As a spontaneous caster, metamagic feats are a bit more annoying to use in combat.  There are ways to get around it, but you’ll often be better served by metamagic rods.  Still, knowing at least three is a prereq for Spell Perfection.  I recommend feats useful outside of combat if possible.

Extend Spell:  Possibly the most popular metamagic feat, this is an old standby that’s great for buffing before a dungeon delve.  Gets less useful later in your career.

Intuitive Spell (OA):  You can negate the downside of thought components with a move action; using this feat will take a full round action.  Thus, it’s basically useless.

Logical Spell (OA):  The big weakness of psychic magic is emotion components.  There are ways to defend against negative emotion effects, but inevitably something’s going to get through.  This is handy in rod form, but you won’t regret simply knowing this feat either.  

Persistent Spell (APG):  You’ll be forcing a lot of saving throws over the course of your career.  This is the feat to use when you have to stick that save-or-lose.  Combos well with phrenic amps for particularly desperate straights.  

Piercing Spell (UM):  In a game that heavily features SR, this feat is probably green.  It stacks with Spell Penetration and phrenic amps, and is a pretty modest level adjustment.

Quicken Spell:  Ah, Quicken Spell.  Trade higher level slots for superior action economy.  Old wisdom suggests grabbing this around level 11 or 13.  You’ll finally have the slots to use it, and you probably won’t have access to rods for several more levels.  You might consider retraining it once you get 9th level spells, as divide mind renders it largely obsolete.  

Teamwork Feats

These are mostly of concern to the Rapport discipline psychic.  Most of these are designed for people fighting in the heat of the fray, which is where you don’t want to be.  As such, I’m going to just mention the ones possibly worth your bonus feat slots as a Rapport psychic.

Lookout (APG):  This is insurance against being surprised, which can spell your doom.  No prereqs, just stick near your buddy in a danger zone.

Tribe Mentality (KoG):  compulsions and emotion effects are bad news for your psychic magic.  This feat is nice in that you don’t need to be adjacent to your buddy, and there’s no prereqs.

Trait Selection

If your game features the optional trait rules (and most seem to these days), you basically have two options: pick something flavorful or appropriate for your backstory, or min-max them like the conniving Munchkin you are.  We’ll be looking at ‘optimal’ traits, many of which are basically half-feats.  There are so many of these damn things, from so many different sources, that I’m basically going to throw up my hands and say ‘look them up on the SRD.’  

Reactionary:  This applies to any similar initiative-boosting trait.  Going first is very important, so any trait that boosts this for you is gold.

Magical Lineage:  One of the only examples of metamagic reducers in Pathfinder, a wisely-picked spell coupled with this feat is potent indeed.  Combined with remove fear, you’ll have a defense against emotion component disruption from level 1.  

Dangerously Curious:  Traits that give you new class skills are great.  This one gives you Use Magic Device, which couples well with CHA-discipline psychics.  UMD will greatly expand your versatility, as well as give you something to do if you’re locked out of emotion components by fear or what-have-you.

Resilient:  Any of the save-boosting traits are great.  Fort. in particular is a weakness deserving of mention, but really any of these are a solid choice.

Focused Mind:  Most folks might not have the room to fit in a feat like Combat Casting.  Traits like this are a nice compromise.

Fate’s Favored:  Awesome for half-orcs with the sacred tattoo racial trait, this is a handy trait for anybody really.  Pick up a four leaf clover or a luckstone! 

Archetypes

There are four archetypes for the psychic included in Occult Adventures.  Let’s take a look:

Amnesiac

Amnesiac is an odd archetype.  All it alters is your spells known, and adds a mechanic to ‘remember’ new spells somewhat on the fly.  This is a decent archetype for flavor, or for the indecisive who wish to ‘test drive’ spells before locking them in.  It doesn’t really improve your flexibility as much as you might think; you only gain ‘amnesiac slots’ for your two highest spell levels, and the fact that you round up from your normal spells known means you’ll usually just have two ‘wild card’ spells a day.  While this is more than a normal psychic technically, you can only attempt to remember a new spell once per hour.  Depending on the pace of your game, this could very well mean you only get to use this ability once per major event in the story, and it doesn’t necessarily benefit pre-adventure buffing more than a normal psychic with a solid selection of spells known.

For a slower paced campaign with lots of downtime, this archetype is significantly more useful.  You can pick up more niche spells that a spontaneous caster would be less inclined to take, such as certain divinations, the create demiplane spells, or more niche healing spells.  

Weirdly, this archetype is most effective post level 18, when you can cycle through the 9th level spells and rent the more niche or less attractive ones.  There are precious few ways to expand your 9th level spells known, and in this area the amnesiac has a certain advantage.  

This archetype is more attractive to non-human characters with a more limited spell selection.  It’s fun, but it can also be unnecessarily complicating things.  It’s also unique in that should your memory ever be truly restored, you revert back to a normal psychic.  Basically, if your GM gets tired of it, he can take it away from you at the drop of a hat.  It’s worth noting that this archetype stacks with all other archetypes.

Formless Adept  (CHA)

Formless Adept is best viewed as a ‘super discipline,’ since it basically replaces a normal discipline while ‘locking’ a few phrenic amp choices.  This is here for people that liked the Psion Uncarnate from the 3.5 days of old.  It has some cool abilities, but it lacks ‘endurance’ compared to regular psychics; once you’re out of formless body uses for the day, you basically have no class features other than your spells and phrenic amps.  It also lacks a phrenic pool replenishment mechanic, which holds it back from a better rating as well.

This archetype favors touch spells, mind-control, and generally being creepy and spooky.  You make a pretty good spy and scout with this as well.

Formless Body:  This ability starts off as mostly crap, but gets better if you stick it out.  While the defensive benefits are nice, the main reason it’s a pain is that your bonus spells can only be cast while using it.  Your Formless Spell amp helps this a bit, but it ultimately means that your access to bonus spells is more limited than a normal psychic’s.  From 5th level onward it shouldn’t be too much of a hindrance once you get access to the 1-minute increment versions.  Remember that your daily allotment of minutes and rounds per day has a separate pool for each of the three forms.  A 13th level formless adept with an 18 Charisma would have 7 minutes of blurred body, 7 minutes of gaseous body, and 7 rounds of incorporeal body a day.

Formless Spell:  This is an automatic pick for your first phrenic amp.  It takes some of the action economy sting off of your formless body ability, but with no way to replenish pool points, every time you use it will be precious indeed.

Bonus Spells:  To compensate for your limited bonus spell access, you get double the bonus spells, and their durations are not tied to your formless body usage, which is a kindness.  Let’s take a look:  chill touch* + expeditious retreat, levitate + stricken heart, gaseous form + vampiric touch, shadow step + telekinesis, dominate person + synapse overload, disintegrate + enemy hammer, finger of death + telekinetic sphere, etherealness + power word stun, dominate monster + telekinetic storm

That’s a pretty strong list of bonus spells, certainly competitive with the best discipline lists.  Try to save uses of formless body so that you aren’t ‘locked out.’  

Psychic Possession:  Greater possession is a fine spell, one you very well might consider learning given your proclivities.  Your incorporeal body is always going to be extremely limited in duration, which means this is best used in a combat once or twice a day.  You can probably cause some damage with it, certainly, but I’d much rather just learn the spell and have my 15th level phrenic amp.

Formless Master:  Your capstone isn’t as good as remade self, but it does improve your formless body ability; it’s okay.  

Mutation Mind

 Oof.  This archetype is painful.  Unless you like the flavor, I’d highly suggest skipping it.  The Strength bonuses you get are pretty much wasted on your squishy chassis; you don’t want to be punching things!  The fact that your INT takes a hit while you’re under the influence is the real travesty here.  There are a few tools that a touch-spell focused psychic might consider… until 7th level and Phrenic Empowerment rears its ugly head.  Using your phrenic amps then becomes a liability as you risk further damaging your INT score should you be hurt in a fight.  Stay far away from this one.

Psychic Duelist

Psychic duels are a risky, costly endeavor.  As-written, barring a very intrigue-laden, highly psychic sort of game, they’re probably best avoided.  You’re basically focusing an archetype, and possibly some feats, on a single spell.  If you really like the idea of X-Men-style tests of mental might, you can make it work, and this archetype will make you better at it than anyone else.  This is sort of like being the grand-master of using poison; nice on paper, but usually more trouble than it’s worth.

With all of that said, you don’t actually lose a whole lot from this archetype: you replace a bonus spell known, a phrenic amp, and your higher level SLA’s, which isn’t too steep a price to pay.  In exchange, you learn instigate psychic duel with a built-in auto-Heighten, can learn manifestation-amps instead of phrenic amps, and by level 9 can use psychic duel attacks on creatures even if they’re not actually in a psychic duel.  

The level 9 ability is interesting; it reminds me of the Words of Power system, in that you can spontaneously generate attacks on the fly, targeting whatever saves or AC you want, possibly even inflicting negative statuses.  Instigating actual duels still have benefits, especially if you’ve picked up the dueling feats, and it requires a full-round action to use, so it’s not very efficient action-economy wise.  I’m surprised there’s no range limit or other restrictions here; you could possibly murder someone in a crowded feast hall and nobody would be the wiser.

Even with the 17th level Expert Manifester ability, using these abilities will eat through your spell slots pretty quickly.  

Equipment Considerations

Your mind might be a powerful weapon, but a siege engine can’t operate without support.  Items magical and mundane will make your job easier, keep you alive, and do the opposite for the enemy.

Mundane Gear:

Full Plate Armor:  Even though you don’t care about attack rolls in all likelihood, non proficiency and armor check penalties do apply to Dex-based ability checks, including Initiative, which you don’t want to penalize if possible.  That said, at very high levels you could slap various special abilities and materials on a suit of full plate to lower the ACP to 0 or so.  

(Mithral) Chain Shirt:  Perhaps a bit more reasonable, a mithral shirt isn’t outrageously expensive, and can be enchanted further.  No armor check penalty, and it’s strictly better than mage armor.  

Darkwood Heavy Shield / Mithral Heavy Shield:  The shield spell is hard to beat, but these don’t cost you spell slots or spells known, and can be enchanted to match and eventually surpass it.  

Light Crossbow:  At low levels, a ranged weapon is better than most damaging cantrips.

Dagger:  It’s handy to have a backup weapon for desperate situations.  A dagger is nice for utility as well.

Automatic Writing Planchette (OA):  The DCs for the automatic writing occult skill unlock are pretty high; this thing’s an easy +2 for cheap.  

Occult Reference Material (OA):  If you’ve got the time, this is a modest investment for a nice bonus on topics that might be common in an occult campaign.  

Prismatic Crystal (OA):  These are nifty little trinkets; you might as well enjoy the benefits of being psychic!  

Spirit Trumpet (OA):  Ghost sound and ventriloquism are both pretty handy spells, and this mundane item boosts their disbelief save DC by 1.  It’s a steal.

Talking Board (OA):  This cheap item gives you all sorts of useful goodies in a spooky occult game, especially if there’s more than one occult character in the party.

Alchemical Gear:

There’s lots of handy alchemical items; a psychedelia discipline psychic benefits from having Craft (alchemy) ranks in particular.

Magnetized Paper (OA):  Another boost to the automatic writing occult skill unlock for those interested.

Acid, Alchemist’s Fire, & others:  Area-effect damage isn’t the psychic’s strong suit, and these items will be particularly handy the first time you fight a swarm at low levels.

Antitoxin, Antiplague:  If you know you’re heading into a poisonous or disease-ridden zone, these items are a cheap way to bolster your weak Fort. saves.

Magic Items:

Headband of Vast Intelligence:  Make peace with your headband slot, because you’ll want the best one of these you can afford.  Later in your career, you’ll want to spring for a Headband of Mental Prowess boosting both INT and your discipline’s ability if you can.

Belt of Mighty Constitution / Incredible Dexterity:  You can probably snag one of these as a hand-me-down from a melee character.  These are primarily for shoring up your defenses and boosting initiative.

Cloak of Resistance:  Boring, yes, but saving throws are important.  Faith discipline psychics get a bit more flexibility here.

Hat of Disguise:  These things are just the best.  In an intrigue heavy or social game you can get up to all kinds of mischief.  

Handy Haversack:  Useful for lugging your stuff around, especially if you dumped STR.

Runestones of Power (ACG):  These are basically pearls of power for spontaneous casters.  They’re certainly handy, but they are quite expensive for what they do, and you shouldn’t be particularly strapped for spell slots.  Probably not worth purchasing, but you can put them on your wish list for inquiring GMs…

Ring of Psychic Mastery (OA):  With the above in mind, it’s actually cheaper to own runestones of power, levels 1 - 4, than it is to buy this ring, which does what those four runestones would do while eating up a ring slot.  IT’S A TRAP!

Ring of the Hidden Psyche (OA):  This isn’t something you buy; it’s a plot device.

Metamagic Rods:  You want these!  Ideally several different kinds.  Some are very reasonably priced, such as the lesser rod of extend.  Others are outrageously expensive, but still incredibly useful and powerful.  On your wishlist: Quicken, Persistent, Logical, and Piercing to name a few.

Pages of Spell Knowledge (UE) / Spell Lattices (ACG):  Paying money for more spells known is a worthy exchange.  They get pretty expensive for higher level spells, so these are best reserved for niche or utility spells you’d prefer not to have in wand form.  The 1st level ones are a steal; anything higher than 3rd level is getting a bit too expensive.

Wands:  1st level wands are the best deal.  Wishlist: endure elements, remove fear, air bubble.  

Staves:  Staves are expensive, but they’re a nice tool to stretch out your adventuring endurance in higher level play.  Typically, you find one in a treasure pile that you can use.  You might have trouble finding a staff you can use without UMD, since most staves feature flashy blast spells not on your list, or else healing spells.  You’ll probably wind up with something abjuration, enchantment, illusion, or divination-focused.  

Potions:  Potions are handy, especially if you’re under a fear or negative emotion effect.  Potions of remove fear should always be on hand, as well as healing potions.

Dreamcatcher (OA):  In an occult game featuring lots of dream-wackiness, this is a pretty cheap item that gives you a nice defensive edge during sleepytime.

False Coin (OA):  Nice and cheap, but surprisingly handy in an intrigue-laden game.

Flying Skiff / Flying Sled (OA):  These things are preposterously expensive, but if you can manage to construct one yourself, they’re a pretty stylish way to get around.  Seems to be asking your GM to destroy it in a climactic encounter, though…

Four-Leaf Clover (OA):  Much cheaper than a luckstone, these are handy little trinkets useful for any character.  Even better if you’ve got the Fate’s Favored trait.

Lucky Horseshoe (OA):  Much like the four-leaf clover, this is like a discount luckstone.  It’s nice that it’s ‘always on,’ but you can probably get by with a four-leaf clover until later in your career.

Orgone Accumulator (OA):  If you’ve got a permanent base, lots of time on your hands, and no divine caster in your party, I suppose this weird item would be worth having around.  As a high-level adventurer, your time is precious, dammit!  

Shrunken Head (OA):  This creepy doodad is extremely expensive, but if you’re into fear spells it’s a nice high-level toy with built-in defense.

Talismans (OA):  You don’t have a whole lot of high-priority things for your neck slot, so the various talismans are pretty nifty to have around.  Obviously the greater ones are better in the long run, but the lesser ones are handy in a pinch.  Danger sense, freedom, good fortune, and life’s breath are the real stand-outs.  

Spell Selection

For all your phrenic amps, discipline abilities, feats, and items, nothing is more important than your spell selection.  Anything that expands your basic allotment of spells known is a really good thing: human favored class bonus, spell lattices, and the Expanded Arcana feat are all mega-useful.  More spells known means more versatility; every expansion pushes you closer to Tier 1.  While a 20th level human psychic may not technically be Tier 1, they’ll know so many spells that they might as well be.

The psychic spell list is no slouch.  It draws from multiple sources, from core to the APG, ACG, UM, and others.  You’ll find druid spells, witch spells, inquisitor spells, and a few cleric spells.  The psychic also gets exclusive access to a number of unique spells, including the ‘undercast’ spells (psychic bloodline sorcerer excluded).  As mentioned previously, its main ‘weakness’ is a lack of flashy direct damage attack spells.  Mopping up crowds of mooks is not your job; leave that to the sorcerers and wizards.  You’ll be very strong at information gathering, mental and emotional manipulation, buffing, and debuffing.  

As a general rule of thumb, try not to double up on spells that are too similar to each other.  Try to have spells that target a variety of saving throws, and grab a few that have no save allowed or effects on a successful save.  While offense is important, don’t skimp on defense, and be sure to make room for a utility spell of every spell level you can cast if you can.  Your discipline and archetype will supplement your selection and often make certain playstyles easier.  Don’t forget you can swap out spells known starting at 4th level and every even level afterwards; this is a great opportunity to grab utility or niche lower level spells as your lower level offensive spells become obsolete.  If you like the undercast spells, grabbing the latest version of one allows you to instantly replace lower level versions, giving you some nice versatility.

0-Level Spells / Knacks (Known: 9-12*)

You don’t have to sweat knack selection too much; grab the essentials, but feel free to go for flavor.  It’s probably not worth wasting the human favored class bonus here; pick up extra hp or a few throwaway skill ranks.

  • daze - My favorite offensive knack at low levels, it quickly becomes obsolete.
  • detect magic
  • detect psychic significance - In an occult-heavy game, this knack can be flavorful and fun.  
  • ghost sound
  • grave words - Almost useless, but it has great creepy flavor.
  • lullaby - This is potent for a 0-level spell, and Dream discipline psychics absolutely need this.
  • stabilize - This is great to have in emergencies.  Who needs the Heal skill?
  • telekinetic projectile - You’re better off firing a crossbow, but it’s got fun flavor.

1st Level Spells (Known: 5-7*)

Remember: 1,000 gp nets you a 1st level spell lattice / page of spell knowledge; eventually you can ‘learn’ any of these spells for a pretty modest fee.

Standouts: charm person, enlarge person, heightened awareness, ill omen.

  • alarm
  • burst of adrenaline - This is actually a pretty cool spell.  It’s basically +4 to a Ref. or Fort. save, and has utility outside of combat.  The round of fatigue afterwards is a bit of a pain, so use judiciously.  
  • burst of insight -  While it’s fine out of combat, being dazed for a round afterward makes this unusable in a fight.  Best to skip this one.
  • cause fear
  • charge object -  Paizo really wants you to take this spell.  The casting time is obnoxious, though, and it doesn’t really do a whole lot.  You can basically use it to leave secret messages to other psychic characters, which is only useful in an intrigue-heavy campaign.  More likely, this is an NPC spell for GMs to leave clues for psychic characters to discover.  
  • charm animal - You can get speak with animals as a 2nd level spell; animals can be an unlikely source of information, and you’ll be glad to know this the first time you encounter a T-Rex.
  • charm person
  • color spray
  • decrepit disguise - another one of the ‘do stuff to items’ spells, this one might be useful for smuggling contraband.  Probably another NPC spell.
  • deja vu - No save, which is noteworthy.  It can throw a wrench in enemy tactics, but isn’t very useful against foes that repeat the same thing every round regardless, such as most melee fighting characters and monsters.  It’s a nifty spell to Quicken later on to prevent casters from escaping (or force them to flee, if they recognize the spell via Spellcraft and don’t want to risk a round of repetition).
  • detect thoughts - You get early access to this spell, and eventually get it for free.
  • endure elements - Get this in a wand.
  • enlarge person - Not for you: use it on your big dumb meatshield.  
  • feather fall - You’ll be glad to know it when you need it.
  • feather step - Eventually becomes obsolete, but at low levels difficult terrain is a massive pain.
  • forbid action - This is an interesting combo with deja vu.  Hit them with deja vu first, then try to forbid the action they have to repeat; they’ll become confused.
  • glue seal - This is the closest you get to grease.  It’s got a lot of utility in and out of combat.  This is also a rare Reflex save spell on your list.
  • heightened awareness - Great duration.  This spell is strictly better than anticipate peril.
  • Hermean potential (LoD) - Consider grabbing this once you’ve got some caster levels under your belt.  It’s a nice little defensive buff.
  • ill omen - A fine debuff that gets better with age.  Avoid using it on casters.  No save!
  • illusion of calm - As long as this spell is up, you don’t have to worry about casting defensively.
  • interrogation - You get detect thoughts as a bonus spell, so you don’t need this unless you want to be a bully.
  • invigorate - This is handy if you’ve got a barbarian in the party.
  • liberating command - This spell can get your friends out of a jam.
  • long arm - This is a must-have for psychics interested in using melee touch spells.
  • mage armor - You’re better off using actual armor.  Hooray psychic magic!
  • magic missile - One of your rare direct damage spells, this old staple couples well with certain metamagic feats such as Toppling Spell.  It’s a decent offensive backup spell.
  • mind thrust I - The mind thrust line eventually becomes pretty decent, but the lowest-powered version of it is pretty crappy.  At high levels when you don’t care about your 1st level slots, it’s an okay way to inflict some damage if you don’t feel like wasting higher level slots.
  • mindlink - This is an okay choice for a spy, but eventually you’ll have more telepathy than you’ll know what to do with, making this largely obsolete.  
  • moment of greatness
  • psychic reading - You need to make a skill check (which you can fail), and there’s still a chance the information you receive can be false.  Just stick with detect thoughts.
  • quintessence - Yet another fiddly-item-spell, I’m having trouble figuring out a good use for this one.  
  • remove fear - Just about all psychics should know this spell; it defends against one of the primary threats to your emotion components.
  • shield - Likely better than an actual shield for a good chunk of your career, and the protection from magic missiles is always handy.
  • silent image
  • sleep - This low-level nuke should be swapped out by around 4th level or so.
  • sow thought - For the more subtle enchanters out there.  *INCEPTION BWAAAM*
  • summon minor monster / summon monster I - Mostly useful for utility and trap-springing.  
  • telempathic projection - This is a nice spell for helping out the party face (which might even be you).  This is just a fun, versatile tool in social situations.
  • thought echo - This would be better if it had a longer duration.  As it is, it’s just too niche to be valuable.  
  • true strike - You don’t have a lot of spells that require attack rolls.  If you like the various bad-touch spells, though, this might be more valuable for clutch situations.
  • unprepared combatant - This helps the party blaster out, and it’s a good spell to get off before an ambush.
  • vanish

2nd Level Spells (Known: 5-7*)

This level is heavy with single target Will save-or-suck/lose spells.  You probably only need one of them; pick the one you like the best, if you are even desirous of such a spell.  A spell lattice / page of spell knowledge for this level is 4,000 gp, which isn’t too bad at higher levels.

Standouts: invisibility, mirror image, silence

  • acute senses - This is a great scouting spell.
  • anticipate thoughts - This spell is better in the hands of someone in the thick of the fighting.  Ideally you don’t want to be getting attacked in the first place.
  • apport object - The range limitations on this spell prevent it from being more than a parlor trick.  A decent NPC spell for GM trickery.
  • aversion - This spell doubles as a useful tool for skullduggery and an in-combat debuff / battlefield control.  Even if they make their save, the target is sickened; pick the spot where you’re fighting for best results.
  • bear’s endurance, bull’s strength, cat’s grace, eagle’s splendor, fox’s cunning, owl’s wisdom - Pick the version most useful to you and your party; they’re all useful spells, although they grow obsolete as better magical gear is acquired.
  • blindness/deafness - A solid Fort. save choice for this spell level.  Brutal duration.  You don’t get glitterdust, so this is a fine pick for blinding foes.
  • darkvision
  • demand offering - You don’t want to be right next to an enemy in a fight.  Perhaps this might have some use in a non-combat situation, but there are better options.
  • detect mindscape - In an occult-heavy game, this spell is decent insurance.  If you ever are suspicious that you’re in a fake reality, cast this spell to learn the truth!  If only Leo had had this at the end of Inception…
  • effortless armor - If you decide to use heavy armor, this spell can alleviate most of the trouble it causes you in situations where mobility is vital.
  • enshroud thoughts - This is as useful as a shorter duration, much more limited nondetection spell would be… which is not very.
  • euphoric cloud - Most of the useful fog spells aren’t on your class list, but you do get this one.  It’s got a short duration, but it’s basically a mild save-or-lose.  It targets Fort., and is SR: no, which is pretty rare for you.
  • false life - Great duration.
  • focused scrutiny - An interesting option for a party face or spy.
  • ghost whip - This spell is better off in somebody else’s hands.  You just won’t have the CMB to make this worthwhile.  Stick to magic missile for ghost fighting at these lower levels.
  • haunting mist - Another rare mist spell, by the RAW you yourself are subject to the WIS damage and fear effect, so I wouldn’t recommend it.
  • hideous laughter - A good choice for a single-target Will save-or-lose for this level.
  • hold person - Very similar to hideous laughter or id insinuation I.
  • hypercognition - Take investigative mind instead.
  • id insinuation I - Similar to hideous laughter or hold person, this spell locks an enemy down.  Its main advantage over the other two spells is that the target doesn’t get a new save every round, but you must concentrate on it.  For an undercast spell this is a decent use of a 2nd level slot.
  • implant false reading - The latest in the GM / NPC item-fiddly spells, this is for leaving red herrings and misleading ‘clues’ for the party’s investigations.  Not terribly useful for a PC to know in any but the most intrigue-laden occult games.
  • inflict pain - In the same tier as hold person etc., this spell is a save-or-suck rather than a save-or-lose, but still has an effect on a successful save.
  • instigate psychic duel - The psychic duel system is a bit clunky and potentially wasteful of your daily resources.  This does have some interesting potential use if you get creative with it.  Fighting someone in a public area unbeknownst to anyone else is certainly useful, but it can also be of use in a more traditional dungeon environment.  Your opponent is just as defenseless in the real world as you are; if you’re fighting a lone enemy, your allies can gang up on the nigh-helpless target while you just focus on defenses.  Most enemies will probably be too confused to understand their offensive options in a duel to begin with.
  • investigative mind - You probably won’t have much trouble making Knowledge DCs, but this spell is better than hypercognition for what it does.
  • invisibility
  • ironskin - Basically a lower-duration barkskin, if you’re desirous of boosting your AC this is a pretty good spell.
  • knock - Probably better to wand this once you’ve got the cash.
  • levitate - Flavorful, and good defense and utility.
  • mental barrier I - The first of the immediate action defensive undercast spells, they all suffer from a similar problem: casting defensive buffs in advance of a combat scenario is 90% of the time a better use of your resources.  They’re okay if you get ambushed a lot, but then you’ve probably got bigger problems.  This takes up the same slot as an extended shield, which will last much longer.  By the time you don’t care about your 2nd level slots as much, enemies probably won’t be throwing around mind thrust I and II anymore.
  • mental block - In the same tier as hold person and other single-target Will save spells.  This one sucks for the target if it gets through, basically reducing any PC to an NPC class or worse for the duration.
  • mind over matter (OO) - This spell is probably of most use for ‘healing’ purposes on yourself or your allies.  Potentially handy in certain situations, but I’d rather rely on a wand of lesser restoration.  Not a bad potion to have in a pinch, though.
  • mind thrust II - You’re dealing on average +1 damage per die.  I’d rather cast the 1st level version for the trouble…
  • mindshock (OO) - This spell isn’t for most psychics.  If you’re multiclassing in some sort of melee build or gestalt, this spell is merely okay.
  • minor image
  • mirror image - Arguably your best defensive spell for a long, long time.
  • object reading - Best leave this for the Occultist.  Fun for flavor, though.
  • oneiric horror - This spell has an edge on hideous laughter, hold person, and its cousins in that the target is fatigued afterward, basically a save-or-lose-then-suck.  
  • pain strike - Another decent choice for a Fort. save spell at this level, the main benefit is the sickened effect; everything else is gravy.  WARNING: Evil!
  • paranoia - Yet another Will save-or-suck/lose.  So many choices!  No additional saves to end this one, but it’s less useful against a singular target.
  • piercing shriek - Another Fort. save option, staggered is a crappy condition.
  • pilfering hand - Essentially a lower-powered, more limited telekinesis, this spell is great for snatching up the MacGuffin.
  • placebo effect - No emotional components!  This is a great ‘fake cleric’ spell.
  • psychic leech (OO) - Works on a failed save, which is nice.  Fatigued isn’t the worst condition, although it’s a pain for barbarians.  It’s better on a multiclass or gestalt build that can take advantage of the boost to physical stats.  No emotion components, interestingly.
  • psychonaut manifestation (OO) - Essentially a short duration random mutagen that punishes you for trying to change the bonus/penalty.  You don’t want this kind of randomness, even if it’s thematically appropriate for psychedlia discipline psychics.
  • pyrotechnics - A rare big-and-flashy spell, and a good one at that.  Potentially SR: no, and can target different saves.
  • resist energy - A bit boring, but this spell saves lives.
  • rope trick - Consider getting this on a spell lattice / page of spell knowledge.
  • scare
  • see invisibility - In the absence of glitterdust, this spell is even more vital.
  • shatter
  • silence - With no verbal components to worry about, this spell becomes a sick-nasty ace up your sleeve.  Shut down enemy divine and arcane casters, improve stealth, defend against sonic or language-dependent effects… This just might be your best 2nd level spell.
  • sonic scream - This odd spell is a rare Ref. save area-of-effect damage spell.  It’s not much better than burning hands, but it’s an option to be aware of.
  • speak with animals - Most of your telepathic abilities don’t work on animals, but this spell enables you to communicate with them; surprisingly useful if you think outside the box.
  • spectral hand - This is blue for psychics interested in using touch attack spells, of which there are several decent options.  It’s the safest way to deliver touch spells (barring metamagic or other means) for a good chunk of your career.  Everybody else can skip this.
  • spider climb - There are lots of scenarios where this is preferable to levitate.
  • spontaneous immolation - A rare elemental damage spell.  It’s not the greatest, but it might be handy to know at least one fire spell.
  • suggestion
  • summon monster II
  • thought shield I - The first iteration of this undercast spell is actually pretty handy to have as backup, especially at higher levels when your 2nd level slots are less valuable.  Mind-affecting things are bad news.
  • twilight haze - The duration is much worse, but this is another substitute for fog cloud and similar magic.

3rd Level Spells (Known: 4-6*)

Some great spells come online this level.  Most of your Will save-or-lose spells from the last level are still relevant, so feel free to branch out for utility, summoning, and battlefield control here.

Standouts: dispel magic, fly, gaseous form, haste, slow, summon monster III

  • analyze aura - Aura sight and arcane sight are the same level and last much longer without requiring concentration.  Detect thoughts probably reveals a target’s emotional state pretty well.
  • apport animal - You can’t use this on familiars… so what’s the point?
  • babble - Another Will save-or-lose, the target doesn’t get additional saves, which is nice, and nauseated is one of the worst conditions.  The additional fascinating effect is a double-edged sword, since it can affect your allies.  Use with care.
  • burst of speed - This is a handy panic button.
  • catatonia - Situations where this would be useful are exceedingly rare; this combines interestingly with shrink item, but it’s going to be hard to justify knowing this for most characters.
  • chain of perdition - This is decent battlefield control option.
  • cognitive block - There are much better ways to harass enemy casters than this.
  • deep slumber - Vital for Dream discipline psychics, okay for everybody else.
  • dispel magic - Even better for spontaneous casters to know.
  • displacement
  • dream shield (OO) - This spell is a bit high level for what it does, and is niche enough that I’d only suggest taking it as a bonus spell known from the human favored class bonus (or something similar).  In a very occult-y game, where the threat of dream manipulation is more pronounced, this is a useful form of defense.
  • ectoplasmic snare - Similar to chain of perdition, it inflicts more of a hassle but requires concentration and a ranged touch attack roll.
  • ego whip I - The first iteration of ego whip isn’t really worth the slot.
  • eldritch fever -  As an anti-caster debuff, this spell is much better than ego whip at this level.  Targets Fort., and makes the caster sickened, which is the primary benefit.  Warning: Evil!
  • emotive block - Share the burden of emotion components with this little spell!  Best combined with another teammate with a wicked Intimidate modifier, this can cause no end of trouble for enemy spell casters.  Also great if you like fear or emotion-based spells yourself.
  • excruciating deformation - Another Fort. save spell, it ‘sticks’ regardless of a successful initial save, and is a pretty awful debuff.  Warning: Evil!
  • fly
  • gaseous form - Already a pretty good spell, it’s even better for a psychic spellcaster!  With only mental components to worry about, you can cast spells with impunity while gaseous.  It has great duration, and lots of utility and defensive benefits; however, you’re slowed down considerably.
  • guiding star - Creative use of this spell is really handy, especially when you get access to teleport.
  • haste
  • heroism
  • id insinuation II - This is a nice step up; lock two targets down.
  • implanted projection (OO) - This is a sort of early-access implanted suggestion with less control over the outcome.  This is probably only useful for pranking or high-concept intrigue.
  • jealous rage (OO) - This is probably only worth using in combat, but your enemies need to buff, heal, or do some other action that benefits their side in order to trigger this.  Why not just use a spell to kill or directly impede your enemies, rather than wait for them to use tactics you have no control over?
  • loathsome veil - Similar to deep slumber, this is a nice, big, area of effect spell that can hamper multiple targets.
  • major image
  • mental barrier II - I still don’t think this is worth a 3rd level slot.  No duration increase?  Harsh.
  • mind thrust III - For single target damage, this line is finally starting to get okay.  You still have better options, but at higher levels when you can afford to blow 3rd level slots, this is okay to undercast.
  • mindscape door - Mindscapes are bad news.  In an occult game featuring lots of psychic magic being thrown around, there’s a good chance you might end up in an imaginary location of an enemy’s devising, whether it be a psychic duel or something more insidious.  When that happens, this spell is one of the only ways out.  You can also use it to rescue your friends.  Basically, the closer your game resembles Inception, the more valuable this spell is.  
  • node of blasting - This spell is a bit too niche for most spontaneous casters to take, but it certainly can be of some use to the creative; just read Order of the Stick and see what V gets up to with explosive runes.
  • object possession - What do you plan to do with your animated chair that must remain within close range of your helpless body?
  • pellet blast - A rare AoE Ref. save direct damage spell.  Too bad it’s horrible.
  • purge spirit - I’m a bit unclear on this spell.  It looks like RAI it’s only supposed to work on ghosts, mediums, etc.  If so, it’s too specific.  If not, it’s comparable to mind thrust at this level.
  • quell energy (OO) - You know what’s one level lower, doesn’t require your enemy to fail a save, and lasts 100 times longer?  Resist energy.  
  • sensory amplifier (OO) - This is a risky, bizarrely specific short duration buff, and a crappy debuff rolled into one, that affects enemies and allies.  I can’t think of a use for this.
  • shadow enchantment (OR) - There aren’t a whole lot of enchantment spells on the sorcerer/wizard list that aren’t already on the psychic list, but this spell does afford you lots of flexibility in a single spell known.  Unless your illusion DCs are really pumped up, though, you’re doubling the enemy’s chance of negating the spell (or at least reducing its effect).  This is also a risky spell if your game features lots of enemy spellcasters throwing around Spellcraft checks to ID spells as they’re cast.
  • share senses - I just wanted to point out it’s weird psychics get this spell on their list, since they can’t get a familiar outside of feats.  Note you could also learn witness at this level instead.
  • shrink item - If you can manage to fit it in, you can get up to some shenanigans with this utility spell.
  • slow - This is your best offensive spell this level.
  • speak with dead - Great for Lore discipline psychics.
  • summon monster III - Summon spells are finally starting to get really good.  Lots of great options this level.
  • synaptic pulse - This is dangerous to use if you’re amongst your allies.  It’s an okay panic button to buy you time to escape, but it’s difficult to use offensively.
  • synesthesia - This isn’t enough of an upgrade to your other Will save-or-lose spells to make it really stand out.  Functional, but you can skip it.
  • telekinetic maneuver - Early access.  Chain of perdition doesn’t require concentration if you’re into combat maneuver harassment.  
  • thought shield II - A bit pricey for a 3rd level spell, but +6 to Will saves is still pretty solid, and the undercast version is still very relevant.  
  • vampiric touch - A staple for the touch spell enthusiast; everyone else can skip this.
  • wall of nausea - Some fine battlefield control for this level.

4th Level Spells (Known: 4-6*)

There’s some downright fantastic stuff for you this level.  Time to get some crowd control!

Standouts: condensed ether, confusion, dimension door, greater invisibility

  • aura alteration - This is basically a long duration undetectable alignment and magic aura spell wrapped into one.  Useful for spies or intrigue heavy games.
  • aura of doom - Awesome duration.  Foes have to be a bit close for comfort, but it’s great for fear-stacking.
  • black tentacles
  • calcific touch - Amazing spell for bad-touch psychics, this thing’s a dragon-killer.  Compare to fleshworm infestation.
  • charm monster
  • condensed ether - SR: no and no save, this is the occult answer to solid fog.  Absolutely lovely battlefield control.
  • confusion - This spell is absolutely terrifying.
  • create mindscape - Mindscapes haven’t become truly scary just yet, but this spell is a potent tool for the creative.  It’s best used on non-casters: distracting guards, isolating lone enemies, or even just holding private conversations.  You may need your companions to lug your useless body around in the meantime, however.  Note that you don’t need line of sight or effect.
  • crushing despair - I prefer fear at this level, but this is still a good spell for softening enemies up.
  • detect scrying - Worth swapping out for at later levels.
  • dimension door
  • dimensional anchor - Another later-game swap out; it’s hard to fit in, but you’ll be glad you know it when you need it.
  • ego whip II - Compare to confusion.  This can’t compete (nor will it ever).
  • enchantment foil - Defense and trickery wrapped up in a nice, long-duration package.
  • fear
  • fool’s teleport (OO) - This is a fun little spell, but niche.  If you’d actually like to escape, you’re better off teleporting.  Ideally this spell puts your enemies off their guard, allowing you to eavesdrop or attack with surprise to finish them off.  
  • freedom of movement - A life saver.  If you can get a ring of this at later levels, feel free to swap it out unless you’re buffing the party as well.
  • ghost wolf - You don’t get phantom steed, which is a great transportation spell, so this is a possible replacement with some in-combat use as well.  You’re getting close to teleport though.
  • id insinuation III - You had a good run, but confusion makes the final forms of id insinuation basically obsolete.  Confusion has better range, can affect numerous targets, and doesn’t require concentration.  Even compared to moonstruck, this is largely inferior.
  • intellect fortress I - Too narrow, too short, too costly.
  • greater invisibility
  • mental barrier III - A 4th level slot for a really good chance of negating a hit?  Possibly worth it in clutch situations.
  • mind probe - The chance for the target to deceive you makes this unattractive; you can accomplish most of this with detect thoughts and guiding questions.
  • mind thrust IV - Getting hit with this sucks, certainly.  But it’s in the shadow of confusion and black tentacles at this level.
  • mindwipe - It’s no enervation, but negative levels are a pain.
  • greater oneiric horror - This spell hasn’t aged particularly well.  There are better options.
  • out of sight (OO) - For most stealth tasks, invisibility will serve you better.  This is an interesting option combined with a disguise, though; you could interact with guards or other people, then have a decent chance of them forgetting the whole interaction.  A fun bonus spell to pick up for spies or sneaks.
  • phantasmal killer - This is your first true save-or-die.  It’s hard to get through, but if you’ve got your DCs pumped this can certainly be unpleasant.  Best combined with other debuffs as a finisher.
  • planar adaptation - Early access, for some reason.
  • lesser planar ally -  It’s worth noting that psychics get access to both the planar ally line and the planar binding line.  Neither of these spells are especially attractive to a spontaneous caster with limited spells known, but if you are interested in calling outsiders (it’s certainly a potent tool!), the planar ally line is safer.  Yes, it costs money, but you don’t get native access to the magic circle spells, which makes planar binding a huge liability.
  • rainbow pattern - There are stronger AoE spells this level, but this is worth noting if you’ve invested in Spell Focus (illusion).
  • resilient sphere - Hey, a Ref. save spell!  It’s okay!
  • riding possession - Things are getting spooky.  Like all possession spells, you need to work carefully with an ally to safeguard your body.  Otherwise, this is great for spying, scouting, and messing with people.  If you’re just interested in scouting, arcane eye is probably safer and gives you more control.
  • scrying
  • sending
  • lesser simulacrum - You can get up to some terrifying things with this spell.  It’s hard to justify taking as a spontaneous caster, but it’s the closest you’ll get to animate dead for a long time.  You have enough enchantment abilities to exercise pretty fine control over it, and it’s considerably cheaper than its more powerful version.  Consider taking as a human favored class bonus spell if you’ve got a slower campaign pacing with plenty of downtime, otherwise skip it.
  • spell immunity - A strong defensive buff for the informed adventurer.  Good choices at this level include confusion, enervation, and charm monster.  Less useful if you’re not regularly going up against spell casters.
  • stoneskin - It’s a bit expensive, but this buff saves lives.  You might want to wait until the communal version next level, as it’s a better deal.
  • summon monster IV
  • synaptic scramble - Inferior to confusion.
  • telekinesis - Nice and versatile, and pretty iconic for a psychic.  Early access!
  • telekinetic charge - A bit expensive for a 4th level slot, but your heavily buffed meatshield friend is the worst kind of projectile.
  • telepathic bond - Eventually you’ll have native telepathy, but this is nice for long-distance communication.
  • thought shield III - This is almost like an early-access mindblank.  Pricey, but if you blow a Will save with this you’ve probably rolled a natural 1.
  • thoughtsense - If this had a better duration, I’d rate it higher.  Still handy for scouting or preventing an ambush.
  • triggered suggestion - Flavorful and fun, but probably beyond the scope of most games.
  • unbearable brightness - This is kind of a crappy spell, but I just wanted to point out that you don’t have a lot of ways to deal with magical darkness.  This spell, and eyes of the void are two options, both 4th level spells for you.  Otherwise, you might have to resort to dispelling.
  • wall of blindness/deafness - Another decent Fort. save option this level, with some battlefield control.

5th Level Spells (Known: 4-6*)

Some real heavy hitters come online this level.  You come into your own as a battlefield controller with the darling etheric shards.  You also gain access to some of your most potent compulsion and possession spells.

Noteworthy: dominate person, etheric shards, possession, teleport.

  • dismissal
  • dominate person
  • dream scan - It’s a cool spell, but the potential to be deceived makes it less attractive than similar divinations.
  • echolocation - Awesome duration, but shorter range than thoughtsense and doesn’t function in silence.
  • ectoplasmic hand (OO) - This spell is a tad high level for what it does, but it is still an upgrade of spectral hand for touch spell enthusiasts.  Metamagic should be a reliable option touch spells delivery at this point, but this is a nice choice for your highest level touch spells for several levels.
  • ego whip III - This is starting to get okay as a single-target debuff.  A -3 to Will saves and 1d6 rounds of staggering is unpleasant, certainly.  But compare it to dominate person or feeblemind.  This just doesn’t have the weight of a proper 5th level spell.
  • entrap spirit - A bit too niche for a 5th level spell known, unless you’re playing the Pathfinder equivalent of Ghostbusters.
  • erase impressions - This is a GM spell.
  • ethereal envelope - Inferior to rope trick in pretty much every way.
  • etheric shards - Finally, OA pulls through!  This spell is brutal.  SR: no, preposterous duration and (shapeable!) area, and combos well with illusions like hallucinatory terrain or mirage arcana.  The damage is secondary to the amazing battlefield control this provides you.  Make your own invisible maze of death!
  • explode head - Comparable to mind thrust V, it targets Fort. and isn’t mind-affecting.  Flavorful and fun, but there are better options.
  • fabricate
  • feeblemind
  • hold monster
  • id insinuation IV
  • intellect fortress II
  • life bubble - Even better in an aquatic, spacefaring, or wacky plane-hopping game, of which many eventually turn into around this level.
  • mass charm person (UI)
  • mass suggestion - early access
  • mental barrier IV - Stick with mental barrier III
  • mind fog - Considering how many Will save spells you might accumulate, this might very well be worth picking up.  Try layering it over etheric shards or condensed ether.
  • mind swap - You learn possession at this same level; why offer them the keys to your meat prison?
  • mind thrust V - While still serviceable, psychic crush is the new hotness this level.
  • mirage arcana
  • modify memory - Flavorful, but pales in comparison to dominate person and possession.
  • object possession - Why not just pick possession?  You can do better than a CR 5 animated object at this level.
  • overland flight
  • phantasmal web - This is a fun offensive illusion.  Beware: true seeing is likely starting to rear its ugly head.
  • plane shift - Hopefully this can be somebody else’s job, but if not, this is handy to have.
  • possession - It takes some planning and prep, but this is a really open-ended spell useful for all sorts of things.  As long as you’ve got friends guarding your useless corpse, go nuts.  It’s not mind-affecting, so you can possess undead, plants, oozes, you name it.
  • psychic asylum - This is a cool spell, but not strictly necessary.  
  • psychic crush I - The big kid of the offensive undercast school, it is a partial-save fiesta.  Think of it as a less all-or-nothing phantasmal killer.
  • remote viewing - For all its flavor, it doesn’t provide you with information you can act on with any certainty.
  • retrocognition - This is better than remote viewing, but it’s got stiff competition this level.
  • spell resistance
  • suffocation - Very comparable to psychic crush at this level.
  • summon monster V
  • synapse overload - A fun option for touch-spell enthusiasts.
  • greater synaptic pulse - The duration increase is an improvement, but this spell is still best as a panic button to cover an escape.
  • telepathy - You eventually get this for free, permanently.  Hooray!
  • teleport
  • thought shield IV - Stick with thought shield III, unless you’re scared of psychic crush.
  • tower of iron will I - The ‘final’ defensive undercast spell series is pretty unimpressive.  Compare to spell resistance: better duration, less restrictive.
  • true seeing
  • wall of ectoplasm - Your first true ‘solid’ wall spell, this is perfectly functional, but pales in comparison to etheric shards.
  • wall of force - A little bit more restrictive than wall of ectoplasm and shorter duration, but much harder to break through.
  • wall of sound - A combination of wall of fire and wind wall, this is another useful battlefield control spell.

6th Level Spells (Known: 3-5*)

Your list of options begins to shrink; there’s not as many must-have spells this level.  Indeed, you might be tempted to just grab some more 5th level spells.  

Noteworthy: greater create mindscape, greater dispel magic, getaway

  • blade barrier - wall of sound sure had a short run, didn’t it?
  • cloak of dreams - This is the final important offensive tool for dream discipline psychics.  The downside is it requires you to get uncomfortably close to your target; most other psychics should skip this.
  • contingency - This is less attractive for a spontaneous caster, but it’s still an incredible defensive tool.
  • death pact (OO) - This takes far longer to cast, and only lasts a fraction of the time that dominate person does.  While you can (attempt) to kill your thrall via this spell, there aren’t a whole lot of scenarios I can think of where that would be especially valuable.  Dominated targets are often disposable; this would only be useful if you wanted someone dead, but wanted to control some of their actions before they died.  Probably only of use for a GM, or in an intrigue laden setting, but this is generally inferior to regular dominate person.
  • greater create mindscape - This terrifying spell is extremely versatile.  It has all the utility of create mindscape with a better duration and the potential to target multiple creatures, including your allies.  You can enhance the favored spells of yourself and your party, or you can simply create a dead magic zone and hide behind your meatshield friend of choice as they beat up a defenseless squishy caster.  It’s best to explain how this spell works to your allies and train with them; alert them telepathically before you cast the spell so they can willingly fail their saves.  If you get this to work against a lone foe, you basically shunt them into a private world where you make the rules and the deck is heavily stacked against them.
  • major curse - You don’t gain access to bestow curse, but you do get this more powerful version of it.  It’s okay for a Fort. save spell at this level.
  • disintegrate - A rare ranged touch attack spell, it’s also one of the best.  Utility and horrific damage potential on a Fort. save.

Appendix: Miscellaneous Esoterica

Oddities of Psychic Magic

As the new ‘third’ type of magic, psychic magic has some strange eccentricities.  Barring further FAQ’s and errata, some of these things might have to be adjudicated by GMs on the fly.  

Emotion and Thought Components:

        Instead of somatic components, psychic spells have emotion components, and instead of verbal components, they have thought components.

PROS:  You can cast in armor unimpeded, with your hands full, or even while paralyzed or bound.  It’s silent, stealthy, and subtle.  

CONS:  Thought components increase any concentration DCs by +10!  This can be negated by taking a move action to ‘center’ yourself, so thought components are not particularly difficult to overcome.  You can’t use emotion components if you are under the effect of a negative emotional status, including any kind of fear.  This means as soon as you are shaken, you can’t cast a large majority of spells.

Emotion components are thus the one you have to worry about the most.  Occult games seem to be on the creepy side, so fear is going to be a constant threat.  Worse, the simple Intimidate skill can shut you down, and there’s very little you can do about it.  Thus, it pays to be prepared:

  • potions of remove fear - 50 gp a pop, suppresses fear for 10 mins., more than enough time to resolve a problem.
  • Logical Spell - This +1 metamagic feat removes an emotion component.  Full-round action to cast, but as soon as you have 2nd level slots you can fire off a logical remove fear and be on your way.  
  • Magical Lineage trait + Logical Spell + remove fear - This combo is available at level 1, and is a surefire way to overcome fear.  That’s a feat, trait, and spell known tax if there ever was one.
  • greater heroism - A late-career spell that provides blanket immunity to fear.
  • Divine caster allies - remove fear can be someone else’s job.  Paladin’s radiate an aura of courage that can help you avoid spooks, and their mercies can cure you with ease.  
  • Discipline powers - The abomination, pain, and tranquility disciplines come with fear immunity (or in pain’s case, paladin mercies).
  • wands of remove fear - Nothing’s stopping you from using spell-trigger items while shaken, and wands are cheaper than potions in the long run.
  • contingency - A contingent remove fear or greater heroism when you are subject to a fear effect is good insurance.
  • Logical metamagic rod (OA) - If you don’t want to blow a feat, you can burn gold instead.  Certainly handy rods to have when the chips are down.
  • ring of delayed doom (UE) - Expensive, but buys you precious time.
  • rod of steadfast resolve (UE) - In the absence of a paladin, this is a passable substitute, and applies to emotion-effects as well as fear.
  • rod of absorption - A late game acquisition, this item is a potent defensive and offensive tool.
  • cap of the free thinker (UE) - Roll twice on all mind-affecting Will saves.  Not unreasonably priced, either.
  • headband of unshakeable resolve (UE) - Another nice fear defense, this takes up the headband slot and thus is competing for your headband of vast intelligence / mental prowess.  Only take it if you can add ability enhancing effects to it using Unchained variant rules or something similar.

Basically, a psychic caster has to worry about negative emotions in much the same way that a regular caster has to worry about things like grappling, etc.  It’s not an impossible obstacle, but it’s the price you pay for the other advantages of psychic magic.  Intimidate tactics are perhaps the most difficult thing to defend against, and characters up against psychics are well advised to try to use the Intimidate skill.  Fortunately, barring experienced anti-psychic hunters, Intimidate tactics seem relatively rare in most pre-made adventures.  

Expensive Material Components

Psychic spells don’t have material components, except for expensive ones.  Interestingly, you can substitute items of equal or greater worth as long as they have some sort of personal significance to your character.  This can let you potentially liquidate treasure in a pinch, especially if obtaining an item is associated with a struggle or challenge.

Mythic Adventures and Psychic Magic

Fun fact: currently by the RAW, psychics can’t benefit from many options in Mythic Adventures.  The Archmage arcana and Hierophant’s divine surge specifically only work with arcane or divine spells.  Mythic spells still work the same, but you’ll lose access to several of the main caster archetypes’ powers as a psychic caster.  Hopefully there will be a psychic-friendly mythic archetype in an expansion soon.  In the meantime, you can probably get by with a Trickster or Marshal archetype and substitute several archetype abilities with mythic feats.

Words of Power and Psychic Magic

There is no list of effect words for psychic magic, and it is unlikely there ever will be.  As something of a scholar on words of power, I may devise my own suggested list of spells; that said, words of power is best in the hands of a blaster such as a sorcerer, so it’s probably best avoided regardless.

Occult Skill Unlocks

Any occult character can use occult skill unlocks with select skills.  These are largely flavorful, adding to the creepy atmosphere of an occult game.  Unless otherwise noted, you can attempt an occult skill unlock once per day.

Automatic Writing (Linguistics)

Once per week, you can attempt a Linguistics check to scribble strange words that may have significance pertaining to your future.  For half of your career, this equates to a 1/week augury spell; from level 10 onward, it’s a 1/week divination.  Pretty handy if you’ve got the time, and you don’t really lose much by trying and failing.  Lore discipline psychics will be better at this than everyone else.

Dowsing (Survival)

Psychics are unlikely to use this unlock, since Survival isn’t a class skill.  This is basically a 1/day locate object spell with more restrictions on what it points you towards.  

Faith Healing (Heal)

The main problem with faith healing is that it is very time consuming, and usually only suppresses afflictions.  Heal isn’t a psychic class skill, so this is unlikely to see use from them.

Hypnotism (Diplomacy)

While this is largely the Mesmerist’s schtick, you can still do well at hypnosis.  The DCs are pretty high, but implanting suggestions is a cool trick.  Recalling forgotten knowledge is a little more niche, but still potentially handy in a puzzle scenario.

Phrenology (Knowledge [Arcana])

A psychic is likely to be pretty good at this.  Spells can tell you most of the information available, but the higher DC checks can potentially reveal interesting or useful facts, especially to a necromancer (how many HD did this dead dragon have?).  

Prognostication (Sense Motive)

Old-timey fortune telling essentially.  The high DC checks can provide information similar to automatic writing pertaining to a specific creature, but magic provides most of the other information more quickly and efficiently.

Psychometry (Appraise)

Reading objects has a much lower DC than most other occult skill unlocks, which makes it accessible even to those without Appraise as a class skill (such as psychics).  However, there is the chance of being deceived, not only from a failed skill check but also by the various ‘fiddly item spells’ in Occult Adventures, so take whatever knowledge you gain from psychometry with a grain of salt.

Read Aura (Perception)

Of all the occult skill unlocks, this one might be the least useful.  The main problem is that it takes 10 minutes of concentration, so there’s no guarantee the subject won’t wander away.  Very basic spells reveal most information this can provide for you.

Psychic Duels

Two minds grappling for dominance using pure thought and imagination as weapons… The concept of the psychic duel is a staple of fantastic psychic power.  Occult Adventures introduced the psychic duel subsystem, perhaps as an homage to the attack and defense modes of old.  You will only see these rules pop up in two situations: when somebody successfully uses the instigate psychic duel spell, or when a high level psychic duelist (psychic archetype) uses the Thought Made Real class feature.

In a psychic duel, movement and positioning doesn’t matter, and most skills also don’t matter; you can’t hide from your opponent on a binary mindscape.  There are only six ways to exit a psychic duel:

  1. The instigate psychic duel spell’s duration expires.  This will probably never happen, as it lasts minutes/level.
  2. Both duelists agree to end the duel as a free action.
  3. A duelist is dropped below 0 hp.
  4. A duelist successfully casts mindscape door.
  5. The original caster of instigate psychic duel successfully leaves his own mindscape (which the opponent can try to prevent by forcing the caster to make and fail a Will save).
  6. A non-participant takes a standard action to rouse a duelist out of the ‘dueling torpor.’  This gives a duelist another save against instigate psychic duel.

During the duel, both participants are unable to act with their physical bodies.  They are not helpless, but they lose their DEX bonus to AC and can take no actions.  This is why, in a dungeon environment, you never want to instigate a duel if there are multiple enemies around, especially if you are alone.

During the duel, you have a number of options available to you.

  1. Full-round action: create an offensive manifestation (direct attack).
  2. Swift or immediate action: create a defensive manifestation (ready defense or respond to an attack).
  3. 1 full round: create a thought-form creature (generate a proxy creature to fight for you, similar to a summon).
  4. Full-round action: direct a thought-form creature.
  5. Full-round action: cast mindscape door.
  6. Full-round action(?), caster of instigate psychic duel only: attempt to escape your mindscape.  The wording is a bit unclear what sort of action this is, since instigate psychic duel isn’t technically dismissable.
  7. Free action: agree to end the duel.

A duelist retains their AC, touch AC, saving throws, highest melee and ranged attack bonuses, current hp, and ability scores.  No other special offensive or defensive abilities can be used, except for fast healing and regeneration.  Healing and damage your body receives in the real world applies to a duelist, and vice versa.

Creating and Spending Manifestation Points

Actions in a psychic duel are governed by the generation and expenditure of manifestation points, or MP.  You can only spend a number of MP on a single manifestation up to your highest spell level, or ¼ your HD for non-casters.  

There are basically two ways to create MP: spend class resources (spell slots, grit points, etc.), or withdraw from a pool calculated by your HD and mental ability scores.  Spending class resources is often inefficient, but it’s the safest way to generate MP.  Drawing from your ability score/HD pool has long-lasting repercussions after the duel.  If you take any points from the HD/ability score pool, you’re fatigued for 24 hours.  Spend over half, and you’re exhausted.  Spend them all and you fall unconscious.  There is no way to recover from this aside from miracle or wish.

Your enemies can afford to use their HD/ability score pool more than you: they’re likely in a life-or-death situation.  You will have to deal with the 24 hour fatigue or exhaustion for the rest of the day, meaning you’re almost always better off using spell slots.

Because of this, there is a pecking order of threats for most psychic duels:

  1. Full casters (1-9 spell levels):  Full casters have the most spell slots to burn, and will have an edge on max MP they can spend.
  2. ⅔ casters (1-6 spell levels):  They don’t have the endurance of full casters, but they’ll have an MP expenditure advantage on equal HD non-casters.
  3. ½ casters (1-4 spell levels):  Characters like rangers, paladins, and bloodragers will have an edge on non-casters until level 20.
  4. Non-casters:  Everyone else will have a limited max MP expenditure cap, and often no choice but to use their HD/ability score MP pool, risking fatigue or worse after the duel.

There are two categories of creatures that break this rough hierarchy:

  1. Creatures with lots of spell-like abilities:  Powerful outsiders, fey, dragons, and other magical creatures can use their SLA’s to create MP, giving them better endurance and options than non-magical creatures and casters.
  2. Ultra high HD creatures: The oldest dragons, kaiju, Cthulhu, etc., have far more than 20 HD.  Cthulhu has 36 HD, for instance, giving him the same MP cap as 9th level spells, and it will take him a while to burn through his MP.

It’s safest to duel non-casters, growing increasingly risky as you go up the chain.  Big scary monsters and outsiders are something of a wildcard, and quite risky to engage.

As you can see, fighting a psychic duel quickly becomes quite costly.  You can generate MP from multiple sources, but after a few rounds you’ll likely be tapped of limited class features like phrenic pool, arcane pool, ki points, etc.  The key to winning a psychic duel is thus to manage your MP.

Thought-Form Creatures:  Creating a thought-form creature is the best use of your MP for offense and defense.  You spend a certain amount of MP creating it, and afterwards you can keep attacking with it without spending more MP.  The more MP you spend improving your creature, the more damage you risk taking should it be destroyed, but in the meantime your creature tanks enemy thought-form creatures automatically.  Your priorities for improving your thought-form creature are as follows:

  1. Creature defenses:  You want your creature to tank for you.  It needs to be the wall upon which your opponent’s attacks break, thus causing him to waste MP.  This buys your allies more time to take out a target in the real world if you aren’t alone.  Increase AC and HP first, then consider boosting saving throws.
  2. Protective Ability:  for a modest cost, your creature improves your defenses against offensive manifestations.  It’s yet another incentive to attack your creature instead of you.  This forces your opponent to either waste MP with direct attacks, or create a creature of their own.
  3. Extra Attacks:  Every time your creature attacks saves you MP and potentially forces your opponent to waste MP on defenses.  It is the most efficient use of MP offensively (especially for arcane, divine, and non-casters), particularly if you’re trying to win the duel fair and square.

Offensive Manifestations:  Directly attacking your foe or any thought-form creatures is extremely costly.  For psychic casters, it can possibly be worth it in an attempt to knock-out a weakened opponent quickly.  Arcane casters can try it in desperation, but divine and non-casters have a horrid cost-to-damage ratio (d4 damage instead of d8 or d6 respectively).  Attempting to inflict negative statuses is quite costly, and most only last 1 round.  The entangled and shaken conditions are not too expensive and last longer than 1 round, making them potentially worth it.  If you spend any MP on negative statuses or anything other than a flat attack roll delivery (touch attack, saving throw, etc.), your attack will start to fall behind on maximum damage a great deal.  Thought-form creatures are always more efficient!

Defensive Manifestations:  Though likely cheaper than offensive manifestations overall, using a defensive manifestation every round quickly grows costly.  Never use disruptive counter if you have a thought-form creature out (which you should), since it dismisses your creature.  Psychic redirect is often your best bet; shunt attacks onto your creature and let it tank for you.  The rest of the defenses should be used on a case-by-case basis depending on how the duel is going.  Ideally your creature absorbs all the punishment, saving you MP.

How to win a psychic duel:  If you’re dueling a weak opponent with low hp, you might be able to overwhelm him quickly with an all-out attack.  In most situations, though, you want to do two things: waste your opponent’s resources, and waste your opponent’s time.

Spend the first round of every duel building the tankiest, turtle-iest thought-form creature you can.  Then sit behind it and wait.  Your opponent will either waste MP trying to directly attack you (which you can re-direct to your creature), or make a creature of their own.  The duel then becomes a Pokemon match.  Every round, your creature gets to attack, but this is secondary to weathering your opponent’s attacks.

If your goal is to win an actual duel, it becomes a battle of attrition.  Once your opponent is low on MP sources, you’ll have them at your mercy.  

If you’ve trapped your opponent in a battle with allies in the real world, your objective is a holding action.  Keep your opponent in the duel, weather their attacks, and buy your allies time to beat their useless meat-body up in the real world.

Familiars, Possession, Mindscapes, and You

Familiars are useful for all sorts of reasons, and it isn’t too hard for a psychic to acquire one (feats, VMC, etc.).  The various possession spells can be quite useful, as can instigate psychic duel and create mindscape.  These spells leave your physical body quite vulnerable, so it’s best to leave your meat-sack in the care of your allies.  Should you find yourself alone, however, your familiar can serve as your best defense.  Through your familiar’s empathic link, you’ll have a good warning system should your body become endangered, allowing you to end a possession or similar spell and get back to your body.

Multiclassing

As a full caster, it’s generally advised to stick with your class and focus on gaining more magical power.  However, there are a few considerations for the curious:

  • Paladin dip:  A more martial psychic would benefit from a 3 level foray into paladin.  You gain heavy armor proficiency, CHA to saves, and most importantly immunity to fear.  Couples well with the faith discipline thematically.
  • Loremaster:  This prestige class doesn’t specify arcane or divine magic, you just need to know seven divinations.  A lore discipline psychic could have fun here.  You don’t lose a whole lot from this PRC in the grand scheme of things, especially if the game isn’t likely to reach level 20.  You’ll possibly miss major phrenic amps and your final discipline ability in exchange for some defensive boosts, a bonus feat, or extra bonus spells.

Half-elves are good here, as is the Magical Knack trait.  I’ll list more potential combos as I learn more.

Variant Multiclassing

Introduced in Pathfinder Unchained, the VMC system is potentially more attractive to psychics; you don’t lose out on spellcasting and class features, but you pay the cost of half (!) your feats.  Note that you lose your 19th level feat, locking you out of Expanded Arcana for another 9th level spell.

VMC Alchemist

Thematically appropriate for a psychedelia discipline psychic, the addition of bombs is a nice offensive option for the lacking-in-explosions psychic.  The 15th level poison use abilities are something of a waste unfortunately.

VMC Oracle

Curses can give you access to bonus spells or other benefits at the cost of an inconvenience of some kind.  Note you can nab heavy armor proficiency from the Battle mystery, healing from the Life mystery…  There’s lots of goodies here.  Combos well with the faith discipline.

VMC Sorcerer

Consider the Arcane bloodline.  You get a familiar (or bonded item), the metamagic adept ability, a bonus spell known, a bonus feat, and +2 to the save DCs of a single school of magic.  You come out about even if you had spent the feats on similar abilities, but you don’t have to worry about CHA for the Arcane Heritage feat line.

The various other bloodlines can give you some pretty decent abilities, and there’s usually nice synergy with both classes being spontaneous casters.

VMC Witch

The main draw here is access to hexes, and you can get a familiar as well, so there’s lots of fun options.  Even with the restrictions, the slumber hex is a great help to dream discipline psychics.  Extra debuffs, healing… this is a pretty good choice.

VMC Wizard

Another strong choice.  Easy access to a familiar, and many of the school powers are quite useful.  Divination and Illusion are great, as is Enchantment for a more social psychic.  There are several useful arcane discoveries as well, such as Infectious Charms or Time Stutter.  

Psychic Bloodline Sorcerer

This new sorcerer bloodline is worth mentioning.  The bloodline arcana makes your sorcerer spells count as psychic magic, so you lose traditional spell components in exchange for emotion and thought components.  Psychic fireballs!  Psychic chain lightning!  This is a pretty cool arcana.

Your bonus spell list includes nearly every undercast spell, which you eventually automatically ‘update’ for the latest version; technically a psychic sorcerer has more higher level spells known than his peers after level 9.  However, the usefulness of these spells rarely rises above a black rating in my opinion, so keep that in mind.

Psychic Detective

This investigator archetype swaps out alchemy for psychic casting, drawing from the psychic spell list up to level 6 spells.  If you’re interested in this archetype, you can use the spell ratings above to help guide your selection.  This is a pretty neat option for investigators; see if you can convince your GM to let it stack with Empiricist!

Psi-Tech Discoveries

Introduced in Occult Realms, psi-tech discoveries marry psychic magic with advanced technology.  If you’re introducing wacky aliens and robots into your game, why not give them psychic powers instead of regular magic?  

There’s nothing stopping a psychic from learning psi-tech discoveries; you don’t need to be a special race, or have a specific discipline or archetype; they’re up for grabs!  Maybe check with your GM first, if they’re too futuristic.  Really, barring a few exceptions, several of them could just be re-fluffed as new forms of psychic power.

Importantly, a psychic can learn a psi-tech discovery in place of a phrenic amplification or a feat.  So if you’re running out of feats to take, maybe learn how to shoot lasers?

Most of these are only worth taking if your campaign involves technological equipment, otherwise they’re dead weight.

Artificial Ascension:  Error: 404, psi-tech discovery not found.

Err… okay, you can only learn this discovery at level 20.  You don’t get a new phrenic amp at level 20, nor do you gain a new feat at level 20… so how are you supposed to learn this?  Epic levels?

Even if this wasn’t impossible to take, it’s… extremely campaign dependent.  You need robots, a specific subset of construct, in order to download your brain.  And if your new robot body is destroyed… you’re screwed.  Hope you like not having a CON score!  You become an AI with this ability, and can keep stealing bigger and better robot bodies, but… yeah.  Moving on.

Disease Transference:  What’s futuristic about diseases?  I don’t get it.  This ability is terrible, by the way.  You can remove a disease from someone, and transfer it to yourself or another person you’re touching.  However, you need to succeed at a caster level check to do so, equal to the original disease save DC +10!  Yikes!  You won’t stand a chance of doing this with any consistency until you’re well into the double digits, and even then it’s not guaranteed.  Then, assuming this works, the new target has to save against the disease or start suffering the consequences.  You could just… you know… use the Heal skill?  

But hey!  You can cast diagnose disease at-will!  Enjoy!

Force Field:  This ability starts off okay when you first get it at 3rd level, but it quickly becomes too expensive for what it does.  It costs precious phrenic pool points, and doesn’t do a much better job than false life.  The fast healing ability is kind of neat, but costs 3 phrenic points, which is way too much.  Finally, if your shield runs out of temp. hp, you can’t use it again for 24 hours.  You can cast false life as many times as you want in a day, as long as you’ve got the spell slots…

Kinetic Enhancement:  Okay, this is approximately on the same power level as a feat, so it might be worthy of taking (as a feat, not a phrenic amp).  You add your INT mod to CMB and CMD, as well as STR checks to lift or break stuff.  Notably, this lets you double your INT bonus to CMB when using spells such as telekinesis, which already substitute your INT for your STR when making combat maneuvers.  If you like the telekinesis line spells, this can give you a nice edge.  This also doesn’t take much re-fluffing, since telekinesis is part of the base game regardless of high technology.

Laser Blast:  Direct damage is hard to come by for the psychic, but starting at 11th level you can fire lasers!  Pew pew!  This ability is powered by your spell slots, so you can burn low level slots that aren’t as important to you to use this ability.  It does fire damage, which is something of a rarity for you, and it is also a supernatural ability, so it overcomes SR.  I wouldn’t use higher level slots on it, and it does have a pretty short range, but it’s an interesting option if you’re looking for something new to do and have all your important feats.  Just don’t expect to be dishing out mega-damage here.  It’s something to do when you don’t feel like using better spell slots, but still want to participate in a fight.

Mind Over Augmentation:  Does your game involve cybertech?  Yes?  Then this is okay at high levels if you’ve got the money and time to augment your meat-chassis.  You basically can ignore your CON score and get even more mileage out of your INT score for the purposes of determining your cybertech limit, but it’s going to get expensive fast.  Cybertech is basically slotless magic items that are twice as expensive as their regular counterparts, so you’re definitely not saving money in the long run.  However, you can’t have cybertech stolen, and I think it would still function in an antimagic field (maybe?  Sure?).  This is going to be mega campaign dependent.

Remember: you gave up a feat for this, so you’re not getting your money’s worth until you have cybertech installed that surpases the original limit imposed by your CON score.

Psychic Battery:  Again, this ability is so campaign-dependent that it’s almost impossible to rate.  If your game involves non-worn technological items, you can use this ability to provide charges, but they’re not permanent; you have to use them in one round.  It eats up your swift action too.  That said, charges are precious, especially if you’ve got some sweet charge-powered gear on hand, so this ability helps your tech ‘endurance.’  It’s basically mandatory for Mindtech discipline psychics, as it powers their phrenic pool recovery mechanic.

Radiation Resistance:  Most games will never see the dangers of radiation, making this useless.  But in games that involve radiation, this is worth a feat if you can spare it.  It gives you a boost on your Fort.-weak self vs. the dangers of radiation, which are really nasty.  Even low-level radiation causes CON drain, so anything that helps you avoid that is good!

Techsmith:  All this does is save you time.  Time is precious, certainly, but if you’re playing in a game that has the downtime, and the facilities, to make tech equipment, you can probably afford to spend the extra days.  Save yourself a feat, folks.
