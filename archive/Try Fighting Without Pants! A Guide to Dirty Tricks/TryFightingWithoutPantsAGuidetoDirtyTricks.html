Try Fighting Without Pants! A Guide to Dirty Tricks

-AncientSpark

 

The Dirty Trick is a combat maneuver introduced in the Advanced Player’s Guide and was intended to cover all sorts of situations of skullduggery, comedy, and general shenanigans in combat situations. Unfortunately, with the way Pathfinder handles combat maneuvers, any individual combat maneuver needs a LOT of support to work and Dirty Trick simply did not have that at the time. Recently, with the printing of the ACG and the Dirty Tactics Toolbox, however, Dirty Tricks got some massive upgrades in terms of tricks that it can pull off, so while the core has remained the same, there is some additional feats and scaling to make it quite a compelling choice for non-traditional debuffers and non-traditional martial characters.

 

Ratings

 

This guide uses the typical rating system for handbooks, with my own takes on what the ratings mean. Below are the colors and their meanings.

 

It’s Great and you should consider this a mainstay or must-take.

It’s Above Average and it’s usually worth the tradeoffs except for specific reasons.

It’s Average and you should consider whether it’s worth the tradeoffs or whether it fits into your build.

It’s Below Average and you should generally avoid unless you have a good reason not to.

It’s Awful and you should almost never take this.

 

How Does the Dirty Trick Work?

 

Like all combat maneuvers, the Dirty Trick relies on the Combat Maneuver Bonus. Unlike other maneuvers, Dirty Trick covers enough situations that you generally have to say exactly what you’re doing, such as bleeding the opponent in a certain way, hitting a specific part, or using the environment.

 

As a standard action, you declare you’re performing a dirty trick, roll 1d20+CMB, and attempt to pass your opponent’s CMD. If you succeed, you then can inflict one of the following conditions:

 

Blinded, Dazzled, Deafened, Entangled, Shaken, or Sickened.

 

Obviously, these conditions are not equal in strength, so I’ve rated them above, but don’t be afraid to change which condition you’re applying based on the situation. In particular, Deafened is stronger against casters, but falls behind Shaken and Sickened against martial characters.

 

Anyway, by default, the Dirty Trick lasts for 1 round, plus an additional round for every 5 you exceed your opponent’s CMD and your opponent can remove the condition as a move action. This obviously isn’t very appealing, which is why most people avoid Dirty Tricks by default. But, as we’ll see in the Feats section, there’s enough feats in the game to make removing Dirty Tricks extremely painful and keeping the condition even more so.

 

Why Play a Dirty Trickster?

 

The Good:

 

It’s Versatile - All other combat maneuvers have exactly one effect. Dirty Tricks can apply up to 6 different effects, which means that it has an answer for every type of enemy and situation. Which brings us to the next point…

 

It Works Against Everything - You can’t trip a flying or legless enemy. You can’t disarm an enemy without a weapon. You CAN Dirty Trick just about everything, with the only caveat being that some conditions might not work.

 

It’s Less Size Dependent - Don’t get me wrong; size can be useful for Dirty Tricks, but only as a CMB booster. Other combat maneuvers have a size limit compared to your size as to who you can affect, but Dirty Tricks have no such limit. Heck, for Dex based Dirty Tricksters, it may even be to your advantage to be as small as possible for the Dex boost.

 

It Stacks By Adding Different Conditions - Dirty Trick characters are incredibly powerful against single enemies because of this. Since the toughest enemies tend to come in lesser numbers with how most DMs make encounters, this makes Dirty Trick characters stronger in the situations where you most need them to be.

 

It’s Fun - I know this isn’t technically a mechanical advantage, but I seriously recommend everyone try out at least one Dirty Trick character in their Pathfinder career. One of the main dissuading factors of combat maneuver characters (and, to an extent, most martial characters) is repetition and Dirty Tricks allay this by allowing all sorts of different situational effects. Plus, Dirty Tricks allow for all sorts of personal expression for all character types, from the dexterous (“Tie them down by the coat!”), to the brutal (“Poke out their eyes!”), to the comedic (“Pull their pants down!”).

 

The Bad:

 

Conditions Can Be Removed - This is the big one. While this is technically true for the other big combat maneuver (Trip), Trip has the advantage of granting you damage off of their removal. Dirty Tricks grant you no other benefit when enemies remove a Dirty Trick, save for the action economy trade.

 

Dirty Tricks Are Bad for Damage - Other combat maneuvers grant you some sort of damage compensation for pulling them off, usually in the form of AoOs. While there is a feat chain that does let you get similar AoOs, it’s not very practical to get, so most of Dirty Trick is there for pure disablers.

 

It Can Be Inconsistent - Dirty Tricks can go away on their own, which means that even if your opponent doesn’t remove them, you can sometimes feel like your Dirty Tricks are wasted. At worst, a landed Dirty Trick still debuffs for at least 1 round, but it’s still worth mentioning.

 

It’s Feat Heavy - Even compared to other combat maneuvers, the amount of feats you need to get a Dirty Trick character to work is staggering; 5 core feats (Combat Expertise, Improved Dirty Trick, Greater Dirty Trick, Quick Dirty Trick, Dirty Trick Master) and many amplifying feats. If you want to Dirty Trick, you need to seriously devote yourself to it.

 

It’s Non-Functional Before Level 6 or 7 - You can trip and still achieve a lot, if successful. Disarming still takes someone out of the fight pre-6. A Dirty Trick without Greater Dirty Trick is just trading your standard action for their move action, which is worthless.

 

It’s Roleplaying Dependent - Unlike other combat maneuvers, where the condition being applied and the method you’re attempting to do it is usually straightforward, Dirty Tricks are way more freeform. This means you have to get creative with your roleplaying to justify how you’re applying certain conditions with certain Dirty Tricks, and stickler DMs have every right to veto your application of Dirty Tricks if you try and do something that makes no sense. (“You can’t get blood into the opponent’s eyes, he has no blood!”)

 

The ???s

 

Dirty Trick Gets Weapon Bonuses…Sort Of - Combat maneuvers that can be performed with weapons have two advantages; the first is that you can substitute attacks with their combat maneuver and the second is that you can apply weapon-specific bonuses, such as enhancement and Weapon Focus, to your CMB as a combat maneuver. Unfortunately, the first advantage cannot be done with Dirty Trick without Quick Dirty Trick, but the second benefit does apply to Dirty Trick… but only if you directly use your weapon in the Dirty Trick, which means the DM has every right to say “Your Dirty Trick attempt makes zero sense with your weapon, vetoed”.

 

Considerations For Making and Playing a Dirty Trickster:

 

CMB/Attack Bonuses Over Everything: Unlike other combat maneuvers, Dirty Tricks are about pure disabling. It’s generally difficult to utilize Dirty Tricks as a damage option, so you can’t have this “rarely land Dirty Trick, but have it have huge impact” paradigm that some other combat maneuvers can get away with. Not only that, but Dirty Tricksters are so feat starved that you don’t have the ability to maintain the same damage output that other martial characters do in even back-up situations, so your best bet is to specialize enough that your Dirty Tricks will almost always land.

 

Weapons: There are no weapons that have special properties for Dirty Tricks and only a few feats that require specific weapons, none of which are critical. Nevertheless, weapon choice is critical to Dirty Tricks because you want to always be Dirty Tricking with your weapon to apply weapon bonuses. As a result, you should choose a weapon that you’re comfortable coming up with Dirty Tricks with in any situation. A special note goes to Unarmed/Gauntlet in this regard; it’s harder to get your enhancement bonus up with Unarmed Strike, but if you can, it’s very easy to come up with all sorts of Dirty Tricks with an Unarmed Strike and you should highly consider it.

 

Party Play: Like most combat maneuvers, Dirty Tricks apply conditions that your party members can capitalize off of, so you should make sure you and your party is aware of the consequences of each condition. If you have a Rogue, for example, make sure that you’re applying lots of Blind and make sure your Rogue knows that Blind = Sneak Attacks.

 

You WANT to Trade Actions with Your Opponent: Many people get caught up with the fact that Dirty Trick can be removed and dismiss Dirty Trick out of hand. It’s true that if you can inflict a Dirty Trick that is unremovable and crippling, such as with Dirty Trick Master, that is incredibly powerful, but sometimes that is not going to happen. What happens more often in practice is that you will inflict a condition that your opponent will find unacceptable, and they will be willing to spend actions to remove. Your objective, as a Dirty Trickster, is to make that action trade as much in your favor as possible. For most Dirty Tricksters, this means you will eventually get to the point of spending a single attack in a full attack in exchange for your opponent’s standard action.

 

As an example, a famous mainstay of kung-fu films is that the main character pulls off their jacket and kicks it at an opponent, blinding them. The opponent doesn’t sit there like an idiot and try and fight with a jacket over their head and they take it off. Even so, the main character got an advantage because the opponent wasted more time than the main character. That’s the mindset a Dirty Trickster should operate under.

 

Races:

 

Unlike other combat maneuvers, you don’t need to go out of your way to play specific races, since you aren’t restricted by size. You do suffer a -1 penalty for size by using a small character, but that is unlikely to break you and there are some classes that benefit from being small. Thus, you mostly have the same races as typical for your class, with the small exception that you may need 13 Int for Combat Expertise.

 

That said, there is one particular race to pay attention to:

 

Gnome: Gnome has the alternate racial trait Dirty Trickster, from Inner Sea Races. This fantastic trait grants them a +2 racial bonus on dirty trick combat maneuvers and allows them to skip the Int requirement for all Dirty Trick related feats. This is especially worth noting in a low point buy game where Dirty Fighting is not available, but even in games where that’s not true, this still makes Gnomes a great Small race for Dirty Tricks.

 

Classes:

 

Recommend Dirty Tricksters:

 

These classes are good platforms to start off with for making your Dirty Trickster characters.

 

Slayer, The Gold Standard - If you just want the best all-around Dirty Trick character, look no further.

 

Advantages:

-Lots of bonus feats through talents: Out of all the classes, Slayer is one of the few that can most comfortably slot Dirty Trick into a normal feat progression along with other styles like Two-Weapon Fighting and Dex-based fighting.

-Studied Target - Any attack bonus against enemies translates into better CMB for Dirty Trick. This means that even without any other support, a Dirty Trick Slayer can still land Dirty Tricks a reasonable amount of the time.

-Bounty Hunter archetype: The Bounty Hunter archetype’s 2nd level ability, Dirty Trick, allows you to perform a Dirty Trick FOR FREE in exchange for giving up their Sneak Attack dice. Note that you still get your attack. This means that your damage loss from abusing Dirty Tricks is far far less than any other Dirty Trick characters and it also means you can potentially drop Quick Dirty Trick from your setup. Adding your amount of Sneak Attack dice as a bonus to your CMB for Dirty Trick is also crazy powerful, making Slayers competitive as perhaps the third best class for landing Dirty Tricks.

-Sneak Attack and Rogue Talents through Slayer Talents - Because of these two, the Slayer also has access to some of the tricks Rogues get as Dirty Tricksters, albeit not to the same degree due to their lower Sneak Attack progression. See the Rogue entry for more information.

 

Disadvantages:

-Slightly dependent on Sneak Attack - This isn’t even as bad as it seems; Slayers can still Dirty Trick without Sneak Attack with their high base BAB and Studied Target, albeit it becomes somewhat average.

 

Barbarian (or Bloodrager with Primalist archetype), ANGRY MAN ONLY NEED ONE SMASH - Barbarians make for surprisingly powerful Dirty Tricksters, as long as you’re willing to jump through some hoops to achieve Rage Cycling.

 

Advantages:

-Rage - Unfortunately, Rage doesn’t grant as high of an attack/CMB bonus as some other features, but it’s worth noting as it’s better than nothing.

 -Rage Powers (Strength Surge and Savage Dirty Trick) - The real reason you pick Barbarian as a Dirty Trickster. Nothing can beat a Strength Surged combat maneuver in terms of CMB, which means that Dirty Tricks will ALWAYS land. Savage Dirty Trick is also incredibly powerful as, unlike other Dirty Trick conditions, an enemy that fails to save against a Savage Dirty Trick WILL spend a turn doing nothing; they simply can’t ignore the crippling power of Staggered, if they even have a choice since they might be Dazed or Nauseated instead. The save DC for Savage Dirty Trick isn’t even that bad since you have a high Strength score with Rage.

-Untamed Rager archetype - Untamed Rager doesn’t grant anything particularly exciting, but two bonus feats and a further increase in CMB to Dirty Tricks is nothing to scoff at either, so there’s no reason not to take this if you’re a Dirty Trick Barbarian.

 

Disadvantages:

-Requires Rage Cycling - Both Strength Surge and Savage Dirty Trick are once per Rage powers (well, technically, Savage Dirty Trick is once per enemy per rage, but same thing). Since these are your main incentives for Dirty Tricking as a Barbarian, this means you need to either multiclass an Oracle to get Fatigue immunity from Lame curse or get a Cord of Stubborn Resolve. Some DMs also don’t like Rage Cycling as a concept, so talk with your DM to see if this is acceptable.

-Incompatible with Unchained Barbarian - Since Unchained Barbarian got rid of Rage Cycling, you cannot Dirty Trick profitably as a Barbarian. Check with your DM to make sure that they aren’t requiring you to be Unchained.

 

Rogue, Or How to Be Batman - Rogues are possibly the only medium BAB class that can actually Dirty Trick well, for a couple reasons, but be forewarned; you are even more all or nothing than most Dirty Trick characters.

 

Advantages

-Rogue Talents (Underhanded Maneuver and Underhanded Trick) - Underhanded Maneuver is what allows you to compensate for your medium BAB. This also opens up Underhanded Trick, which grants you Improved Dirty Trick as a bonus feat and prevents enemies from removing Blind for one round. Blind is the most crippling of the default Dirty Trick status effects, so forcing an enemy to have to endure it for one round, guaranteed, is a godsend.

-Surprise Maneuver feat - This is what takes Rogue from a mediocre Dirty Trickster to a high-powered one. Surprise Maneuver lets you achieve massive CMB checks against enemies you can normally Sneak Attack, to the point that Rogue may even achieve the second highest Dirty Trick score among all Dirty Trick characters.

-Skulking Slayer archetype - Their level 1 ability, Underhanded Maneuvers, allows you to substitute Sneak Attacks with Dirty Tricks with a bonus equal to your Sneak Attack dice. You do have to give up the attack, unlike with the Slayer’s Bounty Hunter ability, but this bonus stacks with Surprise Maneuver for amazing Dirty Trick checks. This archetype also has a small bonus to use two-handed weapon with their level 3 ability and, as a Rogue, you’re feat light enough that you might be forced into two-handed weapons anyway, so this is a nice touch. Unfortunately, this is Half-Orc only, which is pretty silly; talk to your DM to see if you can get out of that restriction.

-Vexing Dodger archetype - While this is technically an advantage, Skulking Slayer is a much more useful archetype and incompatible, so this is only a second choice. Still, small characters in a monster slayer-type campaign where you’re constantly fighting large enemies can achieve massive Sneak Attacks with Distracting Climber and still do a reasonable impression of a Bounty Hunter with Underfoot Trickster.

 

Disadvantages:

-Completely dependent on Sneak Attack - I’m serious when I say this; a Dirty Trick Rogue does absolutely nothing against an enemy that can’t be Sneak Attacked. Prepare to get completely destroyed in certain encounters. If you still want to Dirty Trick as a Rogue, it may be prudent to take archetypes and feats to increase your ability to inflict Sneak Attacks. For example, the Scout archetype, which is compatible with Skulking Slayer, can be useful. That said, once you land the initial Dirty Trick to inflict Blind, further Dirty Tricks and Sneak Attacks are trivial due to Blind denying Dexterity bonus to AC.

-Somewhat race dependent - The two Dirty Trick archetypes, Skulking Slayer and Vexing Dodger, want specific races. Skulking Slayer can only be used by half-orcs and Vexing Dodger, while not restricted, benefits extremely hard from being a Small race.

 

Brawler, The Fighter’s Cousin First Removed - Like the Fighter, just below, the Brawler is a consistent, but unexciting Dirty Trickster. Nothing wrong with a “just normal” character though. Of particular note, as well, Brawlers are one of the few Dirty Tricksters that can perform unarmed, which is a nice boon.

 

Advantages:

-Martial Flexibility - Less good for the Brawler than other combat maneuvers, unfortunately. Other combat maneuver type Brawlers can use Martial Flexibility to change combat styles if they run into a situation their combat maneuver cannot deal with it, but you’re a Dirty Trickster, you can deal with EVERYTHING. Still something to keep in your back pocket though.

-Bonus feats - Again, do not underestimate the amount of feats a Dirty Trickster will burn through. You need EVERY bonus feat you can get your grubby little hands on.

-Maneuver Training - While Brawlers don’t have an inherent attack bonus, their Maneuver Training more than makes up for it with a nice CMB bonus.

-Mutagenic Mauler archetype - You know how I just mentioned that Martial Flexibility is just a “nice to have”? This archetype lets you trade it away for a Mutagen. I don’t much like the “sit and let me craft a new Mutagen after every fight” style, but you can’t argue with a free CMB boost.

-Shield Champion - This is more gimmicky than anything, especially since you give up Maneuver Training, but this does let you perform Dirty Tricks decently at a range with Throw Shield. Just a small note if you want to build a ranged Dirty Trickster.

-Snakebite Striker - “Wait, this gives up Maneuver Training, why is this an advantage?” The Surprise Maneuver feat. For combat maneuver characters, Sneak Attack dice = CMB bonuses if you can get the conditions right. And, with Snake Feint, it’s much easier for Snakebite Striker’s to achieve that flat-footed condition than other characters (especially since Greater Feint is just two feats away).

 

Disadvantages:

-None. Brawlers are nice and consistent, in case you’re into that sort of thing.

 

Fighter, The Silver Standard - Fighters are reasonable at Dirty Tricking, like every combat maneuver, and are the last of what I would consider “typical Dirty Trick” type characters.

 

Advantages:

-Lots of Bonus Feats - Always worth mentioning; since you’re so feat heavy, a class that grants a bonus feat every two levels cannot be underestimated.

-Weapon Training - Remember, our objective is to Dirty Trick with our weapon, so having an attack bonus that applies to only one weapon isn’t even bad for us. And any attack bonus to translate to our CMB is good.

-Cad archtype - Oof, just as we mention Weapon Training, we have to give it up, especially because Cad has so many nonsense, niche abilities. But, it does compensate for giving up Weapon Training with Dirty Maneuvers. More importantly, it also gives Deadly Surprise, which allows us a bonus Dirty Trick attempt at someone denied their Dexterity bonus to AC. Conveniently, the Blind condition allows us to deny Dexterity bonus to AC, so Deadly Surprise secretly reads “Gain a second Dirty Trick if you land a Dirty Trick to Blind”. Very useful.

-Lore Warden archetype - Annoyingly, there’s two versions of this archetype out there; one from the Pathfinder Society Field Guide and one from the Adventurer’s Guide. Thankfully, both of them work well for a Fighter. They don’t actually give thaaat interesting of benefits, but they only give up Armor Training which you weren’t using much and give massive CMB bonuses in exchange. If you have a choice of which one to take, though, and your group has provisions in case you already have a feat you would take as a bonus feat, take the PSFG version; its CMB bonus is much higher and it gets Combat Expertise much faster.

-Mutation Warrior archetype - I dislike the “sit and let me craft a new Mutagen after every fight” style, but if you’re not taking Cad and/or Lore Warden, then there’s really no reason not to take this for a free bonus to your CMB, especially since you have better things to do than faff about with Armor Training.

-Dirty Fighter archetype - This is mostly mentioned for completion’s sake, as somehow, this orc-specific archetype eventually gets a lower CMB than the base fighter due to giving up weapon training and only getting +2 to CMB. Its other features are not so much better than comparable feats as to make this archetype worth it, but if you want to go through the hilarity of somehow inflicting 6 conditions on one enemy in a single turn, then this archetype does do that in later levels.

 

Disadvantages:

-None. Fighters are consistent, if nothing else.

 

Monk, Quantity over Quality - Monks have lots of support for combat maneuver characters, so it’s unsurprising that you can make a Dirty Trickster out of them. Of note is their ability to spec as an unarmed character, which is an advantage as mentioned in the weapons section.

 

Advantages:

-Maneuver Master archetype - Flurry with Dirty Tricks! Pull down their pants and kick them in the groin at the same time! Yeah, Flurry of Maneuvers is great as always, although the attack penalty is annoying. Still, despite the fact that monks have no attack bonus, the sheer number of dirty trick maneuvers you can pull off in a turn ensures that you’ll probably land one. Eventually. Make sure you don’t occupy your Swift Action because you’re going to be using Meditative Maneuver a lot.

-Wildcat archetype - This is mostly a late game archetype; while Wildcat grants you a CMB bonus to Dirty Trick, it still doesn’t really have much advantages compared to other classes pre-level 13. However, Turn the Tables is a NICE way to solve the lack of damage Dirty Trick tends to bring to the table and Dirty Blow is just overkill.

 

Disadvantages:

-No inherent attack/CMB bonus in class - This is actually quite crippling and makes Monk a bit difficult to figure out in terms of power level. Overall, I think the archetypes above do make up for the lack of attack bonus, but be forewarned that Monk may not hold up in situations where you’re running into enemies with very high CMD.

-May not be compatible with Unchained Monk - Archetypes do not inherently port over to Unchained Monk, which makes it difficult to recommend Monk as a Dirty Trickster in games where you cannot use regular Monk. Check with your DM to see if it’s possible to port over archetypes to Unchained Monk.

 

Deceptively Mediocre Dirty Tricksters:

 

These classes have Dirty Trick support, but, for one reason or another, have some noticeable problems. These classes are not unusable, because otherwise, I would not mention them, but be forewarned you’re taking on some challenges with them.

 

Swashbuckler, The Bronze Standard - Swashbucklers have SOME tricks to make them interesting as Swashbucklers, but they don’t have the sheer number of bonus feats that make Fighters and Brawlers compelling as Dirty Tricksters and their CMB for Dirty Tricks are mediocre compared to the recommend classes. They’re probably the best Dirty Tricksters out of the mediocre category, however, as they have no glaring weaknesses.

 

Advantages:

-Swashbuckler Weapon Training - Your one attack bonus for CMB. Hold onto it for dear life.

-Mouser archetype - You’re here for the Hamstring ability. The ability to perform Dirty Trick as a Swift action for free is very nice and while you can only inflict Staggered with this Dirty Trick, Staggered is probably better than all of the default Dirty Trick conditions anyway. (Also, see the feats section about Superior Dirty Trick)

-Dastardly Trick feat - You know how one of the problems with Dirty Trick is that Dirty Trick can be removed? Yeah, Swashbuckler can get around that with this feat…except that the save DC is Charisma based and thus, not likely to be very high. If you are planning on using this feat, I recommend grabbing the Divine Fighting Technique feat that lets you use Charisma for attacks/damage with starknives and using starknives as your main weapon.

 

Disadvantages:

-None. Swashbucklers tend to be mediocre at Dirty Tricking, but that is not to say that they have any glaring weaknesses either, save for the fact that they are usually forced into the Dex route and forced to burn even more feats than normal.

 

Vigilante, Or How to be Crappy Robin - This makes me unreasonably annoyed that Dirty Tricks are kind of a trap for Vigilantes since they have such unique benefits for it, but well, here we go…

 

Advantages:

-Vigilante Talents (Deceitful Trick, Brutal Maneuver, Expose Weakness) - Deceitful Trick lets you skip Quick Dirty Trick and also allows you to inflict two conditions with a -4 penalty (ask your DM how this stacks with Kitsune Tricks). Brutal Maneuver in combination with Quick Dirty Trick or Deceitful Trick also lets you take a -5 penalty to do BOTH damage and Dirty Trick. Lastly, Expose Weakness allows you to reduce DR with Dirty Tricks, which allows Dirty Trick a damage option, which is unusual.

-Rogue Talents (Stalker Only) - In addition to the support above, the Vigilante can poach the Rogue options for Dirty Trick, which IS nice…but see the Disadvantages section below.

-Serial Killer archetype - If you’re playing a campaign where they’re fine with you being evil, this IS an option to get around the second disadvantage listed below (since they can treat their hidden strike as Sneak Attack), to the point where Vigilante might actually just be competitive with Rogue for being a Dirty Trickster. Unfortunately, the kinds of campaigns where that’s true tend to be pretty limited.

 

Disadvantages:

-All Attack/CMB Bonuses Required Unawareness - This means you really only have bonuses for your first Dirty Trick in combat and considering Dirty Trick has a tendency to extend combat…yeah. Unlike with Monk which can compensate for this to a certain degree, this is utterly crippling to Vigilantes as Dirty Tricksters as they’re incentivized to take massive penalties for their Dirty Trick support. This means that Avenger Vigilantes are just worse than other classes at Dirty Tricks.

-Hidden Strike is not Sneak Attack - This strikes out Stalker Vigilantes as being worth anything. While you can technically use a Stalker Vigilante and try and get all the Rogue support for Dirty Tricks, you can’t take Surprise Maneuver, which is half the benefit to being a Rogue-like Dirty Trickster. At this point, you have to ask yourself why you’re even bothering with Vigilante when you could just be playing a Rogue.

 

Ranger, You Triggered My Trap Card! - This one is a pure gimmick and dependent on DM ruling, but an interesting one, at the very least, and could be worth playing around with. Note that there is a combat style for Ranger specifically designed for Dirty Trick (Underhanded).

 

Advantages:

-Guide Archetype: The fact that there is no inherent attack bonus in the Ranger’s arsenal is criminal and the Guide archetype solves that pretty nicely. In fact, the bonus here is actually quite massive, with its only caveat being that it has an unfortunately small number of uses per day.

-Favored Enemy (at later levels): I heavily dislike Favored Enemy, but, after a certain point, Wands of Instant Enemy become available often enough that you can rely on Favored Enemy as your main in-combat tool for attack bonuses if you dislike the Guide archetype. Especially of note in single enemy type campaigns.

-Trapper Archetype: What you’re here for is the Dirty Trick Trap, which allows you to add a Dirty Trick effect to any other trap. The problem here is that the trap uses its own CMB, that being ranger level + Wisdom, which is laughably bad. On the other hand, however, it’s unclear how other modifiers apply to this CMB (Can you combine it with Guide? Does Improved/Greater Dirty Trick add to its bonus?); if other benefits do apply, it’s possible to salvage its CMB. Make no mistake; while setting up a bunch of Dirty Trick traps before hand is situational, it’s powerful when it does come up because it utterly cripples your opponent’s in-combat actions at no cost to your own in-combat actions.

 

Disadvantages:

-No consistent in class attack bonus - Why Rangers? You either have to rely on Favored Enemy, which is horribly niche or Guide archetype which only works a couple of times per day.

 

Prestige Classes:

 

I’ll be honest, I haven’t prestige classed in about forever and a half, so it’s fully possible that I’m missing some PrCs. Feel free to complain to me if I am.

 

Pit Fighter (Paths of Prestige):

This can be entered by any class if you’re an orc/half-orc, but if you’re not, you have to enter it as a Barbarian. Because this PrC does not progress rage or rage talents, it’s a bit hard to recommend for Barbarians, but for other classes, it’s a definite contender, especially for fighters/brawlers.

Advantages:

-Gets three of the core Dirty Trick feats as bonus feats.

-Weapon Trick at level 3 allows them to do damage equal to their weapon with one dirty trick once per turn. Nice for preserving damage.

-Brutal Trick at level 9 forces an opponent to provoke an AoO whenever they remove a condition from Dirty Trick. About as amazing as you can expect.

 

Disadvantages:

-Gets all of the bonus feats that you want MUCH later than you want them. You thus need to ask the DM whether you can retrain as you get bonus feats or replace the bonus feats from Pit Fighter with new ones if you already have the feats they give.

-Loves crowd and performance gimmicks, which I don’t think most parties bother with. You have to figure out how these work in all sorts of situations, but depending on the DM, this can range from not being a drawback at all to being extremely crippling.

 

Guild Agent (Paths of Prestige):

A potential prestige class for Rogue types, but be forewarned that it takes until level 5 for this prestige class to really take off.

Advantages:

-Crucial Taunt: The level 5 feature grants you a whooping +1/2 class level on a bunch of different checks, one of which is Dirty Trick. Hilariously, it can also let you delay the dirty trick for a couple of days. Amazing in a campaign where you’re meeting enemies repeatedly, such as an intrigue campaign, (“You betrayed me! It’s too bad that I spiked your drink 3 days in advance to give you the runs!”)

-Sneak Attack and Rogue Talents: See the Rogue entry for why this is so powerful.

 

Disadvantages:

-Must have Trap Sense as a prereq if entering as a Rogue. This disables multiple useful archetypes when entering as a Rogue such as Skulking Slayer and, thus, you should carefully consider if this is worth it.

-Medium BAB, but not actually a downside if you’re using the Rogue to enter (see the Rogue entry).

 

Feats:

 

The Core of the Dirty Trickster:

Almost all Dirty Tricksters need the following feats, with a couple of exceptions for Quick Dirty Trick depending on your class features.

 

Combat Expertise (Core)/Dirty Fighting (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Combat Expertise is useless for you, but it’s a prereq for all of the Dirty Trick feats beyond it. If Dirty Fighting is available, take that one instead; it counts as Combat Expertise for prereqs and grants +2 to Dirty Trick while flanking. As normal, bonuses to Dirty Trick CMB are invaluable.

 

Improved Dirty Trick (APG): You don’t provoke AoO for performing a dirty trick and +2 to CMB for doing so. Critical to any combat maneuver.

 

Greater Dirty Trick (APG): An additional +2 bonus to Dirty Trick, but also extends Dirty Trick’s duration and forces the enemy to burn a Standard action to rid themselves of the Dirty Trick. Absolutely critical and the first feat to really cause Dirty Trick to come into its own.

 

Quick Dirty Trick (UC): Substitute your first attack in a round with a Dirty Trick. This allows you to not be as crippled for damage as normal when utilizing Dirty Tricks by preserving your latter attacks. Note that it also doesn’t mention that it has to be a full attack, for whatever odd reason; if you can perform multiple attacks as a standard action or a charge, this becomes even better.

 

Dirty Trick Master (Bastards of Golarion): Now we’re talking! Dirty Trick Master allows you to upgrade a Dirty Trick condition if the enemy is already under the effect of a Dirty Trick. Even if you can only apply one Dirty Trick per turn, it still threatens the opponent’s actions so heavily that they MUST spend actions to remove their Dirty Trick condition. If you can apply more than one Dirty Trick in a turn, Dirty Trick Master is an encounter ender, period; just spam the same condition into your enemy as they watch helplessly.

 

Dirty Trick Amplifying Feats:

 

These feats have specific benefits for Dirty Tricksters and, while they are not as critical as the feats above, they are all very much worth considering.

 

Style Feats:

 

Kitsune Style (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Kitsune Style lets you perform a Dirty Trick after a charge. If you can multi-attack off a charge, then it does a reasonable Quick Dirty Trick impression, but it’s otherwise just a small CMB booster using the Charge bonus and a mobility boost.

-Kitsune Tricks (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): THIS is what you’re here for. Kitsune Tricks lets you apply two conditions with a single Dirty Trick, which ensures that your opponent will most definitely be removing any Dirty Trick that you’re trying to inflict rather than suffer all those penalties. It is also unclear how this interacts with features that apply to specific Dirty Trick conditions, but if you rule, say, Superior Dirty Trick applies to both conditions applied, this gets even stronger.

--Kitsune Vengeance (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Perform Dirty Tricks in place of AoOs. A bit of a niche benefit, but one worth considering since you can do stuff like Entangle enemies as they move or Deafen casters as they attempt to cast. Unfortunately, niche benefits aren’t really good enough to consider when you’re so feat light, but it’s worth keeping in mind.

 

Cloak and Dagger Style (Blood of Shadows): Perform a free Dirty Trick when attacking with a light weapon during a surprise round and perform an AoO when they try to remove a condition with Dirty Trick. The latter benefit is VERY powerful and helps put Dirty Trick on the same level for damage as Trip. So why is this only orange? Because, for some ungodly reason, Paizo saw fit to saddle this feat with Vital Strike AND Weapon Focus as prereqs in addition to Improved Dirty Trick, which means that it’s impossible to fit into any reasonable build except as a fighter/brawler/slayer.

-Cloak and Dagger Subterfuge (Blood of Shadows): Dirty Trick when you perform an AoO and Steal when you Dirty Trick. Strictly better than Kitsune Vengeance, but, just like Cloak and Dagger style, hard to recommend with the prerequisites this requires.

--Cloak and Dagger Tactics (Blood of Shadows): Hitting a flat-footed target or Vital Striking allows you to Dirty Trick. This Dirty Trick also inflicts a greater status if they fail a Fort save. Awesome benefit and might actually be worth building towards, except that the save is Int based and hard to boost. Also, broken record, but prereqs and all.

 

Fox Trickery (Ultimate Intrigue): I’m putting this here just to vent; who the heck makes a Dirty Trick feat in a style locked behind two other style feats that have zero benefit to Dirty Tricks? Seriously. Anyway, this feat allows you to dirty trick as an AoO and grants a +4 bonus to Dirty Trick if your Int is 19 is above. Now, how many hoops did you jump through to get this again?

 

Class/Class Feature Specific Feats:

 

Surprise Maneuver (ACG): Add your Sneak Attack dice to your CMB when you “Sneak Attack” with a combat maneuver. Yes, this feat is as powerful as it sounds. No, you aren’t skipping it if you have any sort of decent Sneak Attack dice.

-Accomplished Sneak Attacker (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Doesn’t actually have Surprise Maneuver as a prerequisite, but you’re only really considering this is you do have or plan to get Surprise Maneuver. Adds +1d6 Sneak Attack, which allows you to boost both CMB and damage for when you aren’t Dirty Tricking. Even better since some Sneak Attackers can apply number of Sneak Attack dice twice for Dirty Tricks.

 

Dastardly Trick (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Spend a panache as you perform a Dirty Trick to force an enemy to roll Will save every time they try to remove it. An interesting feat and gets around one of the specific flaws that Dirty Trick has, but the Will save is not reliable and it relies on a rather scarce resource (Panache). Really only worth it if you can boost your Cha sky-high.

 

Equipment Tricks:

(Note: What’s listed here is the trick, which accompanies a number of other tricks that fall into “Equipment Trick (equipment name)”. For example, Heel Crush is part of Equipment Trick (Boots).)

 

Heel Crush (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Allows for a new Dirty Trick condition; halve land movement speed. I’d almost always apply Entangled first, but, under the assumption that this counts as a condition for stuff like Kitsune Tricks, this is a good secondary condition in case the enemy runs away. Of course, if that does not apply, then this feat is garbage. Also, this feat’s rating is under the assumption that using this feat allows for you to be using unarmed as your Dirty Trick rather than your “boot heel”.

 

Binding Trick (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Enemies cannot remove dirty tricks imposed by you if they are entangled by a net you’re controlling. Nets are actually pretty hard to escape; at minimum, they require a full round action and not every enemy has either Escape Artist or a good Strength score. The real problem is whether you’re going to spend the time to re-roll up your net after the first time you use your net, plus you have to be net proficient, but the reward is definitely there.

 

Dirty Pick (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Thieves’ tools are now improvised weapons that deal 1d3 piercing damage, you don’t provoke AoO for blinding with thieves’ tools, and masterwork thieves’ tools are considered +1 weapons. I’m not sure why you would ever use these unless you could use improvised weapons without penalty, and even then, you can just pick up a stick and stick them in the eye with it without a feat to do so.

 

Teamwork Feats:

(Note: These are rated with the assumption that teamwork feats aren’t a problem for your party, whether you have Solo Tactics, the ability to apply teamwork feats to multiple allies, or your party collectively benefits from them enough that multiple people are willing to take them. Do not use them otherwise).

 

Coordinated Maneuvers (APG): +2 to CMB. Certainly not bad.

 

Underhanded Teamwork (Pathfinder Society Primer): If an ally with this feat performs a dirty trick, increase its duration as an immediate action. Honestly, if your Dirty Tricks are bad enough, the enemy is just going to spend its action to remove it anyway, so taking a feat specifically for one round of duration isn’t worth much, especially as a teamwork feat.

-Improved Underhanded Teamwork (Pathfinder Society Primer): Perform a second dirty trick when an ally succeeds at a dirty trick. The problem here is that it takes the same action to remove both dirty tricks, at which point you could just take Kitsune Tricks and not jump through the hoops that this feat requires. That said, if you don’t want to take Kitsune Tricks (maybe you’re Cloak and Dagger style?), this is a backup.

 

Divine Fighting Techniques

(Note: These use the SRD names: Check the Weapon Master’s Handbook for their original names).

These feats are divided into Initial and Advanced benefits. You only need one feat to receive both the initial and advanced benefits (with advanced benefits requiring additional prereqs), but I will only rate the individual parts of each feat that are relevant to Dirty Tricksters, however.

 

Advanced - Divine Crossbow (Weapon Master’s Handbook): Ranged dirty trick to pin. Not really worth it as your sole Dirty Trick method because Dirty Tricks want to apply lots of different conditions. This is a potential back up option, of course, but then you’d have to use a crossbow and no one wants to do that.

 

Initial - Blade and Tankard Style (Weapon Master’s Handbook): Use a tankard as a light mace and, among other benefits, you may dirty trick in place of its off-hand attacks to blind. The fact that you can only blind with it is a bit of a bummer, but additional Quick Dirty Tricks isn’t necessarily bad. What really keeps this down is that it’s Two-Weapon Fighting, which is a hard style to pull off with Dirty Trick due to the number of feats required, but if you can, hey, be my guest. Also note that you can replace your proficiency with shields as a CG fighter or swashbuckler.

 

Advanced - Blade and Tankard Style (Weapon Master’s Handbook): +2 to dirty tricks with Tankards, with some other benefits that don’t matter if your Dirty Trick specialist. Pretty niche and something of a last feat you would ever take. Thankfully, the Initial Blade and Tankard Style benefit is good enough that this is more of a cherry on top than anything.

 

Advanced - Flensing Chain (Weapon Master’s Handbook): Sickening a foe forces them to make a Fort save or be nauseated. The problem is that the prereq for this is BAB +10, which is close enough that you could just take Dirty Trick Master instead. I’d really only consider this if I couldn’t get more than one Dirty Trick in a turn.

 

Advanced - Way of the Mace (Weapon Master’s Handbook): Hitting an enemy without their Dex to AC allows you to Dirty Trick them as a Swift Action. Surprisingly solid and makes Way of the Mace pretty good for Rogues. The problem is that you need to take Improved Feint.

 

Initial - Way of Thunder (Weapon Master’s Handbook): - Deal nonlethal damage equal to 1d6+Charisma modifier and give the option of reducing Charisma based checks instead of increasing duration on a higher Dirty Trick benefit. While Dirty Trick might suck for damage, I’m not THIS desperate to get damage on Dirty Tricks.

 

Other Feats:

 

Dirty Critical Hit (Dirty Tricks Handbook): Affect the target with a Dirty Trick if you criticaled. Interestingly, you don’t have to actually make a CMB check to affect them; the effect is automatic. That said, you probably are more concerned with the consistency of landing a Dirty Trick rather than fishing for it with crits on attacks, so this really only has uses with the Bounty Hunter archetype or if you’re having problems applying more than one Dirty Trick per turn.

 

Dirty Disarm/Dirty Grapple (Dirty Tricks Handbook): Perform a Disarm/Grapple and a Dirty Trick at the same time as a full-round action. You already have enough feats to take that it’s a bit hard to recommend faffing about with other maneuvers as well, not to mention that this is hardly efficient for your actions. Some groups use feat tax rules, at which point this is marginally better, but this feat only uses the lower of your two CMBs for the maneuver, which hurts this feat’s ability even further.

 

Net Trickery (Ultimate Combat): For the grand price of gaining proficiency with nets and two other net feats, your ultimate benefit is…a more restrictive Quick Dirty Trick. I suppose if you’re already dead set on using the net as your main weapon, you might as well take this, but this is stretching the amount of feats that you’re taking already.

 

Mud in Your Eye (Heroes of the Street): In Urban or Subterranean terrains, dirty trick to blind at a range with dirty stuff. The terrain restriction is what really hurts this, plus the wasted feat prereq. Rangers can perform this on any of their favored terrains, which makes it slightly more usable, although probably something of a ninth-best feat.

 

Superior Dirty Trick (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): One specific condition is upgraded as to require a full-round action to remove. By default, this is useful, but not amazing, as an enemy giving up their standard action and an enemy giving up both their standard and move action is not THAT disparate except in chase situations. However, what is not clear by this feat is whether this applies to multiple conditions applied at once or whether it applies to non-standard conditions gained from class features. Yes to the former would make it interesting to characters with Underhanded Trick and Kitsune Tricks and yes to the latter would make it absurdly powerful for Mouser Swashbucklers as an unremovable Stagger.

 

Trick Spell (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Metamagic feat that increases the spell level by 1. Can only be applied to enchantment spells that have a Will save that affect one target. Enemies that fail the Will save are subject to a free Dirty Trick attempt. This all sounds appealing and all, until you realize that, if an enemy already failed their save against an Enchantment spell, they’re likely under a greater condition than any Dirty Trick can already attempt. Perhaps could see use in some weird Magus build, but I’m not sure a Magus can get their save DC high enough…

 

Weapon Focus (Core): The old standby +1 to attack. For other combat maneuvers who eventually run out of feats to take, that may be the option, but for you who has a million feats to get through, Weapon Focus should be the last last resort for you, unless you’re a Fighter/Brawler/Swashbuckler who can progress further down the Weapon Focus chain.

 

Items

 

There are startlingly few items that specifically aid Dirty Tricks, so most of what you’re looking at are general CMB boosters. Also note that I’m listing CMB relevant equipment here, but any equipment that boosts attack (such as the Brawling property for armor) should also be considered.

 

Weapons

 

Dueling (Pathfinder Society Field Guide) - +1 property - Not to be confused with the other Dueling property from Ultimate Equipment. Grants +2 luck bonus to CMB per +1 enhancement bonus of the weapon if you’re using the weapon. Luck bonuses are pretty rare AND this stacks with the enhancement bonus of the weapon. One of the big reasons why you want to always be using your weapon in Dirty Tricks.

 

Brawling (ACG) - +1 property - Can only be applied to brass knuckles/cestus/sap/light bludgeoning weapon. Grants +1 enhancement bonus to CMB per +1 enhancement bonus of the weapon. I suspect that this is not intended to stack with the enhancement bonus of the weapon if you’re using the weapon in the maneuver, at which point this really only has niche uses for Dirty Tricks without using your weapon, but if it DOES stack, you should apply this.

 

Armor/Shields

 

Burglar’s Buckler (Ultimate Equipment) - Specific Shield - A +1 buckler with +2 competence bonus to dirty tricks and a bunch of side, roguish benefits. What knocks this down is that many people probably aren’t interested in using shields at all, let alone bucklers, but those that do have no reason not to use this for dirty tricks.

 

Wondrous Items

 

Gauntlets of the Skilled Maneuver (Ultimate Equipment) - Hands - +2 to a specific combat maneuver. Dirt cheap at only 4000 gp. Should be your go-to item.

 

Gauntlets of Pulverizing Disruption (Dirty Tactics Toolbox) - Hands - After performing an extremely situational touch attack, you store charges that you can spend to get bonuses on touch attacks. The bonus isn’t even bigger than Gauntlets of the Skilled Maneuver!

 

Tattoo, Serpentine (Magical Marketplace) - No Item Slot - 3 times per day, perform a dirty trick as an immediate action after hitting with unarmed, monk weapon, or performance weapon. It’s only a limited amount of times per day and there’s some restrictions to it, plus you might be hard up for swift actions, but it’s only 2000 gp, so it’s cheap enough to fit into most mid-level builds. It certainly makes triggering Dirty Trick Master easy, at the very least.
