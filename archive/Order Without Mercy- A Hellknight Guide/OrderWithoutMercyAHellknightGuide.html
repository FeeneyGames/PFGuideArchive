“Hellknights aren’t just order’s enforcers, they’re its executioners.”

So, the Hellknight’s caught your eye, huh? Hardly surprising, as it’s a very nice and rather unique prestige class that looks badass as hell to boot. It’s got the flavor of a Paladin without that pesky “Lawful Good” attachment, and gives you some added flair for those Fighter-types who might otherwise feel lackluster when it comes to the roleplaying part of a TTRPG. On top of all that, the lore behind the Hellknights is, in my opinion, some of the best that Paizo has ever given us, if you check the right places. Regardless of what caught your eye, now you’re here, and it’s my job to teach you how to champion law with maximum efficiency.

I’ll be writing this guide (unsurprisingly) in the all-too familiar fashion of Treantmonk’s guides. If you haven’t checked out any of his stuff, I highly recommend it- the guy’s a legend, and his guides absolutely helped get me to the point I’m at now when it comes to game knowledge.

One last thing before we get into it, though- this is really not an optimization guide. This is a guide where I point you toward the good options, warn of the bad options, and leave you to build your characters however you like. I might add some sample builds at the end if enough people check out this guide, just to give some examples of what my ideas of a unique Hellknight could look like.

- Class Overview -

If you hadn’t noticed already, the Hellknight reads like an anti-chaos Paladin. Looking a bit closer, though, it’s very different in some very key ways. The Paladin class puts most of its focus on being a self-buffing megatank with a zillion auras of “You Are Safe Now”, and the Hellknight is absolutely not that sort of a team player. You have no divine magic keeping you safe, and in combat, your abilities keep you concerned with you, and scaring the living hell out of anything watching you do it.

Here’s the color scheme I’ll be using for most of this guide:

Purple- These are the best options available. If you have the option to take them, take them.

Blue- Not quite the same as purple, but still hard to go wrong picking one of these.

Green- A solid option, not fantastic but still not bad at all.

Orange- These options aren’t awful, but they may be situational or just plain boring.

Red- Bad! No touchy! Only take these for flavor, or if you legit have no other choice.

ABILITY SCORES:

With most classes, archetypes, and PrCs, there’s some wiggle room for ability score distribution, but not here. Depending on what class you started out with, there’s two real options:

Charisma needed: Str>Con=Cha>Dex>Wis>Int

Charisma not needed: Str>Con>Dex=Cha>Wis>Int

With that out of the way, there’s only one more thing to consider before you start building your character, which is your role in the party.

- Class Role -

As you probably know by now, there are a lot of roles that need to be filled in any given adventuring party. Treantmonk outlined them, and I’ve renamed them here to make them a bit nicer.

The Face: You’ll more than likely have a high Charisma, which makes it easier to pull this one off, but considering that the Hellknight class and the majority of the good options for entry into the PrC have a measly 2 ranks/lvl and your Int will probably suck anyways, you probably will not have the ranks to make this possible. If you rolled a really high Int, then go for it, but otherwise? Drop it.

The Sneak: Don’t. Just.. don’t. Your class features pivot around you wearing fancified full plate, and you don’t have the skill ranks for Stealth and Disable Device. Do your party a favor and skip out on this one.

The Healer: If you want to play a support Hellknight, check out the Signifier. You’re playing a full martial class here. Starting with a class like the Warpriest, Inquisitor, or Paladin might make this orange, but you’ll be losing out on so much level progression that you’ll be hard-pressed to make this effective.

The Caster: Again, check out the Signifier if you want to play a caster Hellknight. This PrC is made explicitly for martial characters,

Front Line Skirmisher: This is absolutely the easiest role to build and play. Pump Str, take Power Attack, hit everything you can as hard as you can. With a full BAB and d10 Hit Die, you’ll be very conveniently prepared to both deal and take some hefty hits. It may take you a round to catch up to the bad guys with your slowed movement speed, but you’ll hit hard once you get there.

Rain of Arrows: This is hard to pull off due to the massive amounts of feats that a good archer will need, but if you’re starting with a Human Fighter it’s doable. Your tankiness is wasted in the back lines, though, and your Hellknight Plate caps your Dex before you can use it as much as you’d probably like.

The Assassin: So hear me out here- with the right build choices, you can get access to some real nice teleportation effects, and your single-target damage is amazing. You won’t be an assassin in the conventional sense, but you can get some sweet anime action going with this one.

The Tank: And this is your best bet. With full BAB, d10 Hit Dice, and specially made, megasexy full plate armor? Your main shtick will be to stand directly in the middle of your caster friends and the bad guys, and become a terrifyingly insurmountable wall of steel between them. You will make combat as hard as possible for the bad guys, with some control disciplines to negate casters and smite chaos to keep the BBEG under your thumb. If you’ve got the feats for it, take advantage of things like trip & reach weapons to be a further annoyance to those pesky foes.

- Class Features -

Prerequisites: Must be lawful, proficient in heavy armor, BAB +5, and a few skill ranks. That’s all fluff, though- what you’re really looking at is the trial by combat. I really can’t do this justice on my own- if you haven’t already, I’d recommend reading Path of the Hellknight. It’s one of the best fluff books Paizo has put out, and it gets pretty detailed on the specifics of the trial.

Skill Points: 2 per level. Literally the worst you could get.

Hit Dice: D10, pretty much the best you can get unless you’re a barbarian.

BAB: Full BAB, you’ll be getting a lot of use from Power Attack.

Saves: Will is the scariest one to be losing out on, and your previous class compared with your high Con should keep your Fort save good. Reflex will suck, but your hp will be high enough to deal with a rogue fireball or two.

Class Skills: ...Eh? Perception is cool, you probably had Intimidate already, Sense Motive is a bit situational, but still neat. Could be worse.

Aura of Law: Complete fluff, will have next to no impact on your actual gameplay. It’s cool fluff, though.

Detect Chaos: Super useful depending on your campaign, but then again, if you’re not regularly facing chaotic enemies, it wouldn’t make sense to have an adventuring Hellknight, so.. I like it.

Smite Chaos: Same as above, if you’re playing this class you’ll be using this a lot. Pick one bad guy, beat the hell out of them. There are some discipline abilities that amplify this a bit, but if you really want to make this usable, I’d pump your Cha as much as you can.

Order: Your choice of order is what further defines your devotion to law. Every order has its own tenets, abilities, and disciplines, and while your choice won’t have a massive impact on your playstyle, it’ll change up your RP quite a bit.

Discern Lies: A bit situational in your typical dungeon run campaign, but if you’re playing a bit more RP-heavy, this is great.

Hellknight Armor: The iconic ability of this class, and what places you a cut above a basic Fighter tank. You’re faster, lower ACP, and higher Dex cap. Basically a better Armor Training.

Disciplines: A lot of these are just fantastic. You can specialize your playstyle here, grabbing buffs & debuffs as you deem appropriate, as well as a bit of utility. I love them.

Force of Will: The fact that it applies against such a specific group of abilities sort of blows, but at the same time, some of these are going to be the spells that will absolutely destroy you if you fail against one of those spells. Good, just wish it were better.

Lawbringer: Situational, as pretty much nothing other than chaotic outsiders are going to have DR/lawful, but if you’re facing a lot of those it’s good.

Infernal Armor: The Charisma check bonus is going to be used extremely rarely, but it’s the defensive buffs that matter here. Practically immune to fire, as well as resist 10 to the next two most common types. Plus, perfect darkvision at any range. I love this, and so should you.

Hell’s Knight: A free +1 or +2 bonus to any weapon you want. The bonuses you get aren’t the best, but it’s useful nonetheless. You get fire proper immunity, too, which is cool.

- Fighting Styles -

One Big Weapon: This is gonna be hard to screw up. Buff up your Strength, get Power Attack, think about Vital Strike if you just really need mobility super super bad. Grab a greatsword, or maybe a falchion for those higher crit ranges.

One-Handed Weapon: This will probably entail a shield as well, just no feat investment into it. I’d recommend a quick-draw shield with the Quick Draw feat- you can take out & put away your shield as a free action, meaning during your turn you attack with two-handed benefits and get the AC bonus out of your turn. You probably won’t have the feats to make this really good, but your AC will be higher, just at the cost of a feat.

Reach Weapon: Same benefits of the One Big Weapon, but a little less damage. You’ll get more area control, and the higher Dex cap that comes with your Hellknight Plate lets you get a bit more usage out of Combat Reflexes, potentially turning you into a AoO machine.

Ranged Weapon: You don’t have the Dex for this, and you don’t have the feats for this. Bring a bow along anyways, just for those occasions where you need the range, but it shouldn’t be your main weapon.

Two-Weapon Fighting: Like ranged weapons, but so much worse. You don’t have the Dex, and you don’t have the feats. Do yourself a favor and forget that this option exists.

Sword & Board: More than likely, this is what you think of when you imagine anything with the word “knight” in the name. Unfortunately, this requires a lot of resources to make it really good. I’ll say it plainly, you don’t have those. However, AC is nice, and if you don’t mind losing out on damage and range, this is a solid okay.

Natural Weapons: They’re an okay backup, but I would never recommend building around them. In almost every single situation it’s possible to do way better with actual weapons, and this is no exception. Orange if your race gives you a few for free, red otherwise.

- Entry Classes -

In order to take a level in this PrC, your character sheet needs a few things.

Lawful Alignment: Obvious, and easy.

Heavy Armor Proficiency: Also obvious, but unfortunately might take a feat for some classes.

BAB +5: Unfortunately, kicks back medium BAB classes to level 7.

Skill Ranks: Makes it real hard for those Fighters/Paladins who dumped Int and lack ranks.

You have a lot of freedom here, but I’m going to assume you want in on this as soon as possible, so entry level will play a role in my rankings.

Antipaladin: I’m conflicted about this class. All the annoying teamwork-centered parts of the Paladin are gone, even giving your enemies penalties to saves against your fear effects. The scaling abilities though, like your cruelties, touch of corruption, spells, and channeling, will be completely useless, and your skill ranks are so low that without a high Int you’ll only have 3 free ranks at level 5. Plus, in most games, a strong evil aura will probably get you killed. On top of all that, you’ll need to take the Tyrant archetype to get rid of that Chaotic alignment. Fearmonger is interesting, and if you pair it with an Intimidate build, you just might be able to make it work really well. Up to you.

Barbarian: In order for this to even sort of work, you’ll need to convince your GM that Barbarians can be Lawful. If you get away with it, then you’ll need to worry about armor proficiency, which will absorb one of your oh-so-precious feat slots unless you take Armored Hulk. Rage is, though, as always, amazing. A +4 on my two most important stats and a bonus on Will saves? Yes, please. Armored Hulk makes this amazing, in my eyes, as not only is your damage going to be buffed, but you’ll have more hit points, better defenses, and be faster.

Bloodrager: In my eyes, this is just a worse barbarian if you’re not going to be fully leveling in it. Your bloodline abilities likely scale with level, which you won’t have, and spells are next to nothing. If you wanna be a big angry tank man, play Barbarian.

Cavalier: Thematically, they make for super cool knights. Take the Horse Master feat ASAP once you start in the PrC, and you’ll be okay. Do not pick a non-horse mount. Just do not do it.

 

Fighter: Of course this is good. You’re a fully customizable murder machine with 3 extra feats before level 5, with the only downside being your skill pool. But then Paizo just had to go and make my day by introducing the Armiger archetype- trade one bonus feat for a better version of bravery and eliminate your need for skills. If you want an easy in, this is it.

Gunslinger: If you haven’t got access to advanced firearms, there’s barely a point in playing this class, and it is very very red. Even if you do, they don’t exactly make for great Hellknights, as there’s next to no overlap in their abilities, but I suppose you could make it work if you’re dedicated enough.

Inquisitor: I wish this was better, I really do. Unfortunately, with a late start in the PrC, no heavy armor proficiency, as well as near every single class feature becoming obsolete with multiclassing, the Inquisitor just isn’t meant to wear that Hellknight plate.

Magus: Same deal as the Inquisitor, except worse in every way. Int should never be your primary stat, and if it is, something here is very wrong. Your damage will suck, your AC will suck, your spells will suck, and your hp will suck. Skip this one.

Monk: Everything about these two classes wants to fight. Sure, monks can be frontliners too, but not a single ability here is worth bringing into the PrC.

Paladin: Now this is very nice. Full BAB, good Hit Dice, synergetic abilities, and the ability to stack smites? The only problematic things here are going to be your lay on hands and your spells- once you start leveling in Hellknight, those wink out in usefulness pretty quick. There are some archetypes to alleviate this, however. Divine Guardian is interesting, giving you an extra option for the Bodyguard feat tree if you choose to take it. Iomedaen Enforcer is purple if you can convince your GM to let smite chaos stack, green otherwise (you’ll still have the extra uses, just weaker). Legate seems cool, but the armor you summon is based on caster level and will be useless once you get your plate. Temple Champion, in my opinion, is the one archetype you should never leave behind when playing a Paladin/Hellknight combo. Replace some useless class features (to you, anyways) with your choice of literally anything offered by a Lawful deity. Your options are limitless, though my personal favorite is the Travel domain/blessing- your mobility will be terrifying.


Ranger: Some bonus feats and small combat buffs, but altogether you’ll be able to pull together a Fighter with better synergy than this. Your companion/spells immediately become useless as soon as you start leveling into Hellknight. Skirmisher and Infiltrator both give you some neat options, but hardly worth taking the entire class for.

Rogue: The idea of a sneak-attacking steel behemoth is more than a little funny to me, but unfortunately, not funny enough to warrant using this class to me. Your BAB and hp suffer and the only thing you’ve got going in your favor is a few spare d6 of damage. Subpar at best.

Samurai: I’ll be honest, I just don’t like this class. It’s always felt like a pale imitation of the Cavalier to me, and judging by the complete lack of support for it from Paizo, they feel the same. It’s not an awful idea to make this work with the Hellknight PrC, but I personally would choose something else.

Slayer: Slayer is honestly a really cool class. Unfortunately, it just doesn’t give us much to work with here. You don’t have armor proficiency and your sneak attack is lacking, and the class itself lacks any strong motivators to make it an attractive choice for a Hellknight. Not awful, but not exactly good, either.

Swashbuckler: Sure, you’ll have nice hit points and be able to enter at level 6, but the class is strongly Dex-based and really does not like heavy armor. Hard pass.

Warpriest: Trying to make this work just will not work. You’re MAD as a hatter, losing your spells, and entering the PrC late. Boo!

- Races -

I realize that there are dozens upon dozens of races that are in this game, but for the purposes of this guide, I won’t be including very many of them. If you have a specific race you like and works well, use that one. If not, check these out.

CORE RACES

Dwarf: Dwarves are absolutely my favorite core race, but unfortunately, they just don’t work well here. The benefits of Slow and Steady are lost by your second level in the class, and you have a penalty to one of your key ability scores. However, they gain access to some really nice racial feats, making it a hard choice to pass them down when it comes to Cleave builds.

Elf: You are offered nothing to gain here, and everything to lose. Hard pass.

Gnome: For one, I hate gnomes. For two, they have a Strength penalty, small size, and slow speed. They’d just make awful knights.

Half-Elf: The floating stat boost is nice, and a lot of the racial traits are just nice to have. Nothing special, but not a bad choice.

Half-Orc: Floating stat boost again, love that, and the racial traits are a lot nicer. Ferocity is great for any tank, and depending on what alternate traits you pick, you can end up with luck bonuses to saves, intimidate boosts, darkvision, and any number of other neat things.

Human: Bet you didn’t see this one coming. Humans are uniquely good at anything- they get to increase whichever stat they like, they get help with the skill ranks problem, and a bonus feat to help move you along. We may be boring, but at least we’re good at it.

OTHER RACES

Aasimar: They’re good at everything. Manipulate those ability scores however pleases you, then reap the benefits. The racial feats are worth considering if you’ve nothing else to take. Especially thematic if you’re playing a Paladin.

Aphorite: Extremely thematic for a paragon of law, and not too shabby mechanically either. Ability scores are alright, but nothing special. Darkvision is nice, and protection from chaos will work nicely for you, as will some extra defensive boosts. Crystalline Dust is great, miss chances are always good on tanks.

Hobgoblin: A bit boring, but their canonical disposition works well with an order-obsessed zealot and their stats fit neatly. I like them.

Nagaji: Stat bonuses are perfect, you gain some neat new attacks, and extra AC. What more could you want?

Tiefling: Pretty much the same as the Aasimar. Shuffle those bonuses, then pick a racial feat or two and kick butt in every battle.

- Hellknight Orders -

“Need, menace, fear: every soul understands these masters… and they obey.” -Daidian Ruel

Canonically, it will be near impossible for you to join any of the Minor Orders, but maybe your GM is nicer than me when it comes to that. For those, all rules other than favored weapon are the same as their analogous major order.

The Chain:

Discipline: Adds a slow effect onto your smite chaos ability, which is amazing for shutting down a heavy hitter on the bad guy’s team.

Reckoning Bonus: +2 bonus on your favorite combat maneuvers. Yum.

Boons: First one is situational and a little sucky, but the next two are great.

The Gate:

Discipline: Summon some hellish friends. Once you get past 14th level, though, this stops scaling, so it goes down to orange.

Reckoning Bonus: +4 bonus against divination spells. Cool, but extremely situational.

Boons: These are meant for a signifier. Unsurprising, since the Gate is the order of spellcasters, but you should probably not take these.

The Godclaw:

Discipline: Gain some pretty awesome abilities, depending on your choice. I recommend the Travel domain, because the mobility and speed increases are amazing.

Reckoning Bonus: As long as your buddies are lawful, you heal more whenever they heal you. Fantastic for any tank.  

Boons: Some awesome debuffs for free, and the third boon gives you free auto-revive and megaheal.

The Nail:

Discipline: Amazing bonuses to movement speed and Strength, but the duration sucks.

Reckoning Bonus: I genuinely do not remember the last time I rolled this skill.  

Boons: Situational buff, then a borderline useless divination, then something your wizard should have been doing for five levels by now. No thanks.

The Pyre:

Discipline: Fun for forcing an NPC to do something they don’t want to. I like to think of it as an angrier geas.

Reckoning Bonus: Useful, until you become literally immune to fire as a feature of this class.

Boons: The first one sucks. For the second, fireball will never not be good, and the last one? Oh my god, it is so ridiculously powerful. Hey, that BBEG cultist not being too nice to you? Literally just absorb ALL of his class features.

The Rack:

Discipline: Dependent on a Will save, but if they fail? You’ve just shut down that caster for 1d4 rounds, giving you ample time to beat their defenseless selves into submission.

Reckoning Bonus: At the level at which you’re taking this, it is highly unlikely that you’ll come across any poisons whatsoever, rendering this somewhat useless.  

Boons: Just like your Discipline, but a higher DC and higher range. The next two are cool, but they pale in comparison.

The Scourge:

Discipline: Get low-light vision, and then, just for funsies, you get literal x-ray vision. Cool.

Reckoning Bonus: Take less bleed damage. Most bleeding is stopped by magical healing anyways, so by the time you save yourself 2 or 3 hp with this, it’ll already be fixed by your healer.

Boons: Some pretty nifty ways to buff and debuff after getting kills, and on the off chance it works, phantasmal killer is great.

- Disciplines -

Every 3 levels, you gain one of these. At 3rd, it has to be the one belonging to your order, at 6th, it can be any discipline not belonging to one individual order, and at 9th, it can be any discipline. Minor orders go off of their analogous orders, except for the Glyph and Torrent for some reason.

Brand (The Pyre): If you often find yourself with a need for forcing an NPC to do what you want by way of threat, this is an effective way to do it.

Call Armor: This is so incredibly cool to envision, but unfortunately, it’s hard to think of common, practical uses of this ability. If you feel like your build is already effective enough and just want something cool-looking, go for it.

Censor (The Rack): If you use Smite Chaos on a creature, they’re muted on a failed save. Incredible if you often find yourself up against caster bad guys.

Command: A number of enemies equal to your level have to do whatever you want on a failed save. If being charged by a small army, you can just make all of them run away in terror.

Dispel Chaos: If you frequently find yourself up against chaotic outsiders, this is good, but otherwise, the AC bonus isn’t enough to rate this too high.

Favored Quarry: Functions like a Ranger’s Favored Enemy, but only skill checks. You don’t really get enough of these to warrant wasting them on skill checks, which you’re already going to be bad at.

Fearsomeness: This is incredible for an Intimidate build. Cause your enemies to immediately become frightened on a successful check instead of shaken, which gives you massive impact on the battlefield.

Invoke Glyph (The Glyph): Cast glyph of warding 1/day, except you can only have one at any given time. You also get a bonus on saving throws against the spell, but both abilities are so incredibly situational that they’ll give little to no benefit in actual gameplay.

Mental Intrusion: This is a very cool ability. You can screw with your enemies as well as stealthily coordinate with your allies, both of which are pretty helpful.

Onslaught (The Nail): These are great bonuses, but they really just don’t last long enough to be much use.

Pentamic Faith (The Godclaw ONLY): This is unique in the fashion that only Hellknights of the Godclaw can select it, even at later levels. You can get some great bonuses from this. Personally, I like to pick the Travel domain.

Seek the Taken (The Torrent): Curiously, the wording in the source book allows a member of any order to choose this discipline at level 6, though members of the Torrent must take this at level 3. You can cast enlarged locate person, which is unfortunately a bit situational.  

Shackle (The Chain): When you use your smite ability, the enemy is targeted by slow on a failed save for 1d4 rounds. If a big bad guy is making way too many attacks or you just need to run away, this essentially shuts them down.

Shadow Step: Use enlarged shadow step, with character level as your CL. Grants you some nice mobility and speed, even if you aren’t going to be too stealthy with it.

Summon Devil (The Gate): This is useful if you entered the PrC at 6th level, and it scales nicely for a while, but stops being useful very quickly after you stop being able to level in Hellknight.

Summon Servant of Law: Same as above, except with Inevitables instead of Devils.

Trace: In my opinion, this is just a slightly different version of Seek the Taken. Same rating.

Tracker: This is just not good. Use Trace or Seek the Taken if you want this.

Versatile Intimidation: If you frequently find yourself stuck in social situations, this can be somewhat useful. You pick one skill and roll Intimidate instead, garnering the same effects, picking another one at level 9.

Vigilance (The Scourge): Low-light if you didn’t have it already, plus a cool x-ray vision ability. Nice for checking into the next room of the dungeon without having to bust the door down.

Wrack: The damage is awful, and the only useful part is based off a save. Ew.

- The Test -

“In order to become a Hellknight, one must literally face an immortal champion of Hell.”

Most of you, more likely than not, know nothing more about the Hellknight test than the couple of sentences offered in the PrC’s entry requirements. That’s fine, nothing against you, but as the resident expert on the executioners of order, I’m here to educate you. Since you probably don’t have access to a copy of Path of the Hellknight, I’m going to provide you with an exact excerpt from the book.

        “The Hellknight test requires each would-be Hellknight to slay a devil whose Hit Dice exceed her own. Such a test is typically performed to fulfill the requirements of the Hellknight or Hellknight signifer prestige class, which many classes can access by 5th level. As such, 6-HD bearded devils are the fiends most commonly battled during the test (higher-level initiates must face higher-level devils). Those taking the test are allowed to carry into battle any weapons, armor, wands, potions, and other equipment they wish. Additionally, they may receive the benefits of allies’ magic, so long as it is cast prior to the test’s start. The ceremony preceding the test typically lasts over an hour, meaning that spells with a duration in minutes typically wear off before combat begins. During the test, should an armiger receive aid from outside the testing ring, she is disqualified and imprisoned (along with her conspirators). Such cheaters typically face a brief trial and execution.”

As you can see, the rules require you to have built a fairly self-sufficient character. A Bearded Devil can deal some pretty big damage, and its Infernal Wound ability makes it hard for you to heal. The bleed damage puts the pressure on you to kill it quick, and its DR only makes that even harder. Here, I’ll list a few tips on ensuring you win this fight, as there’s no point in making this build if you die before even entering into the PrC.

SPELLS/BUFFS:

Going into the test, you are allowed to bring in any potions or oils that you like. May cost a bit of gold, but not dying is worth it. In addition, you can have spells cast on you before the ceremony begins, so long as they have a long enough duration.

Align Weapon: You’ll have to use an oil for this, but it lets you circumvent the enemy’s DR. Silver weapons or a silversheen will probably be more useful, though.

Barkskin: If you have a druid buddy whose CL is 6 or higher, ask them very nicely to cast this on you pre-ceremony. The AC bonus will last long enough to go into the fight, and you don’t have to spend a dime.

Bull’s Strength/Bear’s Endurance/Cat’s Grace: Duration means you have to get a potion, which will be more expensive than a 1st-level spell, and this really doesn’t give too great of a bonus to be wasting your first round on it.

Blur: If you’re worried that your AC is going to get you hit by too many attacks, this is an effective way of negating that damage. Needs a potion.

Cure Light/Moderate/Serious Wounds: Since you’ll have to take a round to use this potion, I would usually warn against bringing one in since the Barbazu can probably deal more damage in a round than this will heal, so it’s just a waste of money.

Enlarge Person: The duration on this is only min/level, so I’d recommend dropping 50 gp on a potion of this. For one, it’ll buff your damage, and for two, if you have a reach weapon, you can keep the enemy at 15 ft away and then 5 ft away on every round, meaning they have to waste a move action every round to get to you and can’t full attack.

Magic Weapon: Duration here isn’t long enough to be cast pre-test, and the duration isn’t long enough to warrant this being the potion you pop at the start of combat. I’d recommend skipping this.

Protection from Evil: It’s a little bit funny in how effective this is. You’ll have to bring a potion, but once you do, the devil is completely unable to hit you with natural attacks, meaning if you disarm it or take Step Up, you literally cannot be hit. I’d honestly feel bad using this.

Shield: Get a potion, and suddenly your bearded friend is having a very hard time touching you with any attacks. For those of you worried about your tankiness, this is a good buff.

Silver Weapons/Silversheen: You can buy a silver weapon for pretty cheap, which will circumvent a Barbazu’s DR. Alternatively, if you’re attached to your normal weapon, you can buy a silversheen for 250 gp, which you can apply before the fight to give any weapon you like the benefits of being silver.

TACTICS:

If you’re still not confident in your ability to destroy that Barbazu, here’s a few methods that give you a pretty much 100% success rate.

Stay at a Distance: By using any combination of a reach weapon, Lunge, and enlarge person, or even all three, you can make sure that you can attack at a distance of 15 ft or more. The devil can only attack at 10 ft, meaning that if you step away after every attack, he’s required to waste a move action to get close enough to hit you, preventing a full attack. This will cut the damage you take in half, and it’s a little funny to watch a soldier of Hell chase you around like a Scooby Doo monster.

Up Close and Personal: This mostly only works with a protection from evil spell. By doing that, you can disarm/sunder the devil’s glaive, and then proceed to be completely invulnerable to every single attack that the devil still has. Alternatively, you can use the Step Up feat to make it so that the Barbazu isn’t able to 5 ft away to a distance at which it can use the glaive, saving you a round and having the exact same effect.

TESTING AT A HIGHER LEVEL:

If you aren’t testing at 5 HD, it’s probably because your entry class was only ¾ BAB, and you’re more likely than not fighting a devil with 8 HD. Your two most likely contenders here are the Patraavex (Sire Devil) or a Levaloch (Warmonger Devil).

A sire devil’s most dangerous ability is its Caustic Blood- any time you deal piercing or slashing damage, you must make a DC 19 Reflex save or you and your weapon take 2d6 acid damage, on top of all the damage it can do to you. My recommendation for fighting one of these is to take a potion of protection from evil, then disarm the heavy flail and use a bludgeoning weapon.

A warmonger devil’s leg attacks makes it immune to the Up Close and Personal strategy- those can’t be disarmed, but to my knowledge, they don’t count as natural attacks. Thankfully, it isn’t armed with a reach weapon, meaning even though it’s Large size, you can still use the Stay at a Distance method. Grab a glaive or other such thing, then use Lunge/enlarge person to stay far enough away that he can’t hit you. It’ll probably use its Trample ability at that point, but a 1d8+7 is infinitely less painful at level 7 than all of its other attacks.

- Feats -

“True order can be achieved only when criminal behavior is quelled before it is committed- preferably before it is even considered.”

Luckily, there are no feat requirements for this PrC, so you’re free to customize your feat setup however you like. Go wild!

TEAMWORK FEATS:

        Personally, I don’t like these. They require both you and a party member waste a feat slot, and even after that, their use is situational and usually gives a very small bonus. If you’re really set on taking them, check out this guide.

GENERAL/COMBAT FEATS:

Additional Traits: An interesting choice if you regularly find yourself wanting more traits, but most of them aren’t good enough to warrant a whole feat.

Antagonize: It’s not as broken as it used to be before the errata, but it’s still pretty good. Your Charisma is already going to be great, and your Intimidate is literally required to be good before you enter the PrC. This feat is amazing. Take it.

Blind-Fight: I don’t really see this being useful commonly enough that taking this feat is worth doing. You’ll get use out of it once or twice, but that’s about it.

Bludgeoner: If you’re going Enforcer/Cornugon Smash, and you don’t have another way of reliably dealing nonlethal damage without a penalty, this is a good way to do it. If possible, I’d recommend trying to find a way to make this happen with a weapon with a higher crit range.

Boon Companion: Your only option for boosting a non-horse companion. If you have a horse, take Horse Master instead, because it is infinitely better.

Combat Expertise: Meeting that 13 Int prereq is rough, since it isn’t an important stat for you at all, but if your party doesn’t need any more damage this can be a better choice than Power Attack.

Improved Trip: Other combat maneuvers are cool & all, but this one is by far the best, and you won’t have the feats to afford anything but this one. Stops an opponent from moving, decreases their AC, and wastes their action economy.

Greater Trip: Takes a long time to get here, but it’s so worth it. Bonus on trip attempts, and when you succeed, they provoke an AoO.

Combat Reflexes: As a tank, more AoOs is always going to be great. With your heightened Dex cap from your Hellknight abilities,

Bodyguard: With your higher Dex cap, you may actually be able to take Combat Reflexes and have enough AoOs to make this worth taking. +2 to AC isn’t a whole lot, though, so you’re mostly only taking this for the follow-up.

In Harm’s Way: You’re gonna have a lot of hitpoints, and probably a lot of AC. You can afford to take a few hits for your allies, and it’ll make them really like you if you do it.

Critical Feats: If you have a weapon with a high crit range, these will be very useful. There’s way too many of these to list them all here, but you should be able to tell what will be helpful and what won’t be once you’ve been playing long enough to get this late in the game.

Darting Viper: If you’re proficient in it, switch the Dorn-Dergar from one-handed to reach faster. Because it’s exotic, may be too feat-intensive as a non-Dwarf, so tread lightly.

Dorn-Dergar Mastery: One of very, very few ways to wield a reach weapon in one hand. Unfortunately, requires TWF, which you probably will not have.

Deadly Aim: You will not be in melee range all the time. At later levels, you can work on teleportation abilities, but if you don’t do that, you’ll probably be carrying a composite bow. This makes your backup attacks stronger.

Deathless Initiate: As an orc/half-orc you automatically qualify for this. As a tank, this is basically just an effective increase to your hit points, which will never not be good for you.

Dirty Fighting: If you don’t have the 13 Int, and you want to get those combat maneuver feats, this is the option for you. Frees up your point buy for nicer things, like health and damage.

Dodge: I don’t particularly like these “One feat for 1 AC” tradeoffs. However, this one leads into some interesting feat trees, so if you’re building into those, it’s worth considering.

Endurance: Nonlethal damage is incredibly rare to get smacked with, but the feats this leads to are pretty neat. Worth considering as a Fighter.

Diehard: For a tank, this is great. You’ll have a high Con anyways, so this is basically just an effective increase to your hit points.

Stalwart: Only use this if you’re going with Combat Expertise. Turns your AC bonus into DR/--, which, if you’re facing hordes of small enemies instead of one big one on a regular basis, is way better.

Improved Stalwart: Take this as soon as you hit 11 BAB. Who else can brag DR 10/-- at level 16?

Enforcer: If you’re playing Intimidate, this can be used to deadly efficiency in concert with Hurtful, plus massively increases the duration of your demoralizing when you use it. This is sort of an alternative to Cornugon Smash, trading the lethal for nonlethal damage in exchange for massively increasing demoralization duration.

Horse Master: If you’re a Cavalier, this feat is a must-have. If not, it’s useless.

Hurtful: If you’re playing an Intimidate build, this is a must-have. Attack at your full BAB as a swift action? Yes, please. Mix with Enforcer or Cornugon Smash for maximum efficiency.

Improved Initiative: This is good for locking down that caster before they can start buffing, as well as just generally placing yourself in charge of the battlefield.

Intimidating Prowess: If you’re going off of an Intimidate build, you probably want your bonus as high as possible, and this is what gets you there. It isn’t necessary, but it’s very useful.

Ironhide: If you’re an Orc or a Dwarf, this is neat. Not much else to say. It’s exactly okay.

Fleet: Nobody ever really wants to take this, but sometimes you do it at level 19 because there just isn’t anything else.

Leadership: It’s either broken or banned. I would personally recommend not taking it, as it’s really mean to your GM, but if you insist, it’s very purple.

Lunge: This is amazing for you. Saves your action economy by letting you reach enemies a bit easier, and for those of you with reach weapons, your aura of death just because a 35-ft diameter circle. Take this.

Mounted Shield: You’re going to be pretty tough to hit, and if you have a mount, they’re gonna look like a pretty easy target. A must-have for mounted Hellknights if you don’t have the cash to invest in mount defense.

Power Attack: When has this ever not been a good idea for a full BAB character? Especially good for those of you working with two-handed weapons.

Cleave: This can be a little tough to set up, as usually enemies will go for flanking instead of sitting right next to each other. Still, all the attacks are at full BAB, so you’re nearly guaranteed to hit.

Great Cleave: If it was rare for two enemies to sit next to each other, it’s even rarer for 3+. You won’t be getting enough use out of this to warrant spending another one of your precious few feats.

Goblin Cleaver/Orc Hewer: If you’re playing a Dwarf, this is why. Take that Dorn-Dergar and wreak absolute AoE havoc on the battlefield. Unfortunately, the feat tax to get to this point is pretty high, but these feats are useful nonetheless. I’d recommend getting a reliable source of enlarge person instead of investing in Orc Hewer.

Furious Focus: Unless you’re going Vital Strike, you usually won’t be getting enough use out of this to make it worth taking a feat for.

Cornugon Smash: For those Intimidate builds, this is gonna be your bread and butter. Roll Intimidate to debuff, follow up with this and deal good damage. If you want good damage, mix this with the Hurtful feat.

Racial Heritage: If you want a Cleave build but don’t want to deal with the Charisma penalty of a Dwarf, this is how you’ll get those Dwarf-exclusive feats. Thankfully, humans get an extra feat at level one, so this could be perfect for you.

Saving Shield: If you don’t have the Dex to invest in Combat Reflexes and you don’t need/want to take In Harm’s Way, this is probably a better option than taking Bodyguard for you. If you don’t have a lot of other things vying for your swift actions, this isn’t actually too much of an action absorber.

Shatter Defenses: Once you have a good number of attacks per round, this is going to be great for you. After a round or two, your e        nemies should be shaken already anyways, and after you hit those guys once, they’ll be flat-footed against all your attacks for the rest of the round. Basically gives you guaranteed attacks against them.

Shield Focus: There are a lot of better feats that you could take at earlier levels, but some of the things that have this as a prereq are cool. If you like the idea of focusing on your shield, this is a good choice for you.

Missile Shield: If you have a bit of Dex, you basically get the benefit of Deflect Arrows. This is fantastic for deflecting that first, deadly arrow from an archer.

Ray Shield: It takes a while to get here, which is going to be really hard to get away with unless you started as a Fighter. Same thing as the last feat, but now against spells.

Shield Specialization: Only an option for Fighters, and even then, kinda sucks. Wear a Dwarven Boulder Helm if you’re that concerned about a crit.

Step Up: If someone is trying to 5 ft away in order to avoid your AoOs, this shuts them down.

Toughness: Extra hit points! You’re a tank, you like those.

Quick Draw: Useful for exactly two things. One, if you’re a switch hitter, then this becomes mandatory. Two, a Quick-Draw Shield. Mixing the two of them means you can draw or stow that shield as a free action, which means you can be dealing two-handed damage during your attacks and then pick your shield right back up for those bonuses the second you start to move or end your turn. It’s a little cheesy, but I love this trick.

Skill Focus: Not great. BUT, it then lets you lead your way into…

Eldritch Heritage (Improved and Greater): Your Charisma should be high enough to make good use of this. At first, not super useful, but as you continue to level, you can grab some quite frankly amazing benefits. Check this guide out!

Vital Strike: This is good if you have a lot of abilities that you would prefer to spend your move action on, and you really like staying mobile in combat, but honestly, the damage just kind of sucks. If you’re using a greatsword and are frequently running after the enemy, then go ahead.

Improved/Greater Vital Strike: At this point, you are spending way too many feats on an extremely subpar ability. If you have a weapon that deals 8d6 damage or something, then fine, but otherwise, stay away.

Weapon Finesse: Cool feat usually, but absolutely useless for you. You’re literally wearing full plate. Stop it.

Weapon Focus: +1 on attack rolls. Not bad, just bland. Intimidate builds should consider this, though, if only to take…

Dazzling Display: Good for those of you investing in Intimidate, and very thematic. I personally love the mental picture of a giant behemoth in twisted black armor charging into the fray and terrifying everyone who sees him.

- Traits -

There’s already a massive guide for traits, and I am not bored enough to outline all of them here, so I’ll just name a few honorable mentions. If you want an in-depth study, go here.

Adopted: This is as good as the trait you use it with. Rainbow!

Armor Expert: a -1 reduction in your armor check penalty. You won’t be expected to be good at skills anyways, but this will help a bit.

Blade of Mercy: For those of you using the Enforcer feat, this lets you avoid spending a feat on Bludgeoner and lets you use a weapon with a bigger crit range.

Defender of the Society: It’s got your name in it and grants an extra +1 AC when wearing medium or heavy armor, which you will be.

Disillusioned: This is a significant bonus against some pretty destabilizing debuffs.

Hard to Kill: Reduces the Stabilize DC by a good amount, very useful.

Heirloom Weapon: If you think you can get away with using the same weapon all throughout the game, this is a pretty good way of nabbing an extra +1 to hit.

Hellknight Initiate: Makes your Hellknight trial so much easier, plus it’s thematic.

Helpful: If you have any of the other Aid Another focused abilities this grants another +1 bonus.

Influence: Gain your choice of Diplomacy, Intimidate, or Sense Motive as a class skill. If you want to use Antagonize but are missing one of these skills this is the perfect time to get it.

Magical Knack: If you started with Paladin and want to keep a few spell slots, this is worth looking at.

Sargavan Guard: Just as good as Armor Expert, and allows you to take another combat trait you want more.

Second Chance: A reroll on any saving throw? This is amazing, and an amazing use of a religion trait.

Steel Skin: Don heavy armor in half the normal time, get a free helmet with your family’s symbol, and gain a +2 bonus to Intimidate when wearing the helm with armor. I would honestly rate this green as a whole feat.

Threatening Defender: Reduce the attack penalty by 1 when using Combat Expertise. If you plan to go down the Stalwart feat tree this is a must.

To the Last: If you frequently find yourself in solo combat situations, this is good to stack on top of Adamantine or Stalwart.

Unnatural Presence: If you’re going Intimidate, giving it more utility is always good.
