Akashic Mysteries Guide 1: The Vizier

The Vizier is the first of the classes in the new Akashic Mysteries system, Dreamscarred Press’s port of the old Incarnum system. Many thanks to Ssalarn for being the one to bring it forward! While a lot of the names have changed, the core and soul (ba-dum tish) of the system haven’t.

Long story short on the function of Akasha, for those who don’t know: You create effects called Veils, tied to certain slots on your body called Chakras. Only one of any given Veil, and only one at any given Chakra. You can charge them with bits of your soul, called Essence, to increase their power. As you grow in power, you learn how to step up their effects even more by increasing their capacity for Essence, gaining more Essence in your total pool, and by tying the Veils closely to their respective Chakras. This process is called Binding. The biggest advantage of it all is flexibility, you always have the ability to change most of your build overnight, and you can shift your bonuses around to meet whatever challenges you are facing with a Swift action to reallocate your Essence where it will be most beneficial.

For those coming from Incarnum: Binding no longer locks you out of magic items in the slot, and all three classes have distinct lists (with some overlap). Viziers are the new Incarnate, Daevics look like Soulborns but work like Totemists, and Gurus are a new breed. Almost nothing locks Essence for 24 hours anymore unless it wouldn’t mechanically work otherwise.

Now that the introductions are over, here’s how to read the guide. I will, like everyone else, be using a color-based system to rate various aspects, features, feats, veils, etc. I’m a bit colorblind, though, so Treantmonk’s original doesn’t work so well for me. Here is what I’ll be using in this guide:

RED is a terrible option, and should be avoided in nearly every build. If it’s a class feature, expect to not use it.

ORANGE is generally bad, but might be useful to some builds.

YELLOW is medium-range. Something to take if you don’t have anything else to do. If it’s a class feature, then you will likely be using it whenever your main deal is offline for whatever reason.

BLUE is good, something most builds will want to take a look at and/or use frequently.

SKY BLUE is pure excellence, and/or should be taken by nearly everyone at first opportunity

The Vizier Class:

The Vizier is a specialist in the Mysteries of Akasha, much like a Wizard is a specialist in Arcane magic. All of their abilities stem from channeling this energy, whether through objects, enemies, or allies. On the old Tier List, he probably lands right in the middle of T3, capable individually but shining in flexibility.

Chassis:

HD: d6 means that you aren’t going to be standing up to very much punishment if it comes your way, so try to avoid situations where it will. Thankfully, you are full of class features which will usually let you stay away from the front lines.

Skills: Only 2+Int per level isn’t good, especially given how many you want to take, but you do have Int as a primary casting stat to help make up for it. The gem here, though, is your list of class skills. Perception, Bluff, Diplomacy, Sense Motive, Spellcraft, and Knowledge (ANY) all feature, plus certain Craft skills might come into play depending on the choices you make.

Proficiencies: Simple Weapons is decent for the chosen role, though most often this will not be relevant long. Light Armor and Bucklers, however, give you places to hang valuable enhancements later without needing to worry about nonproficiency. Well, same goes for the weapons, to be honest.

BAB: Poor Base Attack is to be expected from a caster-style class… but you have an edge on this particular drawback that takes some reading to find. Most of your Veils and other effects can use your Veilweaver Level instead of BAB, so this will only matter when you’re stuck using those Simple Weapons.

Saves: Good Fortitude and Will with Poor Reflex. If you have to have one bad save, then Reflex is the one least likely to be from an SOS/SOD. Can’t complain here.

Stat Priorities:

This will vary greatly from build to build. Strength is dumpable unless you’re in a very strange melee variation (remember, you have half BAB). Dexterity is very important for just about any way you do it (light armor and bucklers only), but especially so for those focused on Ranged combat. Constitution is always good, and a minimum of 13 is required for several critical feats (15 for a couple of nice ones). Int is your primary Veilweaving stat… but if you don’t plan on using save-based veils often, you won’t need more than 10-12 for skills. Wisdom is good only for Will saves (and Perception), and Charisma is only used if you’re going for the social side of things. So, for example:

Ranged Combat: DEX>CON>>INT>WIS>CHA>>>STR

“Caster” save-based: INT>DEX=CON>CHA>WIS>>>STR

Class Features:

Veilweaving: The rating here should be REALLY FREAKING OBVIOUS; if you are here at all it is probably for this. Viziers get far and away the best Essence/Veils/Binds progression, all of the standard Bind locations, a unique Chakra (which eventually doubles up), a great selection of Veils for all occasions, and almost more importantly than that you are Int-Based for your Veilweaving effects that have saves. This is good for the same reason Wizards casting off of Int is a good thing for them, you have a LOT of skills you want to take and few natural skill points to do so with. It is worth noting, however, that you only use Int to calculate saves with. If you don’t intend to use Veils which allow saves, you don’t need much here.

Eldritch Insight: This title has several abilities under it, which should be individually reviewed. Different parts will have different importance for different builds. Class levels Counting as Arcane Caster Levels is nice to have if you are multiclassing, but VERY good when you are using a Stave or other items that keys off of your own caster level. Item Investment, though, will likely matter to just about everyone. Free charges in limited-use items is always good, and not needing to UMD items with such investment means you don’t have to cross-class, change stats, or bust feats and traits for it. Problem is, this use locks the Essence for 24 hours, chipping away at your vaunted flexibility. Then again… we’re talking about free charges here. Think of it as saving a couple of gold per charge (depending on the item).

Mystic Attunement: This choice defines how you are planning on doing what you do. There are three distinct Paths from which to choose, each with its own method of doing what it does.Each has three distinct benefits. These are full of VERY powerful benefits to you and your team, so choose wisely!

-Path of the Crafter:  For those of you who miss the old Artificer, these guys channel their power through magic items. They learn to utilize them in many unique ways that others simply cannot match. They start off with a bonus to all skill checks made as part of the crafting process. The next benefit to taking this path is a +1 increase to the DC or Caster Level of any spell or spell-like effect used from a magic item by an ally within 30 feet… and remember that you count as your own ally. Enjoy making and buying such things as 8th-level wands of Magic Missile for maximum benefit. Transfer the Essence is another ability, allowing you to, with an hour of time, transfer all properties between two magic items assuming those properties would be legal on both. Or, you could transfer any number of charges between charged items of like-level spells. No such thing as useless loot anymore! Crafters are also always able to analyze magic with Spellcraft as though under the effects of Detect Magic. All abilities so far are gained AT FIRST LEVEL. Lastly, they end up getting as bonus feats over time many of the Item Creation Feats

*A note on item crafting* When you are making a Magic Item, for the most part the “Requirements” section is more like a set of loose guidelines. As long as it isn’t a Potion, Spell-Trigger (Wands and Staves), or Spell Completion (Scrolls) Item, you don’t need access to the base spells to be able to make it, just increase the Spellcraft DC by 5 for each. If it IS one of those items, the Vizier can make it anyway for a slight DC increase (3+spell level). Given your bonuses to skills rolls for this, plus veils, anything short of 3-4 such penalties will usually end up with a good chance to craft anyway. You can use spells from any source to make those items, such as friendly spellcasters, scrolls, prior staves or wands, etc. Charged items are particularly good for you, since Item Investment is a thing. Finally, if you’re getting ready to make a complicated item and are iffy on the DC, you can ease it off by “chaining” into it by making simpler items of those spells to reduce the harder DC by 5 (or more) each. Just remember that this will greatly increase your costs and the time needed.

-Path of the Seer: For those who like to support their allies, Seers focus on giving out Teamwork feats and eventually the effects of their Veils. They start off by giving a large-radius aura of Improved Speed, which only gets better over time. He also gets to pick Teamwork Feats up for free over time, and can invest Essence in order to Share them ALL with his allies. Remember that drawback, where those feats were almost never taken because everyone had to have them to benefit? Well, now they all do. You can also use your level instead of BAB to qualify, plus remember you have a caster level. This also allows them to use a Veil you have bound to Hand or Feet, though they can’t bind it and have to fuel it with their own Essence, if they have any. The final ability of the path allows you to retrain all feats gained through it every day, which is VERY useful.

-Path of the Ruler: For those who want to utilize their will-save Veils, most of which are social or otherwise help avoid direct combat. You radiate an aura that penalizes Will and Sense Motive, and scales up to a -3 and -6, eventually allowing you to force rerolls. This ignores allies equal to your Int mod, until you gain the ability to designate them to instead gain +2 will. Just make sure your Int is high enough to get them all (or all the ones who care about Will saves… which actually should be all of them).

Chakra Binds were covered under Veilweaving

Improved Essence Capacity: Now here is one of your bigger edges. You can hold way more than the usual amount of Essence in any given Veil/Feat/Item/Feature, matched only by Gurus. Given how you can change the target of your Essence on a Swift action’s notice, this means you can stack up much higher bonuses than it may appear, which will be relevant almost constantly

Veilshifting: Remember that one nagging weakness you had, where you might have the wrong Veils shaped to face a given challenge? Doesn’t occur as much when you can change out your veils on the fly a few times per day. This ability scales exponentially as you go, usable more and more often to redo more of your Veils at a time.

Twinveil Ring: It’s like a bonus feat that stacks with itself! The unique Ring slot is home to several powerful effects on the Bind, and doubling this up is usually going to be a part of your strategy.

Chakra Rebirth: There is no longer any such thing as being unprepared. Period. If you have three second’s warning, you can cause yourself to be properly configured for anything which might arise.

Notes: You notice how much Blue there is? The Vizier is a mighty good class at what it does, but thankfully no one effect really goes into game-breaker territory. They can set themselves up for success overnight and know all of their relevant abilities without the need to refer to a spellbook. Thing is, everything depends on the Veils. Speaking of…

Vizier Veils:

There is a lot to consider when choosing your Veils. First off, the effects for just having them woven and for powering them with Essence are high on the list, along with the effects on Bind. How do these effects further your goals for the day? In addition, each is competing with the others in its slot, since without a feat you can only have one at each location (two if you pick up the Double Chakra feat, later). Because of this, I will be grouping the Veils by Chakra and noting what level the appropriate Bind comes online in order to properly compare them.

Note that you are not limited to your Bind locations when weaving Veils in general, you can form any of them from your list. You just can’t get the Bind effect until it opens for you. For the Vizier, however, you will always have nearly as many Binds available as Veils at any given time, so there is little reason not to if you can.

Finally, a tactics and choice note. Essence can be reallocated every turn as long as you have your Swift free. It is perfectly fine to choose a Veil that you don’t expect to use in all situations (or one with lackluster Essence effects), since in nearly all cases you can just keep it empty and use the spare fuel in the veils that are currently applicable. By default, the Vizier gets 30 Essence and 70 places to put it (not counting Item Investment or Seer Aura), there will always be SOMEWHERE to use the spares. This also goes for Feat choices later.

Hand Chakra (Lvl 2): There are six Veils here, mostly combat effects. Expect to use these HEAVILY as early weapons, and most will have some niche use at least throughout your career.

Hands of the Bard: The Base and Essence effects are nigh-useless to you unless you are multiclassing, and very (VERY) few abilities are Pattern-based, all of which are mind-affecting Illusions (notoriously easy to get bonuses against or immunity to). Binding gives access to a Fascinate boosted by the Veil’s own effect, but that status is weak and falls off in effectiveness quickly. You may use this for a few levels, but don’t count on it for long.

Lashing Spinnerets: This is for mobility rather than straight offense, but boy can it move you. The base effect, with all of one Essence invested, can shift you 60 feet as a Move + Swift action. Works in all three dimensions as well. This is my slot default, assuming I don’t have a reason to use the others. Bind effect here won’t be all that useful immediately, but can provide a good place to rest by the time you’re able to put 3-4 essence into it. Has a Wrist bind, which I will cover there.

Loyal Paladin’s Spear of Light: Melee, but you can use your Veilweaver level in place of your BAB and it gets very good amounts of reach plus solid damage. The Bind effect is a Line of Light, letting you get in on the areas of effect early. Good for ripping chunks out of formations or enemies in tunnels (or Swarms, notably).

Riven Darts: Base effect is Force damage on a ranged touch attack, which is always nice. Essence increases this damage, but the gem is on the Bind. It effectively causes the Veil to scale exponentially with Essence, and being able to dish out constant streams of Force Darts is a very nice thing to have in combat. By level 18 this can be averaging 160 damage per turn alone and without item support (40 per target), using only your Standard action. You can only target one enemy with two of the darts, but it’s still incredible for small groups of targets or just as a fallback Standard action.

Snakehandler’s Gauntlets: The Base and Essence effect is REALLY nice early on, but fade out fast. Aligned attacks are common once you start facing Outsiders regularly. Poison resistance is also nice. The Bind effect is great just as long as enemies aren’t immune, free poison should not be overlooked.

Storm Gauntlets: The Base effect shouldn’t be used by you, since you are unlikely to be any good at landing attacks in melee. Essence effects adds up quick, remember that the damage listed is dealt once EACH in Fire, Cold, and Electric. Bound to the Hands, this becomes a potentially powerful ranged weapon (3d3 from level 2) as long as you can make the ranged touch attack rolls and the enemy isn’t immune (it’s awesome if they are vulnerable to one of the damage types). Falls off a bit later as elemental resistances become more common, but few things will ever be resistant to your entire blast. This has a Wrist bind, which I will go over later.

Feet Chakra (Lvl 4): This is a VERY crowded slot, with 7 potential veils base to choose from. There are some big winners and losers here to look at, as well. Many have effects related to movement or transportation.

Coward’s Boots: Starting off with one of the big winners, untyped bonuses to base movement speed is a valuable effect. I default to this Veil if my Feet are open and I have any extra Veils to be shaped. The Bind effect is just as good, since if you’re moving you’ll likely have the Veil stuffed with Essence for a really good AC bonus… and the feat it emulates also Counts As Mobility, which depending on the DM (and this includes both Paizo’s devs and Ssalarn’s intent) will let you skip a feat on the way to things like Shot on the Run. If you are a Seer and not sharing the Pestilence Cloak, this is probably the one you want to spread around as well.

Horselord’s Greaves: When you just want to get away. The skill bonuses from the Veil and Essence are mediocre, unless you are doing a lot of riding. Obviously much better if you are. The Bind effect to cast Mount at-will is excellent if you need to get from place to place, though, and if you think you might get attacked while mounted the Essence bonus under Bind is VERY helpful. For laughs, convince the DM that riding in the Barbarian’s backpack counts as being mounted on him and give him some bonus AC , HP, and saves. Has a Belt bind as well, which will be covered there.

Lavawalker’s Boots: Another bit of alternate movement, this one over any liquid. The ability to pump Essence for Fire and Acid resist is a nice touch, in case your DM believes in physics while you attempt to actually walk on lava. The Bind effect is fun, though, just as long as you can find (or make) the terrain to take advantage of it.

Pestilence Cloak: Concealment is good. Mind-affecting Phantasm that allows a save to ignore isn’t as much. This one definitely gets better as you go, though be careful about relying on it against anything which might be immune. From level 12-ish to 17 it won’t see use, but at 18 it can hit 7 essence if assisted by a feat, which makes it no longer a Phantasm and therefore must-shape. With a Greater Akashic Catalyst, this can be accomplished at level 11. The Feet Bind is a (slow and Clumsy) fly speed, which is VERY good for this level and solves a lot of problems fast. Has a Shoulder bind, which will be covered there. Under most circumstances, this is probably the best Veil for Seers to be sharing, since spreading around a 20% miss chance is a Big Deal

Polar Snowshoes: Aura of damage is good! Thing is, it harms people on the end of THEIR turn (which makes it easy to dodge), affects allies/mounts, allows Fort for half, and is the relatively easy-to-resist Cold. The damage isn’t great for the level to begin with, either. The Bind effect is a great visual (go Elsa!), but it’s a lot of hassle to go through for a sharable water walk. Upgraded to Orange with the addition of the Frostbite Halo, which makes the damage much more dangerous and makes it a decent delivery method for the bonus-suppression.

Stalker’s Tabi: Another Skill booster, but to MUCH better skills for general use. Acrobatics is not something you generally want to be rolling, but if you have to then you REALLY want it high. Stealth is also nice to have. Feet bind is CRAZY good as long as you aren’t out in the daylight, allowing you to get out of hairy situations or into locked rooms with ease, at will, and as a move action while retaining your ability to act effectively for the remainder of the turn. Has a Belt bind, which I will cover there.

Tauric Brace: This is one that got a lot better after playtesting. Battlefield Control Viziers had a problem with affecting oversized targets with their trip attempts, and this is how you gain the ability to do so. Especially important on Small Viziers (Goblins, Halflings, and the occasional Half-Goblin for example), The Feet bind is half-decent, but the Coward’s Boots do it better and this one won’t stack with the Seer’s speed aura. Has a Belt bind, which I will cover there.

Head Chakra (Lvl 6): Only three native Veils here to choose from, but that doesn’t mean the choice will be easy. The effects wildly vary in-slot.

Horns of the Minotaur: I’m being generous here, since there may be a build I just can’t see. Free extra melee weapon is nice, but it’s MELEE and you are not built for it. Essence and Bind effects are more of the same. If you find a reason for this to be shaped on a Vizier build, please let me know, but otherwise leave it for the Daevics.

Sentinel’s Helm: Another skill booster, but this one includes the all-important Perception. Sense Motive is just gravy on that, but it’s really delicious gravy. If you aren’t doing anything else, this should be your default for the head slot. Uncanny Dodge on-bind is also seriously nice.

Stare of the Ghaele: This is an awesome example of an ability that would be OBSCENE if it didn’t have one glaring flaw. SOS Gaze attack at-will (shaken is a good condition to inflict, Staggered is a fight-ender) that keeps on trying until they fail, only affects enemies, with a good DC and synergy with the Pestilence cloak to make it nigh-certain to go off quickly. The only problem is that it has the Mind-Affecting and Fear descriptors, making it easy to get resistance or immunity to. If your enemies aren’t going to have that, though, this is a must-shape.

Wrists Chakra (Lvl 8): Four candidates here natively, mostly combat. Two big standouts here, including a major build-enabler.

Bangles of the Jealous Seductress: Again, a Fascinate effect. Waste of a slot by now, due to the addition of a few serious Ruler veils. If you choose to shape this, make sure to vaccinate your allies by turning it on and off again with no Essence invested until each makes their save. The Bind is REALLY not worth your time. Its effect is laughable (all the way up to 1d4+8 maximum at level 19) and the trigger is reliant on the first effect succeeding.

Ditchdigger’s Armlets: This is Battlefield Control at its heart, allowing you to isolate or delay some of your enemies while softening them up. Once you get the Bind going, it’s an SOS to anything that can’t fly or burrow. Basically, if you’re not using Hand Cannons as a build basis, this should be your go-to. Falls off a bit after level 10, since flying enemies get more common, but caves and dungeons (or other effects that keep opponents on the ground) will occasionally spice it up again.

Hand Cannons: This would have sucked… but you can use your Veilweaver level instead of your BAB (including Iteratives). The Cannon is a powerful weapon, and once Bound gets more so quickly. The fact that it specifies that you apply ranged combat feats to it is pure gold, and an FAQ about Monk Flurries means that if you have Deadly Aim it’ll calculate bonuses from your heightened effective BAB. Per Ssalarn, this is intended to count as a weapon wielded, which has all kinds of fun attached when you get into other veils or temporary buffs. The Essence effect boosts its range increment a bit, but also gives Enhancement bonuses, which can help punch out DR. Binding it gives energy-based weapon properties, which only add to the punishment you can deal out. Final note: by careful veil, investment, and feat selection you can get the range increment on this Veil over 150ft, making it the longest-ranged non-siege weapon in all of Pathfinder.

Lashing Spinnerets: Rather than putting together a place to sleep, this now generates a powerful and VERY large-area Web effect. Only usable a few times per day, but it can cover enormous amounts of real estate and limit lesser enemies to futile struggling while you take out bigger threats

Storm Gauntlets: Ah, we meet again. The Wrist bind here allows you to channel the bonus through any weapon, but it doesn’t stack with similar magic effects. Hmmm… Twin Veil plus Hand Cannons sounds mighty tasty, and can result in obscene amounts of damage dice being thrown around when paired with ranged combat feats.

Thurston’s Bladeward: The DR here is nice, but not useful against many monsters. It does add up quickly if you know you’re fighting swords or claws, though. The Bind effect is nice to have and will likely represent a significant AC upgrade for you (your Buckler is used mostly to hang enchantments on).

Ring Chakra (Lvl9): There are some real gems here, and luckily for you the class chassis allows you to use two eventually or THREE with the help of Twin Veil. Nobody else can learn these Veils or this Bind slot, either.

Dark Lord’s Ring of Essence Binding: Love having minions, even if it’s one at a time. The creature that steps up isn’t much, but it can be a threat and at the very least a wall. Remember that Seers can share Teamwork feats with them for setup, and borders on Blue with the assistance of the Shared Veil feat. Binding gives an energy drain and full actions, which is nice.

Deathchannel Ring: This is VERY strong if you have a group set to resist or absorb Negative, and turns you into nearly a Cleric channeling. It’s also a damage AoE that targets Will, which is moderately unique. With enough investment, this is a Standard-action room-clear. Binding this veil represents a 70% upgrade to its average damage, do NOT discount it.

Light Whip: I’ve said before how much you hate being in melee. A stuffed Light Whip will more or less ensure that you never are. Tons of tripping (AoE tripping when bound!), uses character level instead of BAB (plus uses Int mod instead of Str), works off of AoOs with a 15-ft threat range, no drawbacks…  you have a decent chance of tripping anything with legs that’s level-appropriate, and there really isn’t much they can say about it. Please also note that the Essence bonus is Untyped, and will stack with any of the various Insight bonuses you can apply.

Ring of the Abjurer:  Your Mage Armor, but so much more. This scales well, and has a very nice Bind effect that can remain useful as long as you might face casters that utilize their low-level slots. Default here, basically.

Stormcaller’s Band: A different kind of Battlefield Control, this Veil is particularly useful if you’re fighting around hazards (whether they were there first or someone made them). Combos well with Bound Ditchdigger’s Armlets, just shove them back down into the pit. The Bind effect shuts down ranged physical hard, and is not to be underestimated.

Shoulders Chakra (Lvl 10): There are five Veils here, tending towards defensive buffs. Not going to lie, this tends to be the least-used Chakra outside of niche situations. Rulers have a real gem here, though.

Bralani’s Brooch: Not much deals electricity damage, but when you run across it you’ll be glad to have this. It is easy to get a LOT of resistance here (up to 15 at level 1, capping out at 50 or more), enough that it is effectively immunity in most situations. Pair with Shape Veil for the Mask of Elemental Adaptation for extra fun. The Shoulders bind is pure miss chance against all ranged attacks, something that is significant for someone who spends no time in Melee. Has a Neck bind, which I will cover there.

Gorget of the Wyrm: Always wanted a breath weapon. As mook-clearing Veils go, this is probably one of the better ways to do it. Pick Acid unless you know an enemy is weak to something else, since it is a rare resist type. The Shoulder bind gives a Fly speed, which can get extremely fast with investment (upwards of 60ft Perfect as soon as you get it!). This Veil also has a Neck bind, which I will cover there.

Mantle of Murderous Intent: Does half-decent damage, and can eventually cover large amounts of the map and ignores allies. The beauty comes with the Binds, though, and tying it to the Shoulders will cause opponents to run in fear on a failed Will. Rulers will probably use this as a primary offense, since their Aura reduces the enemy’s ability to resist. Has a Body bind, which I will cover there.

Pestilence Cloak: The Sickened status is a nice thing to be able to inflict, but it only works if your opponent isn’t using weapons. If you aren’t going for the minor fly speed, though, you want to shape this Veil on the shoulders, if only because the Feet slot has a lot of competition.

Spiked Pauldrons:  Anti-Grapple defense. Again, you don’t want to be anywhere near melee in the first place, but if your DM loves throwing crabs, chokers, and other such hazards at you then this goes up a half-notch. Problem is, unless all this happens in broad daylight, the Stalker’s Tabi Foot Bind is better for escapes. The Bind here allows you to spray spikes around you, but it is very short range and doesn’t avoid allies.

Vorpal Guards: Anti-Crit defense. To be honest, you’re allowed to wear Light armor anyway, so just buy some Fortification. The Bind is more of the same, you can get yourself all the way up to 50% crit resistance if you sink everything you have into this. Really, just not worth it unless you KNOW you’re facing crit-fishers and/or Vorpal weapons.

Headband (Lvl 12): There is a LOT of competition for these slots. Mostly dealing with vision and knowledge with one exception, many of these have a dramatic effect on your capabilities (though not always in combat).

Circlet of Brass: It has to deal fire DAMAGE, which is unfortunate. Otherwise, this would have been absolutely insane. Potentially +9 to any fire-based DC can turn an easy save into a real threat, and the damage bonus applies to all damage rolls with said ability (even if it’s more than one roll per use, such as Scorching Ray or Meteor Swarm). The Bind gives innate access to a Fire ability if you didn’t have one already, but Crafters in particular probably have several means of dealing the damage already (plus things like Gorget of the Wyrm or the Hand Cannon bind).

Crown of inevitable Command: Powerful effect, but too narrow. You can only pick creature types that have racial resistances or immunities across the board, which closes out most foes along with anything that acquires the resistance by any method other than birth. A good Veilshift target if it pops up, though.

Dreamcatcher: There are few better uses for downtime than this. Just remember the seven P’s (Proper Prior Planning Prevents Piss Poor Performance), and then remember that this can give upwards of +18 to Knowledge skills. It locks Essence for 24 hours, so if you get ambushed on an off day you could be short up to a third of your essence… unless you use Veilshifting to wipe it off your forehead and forcefully free it up. The Bind is gravy, letting you snoop on thoughts with a highly-boosted DC, and forcing those who save to keep on saving. Put it this way, taking 20 will take 6 minutes of conversation.

Eye of the Oracle: In-combat, its Shape and Essence effects are a powerful and multi-purpose bonus. Outside of combat, this is essentially a flat bonus to everything you don’t have a veil for already. It’s only weakness is that it provides an Insight bonus, which is the same kind as all of the other Akashic effects. Starts off Sky-Blue, but drops off over time as you get more ways to use Essence. Hits Yellow by level 19, and if you get the Vizier capstone you can consider it Orange. Averages out to blue, and will be one of your most common Veils formed. The Bind effect is among the best magic-analysis abilities in the game, and you get it as a constant effect.

*A note on using the Eye in combat* If you are not getting Insight bonuses from another source, such as Chakra Targeting, you will nearly always want to apply this bonus to your first attack roll each round that would not be capped out by the effect. If you know you can hit on a 2+, don’t waste the one use per round on that shot. Save it for your next iterative, which is unlikely to be capping out. The only time you will want to use it on anything else is if you know that a different use is coming that will be critical, such as an opponent that likes to use SOS/SOD spells or a crucial Acrobatics check to get out of trouble

Eyes of the Hawkguard: Very niche, but when you can apply it this is very good. Note that the increase on range is set number, not proportional, so that 10-ft boost that is barely noticeable to daylight vision or a Compound Bow increment is dramatic when applied to a limited-range Darkvision or Blindsight, or to the range increment of a thrown dagger or blowdart. The Headband bind is nice to have, though you should have other means to defeat invisibility by now. This has a mighty Body bind, which I will cover there.

Frostbite Halo: This one is narrow in application, but helps any time you’re dealing Cold damage (by Storm Gauntlets, Polar Snowshoes, or the bind effect of the Hand Cannons, for example). If your enemies have cold resistance, but not immunity, this jumps in value as well. Finally, being able to cut out opposing Morale and Insight bonuses on the Bind effect suppresses swathes of enemies, most notably Bards, Barbarians, and other Veilweavers.

Neck (Lvl 14): We are starting to get up into the truly serious effects, though there are only three here. Two are repeats from earlier, with enhanced effects in their new bind.

Bralani’s Brooch: This one gets a lot better once you have the ability to plant it on the Neck slot where it belongs. The Spell Resistance it provides starts off requiring a 12 or more on the die to punch out from an equal-level caster, which puts a serious cramp in their style. At level 20, you are looking at having a potential SR of 34 (upwards of 40 with item support!). Virtually impossible for lackeys, and tough for even main bosses.

Collar of Skilled Instruction: This one is available to anyone, but Seer Viziers can make the best use of it once you can Bind it. Giving as many allies as you can a +2 (or more with Teamwork or certain racials) to nearly any action can be good, especially since you can blanket the battlefield with the effect at need. Binding grants a Teamwork feat you qualify for… and Seers already get a bunch as bonus feats, which can lead into chains of them.

Gorget of the Wyrm: The damage die increase is nice, though it’s minor overall. The gem here is the resistance aura, which is enough to cut out most of the damage from average rolls on the breath weapon (allowing you to bathe their area in energy without worrying about thrown books), or else put a sharp damper on enemy energy attacks if you know what type is coming.

Belt (Lvl 16): Another variety slot, and this one is packed full of solid choices (plus a couple that are just nice to have).

Cerebral Catastrophe Cinch: Situational, but very, very strong against Int-casters (who are likely to be targeting you early). Only issue is that it targets the save they traditionally have high. Binding it is where the “catastrophe” comes in, since Confusing an enemy caster can mean Bad Things for the opposing team, regardless of their casting stat.

Horselord’s Greaves: Phantom Steed is a very good way to get a mount, and by this point it has all of its abilities (100ft Fly speed with beyond-Perfect skill bonuses). However, this is also around the time that long-range teleportation starts becoming more common when you have other casters running around. Consult with your local wizard before using the bind on this.

Stalker’s Tabi: Distinctly lackluster compared to its awe-inspiring Feet bind, but useful nonetheless if you find the opportunity for it. if you have a priority to shape on Feet and still want the skill bonuses, though, shifting it to Belt is a good way to do it.

Stone Giant’s Girdle: This provides a good buffer of temporary hit points, not to be trifled with (especially in early levels, given that it regenerates relatively quickly). This falls off fast, but is still a good default for this slot if you aren’t doing anything else. It’s still lots of health that you didn’t have before. The Belt bind isn’t all that great for you, but it’s decent if you expect to face incoming thrown rocks a lot.

Tauric Brace: An improvement over the Foot bind, the main purpose of this is not to damage. Rather, it’s to run over enemies to get to freedom. Usually not going to be worth shaping, but still a possibility.

Waistband of the Wealthy: The skill bonus to Bluff is very good to have, though Appraise is going to be a waste of time with most DMs. The Bind effect isn’t going to be worthwhile in most cases, unless you expect to be somewhere that you can’t easily bring your Bags of Holding normally (you’re level 16 and should be able to afford it by now).

Chest (Lvl 18): Starting to get into the top slots, by the time these binds open up you are comparing to 9ths and build-finishing abilities. These three don’t disappoint, all are good choices depending on what you want to do, even if not bound.

Cuirass of Confidence:  Diplomancers only WISH they had it this good. Even unbound, creatures that were not frothingly hostile to you will stop attacking you on a failed will (since most won’t attack on Indifferent, few on Friendly, and almost none on Helpful). Essence ups your Diplomacy check against these victims, which are already 5 points easier due to being a step more friendly. The chest bind lets you stop swords with a smile, and if you’ve been pumping Diplo these checks can get stupidly high, which makes successfully attacking you an issue. Double Chakra with the Vestments of the Maharaja, below, gets absurd quick.

Heart of the Wight: This is a WIDE variety of effects it can help resist, and something in that variety will be useful at pretty much every point of your career. Most are also immediate fight-enders, and having an edge to those saves is a very good thing. Binding this only makes it better, stopping ever more incoming effects and synching up with the Deathchannel Ring for healing. Just make sure your local cleric knows you’re doing this, or THEIR healing might not work out quite like they want it to.

Vestments of the Maharaja: Skill boosts to two of the more powerful skills out there, and significant ones at that. Binding this Veil, though potentially stupidly strong, is dependent on your GM’s definition of the targets’ essential natures and what it means.

Body (Lvl 20): With good reason, these stand among the strongest of Veils. Binding one is basically an addition to your Capstone.

Bloody Shroud: This is a bit up close and personal for your tastes, but it adds up if you have a particularly tough opponent. You have a few sources of Piercing and Slashing available to you (the best of which is probably your pet Barbarian’s greatsword), and the more sources you bring to bear the better for this one. You will not be using the Bind here, since melee is seriously not your thing and it requires crits to go off.

Eyes of the Hawkguard:  Arguably the strongest effect of any Bind in your arsenal, constant True Seeing is an incredible power to have. Don’t even bother investing Essence; unless you need to ping an enemy hiding a half-mile away, you don’t need to.

Mantle of Murderous Intent: Long-duration Stun against Will in a massive AoE with potentially crazy save DCs earns this one a nod, even among this company.

Stone Giant’s Girdle: Melee still isn’t your thing, but if you feel the burning need to mix it up then this is how you do it. Reach along with a good Strength increase that stacks with most effects.

Robe of the Forgotten Deity: Miss chance is always good to have, but the real gem here is the Bind effect. At-will Incorporeal as a Swift action is crazy good, and you can adjust its duration by adjusting your investment here.

Races:

Quite a few here to hit, but unlike Feats below the number is merely in the dozens. I will start off with the Core and Akashic races, and add to them as I find time. These ratings are very likely to change as soon as the Alternate Favored Class Bonuses are released. Places of frequent special emphasis are going to be stat placement, alternate perception modes, and applicable special abilities or alternate racial traits.

Akashic: Three have been released with two sub-races each for a total of nine. These are all up a notch from what they otherwise would be for one simple reason: each gives a point of Essence as a racial feature, and there is no such thing as having too much Essence.

Gamla: Bonuses to Con and Wis are very nice to have, plus the point of Essence, but Large size and a penalty to Dex are deal breakers unless you are a rare Melee build. The Spit attack can be a lifesaver if you get the opportunity, and you can stuff a LOT of essence into it to up the DC.

-Feelkha: Changing Wis to Int and replacing a couple of tricks you won’t really use with a third hand is actually fairly nice for Viziers, as long as your primary trick isn’t ranged combat. Dedicated BFC and Save-Based builds will love this.

-Alqarn: These guys, though… you like Con, but gaining Str isn’t going to help and the natural weapon will rarely see use

Sobek: The stats are not great to you, costing a point of Will with no real benefit (again, Melee builds only, though some socially-focused folk will like the Charisma). Slow speed on land is also a major drawback to a normally-mobile class. The bite and tail are nice if they come into play, and the water adaptation is very campaign-specific.

-Nameer: Boost to Dex and quick land speed are worth losing 2 Int as long as you aren’t a Ruler. The rest is unchanged, but investing into two claws will be more powerful than one bite if it comes down to it.

-Solhofaat: +4 Con is HUGE, and lets you access powerful feats for nearly no point-buy investment. The natural armor is nice as well, and can add up to +10 eventually. Losing Dex, though, hurts it for ranged builds. Still good for most applications, it just won’t often be your first choice

Suqur: So close to greatness… these guys were literally made to be Viziers, and it shows. Bonuses to your main stats, innate Flight, bonuses to important skill, and a couple of weapons in case you don’t want to use your hands for them. The problem? They lose two Con, which would hurt anyone. Here, it means that you will have to pay for 15 in order to utilize most Akashic feats.

-Hibkha: The +4 Int is amazing, and losing slow speed helps. No Dex bonus, and still loses Con, but if you’re going BFC or Save-Based there is no other way to get +4 Int natively

-Nisir: HERE we go. Dex/Con bonus with a penalty to the dump stat. Trading Perception and Fly bonus away can hurt, but the Disease and Poison resistance is a good replacement, and more importantly can be invested in. Since those effects do their damage over time, you will be able to invest after the condition is inflicted and still benefit.

Core:

Human: They’re good at everything, and that still holds true here. The bonus feat can help speed up the intensive ranged feat chains, or go into Extra Essence if that’s not your thing.This goes double for builds with low Int, since their skills need supporting.

Dwarf: Dwarves make surprisingly good Viziers, especially if you plan on spending a feat or two on heavier armor. Bonus to good stats, penalty to a dump, Darkvision (remember the Eyes of the Hawkguard!), and a couple of good resistances and alternate features (check out Craftsman and Rock Stepper).

Elf:  Think of them as Suqur without the wings and you’re very close. Tons of good alternate features to look at here as well, though Envoy deserves a special mention, especially for Crafters. Last detail is important, these guys give free proficiency with Bows if you want to build along those lines.

Gnome: Another good one, Small size and a bonus to Con is good for you, and Charisma is useful for certain builds. If you know the campaign is going to be standing relatively still, Bond to the Land is excellent. Also look at Darkvision if you can get it.

Half-Elf: Not a BAD choice, but almost anything they can do is done better by either Elf or Human. The one exception to this is multiclassing, if that’s what you’re after then being able to get both Favored is invaluable.

Half-Orc: Floating stat bonus, darkvision, and Ferocity is good. Unfortunately, any good alternate trait usually replaces Ferocity. A half-decent choice, but one that moves up noticeably if you want to take certain Teamwork choices.

Halfling: Just as a general rule, your damage isn’t size-based. Small size with a Dex bonus (while dropping your dump stat) is nice to have. Add the save bonus plus Perception and it makes a very strong contender. They have some good alternate traits as well, in particular Adaptive Luck and Fleet of Foot are worth a look.

Psionic: If you have access to Akashic Mysteries, then Psionics are probably on the table as well. These guys have a couple of edges to them, notably the ability to take Psionic feats and other Psionic effects without requiring a dip. Several also have good stats and abilities for you as well.

Blue: Rating is slightly comedic, all things considered. Small size plus Dex/Int and losing from your dump stat are solid benefits. Darkvision and Repletion are nice to have as well, as are the bonuses to Perception (and, on occasion, Stealth). Be careful going Ruler due to the Pariah trait, but then again if you’re primarily in a Goblin-themed campaign that becomes a benefit.

Dromite: Drops a half-step for people not playing Rulers, but there are no drawbacks at all for people who are. Near-perfect stats, small size, and multiple useful abilities and resistances are all appreciated.

Duergar: Think of these guys as Dwarves, which have no alternate racials and light sensitivity in exchange for a couple of Psi-like abilities and natural psionics. Generally not as good as their above-ground cousins, Expansion doesn’t really help Viziers.

Elan: As a general rule, you aren’t using your Swift much after the first round or two of combat. If you plan on gaining even a few more PP (for example, dipping Psion to go into the Amplifier PrC), Elan are walking around with permanent Immediate-action counters and go Sky-Blue. Floating stats and Aberrant Blood are icing on the cake (the latter mostly for Bloodforge feats)

Forgeborn: One good stat, one dump are raised. The drop is to a dump unless you’re a Ruler, but it’s a HARD dump. The other riders are so-so for you overall.

Half-Giant: Stats are poorly placed for you, and the only abilities they have that help you out are the low-light vision and naturally Psionic. You have better options, this will only be good if you’re focused on melee for some reason.

Maenad: Floating stat is good, but nothing else here is for you. Outburst suppresses good stats for a dump, you don’t get Rage, you don’t use Sonic saves… yeah, usually going to be a pass.

Noral: Losing Dex sucks, though gaining Con is nice. The main reason to pick these guys is to basically have a hero point to spend each day, which helps offset things (slightly). Your DC for Mind Thrust is going to be too low to matter.

Ophiduan: Decent stats, darkvision, scaled hide, and a backup weapon make this choice potentially good.

Xeph: Another contender for the Ruler throne. Awesome stats, resists, and Burst make these guys hard to stop.

Bloodforge: The Bloodforge book, also by DSP, presents a large number of additional races to use. The power levels here tend to be high, so make sure to clear it with your DM first. I’ll present five good picks here, but there are lots more possibilities in the book itself.

Aellar: The stats are well-placed, with a hit to a dump stat. Their base racial traits include a slew of powerful and useful abilities, notably Aerial Supremacy, Wings, and Falcon’s Gaze (plus longbow proficiency from their Elf half). There is also a major gem in the alternates, with Suqur-Blooded giving both a point of Essence and an awesome receptacle (boosting both your ranged accuracy and reflex). Last, but NOT least, their favored class bonus is worth an extra five essence over the course of your career.

Alicorn: Dex/Con is an ideal stat placement for you, and the ability to absorb damage and other maladies helps spread out where the healing goes if you are lightly injured. Detect Evil (or Detect Good) is good to have as well. Make sure to take the Elysian Resistance trait, having an extra veil shaped lets you do a lot of very mean things much earlier than you’d think (Light Whip, Hand Cannon, and Eye of the Oracle from level one, for example)

Half-Goblin: Dex/Con, small size while maintaining speed 30, plus two extra vision modes makes this good. Alternate class bonus for Viziers lets them add small amounts of damage to all elemental damage from Veils… which you can trigger four times per shot of the Hand Cannon (once each Fire, Cold, Electric, and Sonic) by binding both the Hand Cannon and Storm Gauntlets to wrist via Shared Veil. This leads to potentially 100 or more extra damage per round

Half-Hobgoblin: Good stat mix again, but this one is all about the abilities. Darkvision to 90 feet and free Improved Initiative (plus the extra +1 from the Dex bonus) are nice. Wartime Predator, though, is crazy good if you know how to move around a battlefield. High-Ground bonuses are easy to gain when you can gain the ability to fly, and it’s easy to get an enemy flat-footed when your Initiative is that good.

Thrallspawn: Con/Int is great for Rulers and Seers (and not half bad for Crafters), and Aberrant subtype opens up some tasty feats. Acidic Spittle can be very dangerous if you have a way to deny full-round actions, since it has an unlimited duration until removed. The tentacle lets you get some nice area control, but the damage isn’t anything to write home about.  Naturally Psionic also opens up a bunch of options, and free Iron Will gets rid of the occasional annoying prereq. Really, just a good general platform to build off of.

Other: There are very many racial choices out there, I’ll try to hit some of the highlights.

Aasimar: The wealth of alternate racial traits make up the gold standard here, since the base stat arrangement is a hair off for the Vizier. The rest of their traits, from resistances to skill bonuses to Darkvision to Daylight are awe-inspiring. Peri-blooded make some of the best Rulers anywhere, and Garuda-blooded get the stats for a Cannon build, plus the random traits have a very good chance to pop up with a gold mine (bonus Dex, Initiative, Fort or Will saves, Resists, skills…). On top of all of that, they have a selection of racial feats which are a cut above the average for those who have them open.

Goblin: +4 Dex, Small size, an alternate racial trait to get bonus Perception, Darkvision, and penalties to dumps add up to make this selection far better than it may appear on the surface for Cannon builds. Just avoid the feats, they aren’t worth it. No, not even then.

Hobgoblin: Dex and Con bonus leaps out at you, Darkvision is also nice to have. This edged on Sky Blue for one reason, though, the Pit Boss alternate racial trait boosts your Light Whip trips by one. Hobgoblin Seers also gain access to the hilarious Focusing Blow teamwork feat, which can be a good trick to have up your sleeves.

Tiefling: It is a truism that there is a Tiefling for everyone, and the default package is excellent for the Vizier. Most of the Alternate Racial Traits and Variant Heritage are worth a look at in most builds, too. This also opens up a few excellent feat options (notably Armor of the Pit and Fiend Sight). If the alternate trait table is in play, there are quite a few there that benefit you (extra Int, Small size, save bonuses, and Perception skill are all real winners)

Feats:

Let’s face it, there is a LOT to go through here. Though I may eventually do something more comprehensive, the only section that I’m going to cover 100% is the new Akashic selection. Anything else, I’m only going to cover if it’ll be useful to a primary build, on request, or if it is a notable one to avoid.

Akashic Feats: These will be important for you, including a couple that are absolute must-takes (one of which is a must-take as many times as you can free space for).

Access <level> Chakra Slot: Not for you, you get them all anyway. The only time you’d take one of these feats is if you multiclassed to the point that you didn’t get the slot, and since you can’t grab the last couple of slots with these feats that is a DEEP multiclass.

Akasha-Imbued Body: You can pick up around 22 bonus HP with this if you’re determined, which isn’t bad, but there are better ways to do this (and we’ll see it later).

Akasha-Infused Spells: Most Vizier builds can’t use this one effectively, but if a primary caster dips one level of Akasha then this is a good investment.

Akashic Augmentation: For anyone with significant manifesting ability, this stacks multiple benefits rapidly. It increases your potential total power by allowing more room to augment before hitting your limit, plus endurance for the same reasons.

Chakra <Save> Series: These will never be your first choice, but they are excellent ways to fill empty slots. Each gives a point of Essence (always in demand), and can always be filled up fast if you see a particular save is going to be needed (upwards of +7 native capacity for Essence you can turn a near-impossible save into a fighting chance)

Chakra Power: Viziers don’t like making melee attacks, but if you do then this is a must-have. Also works with Piranha Strike, for the dex-based melee builds.

Chakra Targeting: NOW we’re talking! This feat can add a LOT of pain to attack rolls you like to make. If you aren’t a Ruler, I can’t really imagine building without this feat. if you ARE a Ruler, you probably want it anyway.

Daevic Rage: If you’re asking about this feat, I don’t think we’re even talking about the same class. Barbarians don’t want Vizier levels, so this shouldn’t be a question.

Divine Essence: Again only for multiclassers, but if you have access to a Channel pool this lets you bust the limits wide open for your abilities.

ENHANCED CAPACITY: I cannot make this any bluer, since ultraviolet isn’t on my list of colors. I’m serious, if you don’t have this feat at least once or twice I’m sure there is a place you can get it in somewhere. Going to an 8-essence investment in combination with your class abilities is crazy good on most veils, and some abilities coming online 3 levels early (such as Pestilence Cloak’s solid-form or the Dark Lord’s Ring of Essence-Binding plague template) is also highly sought after.

Essence Expertise: A niche combat style, but one thing makes this good for Viziers: the Light Whip Veil. You can trigger Expertise on an AoO, meaning that this can give huge bonuses to your AC and to Trip. This turns one of your main anti-melee defenses into something much more powerful, and I’ve seen total modifiers upwards of +60 on the Trip.

Essence Focus: If you have Psionic flavor and the ability to use your Focus in more ways than one, this is a critical feat to take. Again, it’s going to be niche, but this one is easier to access and activate than most (and doesn’t actually require multiclassing in some cases).

Essence of Movement: Beats the crap out of Mobility if you’re going that way anyway, but you’ll want to avoid it otherwise. If your DM lets you use Veils to qualify for prereqs, then avoid this feat since it’s available from the Foot bind of Coward’s Boots.

Essence of Recovery: Most notable use is for a Paladin who splashes Akasha, this requires some building around but can put your recovery abilities distinctly above what many enemies are ready to compensate for.

Essence Rejection: Do I really need to comment here? I don’t have a darker Red shade to use.

Essence of the Immortal: Requiring Con 15 is tough, but even baseline this will give you 60 HP more than you had. I’ve seen some builds scrape together a 40+ point Essence pool, and 80 bonus HP from one feat is incredible. Arguably one of the strongest feats yet printed. NOTE: This is being reviewed for balance reasons, and may be changed in the future

Extra Essence: If you are an Akashic class and you do not take this, YOU ARE WRONG. Two extra points of essence is clutch frequently, especially in the early game. You WILL take this by level 7, 9  at the latest.

Focused Critical: For the crit-fishers out there, but you really don’t fall into this category often (if ever)

Life Bond: Difficult to qualify for and dangerous for you to use, though it does give you some native healing ability.

Pistolero’s Bind: If you use firearms, this can be half-decent (not great). Otherwise useless.

Shape Veil: This isn’t one you should have to take, but it can let you complete some builds or bridge gaps. Immaculate Touch, Theft Gloves, and the Mask of Elemental Adaptation are good examples.

Shared Veil: Viziers can utilize this through two different Veils (Horselord’s Greaves and Dark Lord’s Ring of Essence-Binding), and getting extra instances of Veils out there and in use (not to mention having full Essence effects by your investment) is strong. Also great for multiclass options. Notable winners here are the mook-sweepers and BFC selections, just remember that they don’t get your Bind effect or Veilweaver level. Seers who are also sharing feats can get extra mileage out of this.

Tainted Chakra: The Evil Veils include some big winners (Heart of the Wight and Dark Lord’s Ring of Essence-Binding, mainly), and adding to their bonuses makes this worth taking even if you didn’t need it for general access.

Treewalker: I’m probably rating this a hair high due to fondness for the effect (and some hilarious moments in playtest), but if trees and lichens are common in your typical environments then the movement options this can provide can be useful. Drops off once you have a decent fly speed, which is unfortunate.

Twin Veil: You have a large number of shapable veils, and some very nice options occupy the same slots as others. This is the solution, and allows double-Binding as well (Triple eventually in the Ring slot).

Veilbound Metamagic: Hugely powerful for those able to use it, much like the metamagic mitigators of old. This will, however, typically not be found on the sheets of Akasha-dominant players since it soaks up so much of their Essence reserve.

Veilbound Resilience: Scales exponentially, up to a maximum of 240 points of healing per day (320 if you put an Expanded Capacity on it). The problem is that it locks essence for the day, which is something to avoid unless it’s oozing out of your pores with nowhere to go. If that is the case (and you already have Essence of the Immortal), then this starts becoming decent. Much like the Dreamcatcher veil, this feat is good to use during known downtime to heal up quick.

Veiled in Purity: The effects are nice, but you do not gain native access to the Veils required to make use of this.

Veil-Touched Nemesis: If you can get a Favored Enemy, this plus a wand/stave/item of Instant Enemy will turn the AC of your enemies into mush for the rest of your team.

Veil-Woven Channel: This is more for Clerics who like healing to use as a means to bolster allies, but if you gain a Channel pool this becomes a great way to spend a supporting action. Healing plus dropping a significant bonus to whatever your team happens to need is far better than healing alone.

View Energy: Oh, if only the Sight lasted longer. This is a decent way to break through most forms of sneaking, but requiring a Move action every round to keep using is going to murder your action economy.

Wildclaw Chakra: A solid investment for Druids splashing into Akasha, this is unfortunately not usable by you without a significant dip (which would cost you at least two bind locations and a point of Essence capacity).

Teamwork Feats: Due to the mechanics of the Seer and the Collar of Skill Instruction, this section deserves to be set apart from others. I will not be including the Betrayal feats, since spreading them around doesn’t do anything. Anything else a Vizier can possibly qualify for, though, gets in. This will be long.

Allied Spellcaster: You qualify through Eldritch Insight, and although you can’t grant the amplified bonus being able to assist allied casters in punching out SR (and them doing the same for you) can make a real difference if it pops up.

Amplified Rage: If you have multiple ragers in the group, you might want to make a half-orc for access to this. Otherwise, don’t bother. You don’t want it.

Back to Back (and Improved): Could be worse, this eliminates flanking advantage from opponents who get it. If you often find yourself or your buddies in this kind of trouble, these can be worthwhile. Remember that using the action for Improved will prevent you from shifting Essence the following turn, so be careful.

Broken Wing Gambit (and Wounded Paw Gambit): Broken Wing requires striking in melee, gives enemies a bonus to hit you, and grants a melee AoO. Usually not going to be a good choice, but if you have a heavily melee party then sharing it might be viable. Upgrading to Wounded Paw, however, means EVERYBODY is sharing in on the action if one of your buddies triggers it.

Brutal Grappler: One team setup that came up in discussion included this plus the Spiked Pauldrons, teaming up with a Tetori Monk or Shapeshift Grapple Druid for massive damage. Then we all realized that the Pauldrons give no bonus to CMB, just CMD, and it fell through. Just skip this, Grappler is not a role that multiple teammates take often.

Cavalry Formation: Again, it’s team-dependent, but given your Horselord’s Greaves having a team full of mounted combatants is more feasible than many. The benefits here are intense, as well.

Circling Offense: A solid set of benefits, but you usually don’t want to be deliberately provoking attacks of opportunity. Tons of ways to avoid them, rather than risking them actually connecting with a primary attack.

Combat Medic: I really wanted to like this one, but let’s face it. By level 5, SOMEONE in your group probably has a means of delivering small amounts of in-combat healing, which negates the need for most of it. If you are in a particularly magic-restrictive campaign and can’t get any such thing (which would explain why Heal is at 5 ranks), this gets a bit better.

Coordinated Charge: This is a bit more like it! I know I harp about you not wanting to be in melee, but this one lets you move around the battlefield much more and assist allies in doing so, while providing a free attack and dogpile. Good as long as you have two allies who can move at least 20 feet in a round, and incredible if you’re on a mounted team (see: Cavalry Formation)

Coordinated Defense: Nice to have, I suppose, but Combat Maneuvers are things your allies are supposed to keep you from suffering, not something they should be getting you out of.

Coordinated Maneuvers: Slightly better than Defense, this will let you stand behind an ally and give him a leg up when he tries to trip or grapple an enemy… or let him give YOU a leg up as you initiate a Light Whip trip on said enemy

Coordinated Reposition: Light Whip makes your threatened area huge, and allied 5ft steps are going to be common. This gives a good edge in mobility if you know you won’t need to reset your Essence next turn, and most allies will love the effect to help get them into position as well.

Coordinated Shot: Long way of saying that you get nice bonuses by having an ally in melee. You love this one, and if your team has at least two ranged it becomes a near-must.

Covering Fire: Very long way of saying “Make a standard-action shot to give your allies +2 AC against the target, including up to one without this feat”. Not a bad thing to do if you move and shoot, actually.

Darting Retrieval: Very niche, though if your guy in the thick of things likes to Disarm this gets evil, quick.

Disarm Partner (and Improved): You are banking on someone who wants to make Disarm checks failing to do so, then you succeeding on multiple rolls.

Distracting Charge: Generally good for any group, most Melee like to charge on the opener and the bonus stacks with everything else when the target survives it.

Duck and Cover (and Improved): Reflex saves are the least dangerous kind, but there is no reason to take more damage than you need to. If you are grouped together and able to use the feat, it will very likely come in handy. Remember that you will usually have a shield of some kind to give friends that cover bonus to AC. Improved version lets you dump damage you would have taken onto an ally who was there with you, which might save your hide and might earn a thrown book.

Elemental Commixture: Nothing says the effect has to be cast from a personal slot. You can have an ally assist on things like Scorching Ray or Lightning Bolt cast from wands or staves. Dust, Mud, Smoke, and Steam are all notably good effects, and the rest aren’t bad to have.

Enfilading Fire: The most common team arrangements tend to be two melee and two ranged. This lets those melee give their flanking bonuses to their ranged buddies, which can come as a nasty shock to the poor victim. Actually stacks with Coordinated Shot, above, and this can make things get a bit silly at times

Ensemble: Very, very niche. Even bards aren’t going to need this much assistance too often. Very flavorful if you’ve got a rock band going, but that’s about it (Barbarians wrecking face with a Mighty Axe gets much funnier, though).

Escape Route: Probably the best default you can get, this plus properly delayed actions and battlefield movement will deny tons of attacks from enemy teams.

Extend the Bulwark: A bit of an odd duck. Giving up your armor’s bonus to AC to give an ally half that for a round can be clutch, but it’s situational. If you have multiple heavy-armor types, they can alternate the person up front, and interestingly enough this Circumstance bonus applies to Touch.

Feint Partner (and Improved): Being able to give this to the entire group means that one guy making one skill check can take huge chunks off of an opponent’s AC for your entire team’s turn. If the group is heavily melee, then Improved will break the action economy in half. If you have multiple members rocking Sneak Attack dice, then this becomes effectively a SOD for your enemy.

Fighting Frenzy: Requires the Rage class feature to learn, which is a wasted level for you. Pass

Focusing Blow: Hobgoblin-only, has a prereq that’s only good in Hobgoblin-heavy parties, and requires allies to deal damage to each other... but breaking Enchantments can potentially turn a hideous Domination into a mere delay if you are willing to deal with it.

Friendly Fire Maneuvers: Basically says that you can ignore the fact that allies are anywhere near enemies, but this usually isn’t needed with smart play. Also good if you have a relatively indiscriminate blaster on the team, +4 against allied Reflex saves can be nice to have.

Harder They Fall: Good benefit if you can afford to burn a turn, but qualifying for it is PAINFUL for you to do.

Horde Charge: There are a LOT of potential bonuses to the Charge in this section, and they all stack up together quickly. If you are already on the right race (or Bloodline), this one can turn your group charging into battle that much more lethal.

Intercept Charge: This will be invaluable for allowing your tanks to do their job, but even the squishier members of your group can act to save the tail of one of your buddies if they are about to take a potentially-lethal hit. Note that you can use this to stop the opponent WAY short of the target, which can let them get some needed movement free.

Lookout: Acting in the surprise round is not to be underestimated for someone who can rearrange his bonuses to suit the situation as a Swift. If everyone has decent Perception, this actually goes Sky Blue for allowing full actions in the surprise round.

Outflank: Goes up noticeably in value when you have a bunch of guys on your team that like getting up close and personal.

Overwhelm: Good effect, but difficult to reliably trigger. Fact is, your Melee guys will often want to be upped in size, which will disable this.

Pack Attack: Might cost 5-ft steps, but this allows you to permeate enemy defensive lines without provoking and it is all in addition to regular movement.

Paired Opportunist: This is one of those feats that looks a lot better than it is. The problem is that you both have to threaten the target, and most opponents who are in that kind of situation aren’t going to be doing any provoking if they can possibly avoid it. barely squeaked into Yellow due to the possibility of builds meant to force attacks of opportunity.

Phalanx Fighter: Goes up a half-notch if you’re crusading against the forces of Evil, you can nearly always arrange for a +1 to +3… but requires you to bunch up.

Precise Strike: Much like Outflank, this feat goes up in value if your team likes to get up close and personal.

Seize the Moment: Might have been upgraded to yellow if this was easier to qualify for or use, but Improved Critical is not exactly a priority for Viziers and this requires a crit-fisher ally to get decent effect.

Shake it Off: I wanted to rate this higher, I really did, but this is very dependent on your team all wanting to be in the same general area. If you regularly form up a square (and you aren’t afraid of AoE attacks from enemies), this is sick. Take this if your group clumps a lot.

Shield Wall: This would be a lot better if it didn’t require BOTH partners to be using a shield. Still, if you happen to have a Warder or a Dominance Daevic on your team, this could end up decent.

Shielded Caster: You won’t be making many of these checks, but by your presence you can give your friendly Wizard a +5 to concentration (or your friendly Paladin/Magus/Inquisitor/etc). This is a HUGE bonus, and makes those checks nearly certain to succeed. You just don’t get anything out of it yourself.

Snapping Flank: Good feat for extra attacks, as long as the party has a bunch of Bites. Unfortunately situational, and not somewhere you want to be anyway.

Spirit of the Corps: If you have one buffer in the party (or really, anyone who can generate Morale bonuses), suddenly EVERYONE gets to benefit at least in part. The invocation is a nonaction, and not limited to your turn (so you can borrow a Barbarian’s Will bonus from rage, for example).

Stealth Synergy: Becomes bluer than the bluest blue that ever blued if you are in a stealth-heavy campaign, since if your team is all rolling checks and everyone takes the highest, there is virtually no chance of failure outside of overwhelming odds. This is more often used by Gurus via the Collar of Skilled Instruction, but do not discount your ability to be a ghost with the best of them once Veils factor in.

Stone Dodger: Really niche applications, but if you fight Giants and/or deal with Sieges a lot, this will be a lifesaver. Feast or famine here, it all depends on the campaign.

Suppress Regeneration: Again, depends on how much it comes up. Turning off Regeneration for potentially 3-4 turns is a game-ender for most creatures who normally rely on it, the trick is triggering it when you can capitalize

Swap Places (and Improved): More methods to get you and your allies repositioned around the fight outside of standard moves, ignoring AoOs. Although most of these feats are good, you won’t need more than one or two if your group knows how to move normally.

Swing About: You get the point by now. Another good movement option, but don’t waste all your slots on that.

Tandem Evasion: Only useful against Dragons, and only against their Breath and Tail Sweep. While they tend to be serious threats, the big things to watch out for are not those unless you’re fighting a brood of them. You have better ways to resist energy, and the Tail Sweep is limited in damage.

Tandem Trip: Let me introduce you to a buddy of mine called Light Whip… and another called Coordinated Maneuvers. Add Essence Expertise, too. When they all apply, you can darned near trip a Centipede.

Target of Opportunity: HERE we go. As long as you have another ally who doesn’t like to be in the thick of things, this busts the action economy wide open. When we are talking about reinforced blasts from the Hand Cannons (Deadly Aim, Chakra Targeting, Essence, Storm Gauntlet Bind, etc) these bonus shots HURT. Any optimized ranged attacker will tell you that the key to true damage is extra attacks… and this gives everyone an additional one per round, effectively.

Team Pickpocketing: Cool and flavorful, check. Effective? Not so check. This is highly niche  and can’t really get much of anything, so unless you are in a mission where this pops up a lot then avoid this.

Topple Foe: One of your main defenses is tripping, so this is going to be life frequently… as long as the enemy is both bigger than you and ground-bound.

Tribe Mentality: The bonus is massive, since your high-Will guys can share with their lower save buddies. The problem is that it’s a narrow subset of spells that have to ALSO hit multiple allies simultaneously. Those situations are very rare.

Underhanded Teamwork (and Improved): Dirty Trick is an unusual specialty, but if your buddy manages to get a Blind off and you extend it by a bit, the game just ended in your favor. Improved suffers from both conditions being wiped at the same time.

Volley Fire: Doesn’t benefit the team as much, unless you are heavily ranged. In that case, allies will LOVE generating +3 and +4 to their ranged attacks.

Wall of Flesh: This is a very good feat with a sharp limit, that is that you have to be Small to take it, and most teams don’t have more than one of the Little Folk. You know what? It can be just YOU now, and your bigger teammates don’t need to meet the prereqs when you share it. Now, they will be BIGGER than they were, which has nice effects on things from your point of view.

Bloodforge Feats: These feats offer quite a few benefits that you can’t really pick up normally via feats.Many Vizier builds will have plenty of room left over for them, and the effects are very often ones you would otherwise be dropping serious Gold or shaping some of your precious Veils for. I am only skipping four feats here. Bloodsong Adept you don’t qualify for. Kinslayer and Resist Kindred are so campaign-dependent that I can’t effectively rate them here. Blood Affinity is unclear.

Mixed Blood: Starting this a bit out of alphabetical order, since this is the “additional feat” I was talking about. Several subtypes give very potent bonuses, such as Low Light or Darkvision, Scent, and resistances. Some give skill bonuses and movement modes, which can help. Others (such as the Alignment and Dwarf subtypes) should be avoided at all costs unless they are absolutely required in order to acquire something you need later. I will be factoring the value of your required Mixed Blood into the value of the feats that require them.

Vestigial/Aerial Wings: While there are other ways to get this instead of sinking 2-3 feats into it (most commonly Wings of Flying or shaping one of a couple of Veils), Flight with good speed and Good maneuverability, plus Fly as a class skill, is not to be discounted. The subtypes accessing this include several good ones, so you aren’t wasting the Mixed Blood to get in.

Altered Life/Unnatural Resistance: You need a good Con score to begin with, and your Fortitude is good anyway. Pass.

Ancestor’s Breath: This one falls under saving you a Veil, in this case the Gorget of the Wyrm. Has the advantage of being able to pick Sonic or Negative Energy as damage types, which are resisted less often… but the disadvantage of not being able to change it overnight (or even faster via Veilshifting).

Blood Frenzy: The bonus has no cap. Any big boss that decides to bring minions is going to have a VERY bad day on his hands. This goes down in value considerable if your encounters tend to be in ones and twos, but that is very campaign-dependent. You are usually going to be killy enough to rack up the bonuses fast. Obviously a no-go for people playing Rulers in a traditional avoid-combat role.

Champion’s Presence: Very rough subtypes to enter with, and the benefit is restricted to those who are sticking to your particular alignment. Broad but minor bonus as well, in a common bonus type that will often be overwritten. Usually going to be a trap, may see some use if your group is both devoted to a given alignment and also lacking a Bard or other similar buffer.

Feral Fighter: Melee stuff. To be avoided.

Sanguine Sorcery Line: There are mostly nifty tricks for you, just remember that Charisma is the save-stat for them and you normally dump that one. Stick to self-buffs and ones that don’t allow saves, like Magic Missile/Mage Armor, Haste/Invisibility, and Dimension Door

Spark of Divinity Line: Same save concern as Sanguine, but these have a lot more of the critical self-buffs you were missing. Unlike Guru and Daevic, Divine Power is not so much of a capstone for you, so you’ll usually not want Greater. Outsider type is VERY easy to get without needing feats, if you’re willing to look for races, but the skill bonus from Mixed Blood for it is decent.

Hold Prey/Instinctive Grapple: You can’t do most of what makes you great while stuck wrestling someone.

Mighty Frame: If you are doing raw damage other than Hand Cannon or Spell based, this can become worthwhile. Thankfully easy to access types.

Overflowing Life: Never rely on this in-combat, but having the ability to start things off at full health reliably throughout even the worst adventuring day is important. Less critical if you have a good source of out-of-combat healing, or if you generally stick to short adventuring days (or if you are a Grendle, since they recover quickly anyway).

Scrambler’s Gift: Climb speed is generally not going to be worth it when you can just as easily acquire Fly with Good or better maneuverability. Avoid this even if you come by the prerequisites without having to go out of your way (and ranks beyond 1-2 in Climb are usually out of your way in most campaigns)

Small Stature: Decent if Stealthy is your goal, but being able to use downsized weapons without penalty is rarely going to be a big benefit (other than, again, being easier to hide). It is very rare for smaller to be better in this game, since this does not affect AC or attack rolls.

Woeful Stench: VERY easy entry, and Sickened is a fight-ender… but this does not exclude allies, targets the save that is traditionally highest on your opponents, and requires you to be up in their faces to work.

Other Feats[a]: There are hundreds of feats out there, but most conventional wisdom stays true. They don’t usually need to get reviewed one by one, you’ve probably seen it all by now. Some highlights:

Power Attack, Piranha Strike, and Deadly Aim: Same thing for different weapons, but very nice to have. If you expect to be able to use it, grab one and it’s Akashic sequel. You have the Essence to fuel it.

Point Blank Shot (series): If you’re into ranged combat, take these and don’t look back. Get, at the very least, PBS and Precise Shot. Rapid Shot is optimal for damage if that’s what you’re going for, but going for Shot on the Run can be fun.

Armor Proficiency (Medium or Heavy): Something to look at if you are a Dwarf, since it won’t slow you down any further. Having these saves you a Ring Veil, so it’s something to consider if you have some space.

Improved Initiative: A classic standby, and still very nice to have. You getting a Swift action available before your opponents means that you can set yourself for the attack.

Combat Reflexes: Take a good hard look at this one. Your Dex is usually going to be high, and a few of your good Veils key off of Attacks of Opportunity (notably Light Whip and Eye of the Oracle).

Eldritch Heritage: If you have the 13 Charisma and three spare feat slots (two for Half-Elves), grab this for Arcane to get a familiar of your choice and Share Veil. Standouts include the ones that can speak, along with the Dodo (+4 untyped bonus to Initiative), and the monkey (which has all Chakra slots to share).

Open Minded: You have a LOT of skills you want to take, and the bare minimum skills per level. If you are in a build that doesn’t lean on saves (and thus doesn’t have a ton of Int), this will help make up the difference. You have a bunch that work perfectly fine at one rank each, so you shouldn’t need more than that.

Equipment: I am not even going to remotely try to go through every possible enchantment, wondrous item, and other mystical gizmo that may or may not help you in your quest. That would quite frankly be futile, since every character is different and every campaign even more so. Instead, I am going to give some general guidelines and point out some major stand-outs to save you some searching. If you know of any item that is undeniably awesome for the class that I missed, please tell me and I’ll look it up for inclusion here.

Generic items: +stat for the ones you care about, +save items, plus movement and vision modes you don’t have, are always going to be important.

Specific Items:  For the Vizier, you want to find CHARGED ITEMS of as wide a variety of effects as you can. You can invest one Essence to make it go without UMD, plus extra for bonus charges. Look to your chosen role for bonuses you need otherwise. Anything boosting Spellcraft is going to be important to a Crafter (Circlet of Arcana is a huge standout from bloodforge). Boosts to social skills are going to be important for Rulers, especially Diplomacy and Bluff. People focusing on the Hand Cannons are going to want to look for items like the Deliquescent Gloves, which add to your weapons.

Armor Enhancements: You are stuck with Light, so raw Enhancement is often going to be good. Fortification will save you a veil slot (and very often your life). Try to grab a wide variety of resistances and immunities where you can, and always remember that you also have a shield slot to put more of them (and help keep things cheaper, too)

Weapon Enhancements: You are looking for anything OTHER than things that help you when actually using the weapon to hurt people. Allying, Defending, or other such enhancements are your priority. Many of these will use the raw Enhancement bonus to determine effect, so keeping around a wand or stave of Greater Magic Weapon is a good idea.

Builds:

Note that these builds are not entirely mutually exclusive. A character I am currently running is an Artificer build with the Hand-Cannon setup as well… and he has enough skills to make him an effective Knowledge and Social skillmonkey on top of it all.

Hand-Cannon Blaster (Seer): A Dex-based build. Hand Cannon as a primary weapon, taking the Point Blank Shot line up to Rapid Shot, Twin Veil to let you use Storm gauntlets, plus Deadly Aim and Chakra Targeting. This is VERY feat-intensive, but the results are massive amounts of outgoing damage, much of that Energy of various kinds, at a high degree of accuracy (you can drop Twin if you REALLY need the room, but this cuts off a good deal of your energy damage). Snap Shot and Improved Snap Shot round things out if you have the room. Recommended Human for the extra feat to come online sooner. Remember that Manyshot only applies to Bows, so will not be useful if you are using a Veil as your weapon. Flip side, though, if you play an Elf or Aellar and use a bow...

Iron Man Artificer (Crafter): Trading on your ability to craft and utilize basically anything, then make a new one or re-fuel out of anything with charges. Works especially well with custom Staves. Allies will love you for keeping prime enchantments and buffs going on their items, no matter what form you find them in. Remember that even one Essence invested in an item will let you activate it all day long without UMD as long as its charges hold out.

Save-based (Ruler): Combining high Int, high Cha (or traits to swap some key skills to Int), and a few Veils that affect the social skills plus the aura reducing the enemy’s ability to resist them all means that a very large percentage of encounters will simply not occur. A bit skill-intensive, requiring Bluff, Intimidate, Diplomacy, and Sense Motive in addition to your normal skill requirements… but all except one of those is a Class skill and you’re likely to have a ton of skill points from high Int. Top it off with the selection of Fascinate effects you have access to when you run into an area that might not be amenable to discussion, and you still have plenty of open slots in case you actually have to fight.

Melee: (Crafter, Credit to Novawurmson of Giant in the Playground). There are actually a couple of ways to go about this. The best is probably to focus in on the Loyal Paladin’s Spear of Light. It counts as a weapon for things like Armory of the Conqueror and Storm Gauntlets, plus gets to draw from your Veilweaver Level instead of BAB (including for things like Power Attack). You also get excellent reach with it, and can support it with tons of active defenses and BFC

Battlefield Control (Any Path): The Vizier has access to a very wide range of effects that tilt the battlefield in your favor. Light Whip combined with Essence Expertise is your defensive core, creating a 15-ft radius circle centered on you where enemies effectively can’t walk. Ditchdigger’s Armlets and Stare of the Ghaele are the basics of your offense, generating difficult terrain and damage plus severe status effects on all foes. You can pick Riven Darts and/or Gorget of the Wyrm (or Ancestor’s Breath from Bloodforge) to clear crowds of minions and allow your primary strikers to better target the bigger enemies, and Stormcaller’s Band if you feel the need to push enemies around as well (especially if you expect to face fliers). Finally, Shape Veil for Crusader’s Shield lets you put instant walls up that take serious effort to punch out.
