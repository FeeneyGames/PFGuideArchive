Abraham Spalding’s Guide to the Arcane Trickster.

The arcane trickster is a complex and interesting prestige class.  It is also one that it is easy to make mistakes in if one isn’t careful and aware of what works and doesn’t work with the prestige class.  I intend to go over the multiple ways you can enter the class, what tends to work well with the class, and what tends to not work out as well so pitfalls can be avoided.

The arcane trickster has the following prerequisites:
Non-lawful alignment
Sneak Attack 2d6
2nd level arcane spell casting and the ability to cast mage hand
Disable Device 4 ranks, Escape Artist 4 ranks, Knowledge (arcana) 4 ranks.

The easy parts of getting in are of course the skills and the alignment, I’m not going to cover these as it isn’t worth the time, instead I’m going to focus on the arcane casting and sneak attack prerequisites:

Sneak Attack:
There are multiple ways to gain sneak attack in pathfinder currently, but not all of them are equal, and some of them are better or worse depending on how you plan to cover your spell casting needs:

Rogue:  Plain old vanilla rogue, three levels in this and you have your sneak attack dice.  Rogue is still a solid choice for getting into the arcane trickster:  You get evasion, a rogue talent (you can get more by spending feats), and trap sense.  However you might want to consider the following Archetypes instead of a simple rogue:
Thug – at third level you can choose to forgo 1d6 sneak attack damage to make targets sickened.  This means they’ll hit less, deal less damage and have penalties to their save throws.  At later levels you’ll be able to debuff while you blast.
Sniper – The reduced penalties with the bow only has use if you intend to grab two levels of arcane archer (more on that later) however the third level ability extends the range you can sneak attack at.  This is of course generally useful… however there are items you can buy that do similar (indeed more powerful) things.  However if you think you’ll be on a shoestring budget this might catch your eye.
Rake -- much like the thug only with a skill check and they are shaken instead of sickened.  However you get it at level 1 instead (but give up trapfinding).  Sickened is the more useful debuff and Thug doesn’t require a save -- but if you are going in and out as fast as possible for the sneak attack Rake is a good choice.

Ninja:  An offshoot of the rogue the ninja makes a great choice for sorcerers that want to go arcane trickster.  The ninja has a specific ninja trick that makes this work, vanish.  The ninja trick vanish allows you to go invisible as a swift action which is one of the primary ways of gaining sneak attack damage with rays.  It also gains poison use which is fairly useless to most arcane tricksters, and the no trace ability, however these later abilities come at the cost of trapfinding, trapsense and evasion.  For some this isn’t a huge deal, for others it is.  All in all I still think it’s a great trade, as anyone can find traps and you can even disable magical traps with something other than the disable device skill, which aside from the bonus on perception and disable device checks is all trapfinding does.  What’s more we can get evasion in a ring so that class feature has a bit less value to the sorcerer compared to swift action invisibility from close to the start of your arcane trickster career.

Vivisectionist Alchemist:  For a wizard (or magus) the vivisectionist alchemist can make an excellent choice to get sneak attack.  It has intelligence based extracts which can either be prepared ahead of time, or on the fly provided you have a minute for each one you want to use.  In addition the alchemist has a better fortitude save than the rogue and several discoveries that can be advantageous to an arcane trickster, as well two bonus feats that can serve you well at lower levels:  Throw anything (alchemist version) and brew potion.  At higher level the throw anything feat will lose significance as (probably) the brew potion feat will too, but both can be useful for the arcane trickster up through the middle levels.  Archetypes to look at to go with vivisectionist include:
Internal – the internal alchemist gains a bonus on saves against disease and the ability to hold their breath for long periods of time… even appearing dead.  This can be very useful for sneaking into places either as a corpse or through waterways (or other areas without air).
Mindchemist – the mindchemist gets bonuses on knowledge checks which can spread out how many skill points you put into each knowledge.  They also get cognatogens instead of mutagens which can help temporarily boost your DCs for spells and bonuses on skill checks.  This comes at the cost of a penalty to strength but you honestly don’t need strength anyways.

Magus(Greensting Slayer) -- Alright.  So it is nice that you can get your sneak attack AND not lose any spellcasting.  However you have to burn a swift action to get that sneak attack and an arcane point.  Also it scales slower and it only works on melee attacks.  This just sets up the greensting slayer to not be a great choice.  Especially since it replaces your normal option for using your arcane pool to improve your weapon.  It doesn’t really mix well with other methods of getting sneak attack either.  Either way you won’t get a BAB increase at level 5, and you won’t get a chance to increase your talents or discoveries because you won’t get them in the first place.  IF you want to have only a single class before going into arcane trickster and you want to be a melee focused arcane trickster this is not the worse option out there.

Other methods of gaining sneak attack dice:  Along with these three classes there are a couple of prestige classes (master spy, assassin, and Inner sea pirate) that grant sneak attack dice as well as a bard archetype.  I will discuss the bard archetype in the section on arcane casting and instead focus on the prestige classes for now.  Assassins gain a sneak attack dice immediately upon entering the class.  If you don’t care for the abilities of the above classes you could simply grab a single level in one of them 4 levels in a casting class and then a level of assassin (or inner sea pirate) to enter into arcane trickster.  This route is slightly better for wizards than sorcerers since the death attack you also gain with assassin is intelligence based.  However the DC for the death attack won’t really ever be impressive and generally will still be fairly useless to you (especially with the setup time).  For those willing to wait the master spy also grants a sneak attack dice on the first level.  However you have to be level 7 before you can take a level in master spy.  The abilities of the master spy might be useful if you are in a campaign that has a lot of intrigue, however generally they won’t be much use elsewhere.

Arcane Casting:

There are several arcane classes that are capable of entering the arcane trickster prestige class:  The bard, the magus, the sorcerer, the summoner, and the wizard.

Bard -- The bard is generally speaking a poor class to choose for arcane trickster as unfortunate as this is.  Bards need four levels in their class in order to enter arcane trickster and that’s the point when their class features tend to start looking really good too.  Also the bard tends to lack a lot of the spells that really work well with the arcane trickster.  However the bard does gain some healing magic and armored casting which could be a plus for a flanking arcane trickster.  Specific archetypes that might like the arcane trickster are:
Sandman bard – The sandman bard gains sneak attack at level 5 and every 5 levels thereafter allowing you to stick to the bard class keeping your caster level up while still meeting prerequisites.  The ability to steal spells from opponents to cast on your own is a nifty trick as well and plays into the theme of the class well too.  In fact sandman bard is the only class that can enter arcane trickster without multiclassing, and can disarm magical traps with the disable device skill.  Finally he gains a class ability that increases the DC of spells he casts when opponents are denied their dex bonus.  Unfortunately the sandman bard still doesn’t have many abilities to use sneak attack with and he’s extremely late entering the class if he goes solo bard to get into arcane trickster.
Sound Striker bard – the sound striker bard gains a sixth level ability to make ranged touch attacks against foes dealing sonic damage.  With the right set up this could be a means of delivering sneak attack damage for a bard, however it doesn’t gain sneak attack on its own, doesn’t stack with sandman well and would cause some pretty late entry into the arcane trickster prestige class.
Archeologist bard – The archeologist bard gains uncanny dodge early and a bonus on all perception and disable device checks.  At fourth level they gain a rogue talent as well, meaning the class can stack well with a ninja entry for sneak attack.  Its ‘bardic performance’ is a self only buff that is a luck bonus at the cost of a swift action.  However it still lacks generally in the spell department.  All in all however it generally synergizes well with ninja.

Magus – The magus can make a decent arcane trickster, however he has to plan more carefully than the sorcerer or wizard does.  The magus has armored casting which is a plus as well as two good saves.  He can also cast and melee at the same time.  A very careful build could conceivably get a full attack with sneak attack and then a blast spell with sneak attack at higher levels, but such a build would be walking a razor’s edge trying to stay effective on all sides at the same time.  Arcane accuracy could help with this and the arcane pool does offer some neat tricks to the magus that no one else has readily available, but don’t expect this class to be an easy one to use with arcane trickster.  However his lack of general spell slots and the general sense of being torn in multiple directions makes this something not for the faint of heart.

Sorcerer – The sorcerer is much more limited on spells known than the wizard and can’t guarantee that he’ll actually have more to cast than the wizard will.  Also his general class features are not as front loaded as the wizard’s are meaning that if you choose sorcerer to go into arcane trickster you need to have a clear plan on what spells you are going to pick and what you are looking to do.  Archetypes (and bloodlines) that go well with arcane trickster include:
Sage – the sage bloodline gains a ranged touch attack that deals force damage and is intelligence based allowing you to gain more skills.  However it doesn’t play as nice with ninja as other sorcerer bloodlines do.
Aberrant – The aberrant bloodline gains a ray at first level and increase reach at third level.  This reach increase can be critical if you plan to rely on flanking to get your sneak attack on since it helps keep you further from the direct line of fire of your enemies.  However it’s bonus spells aren’t overly useful.
Orc – The orc bloodline’s arcana is highly useful to an arcane trickster as it adds bonus damage per dice of damage you do.  Sneak attack is extra dice for damage and therefore would gain the bonus damage too.  However the bloodline powers are pretty poor overall for the arcane trickster.  One option would be to crossblood the orc bloodline with one that has better powers for you… aberrant could be a nice choice for such.
Elemental/primordial dragon – Both of these offer ranged touch attacks and the ability to switch the energy type of spells you cast, helping you to have a bit more versatility.
Draconic – A more focused increase in damage than the orc and the claws aren’t going to be generally useful to you, however you can use the claws if you need a melee weapon for some reason and both will be at your best attack bonus… however even then you should stay out of direct melee, especially when you aren’t using touch attacks.
Seeker -- A seeker sorcerer gives up eschew materials (a fairly weak feat) and his third level bloodline power to gain a version of trapfinding and some small bonuses with his bloodline spells.  If you take the eldritch heritage feats for a bloodline with good powers and this with a bloodline that has a great arcana but poor power choices (like orc for example) then this archetype can be a great match with the ninja.  However I wouldn’t mix this archetype with the aberrant bloodline as you WANT that third level power.

Summoners – Really you just shouldn’t do this.  Your spell list isn’t a good match for the arcane trickster, your primary class features suffer, and arcane trickster doesn’t really offer much to you.

Wizard – the wizard makes a good arcane trickster.  High intelligence plays into both skills (both points and key skill uses) and his many archetypes and specializations play in well with the strengths of the arcane trickster.   Archetypes (and specializations) to look into include:
Admixture (evocation) specialist – you can change up the element of your evocation spells and get a little bonus damage too.  The energy change up grants a lot of versatility and can help you keep from having to prepare a lot of different energy types if you don’t know what you are facing.
Foresight (divination) specialist – you get a bonus on initiative which is middling nice, and there are several spells you’ll want to have around on a regular basis in the divination school.  The major feature of this specialization however is the free d20 roll you can have hanging around for critical checks in a round and the ability to always act in a surprise round.  Arcane tricksters tend to be a bit squishy, but if you can go first you can possibly move to a more defensible location (behind the fighter) or cast a spell to get you out of danger quickly (either teleporting out or blowing holes in the enemy).
Teleportation (conjuration)specialist – if you want breaking and entering a swift action teleport is good for that.  It doesn’t hurt that the conjuration school has lots of good spell choices on each spell level too.
The Thassilonian specialist – the thassilonian specialist can help keep ahead on spell economy.  In return for having your banned schools chosen for you and never being able to cast those spells at all you gain an extra spell slot for your specialized school of magic.  Unfortunately you have to memorize the same spell that you did in the first bonus spell slot again.  However you still gain all your school (or subschool) powers in addition to your arcane bond.
Shadowcaster – The shadowcaster wizard gives up his arcane bond but gains the ability to store a few spells in his shadow.  If you know what you are doing you can make this work for you… however it only covers a number of spell levels equal to half your wizard level so the regular arcane bond might be a better choice for most people.

Putting it together:
So which combination for you?  Good question.   Generally Ninja works best with the charisma casters while I would recommend vivisectionist for the intelligence based casters.  Vivisectionist offers extracts to extend your abilities, the discovery for useful side abilities, and a bit more survivability with the improved fortitude save.  The combinations that are the strongest to me are:


Sorcerer/Ninja – For a sniping arcane trickster this is a great deal.  You have the ability to go invisible as a swift action and then lay down the hurt each round.  As your charisma increases your spells do as does your ki pool.

Wizard/Vivisectionist – The vivisectionists extracts and mutagen can help cover your defenses and utility abilities (those that supplement your skills) leaving your spells free for other purposes.  The bonus on fortitude saves (and extra bonuses on specific saves) doesn’t hurt your survivability and you can grab fortification as a discovery to help against the times you might get hit with a critical or sneak attack yourself.

Wizard/Rogue – Still a great choice.  Evasion isn’t a bad thing and there are plenty of good rogue talents to choose from too.

Magus/Vivisectionist – At lower levels you really keep them guessing.  They think you are a rogue then bam you hit them with a spell, so they go to close and you quick buff then hit them with your sword.  With your mutagen and extracts you can control the field while laying down some hurt.  Your saves are strong too since Magus has good fort and will while vivisectionist has good fort and reflex.  You aren’t as glassy as other arcane tricksters either early on since you have armored casting – just don’t let yourself take on too many jobs at once or you’ll be out of resources and stuck in a creek bleeding to death.

Bard/ninja – Like the Magus/vivisectionist you can mix it up some.  Ninja ki can be spent on extra attacks at lower level so if you don’t have the spells you could simply flank and get in two solid hits with a melee weapon gaining sneak attack damage at the same time.  Combined with your bardic performance (depending on archetype) you could be dishing some decent damage and you have some fall back spells others don’t.  However don’t go thinking you are superman because your  resources are very limited  Make a power play when needed but don’t expect to do so all the time.

Summoner/ninja – again don’t do this to yourself.

Bard (sandman)/ninja/assassin(master spy) – you’re playing a long term game, and possibly looking to be undercover.  This choice would work best in a spy vs spy game where you don’t really want anyone to know what you are fully capable of.  However your abilities are spread out and somewhat unfocused.  Don’t stay too long in any combat and hit very hard very fast if you are in one then leave, before they realize you are made of glass.  Also be prepared for frustration as you will rarely be able to really bring all your abilities to bear and will take quite the long time getting into the meat of your build.

What Came first?  The sneak attack or the casting?

For me what comes first depends entirely on what type of arcane trickster you are playing:

If you are going for the Intelligence synergy of wizard/vivisectionist/AT then you can't go wrong leading off with either.  Typically I would take the other at second level (when the huge spread of first level abilities is most useful) and then finish wizard followed by alchemist.

If I'm doing a sorcerer/ninja, I'm more likely to go ninja/ninja/sorcerer or sorcerer/ninja/ninja.  That way I get to my inviso-sneak attack faster and have something to do with my ninja ki before hand (another attack with a full attack).

If I'm going wizard/rogue (or magus/rogue) I'll typically start with the caster levels -- this is because it gets me my spellbook for free (something some GM's will decide to charge for) and it avoids the, "wait how did you suddenly become a wizard with familiar and spellbook in the middle of nowhere" 'problem'.

Feats:
There are many, many feat options for any character, and the arcane trickster is in the same place.  So what should you look for?  It depends on how you plan to do business.  In combat your main shtick is landing sneak attack damage (generally with spells) without blowing your load each time you get into combat.  You also want to do what you can to avoid being stuck in any one place for a long time as sitting still can make you an easier target.  Specific feats to look out for or avoid:

Teamwork feats – for some people these are some of the best things ever – for you not so much.  Generally you have to be near or adjacent to your allies to gain the benefits of these feats and too often that means being places that will get you squished.  These are probably best avoided.

Toughness – A bit of extra hp never hurt anyone.

Improved Initiative – you want to hit fast and hit often… and in the cases where you are being hit you want to move fast and leave quickly before you get ganked – improved initiative can help in both situations.

Arcane armor training – I swear there is a place for this feat… but for most arcane tricksters it’s not with them.  Wizards can get some decent use out of it, but for magus and bards it’s useless and the sorcerer would probably rather be invisible than wasting his swift action so he can simply cast.  At lower levels mage armor is a better choice and at higher levels there are other (better) options for defense.  As such I really can’t recommend this feat in general.

Persistent spell  -- A lot of the best spells for the arcane trickster are those that do damage but also carry a secondary effect based on a save throw, at later levels it’ll be AoE spells carrying sneak attack damage with them too (and possibly secondary effects) – either way you do not want your enemies making a save throw.  This feat can help prevent that.

Weapon Finesse – Touch spells are your bread and butter when it comes to combat, however you don’t want to be wasting a lot of time and effort raising your strength when you aren’t going to have much use for it.  Instead raise your dexterity and take weapon finesse – you get the attack bonus you need, better AC, better initiative and better reflex saves.

Precise shot (improved precise shot) – for the sorcerer/ninja sniper arcane trickster these feats are going to be rather important.  The arcane trickster doesn’t have much in the way of attack bonus and for a large part of your career you are going to be sniping with rays from afar.  As such you don’t want your opponents to be getting bonuses on their touch AC or taking penalties on your attack rolls.  These two feats help you avoid up to 8 points in combined bonus to AC and penalties on attack rolls.  If you are looking to use rays a lot these feats will probably be a very good investment.

Intensify spell – Hey if you are blowing things up with damage you best get the most you can.  However this will probably be a late game feat if you take it.  After all you have to have enough caster level to actually use up the damage you are getting in the first place.

Spell Penetration (greater spell penetration) – nothing stinks more than losing a spell to spell resistance.  In combat an arcane trickster generally relies on damage dealing spells and most of those allow for spell resistance.  As such getting past that is always a good thing (especially when you are going to be down caster levels already).

Spell focus(greater spell focus) – Again you’re going to want spells that not only deal damage but also make your enemies suffer.  Usually this involves a save throw so you might as well make it as hard for them as you can.

Varasian tattoo – This grants you an (usually) useless ability… and an increase to caster level for one school of magic.  Hey take it where you can get it.

Reach Spell -- There are an awfully good number of spells that would be great if they didn’t require you to walk up and touch your enemy.  For a one level adjustment Reach spell can help you get around that requirement.  With magical lineage (a trait) for chill touch this can be a great low level option for staying out of melee.  However through about level 6 you aren’t going to have the extra spell slots to really get much use out of this metamagic feat so a rod might be a better choice then.  At higher levels when you have a ready source of invisibility or other means of making ranged sneak attacks and more (and higher level) spell slots this becomes a great choice.

Greater Feint -- The feats leading up to this are going to be of middling use to you depending on your build (flanking ATs might be able to get some use out of combat expertise and improved feint).  But greater feint works for all types of attacks and grants you the means of creating sneak attack solutions when other things (like invisibility) simply won’t work.  It also requires less to get to than other options making it attractive for the intelligence based AT.

Piercing spell -- This is an alright metamagic feat for spontaneous casters -- however I would personally rather have a rod of it.  You are going to have plenty of feats you want already and spell resistance can be handled pretty regularly with spell penetration and greater spell penetration.  For the times when you need the bump Piercing spell is nice but a rod should be able to handle what you need there.

Twist Away - Great feat. Now you can have 3 good saves. Buy a Ring of Ferocious Action to deal with the drawback.

Traits:
Traits are a fairly recent addition to the pathfinder rule set.  If your GM allows them you are going to want magical knack.  Reactionary is a good trait as well.  If your GM doesn’t allow you to have them up front grab the extra traits feat at some point to get magical knack and reactionary if he will let you at some point.

Rogue Talents, Ninja Tricks, Discoveries and Arcana:
So there are several classes in this document that offer up choices of abilities.  I’ll list some of the ones I like over all here:

Rogue Talents:

Ninja trick – in this case we’ll be looking at pressure points.  You are going to need to get what you can out of your cantrips for a long time, and if you are dealing 1d3+2d6+1 dex damage (or strength damage) you’re getting a pretty good deal out of them.
Fast Stealth – moving at full speed while hiding can get you out of trouble or into position effectively.  If you expect to be in a sneaky party or needing to hide and move this is a rogue talent for you.
Offensive Defense – You know what else you can do with your sneak attack?  Gain some AC.  Once you gain your capstone ability you can now gain AC against everyone in your AOE effect while also dealing damage with pressure points… a nice little add on.  This shouldn’t be overly helpful however as you shouldn’t be getting swung at that often.
Trap Spotter – If you are having a lot of traps this one can save you some time and headache trying to remember to look for them all the time.  A situational talent however since to be useful there has to be traps and you have to not want to spend time stating, “I’m looking for traps” every couple of minutes.

Ninja Tricks:
Vanish Trick – This trick is one of the major reason to go ninja in the first place.  Take it as soon as you can.
Feather Fall – A rather situational power, but if your GM likes dropping you from heights and you are limited on your spells known this can be a rather useful one.
Pressure Points – As mentioned under rogue talents this is a nice way to offer up a different effect than just more damage against your foes.

Discoveries:
Vestigial Limb --  Hey you’re always going to want to hold stuff.  A wand, a scroll, a potion, a weapon, a metamagic rod, a mithral buckler – keep a hand free by literally having an extra one.
Preserve Organs – Crit happens.  Try and keep it from happening to you with fortification.  You can gain more fortification by spending more discoveries (read feats) on this if you find yourself needing it.  Of course if you are needing it that often maybe you should try getting hit less.
Cognatogen – give your mental stats a bit of a boost at the same time you are increasing your AC.  If you are expecting a big fight this makes a good pre-buff.
Spontaneous Healing – It’s not going to amount to a lot, but emergency self activating healing can be a nice thing to have.

Arcana:
Arcane Accuracy – Look you want to hit, this makes it possible.  Don’t use it every round, but when you are ready to lay down a full attack and then blast a target you don’t want to flub the full attack with sneak attack damage.
Wand Mastery – If you are relying on wands to stretch out your day you want to get the most you can out of them.
Wand Wielder – if you are using wands or staves to extend your day you are probably going to want to be able to use them while using spell combat.

Familiar or Item Arcane Bond?

You might end up with the choice of a familiar or an item bond.  The item bond is useful for a spell on the fly that you might not have prepared if you are a prepared caster and it allows you to enchant an item you normally might not be able to enchant.  However it also leaves you vulnerable to disarms/sunder attempts and generally doesn’t offer much beyond one spell a day.  As such I rate the item bond orange.  It’s useful but you give up a lot more abilities and power for what is a rather limited ability -- but it doesn’t hurt you other than the opportunity cost.

A familiar on the other hand gives either a skill, initiative, or save bonus – generally close to what you would get from a feat.  It also grants you the alertness feat when it is within five feet of you giving you an extra +2~4 on perception and sense motive checks.  You don’t lose anything if it dies and it can be an extra roll for perception and the like as well.  In a combat situation you can have it hand things to you, use wands on you, or use your skills while you are busy doing something else (if you need a lock picked while in the middle of combat for example).  Improved familiars bring even more to the table and can easily be worth a feat to pick up.   Improved familiars that I personally like include the Silvanshee (a healing battery with some combat potential) the pseudodragon (blindsense is a wonderful ability to have around as is telepathy) the imp (fast healing, multiple forms, and is generally fairly hardy), the faerie dragon (spell casting, a nice disabling breath weapon, nice movement options) and possibly the dweomercat cub (pounce, rake,  d.door, detect magic, and a nice dweomer leap ability that you can use to foil casters or get it out of trouble).

Skills:
The bread to magic’s butter skills are what are going to cause you to survive long term.  A straight caster has the spell slots to blow on things like invisibility to sneak past a guard, or fly to get over a wall.  You don’t.  You are likely to be 1~3 caster levels behind the straight caster and needing to use each spell slot as effectively and as efficiently as possible.  To do this you want your skills to carry you as far as possible.  Skills to keep an eye on include:


Escape Artist – you are unlikely to have a good CMD.  As such you are going to want a means of escaping grapples, and this skill is going to be it for you.  Since it’s a skill you’ll have your level +3 as your basic bonus on it and then you get your Dexterity modifier added on too.  Combined with an item for a bonus on it (or at least the grease spell) and you’ll probably be able to get out of a grapple.  Please note this isn’t such a great skill for teleportation subschool wizards since they can just teleport out of grapples.

Stealth – Yeah you are going to want to be able to sneak around – it’s hard to hit what you don’t know is there and stealth offers a great means to get a surprise round in and sneak attack before someone else has the chance to do it to you.

Perception – Knowledge is power and you are going to want to know what is around you before it knows you know.  Perception helps find traps too which helps save other resources for other things.

Disable Device – Face it you’re a rogue (of some flavor) expect to need to sneak past traps on occasion and have this skill ready.

Knowledge(any) – you’re going to want some of each knowledge just so you can keep track of and identify what you are coming across as you go.  As a rogue (possible scout) this knowledge can help make the difference between life and death, and knowing what to use to defeat what monster allows you to avoid wasting spells.

Swim/Climb/Acrobatics – Remember when I said use skills not spells?  Yeah these skills are going to play a large part in that.  What’s more it helps you do so stealthily as skills can’t be detected by divinations like detect magic (where as spider climb can).  It isn’t perfect of course but knowing that you can handle mundane tasks without magic is always a good thing.

Spellcraft – You are going to want this skill.  Identifying spells and effects with it isn’t overly hard so don’t overemphasis it but definitely keep some points in it.  You don’t want to be scouting, moving or finding traps and have to stop to figure out what exactly it is you are seeing.

Sleight of Hand -- It’s not going to be a regular thing, but with your ranged legerdemain ability there might be times where you can rob your opponent blind before you have to kill him.  This will of course make killing him infinitely easier.  However for this to work you will need a lot of ranks in sleight of hand -- but that’s okay since it also lets you hide stuff on your body and perform minor tricks that can have some use.  I know some GMs that allow it to stand in for a profession(pick pocket) or perform check when juggling or performing knick knack magic tricks (like a street magician does) so find out what your GM allows with it.

Now part of the problem is you only have so many skill points to go around of course.  Even if you go with an intelligence based caster you are liable to only get between 7~9 skill points each level and at lower levels that isn’t much.  Personally I would keep perception and disable device maxed out as well as stealth and possibly sleight of hand, I would split the rest of my points between knowledge/spellcraft and swim/climb/acrobatics about evenly.  As you gain more levels and your intelligence goes up you’ll end up getting more and more skill points and you can fill in the gaps then.

Spells:

An arcane trickster has a problem.  He is split among two different classes, one of which doesn’t advance his spell casting abilities.  So each spell he casts has to really shine to make up for the fact he can’t cast all the time.  As such my spell recommendations are generally going to be spells that offer:

Versatility in options – a blasting spell that can be multiple energy types, and/or carries a kicker effect.

Long lasting – spells that linger or give multiple rounds of uses (in the case of blasting spells).

Effects you can’t get anywhere else – spells that offer something that you need, or simply shouldn’t pass up easily.

Information gathering – The arcane trickster does his best when he knows what he’s getting into.  There are several spells that help you avoid being surprised and help you plot and plan your way ahead.  Much like in Ocean’s Eleven you want to do prep work every time you get a chance before you have to do a job.   In adventuring this isn’t always possible, but these spells can help you at least get a cursory glance at what you are up against before it is trying to eat you.

One last thing -- I don’t rate spells.  The situations that each spell is useful is very different and some are useful in different ways -- if those situations or means aren’t available then the spell is near useless (or worse since it is eating up a slot you could use for something else).  Instead I’ll simply highlight spells that I think are on worth noting in general for an AT.

0th level:

Acid Splash – face it you are going to have to get use to a dinky damage spell like this for a while.  But don’t hate it, it’s a ranged touch attack that allows you to easily deliver sneak attack and rider effects.  For your first several levels this is going to be that nine mil they give you in the start of all those first person shooter games.

Detect Magic – Magic is everywhere in pathfinder.  And you are going to want to know where it is, and what it is before it can bite you in the butt.  Detect magic detects a whole host of things, from magical items on ambushers, to magical traps, to invisible objects, to magic doors... the list goes on.  When in doubt detect magic.

Ghost Sound/Dancing Lights – these two spells together can help you distract guards, spook goblins and otherwise mess with the environment before you have to enter it.

Message – So you are scouting and see a means for your party to sneak around and help you set up an ambush… but how do you tell them from 30 feet away without shouting?  The message spell.  This is the comlink in your team’s ears so use it well.  Knowledge and information are power – spread such amongst your allies and dominate.

Daze – At lower levels you won’t always be able to contribute much in damage.  However your enemies are likely to be low on hit dice too, and possibly on save throws.  Daze can save your party from damage – if the enemy doesn’t take an action because of you then you are successfully contributing to the party.  This spell quickly loses its punch however so don’t expect it to work for long.

Mage Hand – You have to have it and you kind of want it too.  You can use mage hand to quietly and stealthily grab items from across a room, move small triggers, set off some traps and other such mischief.  Once you have ranged legerdemain this spell quickly loses its appeal however and you’ll probably not prepare it much.

Prestidigitation – This is like the minor miracle spell.  It can warm food, make it taste better, clean yourself, dry things off… the list goes on and on.  If you want an effect but don’t want to waste a lot of magic on it prestidigitation is probably the spell for you.

Sift – Bards can get some good mileage out of this spell.  It lets you search an area with only a standard action from a distance… but with a -5 penalty on your perception check.

1st level:

Chill Touch – so you end up with a flanking position and you want to unleash your sneak attack pressure point heck on the enemies.  What spell do you use, shocking grasp?  Meh, it’s only going to net you one attack and even then only 5d6 in addition to your sneak attack damage.  Instead go with chill touch – you can get as many attacks as you have caster levels each with sneak attack.  What’s more if you have pressure points you can be looking at 2 points of strength damage per hit.  It can also be useful for chasing away undead – over all it is a very useful spell.

Mage Armor – alright you probably aren’t going to want to prepare this one… but you do want a wand or several scrolls or potions of it if you don’t have armored casting.  Mage armor has a long duration and provides a good bonus for what you are doing.  You aren’t a tank but this can help your odds at lower level without ASF or weight issues.

Infernal Healing – Hey if you can get it a bit of healing never hurts.  Infernal healing makes a better wand than prepared spell however and some GM’s really don’t like it, so don’t expect it to be available.

See Alignment – Paladins get something like this all the time, and now you can too.

Vanish – A short term invisibility spell.  I’m sure you can figure out its uses.  Just remember it is short term and probably shouldn’t be overly relied on as it’s one spell each time you use it.  However if you are getting this off the ninja trick have as much fun as you like!

Frostbite – For a magus this spell is almost interchangeable with chill touch.  It fatigues targets limiting their mobility giving them a 2 point penalty to strength and dexterity and deals some damage.  All in all a tight little spell.  Just wish the damage wasn’t non-lethal… but hey that has its uses too.

Cure Light Wounds – if you are a bard this can be a good back up spell to have available to you.

Ear Piercing Scream – This spell doesn’t carry sneak attack damage until you have your AT capstone, but it can daze opponents and deals sonic damage.  For bards it’s one of the few damage spells they have… for anyone else it’s probably to be avoided.

Snowball - Nice damage (1d6/caster (no save) + staggered 1 round (fortitude negates) + no SR + ranged touched).  A lot of GM’s don’t like it because of what it is and the school it is in.  However it is a nice combination spell.

2nd level:

Alter Self - A good and versatile spell. Don’t have darkvision? No problem! Use alter self to “be” a goblin and get a +2 dexterity and darkvision.

Acid Arrow – This spell doesn’t allow SR and is valuable because of that, it also has long range and offers repeating damage.  It’s biggest use is going to be against casters (to disrupt their spells) and things with SR you can’t beat (golems for example).  Other than that you have other choices that are probably better.

Scorching Ray – Well it’s fire damage, and you’ll find that can suck as fire is the most common resistance in the game.  Early on offers solid damage not dependent on caster level.

Elemental Touch – If you are a flanker this is a nice spell for you.  Multiple touches over time, a choice in damage type and a kicker effect too.  The damage is low, however you’ll be supplementing with sneak attack so you’ll easily make up for that.

Stone Call – Once you have your capstone ability in AT this becomes a nice spell.  No save throw, no spell resistance, it deals damage in a large area and then makes it harder to move in that area.  The damage type isn’t exciting but there are worse choices, and if you have kickers on your sneak attack damage you get them against everything hit.  You can do much worse than this spell at higher levels.

Glitterdust – Blindness is one condition that can allow you to sneak attack opponents with near impunity – glitterdust does that in an area and can help you and your allies find invisible creatures – a nice combination for a non-damaging spell.

See invisibility – not just invisibility but ethereal creatures too.

Burning Arc – this isn’t a bad spell… but it isn’t a great spell either – it doesn’t have an attack roll so you can’t get sneak attack with it until higher level and at that point the DC is too low – overall you should probably pass on this one.

Ghoul’s Touch – sometimes you need to stop the enemy before you can kill him.  Ghoul Touch can let you do that and also offers an area of effect debuff for his allies too.  Unfortunately it’s a fortitude save which is typically what the monsters are the best at so depending on campaign this can easily be something to pass on.

Pyrotechnics – this is a versatile spell.  You can blind multiple enemies with it, create battlefield conditions in your benefit (knock out their light, put them in a smoke cloud and get a debuff from that cloud) and damage fire subtype creatures.  It isn’t an everyday spell but when it comes up it can easily turn an encounter easy for your party.

Fiery Shuriken -- Alright so this spell starts out with two ranged touch attacks and goes up to 8.. each of which can carry sneak attack.  So compared to scorching ray we have: 3x(11d6) or 8x(1d8+7d6) -- that’s either 33d6 or 8d8+56d6... I know which one I like better, but if they have resistance to fire scorching ray might come out better for you.

Invisibility -- While vanish is lower level and greater covers after an attack invisibility has the best duration of the three.  However there are plenty of items that can give you invisibility.  If you can’t get one of those then preparing this spell might not be a bad idea for when you are scouting.  Just remember that it’s only a minute per level and it can be picked up by various magical sensors -- use it as an augmentation to a great stealth score, not as a replacement for your stealth score (something an amatuer like a wizard would do ;p ).

3rd level:

Arcane Sight – A pre-buffable version of detect magic, this takes away the need to concentrate.  Permanency this spell later but it probably won’t see much use early on.

Battering Force – This is a fun one:  Decent force damage and you can literally push your opponents around.  What’s more you get multiple attacks out of it.

Chain of Perdition – This can blind your enemies and generally make life suck for them.  It does so on its own, and ignoring invisibility/fog/darkness while being a force effect.  Again not a low level spell but once you gain a few this can really frustrate your enemies and help you control the field and make sneak attacking easier.

Force Punch – like battering force but with one attack and less likelihood of pushing them around and a melee attack.

Vampiric Touch – deal lots of damage gain lots of temporary HP – not bad.

Heatstroke – it deals damage… and then you also get a ray of exhaustion, except you are more likely to have spell focus for evocation than for necromancy so the DC will be higher and they take penalties for being in armor or heavy clothing.  A nice combination spell.

Blacklight is a good option. It can better than invisibility because it works as a debuff and See Invisibility will not help the enemy. The AT just have to be careful because it can make the rest of the group kind useless in some combats.

4th level:

Dimension Door – A basic getting around spell, useful for grapples and what not too.

Touch of Slime – it’s not doing to deliver sneak attack damage, but it is Con damage and can be a nice fire and forget spell for an opponent.

Detect Scrying – the worst thing that can happen to a scout is to be trying to sneak in and find out you’ve been watched the entire time.  With detect scrying you’ll know if someone is watching you and then you can give off false trails to the sensor without letting them know you know.

Dragon’s Breath – This is a versatile spell that allows you to choose the damage type and method of delivering it.  Once you have you have your capstone dragon’s breath really lets you be more prepared than you realize.

Shout – with your capstone it’s an alright spell, without it avoid this one.  It’s sonic damage which is nice but it’s low damage and you should have enough different choices of damage types that you don’t really need this one.  The kicker however can be nice if you know you are hunting magic users.

Greater Invisibility – This is a great ability, but you probably only take it as a spell if you go wizard – the ninja sorcerer will have enough vanish and later AT greater invisibility rounds that he can probably avoid this spell.

Calcific touch – I generally like this spell but it doesn’t offer much for the AT.  The problem is the spell is all kicker and no damage.

Enervation – a good general spell but you aren’t going to be able to add anything to it – as such it might be one to pass on.

Firefall – when you have your capstone this has a lot of potential – it’s damage in a large area and blinds your opponents and targets multiple saves meaning you’re likely to get at least some effect from it.  You can use it earlier but the damage is pretty low… but damage and blinding means more damage from sneak attack later.

Echolocation – Bards LOOK AT THIS SPELL!  Mind blank doesn’t stop it, and you can use it in darkness, fog, blindness and other conditions to still get sneak attack damage.  Wizards and sorcerers you are going to want this one as a fifth level spell too.

Freedom of Movement – Bards another nice spell – prevent grapples, being paralysis and the like.

5th level and above:  At this point campaigns diverge wildly on what you can expect, and you are at the point of basically being a more caster type now.  Spells beyond this point will still need your consideration but if you basically follow the general guidelines provided here and for other full casters you will do fine.

Equipment:
What you take with you can be the difference between success and defeat.  As such you should carefully consider each piece of equipment you take with you.

If you go with vivisectionist alchemist grab some alchemical splash weapons at lower levels -- you still get your intelligence bonus to damage with them and this can help you provide back up and damage at lower levels.

A wand of mage armor will serve you well through level 6 easily and after that you might want to consider a silken ceremonial robe enhanced with additional abilities like shadowed and glamored on it.  Most of the wands you’ll want to look at are going to be things you’ll use out of combat often (like infernal healing) -- if you use vivisectionist to get here don’t forget that you can use wands of cure light wounds and any spell that is on your extract list as well.

Rings:
There are several nice rings you’ll want to look at:
Ring of Protection -- Always useful though if you can avoid being in places where you’ll get hit you might want to focus on other rings instead.
Ring of the Ram -- A variable ranged attack that is force and can move enemies -- not a bad choice for only 8,600 gp.  It’s basically a wand on your finger leaving your hands free.
Ring of Invisibility -- This has a lot of out of combat potential.  In combat it’s still helpful, but each use is a standard action spent.  As such I would rely more on ninja vanish or greater invisibility then, and this when you want to sneak.
Ring of Freedom of Movement -- lets face it AT’s tend to piss people off just as much as wizards, but tend to do so from closer ranges, as such having a ring of freedom of movement can spell the difference between a bear hug of death and living to kill the target.
Ring of X-ray Vision -- Yeah I like seeing what is behind door number 1 before I open door number 3.
Rings of Wizardry -- these could be useful at lower levels... as it is they probably come too late to really benefit you and for the wizards pearls of power are cheaper.
Ring of Chameleon Power -- Hey a +10 to stealth is nice, and disguise self is simply useful for a scout/spy.  However a hat of disguise and stealth armor leaves your ring slots open for more useful rings, and could be cheaper.
Ring of Ferocious Action - Combine this with Twist Away feat.

Wondrous Items:

Quick Runner’s Shirt - Good and cheap item. Sometimes an extra move action can make all the difference.
Robes of Arcane Heritage -- if you went sorcerer you are probably going to want these to get a bit more out of your sorcerer levels.
Robes of the Archmage -- Armor bonus, Save bonus, bonus on spell penetration... yeah this item is vastly underpriced (even as expensive as it is).  If you come across one use it, it saves your cloak slot for other things as well as giving that bonus on spell penetration rolls.
Robe of Stars -- It’s not bad.  I would rather have other things, but for its price you get a lot of nice abilities and a luck bonus on saves.  It’s also cheaper than robes of the archmage so don’t think twice about getting one if you can.
Robe of Useful Objects -- it’s cheap, it’s in a slot that’s rarely used at low levels and can come with all sorts of useful tools.


Headband of mental stuff -- you are going to want one appropriate for your spell casting and you’ll want to keep it maxed out as much as you can.  Later on look for one that gives bonuses to wisdom as well if you are intelligence based and if you are charisma based go ahead and find one that gives bonuses to all three states... it’s expensive but the extra skill points and bonuses to key skill checks and save throws is a very good tool.

Pearls of power -- If you used wizard to get into arcane trickster these are going to be your babies.  Pearls of power for level 1 are cheap and level 2 isn’t that bad either -- grabbing several level 1 and a couple of level 2 pearls at lower levels can really extend your durability.  I would probably grab 4 level 1 pearls before I grab a headband of intelligence +2 personally -- you’ll get more spells out of it and at lower level having a DC that is one less than maximum isn’t likely to kill you either.

Necklaces:
Hand of glory -- this is underpriced in my opinion.  You get daylight once a day, see invisibility once a day and can wear an extra ring.  That’s a wonderful deal, saving you spell slots and allowing you to get an extra item on too.
Necklace of Adaptation -- Hey no one likes drowning or suffocating to death.
Necklace of fireballs -- as a trap this can be very useful -- it can also kill you dead if you screw up... be careful with these but realize they have their use.

Amulet of Blooded - There’s a lot of options. Buy the one you think it works best for you. I suggest the Celestial one. It gives acid and cold resistance + a good attack (no SR) that can deal damage against evil creatures and heal good creatures..

Sniper Googles -- this extends your sneak attack range to near infinite.  Grab them when you can to make the most use out of the closing time that you can get.

Chime of Opening -- but wait a minute -- aren’t you supposed to pick locks?  Well yes, but sometimes you need to pick every lock within range with a single standard action... at those times accept no substitutes.

Cloaks:
Resistance -- Um... yes please?  If you aren’t wearing robes of the archmage you are going to want a cloak of resistance.  Save throws are come in adventuring and even more so for the arcane trickster who is in combat, handling traps, and scouting -- you are likely to see the bulk of the save throws, prepare accordingly.
Minor Displacement -- If you have robes of the archmage or just insane save throws already the cloak of minor displacement will stop about ⅕ of attacks on you from landing.  That can save your bacon on a regular basis so consider a cloak of minor displacement.  Major displacement isn’t nearly as useful since it is a rounds per day item and costs more.
Arachnida -- Spider climb at will is nice, as is the bonus against poisons, and ability to move through webs.  The once per day web can be useful but the save is low and the price isn’t.
Cloak of the Bat -- a minor bonus you can get elsewhere and fly... but only in darkness.  For stealth missions the ability to be a bat might be useful but you can’t cast in such a form... all in all I would generally avoid this one.

Helms and Circlets:
Circlet of persuasion -- if you are a sorcerer based AT go ahead and get it.  Makes you a bit more charming and helps with concentration checks while being fairly cheap.
Helm of Brillance -- this is expensive and potentially dangerous to yourself.  Besides you have real spell casting when it comes to offensive options.  You can probably pass on this one.
Helm of Comprehend languages and Read magic -- two spells for the price of one helmet.  They are useful and it’s not too expensive, if you find one sure use it, but I wouldn’t hunt one down.
Helm of Telepathy -- Detect thoughts allows a will save... at DC 14?  Pass.
Helm of Teleportation -- hey as a get away device this one is nice.  But its pricey -- if you want it sure but it would be at the end of my shopping list.
Helm of underwater action -- If you are going to be around water I would get one -- the ability to breath cast and function underwater is nice.  Otherwise there are cheaper means of taking care of yourself in the odd water situation (in fact that could be the case anyways -- you might want to pass on this one if you have a necklace of adaptation).

Cyclop’s Helm - Simply overpower. By just 5600gp you can choose a dice/day.

Mask of Stony Demeanor - Just 500gp to get +10 competence bonus to lie and +5 do feint.

Magic weapons:
Um... no thanks.  I mean you generally don’t have the BAB to hit with them anyways, and you have a lot of other things you would rather spend your gold on.  I mean sure keep an adamantine something around, and maybe a mithral one for looks... but generally I wouldn’t magic them up, after all if you are using a weapon it’s because magic isn’t working for some reason and in such cases a magic weapon might not work too.  If you aren’t a magus just keep what’s passed down to you on the off chance you need it.  If you used magus to get into AT you should however really look at a sword of subtlety -- you can buff the enhancement bonus with your arcane pool and it gives an extra +4 to hit and damage when delivering sneak attacks -- which isn’t a bad thing at all.

Magic Armors:

Haramaki is a good option. Not for the AC bonus, but to get some magic stuff like Shadow and Slick. Not that you really need it, however is good to have options.

Rods, metamagic:
The best of these are: Persistent spell, Intensify, Quicken spell, and Reach in my opinion.  Otherwise use them to fill in the gaps of what you need.

Staves:
Generally these are going to be too expensive and you aren’t really going to have the extra slots of the right levels to keep them charged.  You can probably afford to pass on them.
Scrolls and Potions:  
If you go with the wizard and/or vivisectionist whenever you get the chance you should craft scrolls and potions.  These will extend your abilities on the days you don’t craft and can help the party out as a whole later one (passing out potions of cure light wounds can make everyone like you better).  Typically you’ll want spells that don’t rely on caster level or save throws -- so buffs, curing and divination spells will be most of what you want to put on paper or in liquid form.  Remember that you are a member of a team and if you support the rest of your team they will support you too.  Providing them with extra simply makes your case as a valued member that much stronger.

Next up: Tactics of the Arcane Trickster!

First off we need to discuss why knowing what you are doing before you do it is important.  To start this topic I want to do a brief overview of what is called the ‘flight or fight’ instinct.  This instinct is when your body (and your mind) decides if it is going to run or fight in a stressful situation.  No matter which decision is made chemicals pump through your body to give you the best chance to make it out of the situation -- unfortunately these same chemicals aren’t the best in the world for helping you maintain a clear and level head.  This response can be pretty hair-trigger too, for example arguments can cause it... as can video games and yes, even tabletop games like pathfinder (though generally not as full blown).  The second thing that happens in such situations is called technique denigration, this is when you get sloppy doing something that you have practiced or know how to do during periods of stress -- and again applies to everything from actual combat to arguments (“I should have said...”) to video and tabletop games.

So how does this relate to the AT?  In pathfinder most classes and characters have some error room -- a wizard casts the wrong spell in the wrong situation he generally still has other spells left.  A paladin gets a bit too far into the fight and gets flanked she still has her heavy armor and lay on hands to help her get out of it.  Fighters can switch weapons (and occasionally feats).  The AT has a much closer limit however (especially at lower levels) and because of this you have to avoid mistakes as much as possible, you don’t have the spells to waste, and you are incredible squishy (even with focusing on limiting your exposure and using proper defensive techniques).

So how do we avoid mistakes?  By having a game plan ahead of time.  This starts with equipment, carries on to spell choices, and finishes with your tactical choices made in the field.  

Equipment is our starting place because it has the most room for errors, and the best chance to continuously cover weak points.  You want to look for and buy equipment that does one of three things:  improves your durability, covers your physical limitations and finally equipment that lets you know what’s coming.  When I say improve your durability I don’t just mean defenses either -- you need to extend your combat capability and limit the number of spells you need in non-combat situations.  Covering your physical limitations actually builds on improving your durability -- if you are a human you’ll want to look for a source of darkvision for example, if you are an elf you are going to want to probably look at a belt of constitution before one of dexterity, if you don’t have a fly or climb speed you might want to look into slippers of spider climbing.  What you really want here are items that work continuously and compliment your skills and bypasses what you can’t do with skill alone.  Finally anything that lets you see through walls, around corners or spot hidden dangers is great for you.  As an arcane trickster you are uniquely vulnerable when surprised:  You don’t have much for armor or defenses, and you can’t be wasting time and effort to set the battlefield to your advantage once it is joined -- you want to do this beforehand so you don’t waste resources on mistakes or just getting back to even ground with the opposition.

Spell choices are important -- more so than equipment since ever spell for the AT has to be the best spell possible.  Unlike a straight caster you don’t have the extra slots to prepare spells you might not use, and you are spell levels behind even the sorcerer (and possibly bard at some points) so your DC’s aren’t going to be what they could be.  With your kit set you need to pick your spells for high performance, with repeat performance and as much as you can get out of them.

Tactics this is what makes a successful AT.  You see you aren’t some wizard out there playing god, and you aren’t some sorcerer ready to continuously hit your enemy until dead.  Unlike the rogue who might be compared to a submachine gun you don’t have the repeating hit power in one round because of the way spells work.  No you are much more like a sniper or a LAW rocket -- you got limited ammunition, and because of how  hard you hit once they know you are out there they will be gunning for you with extreme prejudice -- so you have to hit them so hard they don’t get back up, and you can’t fire willy-nilly.  Grab as many kicker effects as you can and use them when either you’ll drop an enemy outright, or by using them you set your team up to win and the enemy to lose.  If you burn a pyrotechnics and blind the enemies you can sneak attack them for the rest of the fight without having to spend another second level spell (or other resources), if you can ghoul touch them you can then coup’d’grac them and their fellows have trouble getting near the body.  If your cantrip is packing an extra 3d6 plus a point of strength or dexterity damage as well as the sickened condition you are providing your party with damage and a debuff that makes the enemy more vulnerable and you harder to hit.  Don’t waste time on spells like disintegrate in combat on the enemy -- yes it looks big, but it has three ways to fail and provides nothing other than strict damage.  If you are going to use it in combat do so to take out the wheel on their chariot, or to drop an archway on their heads blocking an exit.  Don’t reveal your nature until you have your head shot and can leave them half dead, dazed, sickened, weakened and tripped right at your fighter’s feet.  At higher levels once you have your capstone these same bonuses will help you out even more -- at this point you are artillery... artillery that lands nerve gas right in their laps along with a fragmentation air-burst shell.  Once you have hit them back off and let your party do the rest while you mop up threats.  Do not be afraid to ready an action to snipe enemy spell casters -- thanks to your unique talents you are more likely to be in a position and have the actual ability to disrupt the caster and unlike the fighter you were going to take a standard action anyways so you aren’t losing anything by not full attacking.

Out of combat you will probably be your party’s go to guy for most anything... especially if you have the right equipment and skills.  Don’t be suckered into using up limited resources on your party -- you are not a buff machine, you are not a ‘make non-combat stuff trivial with magic’ guy and your job isn’t to make them feel better either, you are there because you have the skills and abilities no one else has and the unique tactical advantages and disadvantages to really place them well.

The major difference between you and a wizard is thus -- a wizard takes what he is given and changes it to his benefit, you take what you have and change the situation to your enemy’s detriment.

After level 10.

If your game goes on long enough you will get to the point where you have no levels left to take in AT.  So what do you do then?  It really depends -- how did you get into the prestige class?  If you are a magus following up with some levels in eldritch knight could serve you in good stead:  It will get you more BAB and bonus feats as well as improving your casting (with 3 levels in a class for sneak attack, 4 in magus, 10 in AT and 3 in EK you’ll still get 6th level casting too).  If you are a sorcerer dragon disciple might carry some desirable traits with it’s higher hit dice, bonuses to bloodline abilities and general beefiness.  AT’s with bow proficiency might want to look at the arcane archer for 3 levels -- it can extend the range of your blasting, and give you the means to increase your damage (sneak attack a primary target with your arrow, then the blast goes off for more sneak attack damage to him and those near him).  If all else fails go back into your casting class -- more spells and abilities from that will not hurt you at all.  I would probably avoid more levels in your method of gaining sneak attack -- it will have diminishing results at this point since you can gain most of the benefits of more discoveries/talents/tricks with feats and one more d6 of sneak attack damage isn’t likely to really improve you.
