Categories of Classes

A Primer on How to Quickly Figure Out What the Heck You’re Looking at

Foreword

There’s 44 classes, counting the Unchained ones as different. Adding in the NPC classes, we get up to 49. This is overwhelming, even to old hats. Let’s try to simplify things.

        

        There are seven main ways to figure out what a class is supposed to do. They are BAB, Chassis, Hit Dice, Pets, Proficiencies, Skills, and Spells. By figuring out where a class falls in each of these categories, you can get a feel for what each is capable of.

        BAB determines how good a class is at rolling attacks, and thus how reliably they can do damage.

        Chassis broadly describes a style of play.

        Hit Dice determines how much health the class has, and thus how willing to take hits they are.

        Some classes have Pets, which can be sent into battle, or else aid their masters in a specialized way.

        Proficiencies refer to what sort of equipment the class can use.

        Skills describe how many skills the class can become better at each level.

        Magic describes whether a class can use magic easily, and how easily.

BAB

Base Attack Bonus is how often your class gets better at rolling attack dice simply by gaining levels. There are 3 rates of BAB, ¾, ½, and Full.

Full BAB

        If you have Full BAB, you should try to attack things all the time. That’s literally what your character is for. Full BAB classes are also the only ones who should give serious consideration to Combat Maneuvers. Full BAB characters increase their BAB 1 point each level and start at BAB1.

        Classes with Full BAB are Antipaladin, Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bloodrager, Brawler, Cavalier, Fighter, Gunslinger, Monk(Unchained), Paladin, Ranger, Samurai, Shifter, Slayer, Swashbuckler, and Warrior.

¾ BAB

        Classes with ¾ BAB give up some of their combat potential in order to diversify into something else. While they can perform well by rolling attacks, they usually need to expend some resources in order to do it. ⅔ BAB characters start at BAB 0, gain a point at 2nd, 3rd, and 4th level, then plateau at 5th level before repeating the pattern,

        Classes with ¾ BAB include Alchemist, Aristocrat, Bard, Cleric, Druid, Expert, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Kineticist, Magus, Medium, Mesmerist, Monk, Ninja, Occultist*, Oracle, Rogue, Rogue(Unchained), Shaman, Skald, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Vigilante*, and Warpriest.

        The Occultist can use the Trappings of the Warrior to gain Full BAB, and the Vigilante can take the Avenger specialization to do the same.

½ BAB

        Classes with ½ BAB are not meant to make most attack rolls. They can get away with doing a Touch Attack sometimes, but even those can be risky. Their strengths lie elsewhere. They begin at BAB 0, then they increase their BAB at every even level.

        Classes with ½ BAB are Adept, Arcanist, Commoner, Psychic, Sorcerer, Witch, and Wizard.

Chassis

A Chassis is a community term, not found in any rulebook. It broadly describes a style of play that a class can support, and draws on baselines found in the rest of this document. Though most classes can support being built to fit one or two of these definitions, actually achieving any one of these usually excludes the others. For example, a Paladin built with high Strength will have a high resting power level, and so would be in the Martial category and not the Gish category. A Paladin built with high Charisma would be able to lay down devastating Smites and buff himself with spells, thus outperforming a Martial Paladin while the buffs were active. The tradeoff would be that this Paladin wouldn’t be as effective once the spells and Smites were used, dropping this style of play in the Gish category. The five chassises are Martial, Caster, Gish, Oddball, and Skill-Monkey.

Caster

        A Caster must have access to 9th level spells. That’s about the only real requirement. They usually, but not always, give up decent Hit Dice and Proficiencies in exchange. That’s not to say that they can’t buff themselves up to Gish status even without building on purpose for that, just that it will blow through the whole day’s resources for one fight. In fact, some build styles can put these classes in the Gish category before they start buffing.

Casters include Arcanist, Cleric, Druid, Oracle, Psychic, Shaman, Sorcerer, Witch, and Wizard.

Gish

        As near as I can tell, Gish originates as a term describing a Githyanki Warrior Mage from Dungeons and Dragons. For the purposes of this guide, a Gish is any class which uses magic primarily to enhance its performance in combat. A Gish is fully capable of going into combat without buffs, it’s just dicey. Compared to Martials, A Gish usually has higher peaks of performance, with lower valleys of not-buffed capability. They must have at least ¾ BAB and a D8 Hit Die.

        Gishes include Alchemist, Antipaladin, Bard, Bloodrager, Cleric, Druid, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Magus, Medium, Mesmerist, Occultist, Oracle, Paladin, Ranger, Shaman, Skald, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), and Warpriest.

Martial

A Martial Chassis must have Full BAB, D10 Hit Dice, and they either have at least Medium Armor Proficiency, or they are suited to ranged combat, or they get additional armor some other way. Characters built to be Martial want to be making an attack roll every round, relying primarily on equipment and non-magical class features to get the job done. They frequently have the ability to stay in the fight until they run out of hit points, not relying so much on class resources.

Martial Classes include Antipaladin, Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bloodrager, Brawler, Cavalier, Fighter, Gunslinger, Monk(Unchained)*, Paladin, Ranger, Samurai, Shifter, Slayer, Swashbuckler, and Warrior.

The Monk(Unchained) and may not wear armor at all, but gains a high AC through other means. Similarly, the Swashbuckler may only wear Light armor, but compensate with AC from high Dexterity and class abilities.

Oddball

        Oddball classes don’t fit neatly into any of the other categories. It’s not that they can’t do any of them, it’s just that they fall outside my definitions by emulating a chassis with a class feature.

        The Kineticist is closest to a Gish, using their Kinetic Blast to deal damage and their obscene Constitution to soak hits. A Kineticist can be built more like a Caster or a Martial, but never a Skill-Monkey.

        The Monk is closest to a Martial. They have Bonus Feats like a Ranger, a pool of boosting energy like a Swashbuckler, and they can pretend to have Full BAB and Two-Weapon Fighting when they perform a Flurry of Blows. In fact, the Monk(Unchained) qualifies as a Martial, and may be a good place to start if you want a bare-fisted fighter and aren’t sure what you’re doing.

        The Adept, Aristocrat, Commoner, and Expert are NPC classes, intentionally underpowered and uncomplicated so that a Game Master can throw in an NPC that works well enough on the fly. The Adept is most like a Caster, the Aristocrat splits the difference between a Skill-Monkey and a Martial, and an Expert is most like a Skill Monkey. The Warrior actually qualifies as a Martial. The Commoner is bad at everything.

Skill-Monkey

        A Skill-Monkey’s job is to unlock the door, find the trap, identify the object, argue the case, and interact with the world in a non-violent manner. They must have 8 Skill Points/Level, 6 Skill Points a level and a class ability that enhances skills, or a focus on the Intelligence attribute.

        Skill-Monkeys include Alchemist, Arcanist, Bard, Inquisitor, Investigator, Magus, Ninja, Psychic, Rogue, Rogue(Unchained), Witch, and Wizard.

Hit Dice

Hit dice determine how many Hit points you get when you level up. The bigger the die, the more likely the class is meant to be in the front line taking hits.

Barbarian

Barbarians (Chained or Unchained) are the only class with a d12 Hit Die. This means they get a maximum of 12 hit points every time they level up. If your cheese involves getting hit a lot, start here.

D10

Where people who don’t foam at the mouth start when they want to be tanky. These classes include Antipaladin, Bloodrager, Brawler, Cavalier, Fighter, Gunslinger, Monk(Unchained), Paladin, Ranger, Samurai, Shifter, Slayer, Swashbuckler, and Warrior.

D8

Classes with a D8 for a hit die would prefer to be ignored by the enemy, thank you. They can rotate through the front line when a tougher character goes down, but they do not want to be the center of attention for long. They include Alchemist, Aristocrat, Bard, Cleric, Druid, Expert, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Kineticist*, Magus, Medium, Mesmerist, Monk, Ninja, Occultist, Oracle, Rogue, Rogue(Unchained), Shaman, Skald, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Vigilante, and Warpriest.

The Kineticist tends to have a very high Constitution score, which can cause them to have lots of extra hit points, even if their hit die is relatively small.

D6

If a class with a D6 gets hit, it probably means someone screwed up. These guys do not have the hit points to go blow for blow with much of anything without serious enhancements. They include Adept, Arcanist, Commoner, Psychic, Sorcerer, Witch, and Wizard.

Pets

A pet is any secondary character that comes with your main character. There are numerous ways to get pets, from Feats, to Archetypes, to outright buying one. The classes which come with pets before looking at those exceptions are Cavalier, Druid, Hunter, Ranger, Samurai, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Witch, and Wizard.

Animal Companion

An animal companion is usually a creature of the Animal category, though it is possible to get Vermin, Magical Beasts, and Plants as Animal Companions. Regardless, they must be taught a number of tricks and ordered to perform them through the use of the Handle Animal skill. This limits how creatively they can be used. Though Animal Companions can be specialized into either combat or support roles, they’re usually front line damage dealers.

Animal Companions have really weird BAB progression which is somewhere between ¾ and ½, but frontloaded so they start more powerful. They have a D8 Hit Die, start with 2, and increase their HP every even level except for level 2. They follow the same progression for skill ranks. All this frontloads them as extremely capable combatants at level 1, before a Martial catches them around level 5, leaving them completely behind at level 10.

Classes which come with Animal Companions are Cavalier, Druid, Hunter, Ranger and Samurai. The Druid and Hunter have full access to the list of Animal Companions. The Cavalier and Samurai only have access to horse-like mounts. The Ranger has a shorter list of companions, and those companions are only available after level 4, and are effectively four levels behind the Ranger unless he takes a feat.

Familiars

        A Familiar aids a caster. Where Animal Companions tend to have animal level intelligence, Familiars are quite intelligent and more like an NPC who is bound to a character. There are numerous creative uses for a Familiar, but the most common one is as a scout.

        A Familiar always has the same BAB score as their master, half the health of their master, and the same skill rank allocation as their master, unless the base animal version has more ranks in a particular skill by default. Because their masters are usually ½ BAB d6 HD Full Casters, Familiars are usually not very impressive combatants, but often have useful skills.

        The classes which come with a Familiar are the Witch and Wizard.

Eidolons and Phantoms

        Summoners(Chained and Unchained) get an Eidolon, and Spiritualists get a Phantom. These are similar to Animal Companions in that they are fully capable of combat, but similar to Familiars in that they are intelligent NPCs under the control of a player character. However, they can be dismissed at will and called back into existence with a short ritual. Further, they don’t heal naturally, and must be healed with magic or the Heal skill. Finally, when slain, they are simply banished for a while and can come back the next day.

Eidolons and Phantoms are nearly identical except that they have different Skills. They are both ¾ BAB characters, except they start at BAB1. They follow the same progression track on HD, gaining a new d10 of health at the same rate that they gain BAB. Eidolons gain 4SP/level, while Phantoms gain 2SP/level, but picks two bonus skills at level 1 as part of its emotional focus and automatically gains skill ranks in those.

Proficiencies

        This determines what a character can easily use. There’s three categories, Armor, Shields, and Weapons. Of all the categories, this is the one that is the least indicative. It’s full of loopholes and exceptions that make it much looser than the hard expectations set by the rest of the categories, further you can always take a proficiency feat. Elves and Dwarves can break this expectation particularly well. Elves have racial proficiency with Longbows, Longswords, and Rapiers, allowing them to turn some classes into ranged combatants. Dwarves are always proficient with battleaxes, heavy picks, and warhammers, which can allow them to take some classes into a front-line role.

Armor

        As much as Hit Dice, this information determines how close to the action a character is willing to get. I’m only going to list the heaviest armor a class can wear, if it can wear Heavy armor, it can wear Light armor. If you are not proficient with armor, you apply its Armor Check Penalty to your attack rolls.

                Heavy

        Antipaladin, Aristocrat, Cavalier, Fighter*, Paladin, Samurai, Warpriest, and Warrior.

        * In addition to being merely proficient in Heavy Armor, Fighters eventually learn how to ignore movement and skill penalties in Medium and Heavy armor, while increasing how much of their Dexterity they can use while wearing Armor.

                Medium

        Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bloodrager, Cleric, Druid#, Hunter, Inquisitor, Medium*, Occultist, Oracle, Ranger, Shaman, Shifter#, Skald, Slayer, and Vigilante.

        * The Medium can channel the Guardian spirit to get access to Heavy armor.

        # The Druid and Shifter may not wear armor made from metal.

                Light

        Alchemist, Bard, Brawler, Expert, Gunslinger, Investigator, Kineticist, Magus*, Mesmerist, Ninja, Rogue, Rogue(Unchained), Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), and Swashbuckler.

        * The Magus gains proficiency in heavier armor as they level up.

                None

        Adept, Arcanist, Commoner, Monk, Monk(Unchained), Psychic*, Sorcerer, Witch, Wizard

        *Because the Psychic doesn’t actually feel the penalties from wearing armor without proficiency because they don’t make attack rolls or suffer from somatic spell failure, it’s popular to equip them with Heavy Armor.

Shields

        If a class in the Light and Heavy category has an *, it means that class can use Tower Shields. If a class isn’t mentioned here, it is not able to use shields.

                Light and Heavy Shields

        Antipaladin, Aristocrat*, Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bard, Bloodrager, Brawler, Cavalier, Cleric, Druid, Fighter*, Hunter, Inquisitor, Occultist, Oracle, Paladin, Ranger, Samurai, Shaman, Shifter, Skald, Slayer, Vigilante, Warpriest, Warrior*

                Buckler

        Swashbuckler.

                Psychic

        Because the Psychic doesn’t actually feel the penalties from wearing shields without proficiency because they don’t make attack rolls or suffer from somatic spell failure, it’s popular to equip them with one.

Weapons

        There’s three categories of Weapon Proficiency, Martial, Simple, and Special. All classes are proficient with some weapons, but some don’t get access to a full category, which is why I have the Special category for them. Generally speaking, it’s easier to make a combat character with access to more weapons, but this can be partially bypassed by picking a race with weapon proficiencies, like Dwarves or Elves. If I put an asterisk next to it, it means that class has notable exceptions to its weapon proficiencies.

                Martial

        Martial weapons are usually the most powerful weapons, including the ever popular Greatsword, Longsword, Scimitar, Longbow, and others.

        Antipaladin, Aristocrat, Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bloodrager, Cavalier, Fighter, Gunslinger*, Hunter, Magus, Occultist, Paladin, Ranger, Samurai, Skald, Slayer, Swashbuckler, Vigilante, Warpriest, and Warrior.

        *Gunslingers are proficient with all firearms.

                Simple

        Simple weapons offer a decent range of options, but they often lack the power or utility of Martial weapons. Popular offerings in this category are the Morningstar, Longspear, and Crossbow.

        Adept, Alchemist, Arcanist, Bard*, Brawler*, Cleric*, Expert, Inquisitor*, Investigator*, Kineticist, Medium*, Mesmerist*, Ninja*, Oracle, Psychic, Rogue*, Rogue(Unchained)*, Shaman, Sorcerer, Spiritualist*, Summoner, and Witch.

        Bards are also proficient with the longsword, rapier, sap, short sword, shortbow, and whip.

        Brawlers are also proficient with the handaxe, short sword, and axe-gauntlet, dwarven heavy, axe-gauntlet, dwarven light, bayonet, brass knuckles, cestus, dan bong, dwarven war-shield, emei piercer, fighting fan, gauntlet, heavy shield, iron brush, katar, klar, light shield, madu (leather/steel), mere club, punching dagger, rope gauntlet, sap, scizore, spiked armor, spiked gauntlet, spiked shield, tekko-kagi, tonfa, tri-bladed katar, unarmed strike, waveblade, wooden stake, wushu dart

        Clerics are also proficient with the favored weapon of their deity

        Inquisitors are also proficient with the hand crossbow, longbow, repeating crossbow, shortbow, and the favored weapon of her deity.

        Investigators are also proficient with the hand crossbow, rapier, sap, shortbow, short sword, and sword cane

        Mediums can channel the Champion to gain proficiency in all Martial weapons, plus an Exotic weapon of their choice.

        The Mesmerist is also proficient with the hand crossbow, sap, sword caneUE, and whip.

        The Ninja is also proficient with the kama, katana, kusarigama, nunchaku, sai, shortbow, short sword, shuriken, siangham, and wakizashi.

        The Rogue, Chained and Unchained, are also proficient with the hand crossbow, rapier, sap, shortbow, and short sword.

        The Spiritualist is also proficient with kukris, saps, and scythes.

 

                Special

        Commoner, Druid, Monk, Monk(Unchained), Shifter, Wizard.

The Commoner picks any one Simple weapon to be proficient in.

Druids and Shifters are proficient in nature themed weapons, including the club, dagger, dart, quarterstaff, scimitar, scythe, sickle, shortspear, sling, and spear.

Monks are proficient in a variety of exotic, monk themed weapons, including the club, crossbow (light or heavy), dagger, handaxe, javelin, kama, nunchaku, quarterstaff, sai, shortspear, short sword, shuriken, siangham, sling, and spear.

Wizards have an extremely restricted list of weapons, including only club, dagger, heavy crossbow, light crossbow, and quarterstaff.

Skills

        How many skill points a level you get generally determines how easily you can affect the world through non-violent, non-magical means. This isn’t a perfect metric, as Witches and Wizards only have 2 skill points a level, but often end up with as many skill points as a Rogue because they have high Intelligence, and the same exception applies to any class that favors Intelligence.

8/level

Classes with 8 skill points a level are generally expected to be able to handle every situation with some degree of aptitude. They include Ninja, Rogue, and Rogue(Unchained)

6/level

        Classes with 6 skill points a level are usually well rounded, but they often have an area of specialty. They include Bard, Expert, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Mesmerist, Ranger, Slayer, and Vigilante.

4/level

        A class that gets 4 skill points a level is specialized in a relatively small number of skills, but has enough points to do those well, and perhaps diversify a bit. They include Alchemist, Aristocrat, Barbarian, Barbarian(Unchained), Bloodrager, Brawler, Cavalier, Druid, Gunslinger, Kineticist, Magus, Medium*, Monk, Monk(Unchained), Occultist, Oracle, Samurai, Shaman, Shifter, Skald, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), and Swashbuckler.

        The Medium can channel the spirit of the Trickster to get 6/level.

2/level

        If you get 2 skill points a level, you either have two skills maxed, or a variety of skills with one or two points in them. Some players of these classes dump their Intelligence, locking in their lack of skills to focus on something else. These classes include Adept, Antipaladin, Arcanist, Cleric, Commoner, Fighter, Paladin, Psychic, Sorcerer, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Warpriest, Warrior, Witch, and Wizard.

Magic

        The classes which are able to perform any magic whatsoever are Adept, Alchemist, Antipaladin, Arcanist, Bard, Bloodrager, Cleric, Druid, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Magus, Medium, Mesmerist, Occultist, Oracle, Paladin, Psychic, Ranger, Shaman, Skald, Sorcerer, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Warpriest, Witch, and Wizard.

Flexibility

        There are two modes of flexibility, Prepared and Spontaneous

                Prepared

        A Prepared Caster must memorize spells at the beginning of the day to cast later. In order to cast the same spell multiple times, the Prepared Caster must prepare multiple uses of that spell. This allows a Prepared Caster to customize the spells at their disposal day by day. Prepared Casters include Adept, Alchemist, Antipaladin, Arcanist, Cleric, Druid, Investigator, Magus, Paladin, Ranger, Shaman, Warpriest, Witch, and Wizard

                Spontaneous

        Spontaneous Casters are able to cast any spell they know without needing to prepare it ahead of time, but the tradeoff is that they usually have to pick a small number of spells that they know how to cast. Spontaneous Casters include Arcanist, Bloodrager, Hunter, Inquisitor, Medium, Mesmerist, Occultist, Oracle, Psychic, Skald, Sorcerer, Spiritualist, Summoner, and Summoner(Unchained). Yes, Arcanists are on both lists, they’re weird.

Magic Source

        Depending on the source of the magic, different rules apply.

                Alchemy

        Alchemist and Investigators prepare potion-like items called Extracts at the beginning of each day. Using these is treated like drinking a potion, and so doesn’t have any component except a material component. The main restriction is on the target of the Extract, only the person who made them can use them because they lose their power when given away. However, it is possible for the brewer to research how to keep them potent and then distribute them.

Arcane

        Arcane magic is created through manipulation of the fabric of reality. Some do this intentionally through study, others just have a knack for it and do it instinctively. All Arcane magic is subject to spell failure chance if the caster is wearing armor because it interferes with the movements in the somatic components, though some like the Bard and Magus get special exceptions that allow them to wear Light armor. Further, some Arcane casters like the Wizard need to have access to minor materials for most spells, while others like the Sorcerer can ignore that requirement. Most Arcane spells have somatic and verbal components, though specific spells may drop one, or both of these. The full list includes Arcanist, Bard, Bloodrager, Magus, Skald, Sorcerer, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), Witch, and Wizard.

                Divine

        Divine magic is sourced magic. The magic is a gift from outside the self, whether that is from nature, a deity, or a concept. It is not subject to spell failure due to armor, but is otherwise similar to Arcane magic, including having somatic and verbal components. Divine casters include Adept, Antipaladin, Cleric, Druid, Hunter, Inquisitor, Oracle, Paladin, Ranger, Shaman, and Warpriest

                Psychic

        Psychic spells never include Verbal or Somatic components, and only rarely include material components. Instead, they have emotion and thought components which require the caster to be in full control of their psyche, which means that they cannot cast while affected by harmful emotion effects, and outside distractions are particularly difficult. Psychic casters include Medium*, Mesmerist, Occultist, Psychic, and Spiritualist.

        The Medium can channel the spirit of the Hierophant to use Divine magic.  

Spell Access and Progression Styles

        Spell access refers to the most powerful spell a class can cast. The three categories here are 9th, 6th, and 4th. Inside each access category are one or two Spell Progression Styles. Different casting classes gain new spell levels at different class levels, and they gain additional spell slots at different rates. I refer to these as Progression Styles. Note that with a high enough score in your casting attribute, you can increase how many spell slots you have, especially low level spells.

9th

        A 9th level caster can access the most powerful, reality bending spells on the list, and usually casting spells is the primary reason the class exists. 9th level casters include Arcanist, Cleric, Druid, Oracle, Psychic, Shaman, Sorcerer, Witch, and Wizard.

                        Sorcerer Style

        Sorcerer Style classes start with 3 level 1 spell slots. Starting at 4th level and every even level afterwards, they get a new spell level. They gain a spell slot for each spell level known every character level, up to 6 slots per level. This style can cast a lot of spells, but gains access to new spell levels slightly slower. All Sorcerer Style classes are 9th level casters, and they also happen to be Spontaneous Casters as well.

        Sorcerer Style classes include the Oracle, Psychic, and Sorcerer.

Wizard Style

        Wizard Style classes start with 1 level 1 spell. They gain a new spell level at every odd level, and they gain a new spell slot for each level they have every other class level after the first slot, up to 4 slots per spell level. This results in gaining access to new spell levels more quickly, at the cost of having fewer total spell slots. All Wizard Style classes are 9th level casters, and they all happen to be Prepared Casters as well.

        Wizard Style classes include Arcanist, Cleric, Druid, Shaman, Witch, and Wizard.

                6th

        A 6th level caster can eventually cast level 6 spells, no higher. These classes usually supplement something else with their magic, rather than focus on it exclusively. Further, they all follow the same Spell Progression Style, starting with 1 level 1 spell slot. Starting at 4th level and every third level thereafter, these classes gain a new spell level. The second and third slot at each spell level come at the second and third character level after a spell level has been unlocked, before waiting by 2 class levels to get the fourth, then waiting 3 class levels to get the 5th, with only 5 spell slots per spell level.

6th level casters include Alchemist, Bard, Hunter, Inquisitor, Investigator, Magus, Mesmerist, Occultist, Skald, Spiritualist, Summoner, Summoner(Unchained), and Warpriest.

        Adepts can cast a maximum spell level of 5th.        

                4th

        4th level spellcasters can only ever cast a 4th level spell at most. These classes are not primarily known for their spells, and always use their spells to supplement something else, usually murder. They include Antipaladin, Bloodrager, Medium*, Paladin, and Ranger.

        The Medium can channel the spirit of the Archmage to reach 6th level casting.

                        Bloodrager Style

        Bloodrager Style classes get their first level 1 spell slot at level 4, then they unlock a new spell slot every three class levels. They spend 5 class levels having only one spell slot of each spell level, then they gain a new spell slot every four class levels, capping out at 4 spell slots. Further, they all happen to be Spontaneous Casters.

        Classes that follow the Bloodrager style include the Bloodrager and the Medium

                        Paladin Style

        Paladin Style classes follow the Bloodrager Style, except that they gain access to a spell slot at level 4 only if their attributes are high enough to allow them to. They get a proper spell slot at 5th level. Paladin style repeats the pattern of the Bloodrager afterwards, with the exception that they start each spell level with “0” spell slots as before, gaining a proper spell slot the next class level. Further, they all happen to be Prepared Casters.

        Antipaladins, Paladins, and Rangers follow this style.
